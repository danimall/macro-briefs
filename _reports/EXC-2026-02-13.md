## Executive Summary

Exelon Corporation is one of the largest regulated utility holding companies in the United States, serving over 10.7 million customers through six fully regulated transmission and distribution (T&D) subsidiaries. Since spinning off its competitive generation business as Constellation Energy (CEG) in early 2022, Exelon has operated as a pure-play regulated utility, offering investors a predictable earnings stream, a solid ~3.6% dividend yield, and a low-beta profile (0.43). The company reported full-year 2025 GAAP EPS of $2.73 and non-GAAP EPS of $2.77, beating its own guidance range, and has guided 2026 EPS to $2.81–$2.91.

On the growth front, Exelon has outlined an ambitious $41.3 billion capital spending plan through 2029 and projects 8% rate base growth over that period. This signals a commitment to infrastructure modernization and grid reliability. However, concerns persist around negative free cash flow generation, stagnating or declining approved returns on equity (ROEs) from regulators, and a valuation that, while not expensive, does not offer a compelling margin of safety. Analyst sentiment is mixed, with JP Morgan maintaining a Neutral rating and Wolfe Research downgrading the stock to Peer Perform.

Overall, Exelon represents a classic defensive utility holding — suitable for income-oriented investors seeking stability, but unlikely to deliver outsized total returns. The stock is currently trading at $47.55 versus an average analyst price target of ~$50.82 (as of December 2025), suggesting limited near-term upside of approximately 7% before dividends.

**Market Capitalization:** ~$48.0 billion
**Current Price:** $47.55
**52-Week Range:** $40.81 – $48.51
**Beta:** 0.43
**Dividend Yield:** ~3.6%
**Forward P/E:** 18.3x | **Trailing P/E:** 15.9x

Exelon operates through six regulated utility subsidiaries: Commonwealth Edison (ComEd) in Illinois, PECO Energy in Pennsylvania, Baltimore Gas and Electric (BGE) in Maryland, Pepco Holdings in the District of Columbia, Atlantic City Electric in New Jersey, and Delmarva Power in Delaware and Maryland. The company's business model is centered on the regulated delivery of electricity and natural gas, with revenues primarily determined through regulatory rate cases.

Following the February 2022 spinoff of Constellation Energy, Exelon became a pure-play regulated utility. This transformation significantly reduced earnings volatility and commodity price exposure, but also removed the higher-growth generation business from its portfolio.

### Capital Expenditure & Free Cash Flow

Exelon has outlined a **$41.3 billion capital plan** through 2029, a significant commitment to grid modernization and reliability improvements. While this drives rate base growth, it also results in **negative free cash flow** — a common characteristic of capital-intensive regulated utilities but a key concern for investors prioritizing cash flow generation. The company must rely on a combination of operating cash flow, debt issuance, and potential equity financing to fund its capital program.

### Balance Sheet

While detailed balance sheet data was not available in the provided financial statements, Exelon's regulated utility model provides relatively stable cash flows that support investment-grade credit ratings. The company's substantial capex plan will likely increase leverage over time, though regulatory recovery mechanisms should help manage this. The high beta-adjusted reliability of cash flows typically affords Exelon access to favorable debt markets.

### Dividend

At a current price of $47.55 and an approximate annual dividend of ~$1.71 per share (implied by the 3.6% yield), the payout ratio on 2025 GAAP EPS of $2.73 is approximately **63%**. This is within the typical 60–70% range for regulated utilities and suggests the dividend is well covered, though negative free cash flow means the dividend is technically funded partially through external financing.

## Growth Analysis

### Rate Base Growth

The headline growth metric for Exelon is the projected **8% rate base growth through 2029**, driven by the $41.3 billion capital investment plan. Rate base growth is the primary earnings driver for regulated utilities, as higher rate bases translate to higher allowed earnings (subject to regulatory approval of ROEs).

### EPS Growth Trajectory

- **2025 Actual EPS (non-GAAP):** $2.77
- **2026 Guidance Midpoint:** $2.86
- **Implied Year-over-Year Growth:** ~3.2%

The near-term EPS growth of ~3% is modest and lags the 8% rate base growth, which suggests that regulatory lag, declining ROEs, and other headwinds are compressing the pass-through to earnings. Over a longer horizon, management has historically targeted 5–7% EPS growth, though some analysts have expressed skepticism given stagnating approved ROEs.

### Demand Drivers

Exelon's service territories — including major metropolitan areas like Chicago, Philadelphia, Baltimore, and Washington D.C. — position it to benefit from:

- **Electrification trends** (EVs, heat pumps)
- **Data center demand growth** (particularly in the Mid-Atlantic corridor)
- **Infrastructure modernization** mandates
- **Population density** providing a stable customer base

However, the regulatory environments in several of its jurisdictions (notably Illinois and Maryland) can be challenging, with political pressure to keep rates low.

## Risk Assessment

### Regulatory Risk (High)
As a fully regulated utility, Exelon's earnings are fundamentally determined by regulatory outcomes. Declining approved ROEs — a trend noted by analysts — directly compress earnings growth. The political dynamics in jurisdictions like Illinois (where major energy legislation has imposed obligations) and Maryland add uncertainty.

### Capital Execution Risk (Moderate)
The $41.3 billion capex plan is ambitious. Any delays, cost overruns, or failure to achieve timely regulatory recovery could impair returns and increase leverage beyond target levels.

### Interest Rate Sensitivity (Moderate)
Utility stocks are bond proxies. Sustained higher interest rates would increase borrowing costs for Exelon's capital program and could compress the stock's valuation multiple. Conversely, rate cuts would be a tailwind.

### Free Cash Flow Concerns (Moderate-High)
Negative free cash flow means Exelon depends on external capital markets for funding. While this is common for growth-oriented regulated utilities, it creates vulnerability to credit market disruptions and potential dilution from equity issuances.

### Macro/Weather Risk (Low-Moderate)
Favorable weather boosted 2025 earnings above guidance. This introduces variability, as unfavorable weather in future periods could cause earnings misses.

### ESG Considerations (Low)
As a T&D-focused utility (post-CEG spinoff), Exelon has limited direct emissions exposure. Its role in enabling electrification is broadly positive from an ESG perspective.

## Investment Thesis

### Bull Case
- **8% rate base growth through 2029** provides a clear, multi-year earnings growth runway, supported by the $41.3B capital plan
- **3.6% dividend yield** provides meaningful income with a sustainable ~63% payout ratio
- **Low beta (0.43)** offers defensive characteristics in volatile markets
- **Data center and electrification demand** in Exelon's service territories could accelerate load growth beyond baseline forecasts
- **Constructive regulatory outcomes** (as seen in 2025 with favorable resolution of proceedings) could drive earnings upside
- **Average analyst target of $50.82** implies ~7% upside plus dividends for a potential ~10–11% total return

### Bear Case
- **Negative free cash flow** means the company is not self-funding its growth; external financing introduces execution and dilution risk
- **Stagnating/declining approved ROEs** compress the translation of rate base growth into earnings growth, as evidenced by only ~3% near-term EPS growth vs. 8% rate base growth
- **Regulatory headwinds** in key jurisdictions (Illinois, Maryland) could worsen, limiting rate recovery
- **Rising interest rates** would increase financing costs and pressure the stock's yield-based valuation
- **Limited upside catalyst** — the stock is trading near the upper end of its 52-week range ($48.51 high) with mixed analyst sentiment (JP Morgan Neutral, Wolfe Peer Perform)
- **Post-spinoff identity** as a pure T&D utility lacks the growth narrative that commands premium multiples in today's market

## Conclusion

Exelon Corp is a well-positioned, large-scale regulated utility with a predictable earnings profile and a meaningful dividend. The company's ambitious $41.3B capital plan and 8% rate base growth target provide a credible multi-year growth story. However, the translation of rate base growth into EPS growth has been disappointing (~3% near-term), negative free cash flow remains a concern, and the stock's valuation is broadly fair at ~18.3x forward earnings.

**Overall Recommendation: HOLD**

Exelon is appropriate for income-oriented, risk-averse investors seeking steady dividend income (~3.6%) and modest capital appreciation potential. The total return profile of ~8–10% annually (dividend + EPS growth) is adequate but not compelling enough to warrant an overweight position relative to the broader market or more attractively valued utility peers. Investors should monitor regulatory outcomes, particularly approved ROEs and rate case resolutions, as these are the primary swing factor for the investment thesis. A pullback toward the low-$40s (closer to the 52-week low of $40.81) would present a more attractive entry point.
