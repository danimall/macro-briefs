## Company Overview

Carlisle Companies, headquartered in Scottsdale, Arizona, operates as a building products company focused on roofing, waterproofing, insulation, and related building envelope solutions through its Carlisle Construction Materials (CCM) segment. The company has undergone significant portfolio transformation over the past several years, divesting non-core businesses to focus on higher-margin building products. The stock has a beta of 0.90, consistent with its position as a mid-cycle industrial/building products company — cyclical but somewhat less volatile than the broader market.

The company's primary end markets are commercial reroofing and new commercial construction. Commercial reroofing demand is partly driven by an aging building stock (roofs typically need replacement every 15-25 years), providing a degree of recurring demand. New commercial construction is more directly tied to economic cycles, interest rates, and business investment.

## Growth Analysis

The growth thesis for CSL rests on several pillars:

1. **Commercial reroofing cycle:** The installed base of commercial roofs in the US continues to age. Industry estimates suggest 60-70% of Carlisle's revenue is tied to reroofing/repair rather than new construction, providing a more stable demand base than pure construction exposure would suggest. However, even reroofing demand is deferrable in tight economic conditions — building owners can extend roof life through repairs rather than full replacement.

2. **Vision 2030 strategy:** Carlisle has been pursuing margin expansion through portfolio simplification, operational efficiency, and pricing discipline. The Q4 beat suggests this strategy is delivering, but without detailed segment financials, the durability of margin improvement is difficult to assess.

3. **Construction recovery narrative:** One analyst cited anticipated Fed rate cuts and construction recovery as key catalysts. This thesis faces direct challenge from the current macro environment: Q4 GDP at 0.7%, mortgage rates at 6.25%+, and the FOMC facing an unsolvable information matrix between cutting into elevated inflation or holding through weakening growth. Rate cuts sufficient to meaningfully stimulate construction may not materialize in 2026.

4. **Input cost risk from oil:** Carlisle's roofing products use petroleum-based materials (asphalt, polyiso insulation). With oil at $98-108 (central estimate) and a ceiling of $115-130 if SPR buffer depletes, input cost inflation is a direct margin headwind. The company has historically demonstrated pricing power sufficient to pass through raw material increases, but there is typically a 1-2 quarter lag, and in a stagflationary environment, pricing power may face resistance from cost-conscious building owners deferring projects.

Growth score is constrained by the macro headwinds: GDP 0.7%, retail sales -0.2%, oil-driven input cost inflation, and uncertainty around rate cuts that are central to the recovery thesis. Carlisle's underlying business positioning is solid, but the next 2-3 quarters look challenging.

## Valuation Assessment

At $341.40 and ~19x forward earnings, CSL trades at a reasonable multiple for a specialty building products company with strong market position. The stock is down 21.7% from its 52-week high of $435.92 and up 16.3% from its 52-week low of $293.43.

**Peer context:** Without detailed peer comparison data, the 19x forward multiple can be contextualized against the broader industrials/building products universe. This is neither cheap nor expensive — it's roughly in line with where a well-run building products company should trade absent either strong growth acceleration or recession risk. The problem is that recession probability is 38-42% per the macro framework, which suggests the multiple should carry a discount.

**One analyst target of $428** (from a Seeking Alpha article dated March 9, 2026 when the stock was at $374) implies 25% upside from current levels. That target was set before the most recent selloff and appears to assume rate cuts and construction recovery that have not materialized and face increasing uncertainty.

**Downside scenario:** In a recession, building products companies typically see earnings declines of 15-25%. A 20% earnings decline on ~$18.15 trailing EPS would yield ~$14.50 EPS. At a trough multiple of 15-16x (typical for cyclicals in recession), that implies $217-$232 — roughly 32-36% downside. At a more modest 10% earnings decline and 17x multiple, the stock would trade to ~$278 (18% downside).

**Upside scenario:** If construction recovery materializes and earnings grow 10-15% to ~$20-21/share, a 20-22x multiple would imply $400-$462 (17-35% upside).

The risk-reward is roughly symmetric at current levels, which supports a HOLD rather than a strong directional call.

## Competitive Landscape

Carlisle holds strong market positions in commercial roofing, particularly in single-ply roofing membranes (TPO, EPDM, PVC). Key competitors include:

- **GAF (Koch Industries, private):** Largest US roofing manufacturer
- **Firestone Building Products (Holcim):** Major commercial roofing competitor
- **Johns Manville (Berkshire Hathaway):** Insulation and roofing products

Carlisle's competitive advantages include: (1) integrated building envelope approach (roofing + insulation + waterproofing), which creates specification advantages with architects and contractors; (2) contractor loyalty through training programs and warranties; (3) scale in manufacturing and distribution. These constitute a moderate moat — switching costs are meaningful in commercial roofing because contractors are trained on specific systems, and building owners value warranty continuity.

The building products industry benefits from several structural tailwinds: aging commercial building stock, energy efficiency mandates driving demand for better insulation/roofing systems, and the labor intensity of roofing installation creating barriers to disruption. These factors support the 7.0 competitive position score.

## Risk Assessment

**Macro/Cyclical Risk (HIGH):** The stagflationary environment is particularly unfavorable for building products companies. Rising oil prices increase input costs (asphalt, petrochemical-based materials) while weakening economic growth reduces construction activity. GDP at 0.7% with contracting employment and retail sales creates demand headwinds. Recession probability of 38-42% is directly relevant — construction is one of the most cyclically sensitive sectors.

**Input Cost Inflation (HIGH):** Oil at $98-108 with potential upside to $115-130 directly affects Carlisle's raw material costs. Asphalt prices track crude oil with a lag. Polyiso insulation uses petrochemical feedstocks. While Carlisle has historically passed through costs, the lag creates 1-2 quarters of margin compression, and a stagflationary environment limits pricing power.

**Interest Rate Sensitivity (MODERATE-HIGH):** Commercial construction activity is sensitive to borrowing costs. With rates at 6.25%+ and the Fed facing constraints on cutting (core PCE at 3.1%), the anticipated rate-cut catalyst may not arrive. The Section 301 trade probes add 6-12 months of uncertainty to business investment decisions, further suppressing construction starts.

**Insider Selling (MODERATE):** The February 2026 insider selling is substantial. CEO Koch exercised options and sold ~$15M in stock on February 10 at prices around $412-415. CFO Zdimal sold ~$9.5M the same day. Vice Chair Schwar sold ~$2.5M on February 12. While these transactions involved option exercises (suggesting tax/planning motivations), the magnitude and near-simultaneous timing across three senior executives is notable. All sales occurred at prices 17-22% above the current stock price, which means insiders captured value that outside investors who bought on the Q4 earnings pop have since lost.

**Data Limitation Risk:** The absence of detailed financial statements (income statement, balance sheet, cash flow) in the provided data prevents analysis of leverage, working capital trends, free cash flow generation, and capital allocation priorities. This alone limits conviction.

## Conclusion

Carlisle Companies is a well-managed building products company with genuine competitive advantages in commercial roofing. The Q4 2025 earnings beat and management's confident 2026 outlook support the fundamental quality of the business. However, the current macro environment — stagflation with 0.7% GDP growth, $100+ oil directly inflating input costs, 38-42% recession probability, and uncertain rate-cut timing — creates meaningful headwinds to both demand and margins over the next 2-3 quarters.

The 22% drawdown from the 52-week high makes the stock more attractive than it was in early February, but the risk-reward is approximately balanced at ~19x forward earnings given cyclical risks. Insider selling of $27M+ by three senior executives at $405-417 in February adds a cautionary data point. Without detailed financial statements to assess leverage, free cash flow, and balance sheet resilience, increasing conviction in either direction is difficult.

**Recommendation: HOLD.** CSL is a quality franchise in a challenging macro environment. The stock would become more interesting below $300 (roughly 15-16x forward earnings), where cyclical risk would be better compensated. Above $380, the risk-reward shifts unfavorably given the macro headwinds. At $341, it sits in no-man's-land. Investors with existing positions should hold; new capital should wait for either a more attractive entry point or clearer macro improvement.
