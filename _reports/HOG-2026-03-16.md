## Executive Summary

Harley-Davidson is a ~$2B market cap motorcycle manufacturer trading at $17.83, down 27% over the past year and 43% from its 52-week high of $31.25. The Q4 2025 report was severely negative: a $279.3M net loss (-$2.44 EPS) on revenue of $390.6M, down 9.4% YoY. Full-year EPS of $2.78 masks the deterioration embedded in the back half of the year — the company earned enough in H1 to offset a catastrophic H2, and the trajectory is worsening. The company absorbed $67M in Trump administration tariffs in 2025, with management warning of higher costs in 2026.

The stock screens as optically cheap at 6.3x trailing earnings with a 4.3% dividend yield, but the earnings quality is poor and the macro setup is hostile. Oil at $98-108 directly pressures both Harley's input costs (steel, logistics) and its customer base (discretionary spending on $25K+ motorcycles when gasoline costs are rising). Wells Fargo issued a downgrade warning of 17% further downside; UBS cut its target to $19 from $27; DA Davidson lowered to $25. The insider activity is mixed — CEO Artie Starrs bought $286K in stock on March 9, but director James Farley sold $121K on February 25.

Given the confirmed stagflationary macro regime, secular decline in heavyweight motorcycle demand, tariff headwinds, and deteriorating earnings trajectory, HOG falls squarely into the consumer discretionary AVOID category outlined in the world model.

## Company Overview

Harley-Davidson is the iconic American heavyweight motorcycle manufacturer, headquartered in Milwaukee, Wisconsin. The company operates through three segments: Harley-Davidson Motor Company (HDMC — motorcycles, parts, accessories, and general merchandise), Harley-Davidson Financial Services (HDFS — retail and wholesale financing, insurance), and LiveWire (electric motorcycles). The company has ~112M shares outstanding and a market capitalization of approximately $2.0B.

HOG's core customer demographic skews older (median age ~50), higher-income, and male. This demographic profile creates both brand loyalty and a secular challenge: the company has struggled for over a decade to attract younger and more diverse riders to offset its aging core base.

## Growth Analysis

Harley-Davidson faces structural growth challenges across its core business:

**Volume Decline:** The heavyweight motorcycle market in the U.S. has been in secular decline. Harley's U.S. retail sales have trended downward from ~241K units in 2006 to roughly 130-140K in recent years. Q4 2025 revenue declining 9.4% YoY suggests further volume erosion.

**LiveWire (Electric):** The electric motorcycle subsidiary has failed to gain traction. LiveWire was spun as a growth vehicle, but unit volumes remain negligible relative to the core business, and the EV motorcycle market remains nascent. This segment likely contributed to the Q4 loss.

**International Exposure:** Harley has EU exposure that faces retaliatory tariffs, compressing margins on approximately 25-30% of total sales that go overseas.

**Demographic Headwind:** The median Harley customer's age continues to rise. Efforts to attract younger riders through smaller-displacement models and lifestyle branding have produced modest results at best.

No data supports a credible path to revenue growth in the near term. The 2026 outlook, as referenced in the February 10 earnings release, likely reflects caution given tariff uncertainty and weak consumer sentiment.

3. The secular volume decline means there is no plausible multiple expansion catalyst

## Competitive Landscape

Harley-Davidson has a genuine moat in brand identity within the heavyweight cruiser/touring segment. The brand commands loyalty, community engagement (H.O.G. chapters), and pricing power within its niche. This moat has been durable for decades.

However, the competitive environment is shifting:
- **Indian Motorcycle (Polaris):** Gaining share in the heavyweight segment with competitive product
- **Japanese OEMs (Honda, Yamaha, Kawasaki):** Dominate the broader motorcycle market and increasingly push into Harley's touring segment
- **Electric competitors:** Zero Motorcycles, Energica, and others compete with LiveWire in a small but growing segment

The moat protects market share within a shrinking addressable market, which limits its value as a competitive advantage.

## Risk Assessment

**Macro Sensitivity (SEVERE):**
- Harley sells $20K-$45K discretionary vehicles to consumers facing $100+ oil, declining real wages, and deteriorating employment (NFP -92K). This is the worst possible macro setup for heavyweight motorcycle sales.
- Gasoline price increases directly reduce motorcycle riding frequency and purchase intent among prospective buyers.
- HDFS credit portfolio faces delinquency risk as consumer balance sheets deteriorate — motorcycle loans are among the first to default in recessions.

**Tariff Risk (HIGH):**
- $67M absorbed in 2025, rising in 2026
- Section 301 probes targeting 60 economies could further increase input costs
- EU retaliatory tariffs compress international margins

**Secular Decline (STRUCTURAL):**
- Aging customer demographic with inadequate new rider acquisition
- Heavyweight motorcycle segment structurally shrinking in developed markets

**Credit Risk (MODERATE-HIGH):**
- HDFS carries billions in motorcycle loan receivables
- Subprime motorcycle lending exposure unknown but likely material given the customer acquisition challenge

**Regulatory/ESG:**
- Emissions regulations increasingly favor electric, where Harley's LiveWire has failed to gain scale
- Noise regulations in European cities could restrict riding areas

## Investment Thesis

### Bull Case
- Trailing 6.3x P/E and 4.3% dividend yield provide valuation floor
- CEO open-market purchase of $286K on March 9 signals management conviction
- Brand remains among the strongest in any consumer category
- Share repurchase program provides price support
- If tariff costs stabilize and consumer sentiment improves in H2 2026, EPS could recover to $3.50-4.00, implying 25-40% upside from current price at a 7x multiple
- DA Davidson's $25 target implies significant upside

### Bear Case
- Q4 loss of -$2.44 EPS could be the beginning of a multi-quarter deterioration, not an anomaly
- Wells Fargo warns of 17% further downside (~$15)
- $100+ oil directly suppresses both input costs and end demand
- Tariff costs rising from $67M to potentially $100M+ in 2026
- HDFS credit portfolio faces rising delinquencies in recession
- Secular volume decline means even a recovery merely returns to a lower-high trajectory
- Dividend cut probability rises if 2026 EPS falls below $2.00
- Consumer discretionary BUY calls in the calibration dataset averaged -1.54% — this sector is where the analytical system has its worst track record

### Why The Bear Case Dominates

The bull case requires multiple conditions to improve simultaneously: tariffs stabilize, oil falls, consumer confidence recovers, and credit quality holds. The bear case requires only the current trajectory to continue. In a confirmed stagflationary regime with all six dimensions pointing negative, the probability distribution is skewed toward the bear case.

The insider buying from the CEO ($286K) and two directors is worth noting but insufficient to override the fundamental picture. The amounts are small relative to compensation packages, and one director (Farley) sold $121K during the same period.

## Conclusion

**Recommendation: AVOID**

Harley-Davidson is a strong brand in a structurally declining market, facing cyclical headwinds from stagflation, tariffs, and consumer spending deterioration simultaneously. The 6.3x trailing P/E is a value trap in the current environment — the trailing earnings are stale, and the Q4 loss trajectory suggests 2026 earnings will be materially worse than 2025.

The calibration data explicitly flags Consumer Discretionary BUY calls as destructive (-1.54% average), and the world model confirms active consumer contraction with five independent headwinds. HOG's exposure to oil prices (both input costs and customer demand sensitivity), tariff escalation, and recession risk makes it among the most vulnerable consumer discretionary names in the current regime.

The stock could become interesting at $12-14, where it would price in a more realistic 2026 EPS of $1.50-2.00 at a 7-8x multiple. At $17.83, the risk-reward is unfavorable. Wells Fargo's implied ~$15 target appears more realistic than DA Davidson's $25, given the macro context.
