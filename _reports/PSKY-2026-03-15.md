## Executive Summary

Paramount Skydance Corporation is the post-merger entity formed from the Skydance Media acquisition of legacy Paramount Global, now trading at $9.73 — within 2% of its 52-week low of $9.54 and 53% below its 52-week high of $20.86. Q4 2025 results showed $8.15B in revenue (+2% YoY) but a $573M net loss ($0.52/share diluted), with losses widening versus the prior year. The company is simultaneously pursuing a leveraged acquisition of Warner Bros. Discovery assets at approximately $30/share, a deal that would substantially increase an already-stressed balance sheet.

The investment case requires confidence in three concurrent bets: (1) that the Skydance merger integration delivers operational improvement, (2) that the WBD acquisition closes and creates synergy value exceeding the debt incurred, and (3) that the secular decline in linear TV can be offset by streaming subscriber growth. Without detailed financial statements — income statement, balance sheet, and cash flow are all missing from public filings in our dataset — quantifying downside risk is difficult, which itself constitutes a risk factor. At a ~$10.8B market cap with a 2% dividend yield and 0.37 beta, this is priced as a distressed media conglomerate, and the price action suggests the market agrees.

## Company Overview

Paramount Skydance Corp (PSKY) is a US-based media conglomerate formed through the merger of Paramount Global and Skydance Media, with David Ellison (son of Oracle founder Larry Ellison) serving as CEO. The company operates across filmed entertainment (Paramount Pictures), television (CBS), and streaming (Paramount+). The merger closed in late 2025, and Q4 2025 was the first full quarter of combined operations.

The company is headquartered in New York and maintains the paramount.com web presence. It trades as Class B common stock, implying a dual-class share structure that concentrates voting power — typical for media companies but limiting for minority shareholders.

## Growth Analysis

**Streaming:** Paramount+ subscriber growth was described as modest ("inching up"). The streaming pivot is the company's primary growth vector, but Paramount+ ranks behind Netflix, Disney+, Amazon Prime Video, Apple TV+, and Max in scale and content spend. Streaming profitability remains elusive.

**WBD Acquisition Optionality:** If Paramount successfully acquires HBO Max and the Warner Bros. studio, it would gain one of the most valuable streaming libraries (HBO content) and a premier film/TV production operation. Netflix dropping its bid (confirmed by multiple sources in late February 2026) removes the primary competitor for these assets. This is a genuine positive — Paramount appears to be the last bidder standing for valuable assets. The strategic logic of combining Paramount+ with HBO Max content is sound. The question is execution and financing.

**Linear TV Decline:** CBS remains a top broadcast network but linear TV advertising revenue is in structural decline. Q4 results confirm this trend is accelerating ("TV slump"). There is no visible path to reversing this.

**Content Pipeline:** Post-merger, the combination of Paramount Pictures, Skydance's production capabilities, and the CBS television franchise provides a broad content engine. The Skydance team has a track record of commercially successful films (Top Gun: Maverick, Mission: Impossible). Whether this translates to sustained content superiority at scale is uncertain.

## Valuation Assessment

At $9.73, the stock is trading near trough valuation:
- **Market cap/Revenue:** Approximately 0.33x annualized revenue ($10.8B / ~$32B). This is cheap for a media company with premium IP, but justified given operating losses.
- **No P/E calculable:** The company is unprofitable.
- **Enterprise Value:** Unknown without balance sheet data, but legacy Paramount carried approximately $14-15B in long-term debt pre-merger. If that level persists, EV is likely $25-27B, putting EV/Revenue closer to 0.8x — more reasonable but still reflecting distress.
- **Dividend yield of 2%** provides minimal income support but could be at risk if losses persist.

Comparable media companies: Disney trades at ~2x revenue, Netflix at ~8x, Warner Bros. Discovery at ~1x (pre-acquisition). Paramount's 0.33x market cap/revenue reflects the market pricing in both the operating losses and the execution risk of the WBD deal.

**The stock is cheap on assets but expensive on earnings (which are negative).** Value traps in declining media businesses are well-documented (ViacomCBS from 2019-2024 being one example — the predecessor of this very entity).

## Competitive Landscape

The streaming media industry has consolidated into a handful of scaled competitors:
1. **Netflix** (~300M+ subscribers, profitable, $83B bid for WBD shows financial capacity)
2. **Disney** (Disney+/Hulu/ESPN, approaching streaming profitability)
3. **Amazon Prime Video** (subsidized by Prime membership)
4. **Apple TV+** (subsidized by hardware ecosystem)
5. **Paramount Skydance** (Paramount+, distant 5th in scale)

Paramount's competitive position is structurally weak in streaming but valuable in content production. The Paramount Pictures library (Godfather, Indiana Jones, Mission: Impossible, Star Trek, Transformers) and CBS's broadcast position provide durable IP. If the WBD deal closes and HBO content is integrated, the combined streaming offering would be significantly more competitive — potentially the #3 or #4 global streaming platform.

The risk is that even with HBO content, the combined entity would be subscale relative to Netflix and Disney, while carrying substantially more debt.

## Risk Assessment

**1. M&A Execution Risk (High).** The WBD acquisition is a bet-the-company transaction for a $10.8B market cap entity. Financing in the current credit environment (credit cascade probability 55-60% per world model) could be extremely expensive. Integration of two large media companies simultaneously (Skydance merger just completed; WBD integration would begin immediately after) strains management bandwidth.

**2. Secular TV Decline (High).** Linear TV advertising revenue is in structural decline. CBS revenue erosion will continue. No management team has successfully reversed this trend at any broadcast network.

**3. Streaming Losses (Medium-High).** Paramount+ is subscale and unprofitable. Adding HBO Max content could help but also adds the streaming losses from that business.

**4. Leverage (High).** Without balance sheet data, precise debt levels are unknown, but legacy Paramount carried ~$14-15B in debt. The WBD acquisition would add substantially more. Debt service in a rising-rate, stagflationary environment is expensive.

**5. Dual-Class Structure.** Class B common stock with concentrated voting power limits minority shareholder influence. David Ellison controls strategy.

**6. Macro Headwinds.** Advertising revenue correlates with economic activity. In a stagflationary environment with GDP at 0.7% and retail sales declining, ad spend contraction is likely. Streaming subscriber growth also decelerates during economic stress as consumers cut discretionary subscriptions.

**7. Dividend Sustainability.** A 2% yield on a company generating quarterly losses of $573M raises questions about dividend sustainability. A dividend cut would remove a key price support.

## Investment Thesis

### Bull Case
- WBD acquisition closes at favorable terms, creating a combined Paramount+/HBO Max streaming platform with ~100M+ subscribers and the industry's deepest combined content library.
- Skydance management (Ellison, Shell) successfully executes cost synergies from both the Skydance merger and WBD integration, reaching streaming profitability by 2027-2028.
- At 0.33x revenue, the stock is pricing in permanent impairment; any improvement in operating trajectory provides significant upside. A re-rating to even 0.5x revenue implies $14.50/share (+50%).
- Netflix's exit from the WBD bidding eliminates the primary competitor for valuable assets. Paramount may be able to negotiate improved terms.

### Bear Case
- WBD acquisition closes but loads the balance sheet with $30B+ in additional debt during a credit crisis, resulting in a debt-service burden that prevents investment in content and forces asset sales at distressed prices.
- Linear TV decline accelerates, and streaming losses persist, creating a negative FCF spiral.
- The stock's 53% decline from 52-week highs reflects rational repricing, and the "cheap on revenue" argument ignores that revenue is declining in the most profitable segment (TV) while growing in the unprofitable segment (streaming).
- Macro stagflation reduces advertising spend and consumer willingness to pay for streaming subscriptions simultaneously.
- The dual-class structure and Ellison family control mean minority shareholders have limited recourse if strategy fails.

## Conclusion

Paramount Skydance at $9.73 is a speculative turnaround story layered on top of an uncompleted bet-the-company acquisition, managed by a new team that has run the combined entity for one quarter with widening losses. The absence of financial statement data in our dataset makes fundamental analysis incomplete — we cannot verify debt levels, cash balances, free cash flow generation, or segment profitability. What we can verify is: (1) Q4 losses of $573M, (2) only 2% revenue growth, (3) stock near 52-week lows, and (4) a transformational acquisition being pursued during tightening credit conditions.

The calibration document indicates Consumer Discretionary BUY calls average -1.54% and require an elevated bar. Media is adjacent to consumer discretionary in cyclical sensitivity. The score of 4.3 falls below the 5.0 AVOID threshold established in calibration. The combination of operating losses, M&A execution risk, secular headwinds, and missing financial data supports an AVOID recommendation. Investors seeking media exposure should wait for: (a) clarity on WBD deal terms and financing, (b) evidence of Skydance merger synergies in Q1/Q2 2026 results, and (c) a macro environment more supportive of advertising spend recovery.
