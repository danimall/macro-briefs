## Executive Summary

Associated Banc-Corp is a $4.0B market cap regional bank headquartered in Green Bay, Wisconsin, trading at $24.35 with trailing and forward P/E of ~8.9x and a 3.9% dividend yield. The company is in the midst of executing a multi-phase strategic plan that delivered record organic household growth in 2025, and has received all regulatory approvals for its acquisition of American National Corporation as of March 12, 2026. Management is guiding for 9-10% C&I loan growth in 2026 while expanding into Omaha, Kansas City, and Dallas.

The valuation is inexpensive relative to both the broader market and regional bank peers. The stock has recovered from its April 2025 low of $18.32 but sits roughly 17% below its 52-week high of $29.52. The core tension is between a bank that is executing operationally at an above-average level and a macro environment where recession probability is 38-42%, credit cascades are building, and net interest margins face pressure from an uncertain rate path. The acquisition adds integration risk at a point when the credit cycle may be turning.

## Company Overview

Associated Banc-Corp operates as a bank holding company through its subsidiary Associated Bank, N.A. The bank serves commercial, retail, and wealth management customers primarily across Wisconsin, Illinois, and Minnesota, with recent expansion into new metros (Omaha, Kansas City, Dallas). With approximately $41B in total assets (based on peer context and market cap ratios), ASB is a mid-tier regional bank competing against both larger super-regionals and smaller community banks.

The company filed its most recent 10-K on February 12, 2026, and its last reported earnings date was January 22, 2026. CEO Andrew Harmening has been driving a Phase 2 strategic plan focused on organic growth, geographic diversification, and operational efficiency. The pending American National Corporation merger, which received regulatory approval on March 12, 2026, represents the next step in the bank's growth strategy.

## Growth Analysis

**Organic Growth Trajectory:**
CEO Harmening characterized 2025 as "our strongest year for organic household growth since we began tracking a decade ago with net growth in all [categories]." This is a meaningful inflection for a bank that historically competed in mature Midwest markets.

**Geographic Expansion:**
The push into Omaha, Kansas City, and Dallas represents a deliberate move into higher-growth MSAs. Dallas in particular offers substantial commercial banking opportunity. The 9-10% C&I loan growth target for 2026 implies the new markets are contributing meaningfully to the pipeline.

**American National Corporation Acquisition:**
Regulatory approval received March 12, 2026. The financial terms and accretion details are not in the provided data, but the acquisition adds scale and potentially expands the deposit franchise. In the current macro environment, bank M&A carries elevated integration and credit risk — the acquired portfolio was underwritten under different conditions than what the next 12-18 months may bring.

**Constraints on Growth:**
The Seeking Alpha analysis explicitly flags "decelerating loan growth" alongside resilient NII. If total loan growth is slowing while C&I is accelerating, that implies CRE or consumer lending is contracting. In a stagflationary environment with rising oil costs and weakening consumer spending, this mix shift is prudent but may cap total asset growth.

## Valuation Assessment

At 8.9x forward earnings, ASB trades at a discount to the KBW Regional Banking Index (which typically trades at 10-12x). The 3.9% dividend yield provides a floor. Price-to-book is not available in the data but is likely in the 1.0-1.2x range based on comparable regionals.

**Relative Value Context:**
- The stock is 17% below its 52-week high of $29.52 (reached in early February 2026)
- It is 33% above its 52-week low of $18.32 (reached during the April 2025 selloff)
- The 11.25% total price return over the trailing year (from ~$22.22 to ~$24.72) modestly outperforms a flat-to-negative broader market

The valuation discount is partially justified: ASB is a smaller regional with concentrated geographic exposure, and the macro environment creates genuine earnings risk. The discount is not extreme enough to constitute a compelling margin of safety given the credit cycle concerns.

## Competitive Landscape

ASB competes in the upper tier of community/regional banking — large enough to offer commercial banking, wealth management, and treasury services, but without the scale advantages of super-regionals like U.S. Bancorp or M&T Bank. The Midwest footprint is both a strength (stable, relationship-driven markets) and a limitation (slower secular growth vs. Sun Belt peers).

The geographic expansion into Dallas, Omaha, and Kansas City is a strategic response to this limitation. If executed well, it diversifies revenue and reduces dependence on Wisconsin's economy. If executed poorly — particularly in a downturn — it adds credit risk in unfamiliar markets.

The American National acquisition, assuming it completes on schedule, would add incremental scale. In regional banking, moderate consolidation can create value through branch overlap elimination and deposit funding advantages.

## Risk Assessment

**Macro/Credit Cycle Risk (Primary):**
The world model documents recession probability at 38-42% for Q1-Q2 2026 and credit cascade probability at 55-60%. Regional banks are directly exposed to both channels. Rising unemployment (4.4% and climbing), declining consumer spending, and $5/gallon diesel all increase the probability of loan losses. ASB's C&I-heavy growth strategy is somewhat more resilient than a CRE-heavy book, but commercial borrowers are not immune to stagflation.

**Net Interest Margin Risk:**
The Seeking Alpha analysis notes "lower asset yields" alongside resilient NII. If the Fed holds rates (20-25% probability of no cuts in 2026 per the world model) while deposit costs remain elevated, NIM compression could accelerate. The flat forward P/E (same as trailing) suggests the market already prices some of this risk.

**Acquisition Integration Risk:**
Merging American National Corporation while simultaneously expanding into three new MSAs during a potential recession creates execution complexity. Management bandwidth is finite. Integration costs will flow through the income statement over the next 2-4 quarters.

**Interest Rate Sensitivity:**
Without detailed balance sheet data, the asset-liability mismatch sensitivity is unclear. Regional banks with significant fixed-rate loan portfolios and floating-rate deposit bases face margin pressure if rates remain elevated. The preferred stock analysis from Seeking Alpha (favoring Series F preferred over baby bonds) suggests the capital structure is actively managed.

**Concentration Risk:**
Heavy Wisconsin exposure means ASB's fortunes are partially tied to the state's manufacturing and agricultural sectors, both of which face headwinds from tariffs (Section 301 probes targeting 60 economies) and energy costs.

## Investment Thesis

**Bull Case:**
- 8.9x forward P/E and 3.9% yield provide downside support
- Management executing well on organic growth (record household growth in 2025)
- 9-10% C&I loan growth target represents above-peer organic growth
- American National acquisition received full regulatory approval; accretive if credit holds
- Geographic diversification into higher-growth markets reduces Midwest concentration
- No insider selling; routine acquisitions suggest management alignment
- Stock is 17% below recent highs, offering a potential entry point if macro stabilizes

**Bear Case:**
- Recession probability of 38-42% directly threatens loan quality and earnings
- Credit cascade probability of 55-60% could create broader banking system stress
- NIM faces compression from lower asset yields and stubborn deposit costs
- Acquisition integration during a downturn adds operational and credit risk
- Expansion into unfamiliar markets (Dallas, Omaha, Kansas City) increases underwriting risk at the worst possible time in the cycle
- Small cap ($4B) means limited liquidity and higher beta to banking sector sentiment
- Oil at $98-108 and stagflation directly impact Midwest commercial and agricultural borrowers

## Conclusion

ASB is a competently managed regional bank executing an above-average organic growth strategy, trading at a reasonable discount to peers. The 3.9% dividend yield and sub-9x P/E provide some valuation cushion. However, the macro environment presents genuine and underappreciated risks to the earnings trajectory: rising credit costs, potential NIM compression, and acquisition integration during a downturn.

The "Why Not BUY?" answer is clear: in a confirmed stagflationary regime with 38-42% recession probability and credit cascade risk at 55-60%, regional banks face asymmetric downside. The April 2025 selloff (stock fell from $22.62 to $18.32 in 7 trading days, a 19% drawdown) demonstrates how quickly sentiment can turn against the sector. The acquisition adds a layer of execution risk that would not exist in a more benign environment.

**Recommendation: HOLD.** The valuation is not compelling enough to offset the macro headwinds. A more attractive entry point would be below $22 (roughly 8x what should be trough earnings of ~$2.50-2.60 in a mild recession), where the dividend yield would approach 4.3-4.5% and the downside would be more limited. Alternatively, a clear signal that credit quality is holding post-acquisition (Q2 2026 earnings) would provide the confirmation needed to upgrade.
