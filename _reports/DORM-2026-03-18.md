## Executive Summary

Dorman Products, a leading motor vehicle aftermarket parts supplier, reported Q4 2025 results on February 25, 2026, that included a $56.7M goodwill impairment charge, dragging GAAP net income to $11.6M ($0.38/share) for the quarter. Full-year 2025 sales rose to approximately $2.1B, and management guided 2026 revenue growth of 7-9% with an explicit warning that operating margins will "reduce temporarily" in the first half. The stock dropped 9.1% on the earnings release and has continued declining, falling from ~$127 pre-earnings to ~$101 as of March 17 — a cumulative 20% decline in three weeks and 40% below the September 2025 high of $166.89.

At ~15x forward earnings on $6.64 EPS, DORM is optically cheap relative to its historical trading range. The company benefits from a structural tailwind: the average age of vehicles on US roads continues to rise, expanding the addressable aftermarket parts market. Management is investing in complex electronics (a category with higher margins and fewer competitors) and diversifying supply chains away from China. These are the right strategic moves.

The problem is timing. Tariff exposure is material — Dorman sources significantly from China, and Section 301 probes targeting 60 economies create incremental cost uncertainty. The goodwill impairment signals that at least one acquired business (likely the SuperATV specialty vehicle segment) is underperforming purchase-price expectations. Margin guidance explicitly calls for near-term compression. In a stagflationary macro with diesel at $5/gallon (directly raising freight costs for a parts distributor), consumer wallet pressure on discretionary vehicle repairs, and rising input costs, the earnings trajectory for the next two quarters carries meaningful downside risk.

## Company Overview

Dorman Products designs, manufactures, and distributes replacement parts for motor vehicles in the aftermarket. The company operates across three segments: Light Duty (the core business), Heavy Duty, and Specialty Vehicle. Dorman's competitive advantage lies in its ability to reverse-engineer OEM parts and bring them to market at lower price points, often being first-to-market with replacement parts for newer vehicle technologies. The company serves major aftermarket distributors (AutoZone, O'Reilly, Advance Auto Parts, NAPA) and has approximately 30.2M shares outstanding, yielding a current market cap of ~$3.0B.

The company's guidance for 7-9% revenue growth in 2026 would imply roughly $2.25-2.29B in sales. However, management explicitly stated margins will compress temporarily in H1 2026, which means EPS growth will lag revenue growth meaningfully in the near term.

- Complex electronics category (cameras, sensors, control modules) is a growing segment where Dorman has first-mover advantage and limited competition
- 7-9% revenue growth guidance for 2026 implies meaningful organic expansion

## Valuation Assessment

At $101.30, DORM trades at approximately 15.3x trailing EPS of $6.64 and ~15.3x forward estimates (assuming similar EPS). This is below the company's historical average multiple of 18-22x. The stock has traded as high as $166.89 in September 2025.

Roth Capital reduced its price target to $162 on March 2 (an 11% cut from prior), implying ~60% upside from current levels. The magnitude of that implied upside suggests either (a) Roth believes the selloff is overdone, or (b) the target hasn't fully captured the deterioration in near-term earnings estimates.

The valuation is reasonable if management executes on the 7-9% growth target and margins recover in H2 2026. But the conditional nature of that recovery — dependent on tariff outcomes, consumer spending resilience, and successful supply chain restructuring — means the discount is partially earned. Comparable aftermarket parts companies (GPC at a higher multiple but with its own issues, LKQ) generally trade at 12-18x forward earnings. DORM at 15x is in the middle of this range, suggesting fair value rather than a deep bargain.

## Competitive Landscape

Dorman occupies a defensible niche as a reverse-engineering specialist that is typically first-to-market with aftermarket replacements for newer OEM components. This matters more as vehicles become more complex electronically. Key competitors include:

- **Genuine Parts Company (GPC):** Much larger ($15B+ revenue), broader distribution network, but currently struggling with its own operational issues (221x GAAP P/E, execution risk from auto/motion segment split)
- **LKQ Corporation:** Global presence in aftermarket/recycled parts
- **Standard Motor Products (SMP):** Closest direct competitor in the engineered aftermarket replacement category

Dorman's competitive moat is moderate. The reverse-engineering capability and catalog breadth (over 118,000 SKUs) create customer stickiness with major distributors. The shift into complex electronics widens the moat since the technical barrier to entry is higher. However, the company faces pricing pressure from Chinese imports and private-label programs at its major distributor customers.

## Risk Assessment

**Tariff Risk (HIGH):** Dorman sources meaningfully from China. Section 301 tariff probes and potential escalation of existing tariffs directly hit input costs. The company is actively diversifying supply chains, but reshoring/nearshoring takes 12-24 months to implement and typically carries higher structural costs.

**Macro/Consumer Risk (MODERATE-HIGH):** In a stagflationary environment with rising gas prices and declining consumer confidence, discretionary vehicle repair spending is vulnerable. While basic maintenance (brakes, filters) is non-discretionary, many aftermarket parts purchases can be deferred.

**Goodwill Impairment Risk (MODERATE):** The $56.7M charge signals overpayment on acquisitions. Remaining goodwill on the balance sheet (amount unknown from available data) could face further impairment if acquired business performance continues to deteriorate.

**Freight Cost Risk (MODERATE):** Diesel at $5/gallon directly increases distribution costs for a parts distributor. Dorman's ability to pass these costs through to customers depends on competitive dynamics and contractual arrangements.

**Customer Concentration Risk (MODERATE):** Major customers (AutoZone, O'Reilly, Advance Auto Parts) have significant bargaining power and could pressure Dorman on pricing.

## Investment Thesis

**Bull Case:**

- Complex electronics strategy expands addressable market and raises competitive barriers
- Supply chain diversification, while costly near-term, reduces long-term China risk

- Margin compression in H1 2026 could be deeper or longer-lasting than guided, particularly if tariff escalation accelerates

## Conclusion

DORM presents a mixed picture: a fundamentally sound aftermarket parts business with structural tailwinds, trading at a historically cheap valuation, but facing near-term headwinds from margin compression, tariff exposure, goodwill impairment, and a hostile macro environment for its cost structure and demand drivers. The 7-9% revenue growth guidance is achievable but the earnings translation is uncertain given explicit margin compression warnings.

The absence of detailed financial statements in the data feed limits confidence in the balance sheet and cash flow analysis. The goodwill impairment warrants scrutiny on remaining intangible asset values and acquisition strategy.

**Recommendation: HOLD.** The valuation is approaching interesting territory, but the combination of margin compression guidance, tariff uncertainty, macro headwinds (diesel costs, consumer spending contraction), and the goodwill impairment overhang argue for patience. An entry below $90 (roughly 13.5x current EPS, representing a discount for near-term earnings risk) would offer a more compelling risk/reward. Monitoring Q1 2026 results (expected May 2026) for evidence of margin trajectory and tariff impact is the appropriate next step.

**Why Not BUY:** Three factors prevent a BUY rating despite the optically cheap valuation: (1) explicit management guidance for margin compression in H1 2026 means earnings could disappoint before they recover; (2) the goodwill impairment raises legitimate questions about capital allocation quality that need further investigation; (3) tariff risk is unresolved and could structurally raise costs by 3-5% given China sourcing exposure, with passthrough uncertainty. The stock could get meaningfully cheaper before it gets better.
