## Executive Summary

AES Corporation announced on March 2, 2026 a definitive agreement to be acquired by a consortium led by Global Infrastructure Partners (a BlackRock subsidiary) and EQT Infrastructure VI fund, with CalPERS and Qatar Investment Authority as co-underwriters, at $15.00 per share in all cash. The stock fell approximately 17% on the announcement date, indicating the market had been pricing in a higher takeout or standalone value. Prior to the deal announcement, AES was trading near $18 (based on the 52-week high of $17.65 and the fact that the stock "tumbled 17% on $15 buyout offer"), suggesting the $15 price represents a discount to recent trading levels.

The current market cap of ~$12.3B implies a share price around $13.70, creating a merger arbitrage spread of approximately 9.5% to the $15.00 deal price. Halper Sadeh LLC has already announced an investigation into whether the $15.00 price is fair to shareholders. The deal's fundamentals are straightforward: AES has a diversified fleet of renewable, natural gas, and coal generation assets plus a regulated utility in Indiana and Ohio, and the consortium buyers are among the largest infrastructure investors globally with ample capacity to close.

For new capital, the risk/reward reduces to a merger arbitrage trade. The spread compensates for regulatory review timeline (likely 6-12 months), potential shareholder litigation, and the small but nonzero risk of deal failure. In a macro environment where the Iran conflict introduces broad market volatility and energy policy uncertainty, the spread appears reasonable but not compelling enough to warrant a BUY.

## Company Overview

AES Corporation is a Virginia-based diversified power company operating globally. Its asset base includes renewable generation (wind and solar), natural gas plants, coal assets, and regulated electric utility operations through its subsidiaries in Indiana and Ohio. The company has been transitioning its portfolio toward clean energy, positioning itself as a beneficiary of AI-driven data center power demand and grid modernization spending.

Key operational metrics from news reports:
- Q4 FY2025 analyst estimates: $0.63 EPS on $3.03B revenue (16% YoY EPS growth, 2.4% revenue growth)
- Prior quarter: $0.75 EPS on $3.35B revenue
- Quarterly dividend: $0.17595/share (annualized ~$0.70/share, yielding ~4.1% on recent prices)
- Forward P/E: 7.3x; Trailing P/E: 10.7x
- Beta: 0.94
- 52-week range: $9.46 - $17.65

The company had been building momentum around data center power demand and AI-related energy infrastructure before the acquisition announcement.

## Financial Analysis

Detailed financial statements were not available in the data feed, limiting granular analysis. Working from available metrics:

**Valuation:** At the $15.00 deal price, using the forward P/E of 7.3x (which corresponds to forward EPS of ~$2.05), the acquisition values AES at roughly 7.3x forward earnings. For a utility with renewable transition optionality and data center contract upside, this is low relative to the utility sector average of 15-18x forward earnings. This gap explains why Halper Sadeh is investigating the fairness of the price.

**Dividend:** The $0.70 annualized dividend represents a 4.7% yield at the deal price, which the acquiring consortium will absorb upon closing. Shareholders will receive $15.00 cash, so dividends continue until close.

**Market Cap:** ~$12.3B at current trading levels, implying shares outstanding of approximately 895 million (using the ~$13.70 implied price).

**Debt:** AES has historically operated with significant leverage typical of utility and IPP companies. The consortium's willingness to take on this debt (GIP and EQT specialize in infrastructure with high leverage tolerance) suggests the capital structure is manageable within infrastructure PE return frameworks.

## Growth Analysis

Prior to the acquisition announcement, AES had several growth vectors:

1. **Data center power demand:** AES was positioning to supply power to hyperscale data centers, a multi-year structural demand driver. The company's partnerships, including with Andrew Ng's AI Fund for Haven Safety AI, signaled strategic alignment with AI infrastructure buildout.

2. **Renewable transition:** The company's shift from coal/gas to renewables created a long-duration growth runway with tax credit support from the IRA.

3. **Grid modernization:** Utility operations in Indiana and Ohio benefit from rate base growth through transmission and distribution upgrades.

4. **Revenue growth trajectory:** Q4 estimates showed 16% EPS growth and 2.4% revenue growth YoY, reflecting improving profitability per unit of revenue.

These growth prospects are now effectively capped at the $15.00 deal price for public shareholders. The consortium is buying the growth at a discount, which is the core complaint of the Halper Sadeh investigation.

## Valuation Assessment

The acquisition price of $15.00/share deserves scrutiny:

**Below recent trading:** The stock had a 52-week high of $17.65, and the 17% decline on the deal announcement implies the stock was trading around $18 immediately before the news. A $15.00 offer represents a 17% discount to the pre-announcement price, which is unusual for take-private deals (typically premiums are 20-40% above pre-announcement levels).

**Context:** AES had fallen from higher levels to a 52-week low of $9.46, suggesting the company faced operational or financial challenges that may have pressured management to accept the bid. The stock's recovery from $9.46 to ~$18 over recent months may have been partially driven by M&A speculation (the Bloomberg report on Feb 27 about GIP/EQT being close to a deal moved shares 5%).

**Forward P/E of 7.3x at deal price** is cheap for a utility with renewable assets and data center demand exposure. Comparable US utilities trade at 14-20x forward earnings. Even adjusting for AES's higher leverage and international risk, 7.3x appears to undervalue the franchise. The consortium is likely underwriting to a 10-15% levered equity IRR, which means they see significant value above $15.

**Merger arb spread:** At ~$13.70 current trading vs. $15.00 deal price, the ~9.5% spread over an expected 6-12 month close timeline annualizes to 10-19%. This is elevated relative to typical strategic M&A spreads (3-5% for high-certainty deals), reflecting market uncertainty about either deal completion risk or timeline.

## Competitive Landscape

AES operates in a competitive utility and IPP market alongside NextEra Energy (NEE), Dominion Energy (D), Southern Company (SO), and Duke Energy (DUK). Its differentiators include:

- **Global diversification:** Operations outside the US provide growth optionality but also introduce currency, regulatory, and political risk.
- **Renewable pipeline:** Significant wind and solar development pipeline, though execution has been uneven.
- **Data center contracts:** Early mover in AI-driven power demand, though competition from other utilities and independent generators is intensifying.
- **Regulated utility base:** Indiana and Ohio operations provide stable earnings but limited growth.

The take-private removes AES from public market competitive comparisons. GIP and EQT will likely accelerate capital deployment into renewables and data center power without the quarterly earnings pressure that constrained public market decision-making.

## Risk Assessment

**Deal completion risk (moderate):** The buyer consortium includes GIP (BlackRock), EQT, CalPERS, and QIA. This is among the most well-capitalized buyer groups possible for infrastructure M&A. Financing risk is minimal. Regulatory review (FERC, state utility commissions, potentially CFIUS given QIA involvement) adds timeline uncertainty. QIA's involvement could draw additional scrutiny given the current Iran conflict and Middle Eastern geopolitical sensitivities.

**Shareholder litigation:** Halper Sadeh's investigation could lead to lawsuits alleging inadequate price. These rarely block deals but can delay close or extract a modest price bump ($0.25-0.50/share in some precedents).

**Macro environment:** The Iran conflict and dual energy crisis create regulatory and political uncertainty around utility acquisitions. Energy security concerns could either support the deal (private infrastructure investors deploying into US energy) or complicate it (heightened scrutiny of foreign sovereign wealth fund involvement through QIA).

**Price bump probability:** Given the discount to pre-announcement trading, there is a 15-25% probability of a competing bid or price increase, potentially to $16-17. AES's data center optionality and renewable assets could attract alternative bidders (Brookfield, KKR Infrastructure, or a strategic utility).

## Investment Thesis

**Bull Case:** The deal closes at $15.00 within 6-9 months, delivering ~9.5% return from current levels. Alternatively, a competing bid emerges or the consortium raises its offer to $16-17 under shareholder pressure, delivering 17-24% upside. The low forward P/E and data center demand growth make AES attractive to other infrastructure buyers.

**Bear Case:** Deal fails due to regulatory issues (CFIUS review of QIA, state utility commission objections, or FERC concerns). AES standalone faces headwinds from higher interest rates compressing utility valuations, execution risk on renewable buildout, and international asset challenges. The stock could revert to the $10-12 range if the deal collapses in a hostile macro environment. The 52-week low of $9.46 is a reasonable downside scenario for deal failure.

## Conclusion

AES at current levels is a merger arbitrage situation with a ~9.5% spread to the $15.00 deal price. The consortium is well-capitalized and the deal structure appears straightforward, but the timeline (likely 6-12 months for regulatory approvals), QIA involvement (potential CFIUS review), and shareholder litigation create enough uncertainty to justify the spread. The $15.00 price appears to undervalue AES's franchise given the forward P/E of 7.3x, the data center power demand tailwind, and comparable utility multiples, which supports the possibility of a price bump or competing bid.

For merger arbitrage specialists, the spread may be attractive. For fundamental investors, the deal caps upside at $15 (or modestly above with a bump), limiting participation in AES's long-term data center and renewable growth story. The risk of deal failure in the current geopolitically volatile environment introduces 25-30% downside if the stock reverts to pre-M&A-speculation levels.

**Recommendation: HOLD.** The spread compensates for risk but the asymmetry is not compelling enough for a BUY given deal failure downside in the current macro regime. Investors currently holding should maintain positions to capture the spread. New capital is better deployed in energy and infrastructure positions with more favorable risk/reward profiles.
