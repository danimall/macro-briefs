## Executive Summary

Antero Midstream Corp is a midstream energy company that has delivered exceptional shareholder returns — up 271% over three years — driven by operational improvements, the Appalachian natural gas/NGL development cycle, and most recently the closing of the HG Mid acquisition in early 2026. Q4 2025 EPS of $0.28 beat consensus by 7.69%, and management guided for double-digit free cash flow growth in 2026.

At $23.14, the stock trades at 26.5x trailing earnings, near its 52-week high of $23.35, and the dividend yield has compressed to ~3.9% from historically higher levels. The $100+ oil environment provides a macro tailwind, but AM's revenues are more closely tied to Appalachian natural gas and NGL throughput volumes than crude oil prices directly. Multiple insiders sold shares in March 2026 immediately after receiving equity grants, a pattern that suggests comfort with current compensation levels but not conviction that shares are undervalued. The risk-reward at current prices is balanced, warranting a HOLD.

## Company Overview

Antero Midstream operates gathering, compression, processing, and water handling assets primarily serving Antero Resources (AR) in the Appalachian Basin. The company collects fees based on throughput volumes from AR's Marcellus and Utica Shale operations. This fee-based model provides relative stability compared to upstream E&P companies, though AM remains heavily dependent on a single customer (AR) for the vast majority of its revenue.

Key recent developments:
- **HG Mid acquisition closed February 2026**, funded partly through the $1.2B sale of Utica assets to Infinity Natural Resources. This transaction reshapes AM's asset base and adds development projects.
- **Q4 2025 results:** EPS $0.28 vs. $0.26 consensus (+7.69% beat); revenue grew ~$9.5M YoY.
- **2026 guidance:** Management outlined double-digit FCF growth, driven by the HG Mid assets and organic volume growth from AR's development program.

**Dividend:** At ~$0.90/share annualized (implied from ~3.9% yield on $23.14), the payout appears manageable given the fee-based revenue model. The dividend yield has compressed significantly from the 8-10%+ range seen during the stock's lows, reflecting price appreciation outpacing distribution growth.

## Growth Analysis

**Positive catalysts:**
1. **HG Mid acquisition** immediately adds gathering and processing capacity, with embedded development projects that should drive volume growth through 2027-2028. Management described this as immediately accretive.
2. **Double-digit FCF growth guidance for 2026** is meaningful for a midstream company and reflects both HG Mid contributions and organic throughput increases from AR's drilling program.
3. **$100+ oil environment** benefits Appalachian producers indirectly — higher commodity prices incentivize AR to maintain or accelerate drilling, which drives throughput volumes through AM's systems. Natural gas prices are the more direct driver; the current energy crisis (Iran conflict, Hormuz disruption) has elevated both oil and gas prices.
4. **Utica asset divestiture** to Infinity for $1.2B provided capital to fund growth without excessive dilution or leverage.

**Growth constraints:**
- AM's throughput growth is fundamentally tethered to AR's drilling program. Any slowdown in AR's capital spending directly reduces AM's volume growth.
- The Appalachian Basin is a mature development area with known geology. Growth rates for gathering systems in mature basins typically moderate over time.
- The HG Mid acquisition, while accretive, represents a step-change that will anniversary in 2027, potentially creating a growth deceleration narrative.

## Valuation Assessment

At 26.5x trailing earnings, AM is expensive relative to midstream peers and its own history. The stock has appreciated 271% over three years, compressing the dividend yield from double digits to ~3.9%.

**Context for valuation:**
- Midstream MLPs/C-corps typically trade at 8-14x earnings and 8-10x EV/EBITDA.
- AM's premium reflects its C-corp structure (broader investor accessibility vs. MLP K-1 complexity), its growth profile, and the current energy sector tailwind.
- At the 52-week high ($23.35), there is essentially no margin of safety — the stock closed at $23.14 on March 14.
- The 52-week low of $15.08 implies the stock has rallied 53% from trough, with most of the move occurring post-Q4 earnings and the HG Mid announcement.

**Bull case valuation:** If 2026 FCF grows 15% and the multiple holds at ~25x, fair value approximates $24-26. This provides 3-12% upside plus the 3.9% dividend, for a total return of 7-16%.

**Bear case valuation:** If energy prices normalize, AR slows drilling, or growth disappoints, the multiple could compress to 18-20x, implying $16-18 — a 22-31% drawdown from current levels.

The risk-reward skew is roughly symmetrical, which is consistent with a HOLD at a stock trading near its all-time high.

## Competitive Landscape

AM operates in a niche defined by its relationship with AR. This is both a competitive advantage and a structural limitation:

**Advantages:**
- Fee-based contracts with minimum volume commitments provide revenue visibility.
- Integrated gathering/processing/water handling creates operational efficiency.
- No direct competition for AR's volumes in AM's service area — the infrastructure is purpose-built.

**Disadvantages:**
- Single-customer concentration. AR accounts for substantially all of AM's revenue. Any financial distress, strategic pivot, or production decline at AR directly impairs AM.
- Limited ability to attract third-party volumes compared to larger diversified midstream operators (EPD, WMB, ET).
- The HG Mid acquisition diversifies the asset base modestly, but AM remains fundamentally an Appalachian-focused, single-customer midstream company.

## Risk Assessment

**Customer concentration (HIGH):** AR dependence is the dominant risk. AR's financial health, drilling plans, and strategic decisions are essentially AM's financial health.

**Valuation risk (MODERATE-HIGH):** At 26.5x and near the 52-week high, any earnings miss or guidance reduction would likely trigger meaningful selling. The stock's 271% three-year appreciation creates a high expectations hurdle.

**Insider selling (MODERATE):** Multiple executives sold shares in March 2026:
- Director Brooks Klimley: sold 5,000 shares at $23.16 ($115.8K)
- Sheri Pearce: sold 14,000 shares at $22.76 ($318.6K) after receiving 32,651 share grant
- Yvette Schultz: sold 25,000 shares at $22.81 ($570.3K) after receiving grants
- Michael Kennedy (CEO): gifted 15,000 shares; tax withholding on large grants

The selling pattern — receive grants, sell a portion immediately — is common and doesn't necessarily signal bearishness. But the absence of open-market purchases by any insider is notable at prices near all-time highs.

**Macro/energy price risk:** While $100+ oil benefits the sector broadly, AM's fee-based model means it captures volume upside rather than price upside. A sustained price crash could slow AR's drilling, reducing throughput with a 6-12 month lag.

**Interest rate sensitivity:** Midstream companies carry meaningful debt. Higher-for-longer rates increase refinancing costs. AM's 0.77 beta suggests moderate market sensitivity, but the stock's income characteristics make it somewhat rate-sensitive as an equity (competing with bond yields for income-oriented capital).

**Geopolitical tailwind may reverse:** The current Iran conflict has pushed energy stocks higher. A ceasefire or de-escalation — even if assigned only 12% near-term probability in the world model — could remove the energy premium quickly, and AM at 26.5x has less buffer than upstream names that would see direct earnings impact.

## Investment Thesis

**Bull Case:**
- HG Mid acquisition drives double-digit FCF growth in 2026, potentially surprising to the upside if AR accelerates drilling in response to $100+ oil.
- Dividend growth resumes as FCF increases, attracting income-oriented investors.
- Sustained energy infrastructure demand (Iran conflict duration, LNG export growth) supports Appalachian production growth.
- C-corp structure allows index inclusion and broader institutional ownership.
- Fair value: $25-27 (8-17% upside + 3.9% dividend).

**Bear Case:**
- 26.5x earnings is a stretched valuation for a single-customer midstream company with limited diversification.
- Insider selling immediately post-grant suggests executives view current prices as adequate.
- Any normalization in energy prices reduces AR drilling incentive, slowing AM's volume growth with a lag.
- The 271% three-year run has attracted momentum capital that could reverse quickly on any negative catalyst.
- Multiple compression to peer-average 15-18x implies $13-16, a 30-44% drawdown.

## Conclusion

Antero Midstream has executed well operationally and the HG Mid acquisition provides a clear growth catalyst for 2026. The problem is price. At $23.14 and 26.5x earnings, near the 52-week high of $23.35, the stock prices in the acquisition accretion, the energy tailwind, and the FCF growth guidance with minimal margin of safety. Insider selling in March confirms that those closest to the company view current levels as an opportunity to reduce, not increase, their exposure.

For existing holders, the ~3.9% dividend and growth outlook justify maintaining the position. For new capital, the entry point is poor — the stock has moved from $15 to $23 in under a year, and the asymmetry now favors waiting for a pullback to the $18-20 range, where the yield would compress less and the risk-reward would tilt more favorably.

**Recommendation: HOLD**

The "Why Not BUY?" gate requires a specific, falsifiable reason: the stock trades within 1% of its 52-week high at a 26.5x multiple that is 70-100% above midstream peer averages, and four insider sales occurred in the two weeks preceding this analysis. A pullback to $19-20 (15-18% below current) — bringing the yield to ~4.5-4.7% and the multiple to ~22x — would warrant upgrading to BUY.
