## Executive Summary

Doximity is a physician-focused digital platform that connects over 80% of US physicians and monetizes through pharmaceutical marketing, telehealth tools, and hiring solutions. The stock has collapsed 60.6% over the trailing twelve months, falling from ~$62 in mid-March 2025 to ~$24.59 today, with the most acute damage occurring around the February 5, 2026 earnings report (a 17% single-day gap-down on February 6, from $33.32 to $23.69 intraday low). The current market cap of $3.37B implies approximately 21x trailing earnings on $1.11 EPS.

The core problem is growth deceleration. Net revenue retention (NRR) has declined to 112%, and third-party analysis characterizes the growth outlook as "murky." The company retains strong unit economics — ~90% GAAP gross margins and 720,000 unique prescribers using workflow tools — but these defensive qualities are already reflected in the current multiple. With an interim CFO, persistent director-level insider selling on a monthly cadence, and no visible near-term catalyst for reacceleration, the stock is a HOLD until growth trajectory clarity emerges.

## Company Overview

Doximity operates the largest professional medical network in the United States. The platform serves as the LinkedIn equivalent for physicians, with engagement tools including clinical workflow support, telehealth capabilities, and news/CME content. Revenue is primarily derived from pharmaceutical and health system customers who pay for marketing access to the physician network and for recruiting tools.

The company went public in 2021 and carries a dual-class share structure (Class A and Class B common stock), which concentrates voting power with insiders. The most recent 10-K was filed May 20, 2025, and the most recent 10-Q was filed February 5, 2026.

**Revenue growth visibility** is the binding constraint. Without populated financial statements, I cannot independently verify revenue trajectory, but the NRR decline from presumably higher levels (historically 120%+) to 112% indicates existing customer expansion is decelerating. For a company with high gross margins and a subscription-like revenue model, NRR is the single most important leading indicator of topline growth.

## Growth Analysis

**Positive indicators:**
- 720,000 unique prescribers using workflow tools represents deep engagement penetration within the US physician population (~1M active physicians)
- Platform network effects: as more physicians use Doximity, pharmaceutical companies have greater incentive to advertise, creating a reinforcing loop
- Telehealth adoption created a structural usage tailwind that persists post-pandemic

**Negative indicators:**
- NRR at 112% — down from historical highs, suggesting existing customers are spending at a slower growth rate or churning modules
- The Seeking Alpha characterization of "murky" growth outlook aligns with the stock's 60% decline — the market has repriced the growth premium aggressively
- Forward P/E equal to trailing P/E implies consensus expects ~0% EPS growth
- Pharma advertising budgets face macro headwinds in a stagflationary environment — CPI passthrough to healthcare costs may compress pharma marketing spend

The pharmaceutical marketing vertical is inherently cyclical with respect to new drug launches and pharma advertising cycles. Doximity's growth depends on both expanding the physician user base (largely saturated at 80%+ penetration) and increasing per-customer monetization (which NRR suggests is decelerating).

## Valuation Assessment

At $24.59, DOCS trades at 20.7x trailing earnings with ~$3.37B market cap. For context:

- **Relative to growth:** If EPS growth is flat (as the identical forward/trailing P/E implies), 20.7x for a zero-growth company is rich relative to the broader market but defensible given 90% gross margins and high free cash flow conversion
- **Relative to decline magnitude:** The stock has lost 60% in a year. Much of the growth premium has been removed, but the question is whether the remaining 20x multiple is sustainable if growth continues to decelerate
- **Implied growth rate:** A 20x P/E on a high-margin software business with no debt (assumed given asset-light model) and strong FCF generation implies the market is pricing in low-single-digit growth, which seems reasonable given the NRR trajectory

The stock is neither cheap enough to be a compelling value play nor growing fast enough to justify a growth premium. At 15-16x trailing earnings ($16-18), the valuation would adequately compensate for execution risk. At 20x with flat growth expectations, there is limited margin of safety.

## Competitive Landscape

**Strengths:**
- Doximity claims 80%+ of US physicians on its platform — a dominant network position that is difficult to replicate
- Switching costs for pharmaceutical advertisers are moderate — Doximity offers unique reach into verified physician audiences that competitors cannot easily match
- The physician verification process creates a trust moat that general social platforms cannot replicate

**Weaknesses:**
- The addressable market is bounded by the number of US physicians and the pharmaceutical advertising budget pool
- Competitors include Medscape (WebMD/Internet Brands), IQVIA, and direct pharma sales forces, all of which compete for the same marketing dollars
- AI-driven tools from larger tech companies could erode Doximity's engagement advantage over time if physicians shift workflow to broader platforms

The competitive position is solid but faces a ceiling. Network effects protect the existing base, but the platform has largely saturated physician adoption, limiting the growth vector to monetization expansion.

## Risk Assessment

**1. Growth deceleration risk (HIGH):** NRR at 112% and declining. If this trends toward 100%, the company transitions from a growth story to a mature, slow-growth software business, warranting a lower multiple.

**2. Insider selling (MODERATE-HIGH):** Director Kira Wampler has sold 2,000 shares monthly on a systematic basis (December 2025 at $50.71, January 2026 at $44.65, February 2026 at $37.33, March 2026 at $26.00). Director Timothy Cabral sold 10,000 shares at $50 in November 2025. Interim PFO Siddharth Sitaram has been exercising options and selling the tax portion. No insider purchases are visible. The selling is consistent and spans multiple insiders, though the amounts are relatively small and some appears pre-programmed (10b5-1 plans).

**3. Management stability (MODERATE):** The CFO title includes "Interim" for the PFO/PAO (Siddharth Sitaram), though Anna Bryson holds the Chief Financial Officer title separately. The interim designation on a key finance role during a period of stock collapse warrants monitoring.

**4. Dual-class share structure:** Concentrates voting power with founders, limiting minority shareholder influence on governance decisions.

**5. Macro sensitivity:** Pharmaceutical marketing budgets are discretionary. In a stagflationary environment with rising costs, pharma companies may trim digital marketing spend, directly impacting Doximity's revenue.

**6. Concentration risk:** Revenue is concentrated among a relatively small number of large pharmaceutical and health system clients. Loss of a top-10 customer would have outsized impact.

## Investment Thesis

### Bull Case
- The 60% decline has de-risked the valuation significantly. At 20x earnings with 90% gross margins, the stock could re-rate to 25-30x if NRR stabilizes above 110% and revenue growth reaccelerates to low-double-digits
- Platform network effects with 80%+ physician penetration create a durable competitive position that prevents rapid erosion
- Free cash flow generation (assumed strong given margins and asset-light model) provides capital return optionality through buybacks
- Pharma digital advertising shift is a secular tailwind that should resume once macro headwinds ease

### Bear Case
- NRR continues declining toward 100-105%, turning Doximity into a no-growth, mature platform business worth 12-15x earnings (~$13-17 per share)
- The physician network is largely saturated, limiting new user growth and leaving monetization per user as the only growth lever — a lever that is clearly decelerating
- Consistent insider selling across multiple insiders at progressively lower prices signals that those closest to the business see limited near-term upside
- In a recession (38-42% probability per macro regime), pharma advertising budgets contract, creating a cyclical headwind on top of the structural growth deceleration
- AI tools from Microsoft/Google could erode Doximity's workflow engagement if physicians migrate to broader productivity platforms

## Conclusion

Doximity has strong unit economics (90% gross margins, dominant physician network) embedded in a decelerating growth trajectory (NRR 112% and falling). The stock's 60% decline has removed the growth premium, but the remaining 20x multiple assumes growth stabilization that has not yet been demonstrated. Insider selling is persistent and directionally negative. The interim CFO designation adds governance uncertainty.

The appropriate entry point would be closer to 14-16x earnings (~$16-18), which would provide adequate margin of safety against continued growth deceleration. At current levels, the risk/reward is balanced — too expensive for a value play, too growth-impaired for a quality compounder. HOLD until either (a) NRR stabilizes and revenue growth reaccelerates, providing evidence for multiple expansion, or (b) the stock reaches $16-18, providing a genuine margin of safety.
