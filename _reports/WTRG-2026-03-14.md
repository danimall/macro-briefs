## Executive Summary

Essential Utilities is a regulated water and natural gas utility headquartered in Bryn Mawr, Pennsylvania, operating through its Aqua (water) and Peoples (gas) subsidiaries. The company reported FY2025 EPS of $2.20, beating its own guidance range of $2.07-$2.11, with full-year net income of $616.4 million and Q4 net income of $132.7 million ($0.47/share). Record infrastructure investment and strategic acquisitions drove the outperformance.

The most consequential near-term development is the proposed merger with American Water Works (AWK), which received shareholder approval on February 10, 2026. If completed, this would create the dominant US regulated water utility. The stock trades at $41.65, 18.8x forward earnings, with a 3.6% dividend yield and 0.77 beta. In the current macro environment — confirmed stagflation across all six tracked dimensions, with recession probability at 38-42% — WTRG's regulated, low-beta profile provides genuine portfolio defensiveness. However, the merger introduces meaningful execution and regulatory risk, and the company's capital-intensive growth model requires continued debt issuance (a $500M senior notes offering was announced March 10) at a time when credit conditions are tightening. The stock is reasonably priced for what it is, but does not offer a compelling entry point given the merger uncertainty overhang.

## Company Overview

Essential Utilities operates regulated water and wastewater services (Aqua segment) and natural gas distribution (Peoples segment) across multiple US states. The company serves approximately 5 million customers combined. Its business model is textbook regulated utility: earn allowed returns on invested capital through rate base growth, with revenue visibility provided by state regulatory commissions. Growth comes from three channels: organic rate base investment in infrastructure replacement, tuck-in acquisitions of smaller municipal water/wastewater systems, and the transformative proposed merger with American Water.

## Growth Analysis

**Organic Growth:** Management highlighted "record infrastructure spending" on the Q4 earnings call. For regulated utilities, capital expenditure directly translates to rate base growth, which drives allowed revenue increases through rate case filings. This is a reliable, if slow, growth engine — typically delivering 5-7% annual rate base growth for well-managed water utilities.

**Acquisitions:** Essential Utilities has a long track record of acquiring small municipal water and wastewater systems. These tuck-in deals grow the customer base and rate base simultaneously, typically at accretive valuations because municipalities often sell at book value or below.

**American Water Merger:** This is the dominant growth catalyst. Shareholder approval was obtained February 10, 2026. The combined entity would be the largest regulated water utility in the US by a significant margin. Key unknowns:
- State regulatory approvals across multiple jurisdictions (water utilities face state-by-state review)
- Antitrust considerations given market concentration
- Integration execution and synergy realization timeline
- Pro forma capital structure and credit implications

The merger introduces a binary outcome risk that makes the stock difficult to value on fundamentals alone. If completed, the combined entity likely commands a premium multiple due to scale and scarcity value. If blocked or abandoned, WTRG reverts to its standalone value (likely $36-38 based on pre-merger trading patterns).

## Valuation Assessment

At 18.8x forward P/E, WTRG trades at a modest discount to the regulated water utility peer group. American Water (AWK) historically traded at 25-30x forward earnings, reflecting its premium scale position. Other water utilities like SJW Group and California Water Service typically trade in the 20-25x range.

The discount likely reflects: (1) the gas distribution business (Peoples) which typically carries a lower multiple than pure-play water, and (2) merger uncertainty. If the AWK deal closes, the blended multiple for the combined entity could re-rate upward. If it doesn't, the standalone 18.8x multiple is fair for a diversified water/gas utility.

The 3.6% dividend yield provides a floor of sorts, particularly attractive in a stagflationary environment where real returns on many asset classes are compressed. With the 10-year Treasury yield elevated, the spread over Treasuries is tighter than historical averages, which limits the utility's traditional appeal as a bond proxy.

**FCF yield consideration:** Without detailed cash flow data, I estimate that after capex (which is substantial for an infrastructure-heavy utility), free cash flow yield is likely in the 2-3% range, below the dividend — meaning the dividend is partially funded by incremental debt/equity issuance. This is typical for growth-oriented regulated utilities but is a structural vulnerability in a tightening credit environment.

## Competitive Landscape

Essential Utilities operates in a sector with high barriers to entry (regulated monopoly within service territories), predictable cash flows, and limited competitive risk within existing markets. The competitive dynamics that matter are:

1. **Regulatory relationships:** The company's ability to earn its allowed rate of return depends on constructive relationships with state public utility commissions. Rate case outcomes are the primary determinant of earnings growth.

2. **Acquisition competition:** Municipal water system acquisitions face competition from other regulated utilities and, increasingly, from infrastructure funds. Essential's track record here is strong.

3. **Scale advantages post-merger:** If the AWK deal closes, the combined entity would have substantially greater negotiating leverage with regulators, suppliers, and capital markets. Scale in regulated utilities reduces cost of capital and improves operating efficiency through shared services.

The competitive moat is structural — regulated utility territories are granted monopolies. The question is whether Essential earns adequate returns on its invested capital, which depends on regulatory outcomes.

## Risk Assessment

**Merger Execution Risk (High):** The AWK deal requires regulatory approvals across multiple states. Water utility mergers of this scale are unprecedented. State regulators may impose conditions (rate freezes, local employment guarantees, infrastructure investment commitments) that reduce the economic value of the combination. Antitrust scrutiny of creating a dominant national water utility could lead to divestitures or deal blockage.

**Interest Rate / Credit Risk (Moderate-High):** The $500M senior notes offering demonstrates ongoing capital needs. In the current environment — credit cascade probability 55-60%, rising spreads — each incremental debt issuance is more expensive. A sustained period of elevated rates compresses the spread between the company's cost of capital and its allowed rate of return, squeezing earnings.

**Regulatory Risk (Moderate):** Rate case outcomes are inherently uncertain. Political pressure to keep water rates affordable could limit allowed rate increases, particularly during a period of consumer spending contraction (retail sales -0.2%, gasoline +21%).

**Macro Sensitivity (Low):** Water and gas distribution are essential services with highly inelastic demand. Revenue is largely weather-dependent and volume-based, with limited cyclical exposure. Beta of 0.77 confirms low market sensitivity. In the current stagflationary regime, this is a genuine advantage.

**Integration Risk (Moderate, Conditional on Deal Closing):** Merging two large regulated utilities across dozens of state jurisdictions creates multi-year integration complexity.

## Investment Thesis

**Bull Case:**
- AWK merger closes, creating the dominant US regulated water utility with a premium multiple (25x+), implying ~$55+ share price
- Regulated utility defensiveness provides genuine protection during stagflationary recession (beta 0.77, 3.6% yield)
- Record infrastructure investment drives rate base growth of 6-8% annually
- Water infrastructure is a secular growth theme — aging US pipe networks require $1T+ in replacement over coming decades
- FY2025 earnings beat ($2.20 vs. $2.07-$2.11 guidance) demonstrates operational execution

**Bear Case:**
- AWK merger blocked or significantly conditioned by regulators, stock reverts to $36-38 standalone value (-9 to -14% from current)
- Rising cost of debt ($500M offering at elevated rates) compresses ROE spread vs. allowed returns
- Stagflationary environment forces regulators to limit rate increases to protect consumers, constraining earnings growth
- The gas distribution business (Peoples) faces long-term secular headwind from electrification policy, potentially requiring eventual divestiture at a discount
- Forward P/E of 18.8x already prices in moderate growth; limited margin of safety

## Conclusion

WTRG is a well-run regulated utility delivering above-guidance earnings ($2.20 vs. $2.07-$2.11) with a defensive profile suited to the current macro regime. The 3.6% yield and 0.77 beta provide genuine portfolio protection during confirmed stagflation. However, the stock's risk-reward is dominated by the binary AWK merger outcome, which prevents a high-conviction directional call. At $41.65, the market is pricing in a meaningful probability of deal completion — buying here means accepting ~14% downside to standalone value if the merger fails, versus potentially substantial upside if it closes. Without more clarity on regulatory timelines and conditions, the appropriate position is HOLD for existing holders and WATCH for new capital. The stock would become more interesting on a pullback toward $37-38 (standalone value range) or on concrete positive regulatory signals regarding the merger.

For investors specifically seeking stagflation-defensive exposure with yield, WTRG competes with names like PGR (0.30 beta, 10.7x P/E) and ACGL (0.39 beta, 7.6x P/E) which offer better risk-adjusted return profiles without binary merger risk.

**Recommendation: HOLD**
