## Executive Summary

Teradata reported Q4 2025 results on February 10, 2026, beating consensus EPS estimates significantly ($0.74 vs. $0.55 expected) on revenue of $421M, up 2.9% year-over-year. The company highlighted 15% Cloud ARR growth and strong free cash flow generation for full-year 2025. The stock surged from ~$29 to an intraday high of $41.78 on the earnings release, then proceeded to give back roughly 35% of those gains over the following five weeks, settling into a $26-28 range by mid-March 2026.

The core question with Teradata is whether its cloud transition can sustain enough growth to justify a ~20x forward P/E when the total revenue base is growing under 3% and the company faces intense competition from Snowflake, Databricks, and hyperscaler-native analytics offerings (BigQuery, Redshift, Azure Synapse). Without detailed financial statements in the provided data, the analysis relies on reported earnings metrics, price action, news flow, and insider activity. The picture that emerges is a company executing adequately on a difficult transition but lacking the growth velocity to create sustained upside.

In the current stagflationary macro regime where enterprise IT spending is under pressure, Teradata's position as a niche data analytics vendor with large enterprise customers provides some revenue stability but limited growth optionality. The stock is a HOLD.

## Company Overview

Teradata Corporation is a San Diego-based provider of enterprise data analytics and cloud-based data platform solutions. The company has been transitioning from on-premise data warehouse hardware/software to a cloud-native platform (Teradata VantageCloud) over the past several years. Market capitalization stands at approximately $2.5B with 92.2M shares outstanding. The stock trades on the NYSE at $27.22, approximately 35% below its 52-week high of $41.78 and 48% above its 52-week low of $18.43.

The company's customer base consists primarily of large enterprises in financial services, telecommunications, retail, and healthcare — organizations with complex analytical workloads that require handling massive data volumes. This provides a degree of customer stickiness due to high switching costs, but also constrains the addressable new customer pipeline.

Cloud ARR growing 15% is the headline metric management emphasizes, but this growth rate itself has been decelerating (it was above 50% in 2022-2023 during the early phase of the migration).

## Growth Analysis

Teradata's growth story has two components:

**1. Cloud ARR migration (the positive):** 15% Cloud ARR growth represents the ongoing migration of the existing installed base from on-premise to cloud, plus some new customer acquisition. This migration provides recurring revenue visibility and higher long-term margins. However, 15% growth is mid-teens — acceptable but not exceptional for a cloud transition, and the rate is decelerating as the easier conversions have already occurred.

**2. Total revenue stagnation (the constraint):** Revenue of $421M in Q4 2025 vs. $409M in Q4 2024 represents only $12M of incremental revenue. The cloud migration is largely a revenue-neutral transition for existing customers (in some cases revenue-dilutive as on-premise maintenance contracts convert to consumption-based cloud pricing). Teradata needs to win net-new workloads and customers to grow the total revenue base, and competitive dynamics make this difficult.

**AI/analytics optionality:** Teradata has positioned VantageCloud as an AI-ready analytics platform, and management has discussed AI-related customer interest. This remains early-stage and unquantified. The company benefits from existing relationships with large enterprises that need to analyze massive datasets, but it must compete against well-resourced hyperscaler offerings that integrate more naturally with broader cloud ecosystems.

**Growth estimate:** Forward EPS of ~$1.35 (implied by the convergence of trailing and forward P/E at 20.2x) suggests the market expects roughly flat earnings in 2026. Revenue growth of 2-4% with moderate margin expansion from the cloud transition could deliver low-single-digit EPS growth, but the macro environment creates downside risk to even this modest trajectory.

- Trailing P/E: ~20.2x on $1.35 EPS
- Forward P/E: ~20.2x
- Market cap: ~$2.5B
- EV/Revenue: ~1.5x (estimated, based on ~$1.7B annual revenue run rate)

**Relative valuation context:** Among enterprise software/analytics peers, Teradata trades at a significant discount to high-growth names (Snowflake at 15-20x revenue, Databricks private at similar multiples) but at a premium to traditional legacy tech vendors with similar growth profiles. A 20x P/E for a company growing total revenue 2-3% is not cheap — it implies the market is pricing in the cloud transition delivering meaningful margin expansion or growth acceleration that hasn't yet materialized.

**The FCF yield provides downside support.** If Teradata can sustain $250-350M in annual FCF and continue share repurchases, the stock has a reasonable floor. But a 20x multiple on stagnant earnings in a stagflationary environment where enterprise IT budgets are being scrutinized is not a compelling entry point for new capital.

**Post-earnings price action is informative.** The stock hit $41.78 on earnings day (Feb 11) and has since fallen to $27.22, a 35% decline in five weeks. This suggests the Q4 beat was driven by cost controls and one-time items rather than sustainable revenue momentum that the market was willing to capitalize at higher multiples.

## Competitive Landscape

Teradata operates in the enterprise analytics and data warehousing market, facing competition from:

**Direct competitors:**
- **Snowflake (SNOW):** Cloud-native data warehouse with superior growth and developer mindshare. Teradata's primary competitive threat for new workloads.
- **Databricks:** Private, growing rapidly, expanding from data engineering into BI/analytics, directly encroaching on Teradata's traditional territory.

**Hyperscaler offerings:**
- **Google BigQuery, Amazon Redshift, Microsoft Azure Synapse:** Bundled with broader cloud ecosystems, creating significant competitive pressure through convenience and pricing incentives.

**Legacy competitors:**
- **IBM (Db2 Warehouse), Oracle:** Also transitioning legacy databases to cloud, facing similar challenges.

Teradata's competitive advantage rests on its ability to handle complex mixed workloads at massive scale for large enterprises with existing deployments. Switching costs are high — migrating petabytes of data and rewriting analytical pipelines is expensive and risky. This provides a defensible base of existing customers but makes new customer acquisition difficult, as greenfield deployments overwhelmingly go to Snowflake or hyperscaler-native solutions.

The competitive moat is real but narrowing. Each year that hyperscaler offerings improve, the switching cost advantage erodes marginally.

## Risk Assessment

**Macro risk (ELEVATED):** In the current stagflationary environment, large enterprise IT budgets face scrutiny. Teradata's customer base (financials, telecom, retail) includes sectors directly impacted by economic deterioration. Cloud migration projects may be delayed as CIOs prioritize cost control over transformation.

**Competitive displacement risk (MODERATE-HIGH):** The long-term trend favors cloud-native platforms. Teradata must continue converting its installed base before competitive alternatives erode switching costs through AI-assisted migration tools and improved compatibility layers. Each year of 2-3% total revenue growth increases the probability that the installed base is shrinking on a net basis.

**Revenue concentration risk:** Teradata's enterprise customer base, while sticky, means losing even a handful of large accounts has an outsized impact. No specific concentration data is available, but this is a well-known structural characteristic.

## Investment Thesis

### Bull Case
- Cloud ARR growing 15% provides recurring revenue visibility and should drive margin expansion as on-premise infrastructure costs decline.
- Q4 EPS beat (34.5% above consensus) demonstrates management's ability to control costs and deliver profitability during the transition.
- FCF yield of 10-14% provides downside support and funds ongoing share repurchases.
- At $27.22, the stock is 35% below its recent high, creating a potential entry point if growth reaccelerates.
- AI/analytics demand is a secular tailwind; Teradata's enterprise relationships position it to capture incremental workloads.
- Two analysts reportedly hiked price targets after Q4, suggesting some institutional support.

### Bear Case
- Total revenue growth of 2.9% is insufficient to sustain a 20x P/E multiple in a stagflationary macro environment.
- Cloud ARR growth of 15% is decelerating and largely represents migration of existing revenue, not true organic growth.
- The 35% post-earnings selloff in five weeks signals that the market quickly concluded the Q4 beat was not indicative of a durable improvement.
- Competitive threats from Snowflake, Databricks, and hyperscalers are intensifying. Greenfield wins are rare.
- The GAAP/non-GAAP gap ($0.38 vs. $0.74 in Q4) means true profitability is substantially lower than headline figures suggest.
- Enterprise IT spending contraction in a recessionary scenario (38-42% probability per macro model) would directly impact Teradata's revenue growth and new customer acquisition.
- Zacks reports the stock is "down 28% since last earnings report," reflecting persistent selling pressure.

## Conclusion

Teradata is executing a competent cloud transition with its existing customer base, as evidenced by 15% Cloud ARR growth and a strong Q4 EPS beat. However, total revenue growth of ~3% and a forward P/E of ~20x leave limited margin of safety in the current macro environment. The competitive landscape is structurally unfavorable as hyperscaler-native analytics platforms continue gaining share in new deployments.

The stock's sharp post-earnings reversal (from $41.78 to $27.22 in five weeks) confirms that the market does not view the Q4 results as evidence of a fundamental inflection point. The FCF yield provides reasonable downside support, but the upside case requires either a meaningful acceleration in total revenue growth or a multiple expansion that seems unlikely given macro headwinds and competitive pressures.

**Recommendation: HOLD.** The stock is not expensive enough to warrant an AVOID, but the growth profile does not support a BUY at current levels. A more compelling entry point would be below $22, where the FCF yield would approach 15%+ and the valuation would more appropriately reflect the company's single-digit growth trajectory. Investors should monitor Q1 2026 results for evidence of whether the Cloud ARR growth rate stabilizes or continues decelerating, and whether the macro environment is impacting enterprise deal cycles.
