## Executive Summary

UniFirst Corp has entered into a definitive agreement to be acquired by Cintas Corporation for $310 per share ($155 cash + 0.772 shares of CTAS per UNF share), valuing the transaction at approximately $5.5 billion. The deal was announced on March 11, 2026, after multiple prior attempts by Cintas over several years. Cintas expects $375 million in annual cost synergies by 2030, with closing targeted for H2 2026.

At the current price of $273.31, the stock trades at a 13.4% discount to the $310 deal price. This spread reflects a combination of time value (6-9 months to close), antitrust risk (combining the #1 and #2 uniform services companies in North America), and the mixed cash/stock consideration structure which introduces CTAS share price volatility. Halper Sadeh LLC has announced an investigation into whether the deal price is fair, which is standard for transactions of this size but adds marginal uncertainty.

The investment decision here is straightforward: this is a merger arbitrage situation rather than a fundamental equity analysis. The underlying business metrics — trailing P/E of 33.8x, forward P/E of 31.8x, low beta of 0.67 — are largely irrelevant except insofar as they inform whether the $310 price is a floor or whether a competing bid could emerge. Given Cintas's persistent multi-year pursuit and the Croatti family's controlling interest having now agreed, a competing bid is unlikely. The question is binary: does the deal close, or doesn't it?

## Company Overview

UniFirst is the second-largest provider of workplace uniforms, protective clothing, and facility services in North America. The company operates route-based delivery services, laundering and maintaining uniforms and other textile products for businesses across multiple industries. The company has a market capitalization of approximately $4.75 billion at the current share price, implying roughly 17.4 million diluted shares outstanding.

The Croatti family has historically controlled UniFirst through dual-class share structure, which explains why previous Cintas approaches were rebuffed and why the definitive agreement now carries high completion probability from a shareholder vote perspective — the controlling family has agreed to the deal.

UniFirst's organic growth profile is modest — the forward P/E of 31.8x versus trailing P/E of 33.8x implies low-to-mid single digit earnings growth, consistent with a mature route-based services business. Revenue growth in uniform rental businesses typically tracks employment levels and pricing power on renewals, both of which face headwinds in the current stagflationary macro environment (NFP -92K, GDP 0.7%).

Growth analysis is moot for investment purposes given the pending acquisition. The relevant growth question is whether Cintas can achieve $375M in cost synergies by 2030, which affects CTAS share price (and therefore the stock component of the deal consideration), but this is a CTAS analysis question rather than a UNF question.

## Valuation Assessment

The valuation framework is entirely defined by the merger:

- **Current Price:** $273.31
- **Deal Price:** $310.00 (mixed: $155 cash + 0.772 CTAS shares)
- **Gross Spread:** $36.69 (13.4%)
- **Estimated Close:** H2 2026 (approximately 4-8 months)
- **Annualized Spread:** Approximately 20-40% depending on close timing

The spread is wider than typical for definitive agreements with controlling shareholder support. This reflects: (1) antitrust concerns about combining the #1 and #2 players, (2) the mixed consideration structure introducing CTAS stock price risk, and (3) the 4-8 month timeline to close.

The $310 price appears to be a ceiling. Competing bids are extremely unlikely given the Croatti family's controlling position and the multi-year courtship that preceded this agreement. The Halper Sadeh investigation is ambulance-chasing — standard practice in M&A — and unlikely to result in a material price adjustment.

## Competitive Landscape

UniFirst is the clear #2 to Cintas in North American uniform services. Other competitors include Aramark's uniform division and smaller regional players. The combination of Cintas and UniFirst creates a dominant entity that will face antitrust scrutiny from the FTC/DOJ. The uniform rental industry has high barriers to entry (route density economics, laundry facility capital requirements, customer switching costs from uniform customization) which is precisely why regulators may scrutinize the deal.

The key antitrust question: does the merged entity have sufficient market power to raise prices above competitive levels? In many local markets, Cintas + UniFirst may represent 50%+ share. However, the industry also competes against customer self-service (companies buying uniforms directly and handling laundering internally), which provides an alternative that regulators may consider.

## Risk Assessment

**Deal Break Risk (Primary):** The most material risk is regulatory rejection. The FTC under the current administration has been active on merger enforcement, though the political environment may be more favorable for deal completion given the pro-business posture. Probability of deal failure: estimated 10-20%.

**CTAS Stock Price Risk:** Since 0.772 shares of CTAS are part of the consideration, a decline in CTAS stock directly reduces the effective deal price. If CTAS shares fall significantly (possible given the broader equity market correction environment — S&P 500 experiencing its worst three-week stretch), the $310 headline figure becomes aspirational rather than guaranteed.

**Timeline Risk:** Regulatory review could extend the timeline beyond H2 2026, reducing the annualized return on the spread and introducing additional market risk.

**Macro Irrelevance:** The current stagflationary environment (oil at $100+, GDP 0.7%, -92K NFP) has minimal direct impact on this position because the return is driven by deal spread closure rather than business fundamentals. However, a severe recession could theoretically give Cintas a basis to renegotiate, though this is unlikely given the definitive agreement.

**Insider Activity:** Two insider sales in early 2026 at prices of $201-$238, well below the current price and deal price, are consistent with executives monetizing ahead of the deal announcement rather than signaling concern about business fundamentals.

## Investment Thesis

**Bull Case:** The deal closes on schedule at $310/share, delivering a 13.4% gross return over 4-8 months. The controlling family's agreement makes shareholder approval near-certain. Antitrust clearance is obtained with modest divestitures. CTAS stock holds or appreciates, preserving the full $310 consideration value. Total return including dividends: approximately 14%.

**Bear Case:** FTC blocks the deal or demands divestitures significant enough that Cintas walks. UNF shares retrace toward the pre-deal range of $150-200, representing 27-45% downside from current levels. The asymmetry is unfavorable: 13.4% upside versus 27-45% downside, partially offset by the higher probability of the bull case. A severe CTAS stock decline could reduce effective consideration to $280-290 even if the deal closes.

**Why Not BUY (per calibration directive for scores ≥6.5):** The 13.4% spread offers attractive annualized returns, but the risk-reward is asymmetric in the wrong direction for a fundamental equity investor. If the deal breaks, losses could be 3-4x the potential gain. This is a specialized merger arbitrage position requiring portfolio-level risk management (hedging CTAS exposure, sizing for binary outcomes) that differs from a fundamental BUY thesis. Additionally, CTAS stock component introduces equity market risk into what appears to be a "definitive" price. For a dedicated merger arb fund, this is likely a BUY. For a generalist portfolio, HOLD is appropriate — the deal premium is already substantially reflected in the price.

## Conclusion

UniFirst at $273.31 is a merger arbitrage situation with a 13.4% gross spread to the $310 deal price. The probability-weighted expected return is positive given the definitive agreement and controlling family support, but the downside in a deal-break scenario is severe. The position is appropriate for investors comfortable with binary M&A outcomes and willing to hold for 4-8 months. The stock is effectively decoupled from fundamental analysis — earnings growth, valuation multiples, and competitive dynamics matter only to the extent they inform deal completion probability and break-price floor. HOLD for generalist investors; the spread is insufficient compensation for the tail risk of regulatory rejection without dedicated merger arbitrage hedging infrastructure.
