## Executive Summary

SouthState Corporation is a $8.7B market cap regional bank holding company operating primarily across the Southeastern United States. The stock trades at 11.6x forward earnings and 11.6x trailing earnings on reported EPS of $7.87, with a 2.65% dividend yield. UBS reiterated Buy with a $121 target in early February, and the company reported Q4 2025 earnings on January 22, 2026, which drove the stock above $100 temporarily.

The stock has declined approximately 16% from its February 6 high of $108.46, driven by a combination of broader financial sector weakness (the February 27 selloff triggered by hotter-than-expected PPI data and credit quality concerns) and the deteriorating macro backdrop. In a stagflationary environment with 38-42% recession probability and rising credit quality concerns across the banking sector, SSB faces asymmetric downside risk from its commercial real estate and consumer lending exposure. The valuation is fair but provides insufficient margin of safety for the credit cycle risk embedded in the current macro regime.

## Company Overview

SouthState Corporation operates through its subsidiary SouthState Bank, providing commercial and retail banking services across the Southeast. The company has grown through a series of acquisitions, building a franchise across high-growth Sun Belt markets. With approximately 98 million shares outstanding and a current price of ~$91, the company has an equity market capitalization of $8.7B. The 10-K filing history shows consistent annual reporting since at least 2018, with the most recent 10-K filed February 20, 2026.

## Financial Analysis

**Earnings:** Trailing EPS of $7.87 translates to a P/E of ~11.6x. Forward P/E is also 11.6x, suggesting consensus expects roughly flat EPS in the near term. This is consistent with a bank operating in a mature rate environment where NIM expansion has largely played out.

**Dividend:** The 2.65% yield provides reasonable income for a regional bank and suggests a payout ratio of approximately 30-35% (estimated ~$2.35 annual dividend on $7.87 EPS), which is conservative and leaves room for capital return or reserve building.

**Insider Transactions:** The February 20, 2026 insider activity was dominated by equity compensation vesting (M-code transactions) with associated tax withholding (F-code dispositions). The CEO exercised 32,454 shares and had 16,227 withheld for taxes (~$1.7M). These are routine compensation events. However, President Richard Murray sold 10,156 shares on February 18 at $104.76 for ~$1.06M. This was a discretionary open-market sale, occurring near the stock's 52-week high. While a single sale from one executive is not conclusive, the timing (within days of the stock's peak) and size ($1M+) warrant monitoring. No insider purchases were observed.

**Balance Sheet / Cash Flow:** Detailed financial statements were not available in the data feed, which limits the depth of credit quality, capital ratio, and loan portfolio composition analysis. The 10-K filed February 20, 2026 would contain this data.

## Growth Analysis

SSB's growth has historically been acquisition-driven. The Southeast demographic tailwind (population growth in Florida, Georgia, Carolinas) provides a structural demand backdrop for banking services. However, at ~11.6x forward EPS with flat forward EPS expectations, the market is pricing in minimal organic growth.

Key growth constraints in the current environment:
- **NIM compression risk:** If the Fed does not cut rates (20-25% probability of zero cuts in 2026 per world model), NIM expansion is capped. If rates remain elevated, funding costs stay high while loan demand weakens.
- **Loan demand:** In a stagflationary environment with unemployment at 4.4% and rising, small/mid-market commercial borrowers (SSB's core customer base) are likely to reduce borrowing. Higher diesel costs and tariff uncertainty further suppress commercial activity in the Southeast.
- **Fee income:** Mortgage origination fees are under pressure with rates at 7-month highs and refinance demand down 19%.

## Valuation Assessment

At 11.6x trailing and forward P/E, SSB trades roughly in line with regional bank peers (KRE ETF constituents typically range 9-13x). The 2.65% dividend yield is competitive but not exceptional.

UBS's $121 target (set February 4, 2026) implies ~33% upside from the current $91 price and was established when the stock was trading near $106. This target embeds assumptions about continued earnings growth and NIM expansion that appear optimistic given the macro deterioration since February.

The stock's 52-week range of $77.74 to $108.46 puts the current price at approximately 44% of that range, suggesting the market has already partially repriced for macro risk. Book value is not available from the data provided, but at 11.6x earnings for a bank in this environment, the valuation is fair rather than compelling.

## Competitive Landscape

SouthState operates in a competitive regional banking market across the Southeast. The franchise benefits from:
- **Demographic tailwinds** in Sun Belt states
- **Scale advantages** from acquisition-driven growth
- **Relationship banking model** that provides deposit stickiness

Competitive risks include:
- **Larger banks** (JPM, BAC, WFC) expanding retail footprints in SSB's markets
- **Fintech competition** for deposits and consumer lending
- **Credit union expansion** in retail banking

The company lacks the diversification of larger banks and is concentrated in a region vulnerable to hurricane risk, real estate cycles, and tourism/hospitality sensitivity.

## Risk Assessment

**Credit Quality:** The February 27 selloff (5.9% decline) was explicitly linked to "rising concerns over credit quality" per news reporting. In a stagflationary regime with oil above $95, diesel at $5/gallon, and recession probability at 38-42%, commercial real estate and small business lending portfolios face elevated loss risk. Without detailed loan composition data, the magnitude of this exposure cannot be precisely quantified, but Southeast-focused banks have above-average CRE concentration historically.

**Interest Rate Risk:** Banks are net beneficiaries of higher rates up to a point, but the current regime creates a policy trap. The Fed cannot cut (inflation above target), so banks face the dual pressure of high funding costs and weakening loan demand. SSB's asset sensitivity profile is unknown from the available data.

**Macro Sensitivity:** Regional banks are highly correlated to local economic conditions. The Southeast economy is exposed to tourism/hospitality (oil prices compress travel demand), construction (mortgage rates at multi-year highs), and agriculture (fertilizer cost passthrough).

**Concentration Risk:** Single-region exposure means SSB cannot diversify away from Southeast-specific risks (hurricanes, CRE overbuilding in Florida/Georgia, state-level fiscal stress).

**Credit Cascade Risk:** The world model estimates 55-60% probability of broader credit market cascade by March 31. If this materializes, regional banks face mark-to-market losses on held-for-sale portfolios, widening credit spreads on bank-issued debt, and potential deposit migration to perceived safer institutions.

## Investment Thesis

### Bull Case
- Trading at 11.6x earnings with 2.65% yield provides reasonable entry for a Southeast franchise
- UBS $121 target implies significant upside; institutional sponsorship remains
- Southeast demographic growth provides secular tailwind
- Conservative payout ratio (~30-35%) preserves capital for credit losses or buybacks
- Stock already 16% off highs, partially pricing in macro risk

### Bear Case
- Stagflationary macro creates triple pressure: weak loan demand + elevated funding costs + rising credit losses
- CRE concentration (typical for Southeast regionals) is directly exposed to rising cap rates and weakening demand
- No rate cuts → NIM expansion stalls; recession → provisioning increases; both → EPS declines
- February 27 credit quality selloff signals market concern; this risk has not been resolved
- President sold $1M+ in stock near highs; no insider buying observed
- Regional banking franchise lacks diversification against macro downside
- 38-42% recession probability with all six stagflation dimensions confirmed creates a hostile operating environment for banks with concentrated credit exposure

## Conclusion

SSB is a well-run Southeast regional bank trading at a fair valuation. The 11.6x P/E and 2.65% yield are not demanding, but they do not provide sufficient compensation for the credit cycle risk in the current macro environment. The February 27 selloff on credit quality concerns was an early signal; if recession materializes (38-42% probability), provisioning increases will compress EPS below the current $7.87 level, making the 11.6x multiple less attractive than it appears.

The absence of detailed financial data (balance sheet, loan composition, capital ratios) from the available feeds is itself a constraint on conviction. Without visibility into CRE concentration, reserve coverage, and duration profile, the credit risk assessment relies on inference from sector-level patterns and the one credit-quality-driven selloff.

**Recommendation: HOLD.** The stock should be monitored for: (1) evidence of credit deterioration in upcoming quarterly filings, (2) macro stabilization that would reduce recession probability below 30%, and (3) a price decline to the $77-80 range (approximately 10x trailing earnings) which would provide a more attractive entry point given the risk profile.
