## Executive Summary

United Parks & Resorts, the parent company of SeaWorld, Busch Gardens, and related theme parks, has experienced a severe de-rating over the past year. The stock trades at $32.95, down 44% from its 52-week high of $56.95 and hovering near its 52-week low of $29.62. The decline reflects fundamental deterioration: FY2025 net income fell 26%, attendance declined year-over-year, international visitors dropped, and Q4 2025 EPS of $0.28 missed consensus estimates of $0.46 by 39%.

Management has responded with a $50M cost reduction plan and is citing strong 2026 forward indicators (group bookings pacing up 50%+, Discovery Cove advanced bookings up high single digits). At ~10.8x trailing earnings, the stock appears optically cheap. However, the current macro regime—stagflationary pressures with oil above $100, consumer spending contracting, and gasoline prices directly reducing discretionary budgets—creates a hostile operating environment for a business entirely dependent on discretionary consumer visits. The absence of detailed financial statements limits the depth of balance sheet and cash flow analysis, but the available data points consistently toward operational pressure with limited near-term catalysts for reversal.

## Company Overview

United Parks & Resorts (formerly SeaWorld Entertainment) operates theme parks including SeaWorld (Orlando, San Antonio, San Diego), Busch Gardens (Tampa, Williamsburg), and related properties. The business model depends on attendance volume, per-capita guest spending, and seasonal patterns with heavy weighting toward summer and holiday periods. Market capitalization is approximately $1.55B with ~48.6M shares outstanding.

## Financial Analysis

**Earnings:** Trailing EPS is $3.06, yielding a P/E of ~10.8x at the current price. However, this figure incorporates what management acknowledged as a disappointing FY2025. Q4 2025 EPS of $0.28 represented a 39% miss versus the $0.46 consensus, suggesting the deterioration accelerated into year-end. The Houston Chronicle reported FY2025 profit fell 26%, which would imply prior-year earnings were approximately $4.14/share—meaning the earnings trajectory is sharply negative.

**Revenue:** News sources confirm declines in both attendance and revenue for FY2025. The Orlando Sentinel quoted CEO Marc Swanson acknowledging "fiscal 2025 results did not meet our expectations." Without detailed income statement data, I cannot decompose the revenue miss between volume (attendance) and pricing (per-capita spending), but the combination of both declining simultaneously is a negative signal about pricing power.

**Cost Structure:** Management announced a $50M cost reduction plan on the Q4 earnings call. This is material relative to the ~$1.55B market cap but represents a defensive response to revenue pressure rather than a growth initiative. Theme parks have high fixed costs (maintenance, staffing, animal care), limiting the degree to which costs can flex without degrading the guest experience.

**Balance Sheet:** Detailed balance sheet data is unavailable. This is a significant analytical gap. Theme park operators typically carry substantial debt from prior leveraged buyouts and capital-intensive expansion. PRKS's predecessor entity carried meaningful leverage, and without current debt/equity data, I cannot assess refinancing risk or interest coverage.

**Forward P/E:** The forward P/E of 10.8x (matching trailing, per the data) implies consensus expects roughly flat EPS in FY2026, which is consistent with a scenario where cost cuts offset continued attendance headwinds.

## Growth Analysis

**Near-Term Catalysts (Positive):**
- Group bookings pacing up 50%+ for 2026, per the Q4 earnings call
- Discovery Cove advanced bookings up high single digits
- New attraction and event lineup planned for 2026
- $50M cost reduction provides a bridge to improved profitability

**Near-Term Headwinds (Negative):**
- FY2025 attendance declined with particular weakness in international visitors
- Consumer discretionary spending is contracting across the economy—retail sales fell 0.2% in January, multiple consumer-facing companies (Lululemon, Macy's, Ulta) have guided lower for 2026
- Gasoline prices at elevated levels directly reduce both the willingness to take family trips and the disposable income available for theme park spending
- Oil at $98-108 increases transportation costs for the company (fleet, logistics) and reduces consumer travel budgets
- Breach Inlet Capital sold its entire $14M position, eliminating 6.7% of AUM exposure—institutional de-risking

**Structural Considerations:** The theme park industry faces a capacity problem. Comcast (Universal), Disney, Six Flags/Cedar Fair, and PRKS are all adding attractions simultaneously. Per The Motley Fool, the entire sector declined 20-68% in 2025 despite what "seemed like a perfect climate." This suggests the 2024-2025 post-COVID attendance normalization is complete, and the industry is now competing for a shrinking pool of discretionary consumer dollars.

## Valuation Assessment

At 10.8x trailing earnings, PRKS is trading at the low end of historical ranges for theme park operators. The Motley Fool noted the sector trades at 7-15x projected earnings. This appears cheap in isolation but requires context:

- Earnings are declining, so trailing P/E overstates forward affordability if EPS deteriorates further
- A 26% profit decline in FY2025 and a Q4 miss suggest earnings estimates may still be too high
- The stock has found no durable floor despite approaching 10x earnings, which indicates the market is pricing in continued earnings erosion
- The $1.55B market cap on $3.06 EPS implies the market sees material downside risk to that earnings base

A simplistic scenario: if FY2026 EPS recovers to $3.50 (cost cuts offset attendance pressure) and the market awards a 12x multiple, fair value would be ~$42. If EPS deteriorates to $2.50 (macro headwinds overwhelm cost cuts), fair value at 10x is $25. The current price of $32.95 sits roughly at the midpoint of these scenarios.

## Competitive Landscape

PRKS competes with Disney (Parks), Comcast (Universal), and Six Flags Entertainment (merged with Cedar Fair). PRKS is the smallest of these by a wide margin, which creates both vulnerability (less pricing power, fewer IP franchises) and optionality (acquisition target). SeaWorld's marine life differentiation is real but has historically been a liability following the "Blackfish" documentary era, though the company has successfully repositioned toward roller coasters and thrill rides.

The competitive gap is widening: Disney and Universal are investing tens of billions in park expansions (Epic Universe opened in 2025), while PRKS operates with a fraction of that capital budget. This creates a structural disadvantage in attracting visitors to the Orlando market specifically.

## Risk Assessment

**Macro Sensitivity (HIGH):** Theme parks are among the most cyclically sensitive consumer businesses. Every dollar of revenue is discretionary. In the current stagflationary environment with gasoline above $4, families making travel decisions will disproportionately cut theme park visits. The 38-42% recession probability in the world model directly threatens attendance.

**Energy Cost Exposure (HIGH):** Oil at $98-108 creates a double hit: (1) consumers spend more on gasoline, reducing discretionary budgets, and (2) the company faces higher operating costs for transportation, utilities, and supply chain. Theme parks are energy-intensive operations.

**Balance Sheet Uncertainty:** Without financial statement data, debt levels and covenant compliance cannot be assessed. If leverage is high (typical for the sector), rising rates and declining EBITDA create refinancing risk.

**Institutional Exodus:** Breach Inlet Capital liquidating a $14M position (6.7% of AUM) is a significant negative signal. When concentrated holders exit entirely, it typically reflects a thesis break rather than routine rebalancing.

**Insider Activity:** All recent insider transactions are tax-withholding dispositions (F-coded), which are non-discretionary and uninformative. No voluntary open-market purchases by insiders, which would be a positive signal at these depressed levels. The CEO (Swanson) disposed of 5,597 shares at $36.30 for tax withholding but retains 390,109 shares, indicating significant skin in the game.

## Investment Thesis

**Bull Case:**
- 10.8x earnings on a business with real assets (parks, land, IP) provides a valuation floor
- $50M cost reduction plan, if executed, could stabilize margins even with flat-to-declining attendance
- Group bookings up 50%+ and Discovery Cove bookings up high single digits suggest 2026 demand is improving from the trough
- Potential acquisition target given small market cap and real asset base
- If macro stabilizes, leisure/experience spending could recover disproportionately

**Bear Case:**
- Earnings trajectory is sharply negative (26% profit decline in FY2025, Q4 miss of 39%)
- Consumer discretionary contraction is broadening—multiple independent data points confirm spending pullback
- Oil above $100 directly compresses both consumer travel budgets and operating margins
- Competitive position weakening as Disney and Universal invest multiples of PRKS's capital budget
- No analyst price targets available suggests limited institutional coverage and potential for further de-rating
- Balance sheet leverage (likely) creates refinancing risk in a rising-rate environment
- Stock approaching 52-week low with no technical support established

## Conclusion

PRKS is a deteriorating consumer discretionary business trading at an optically cheap valuation in a hostile macro environment. The 10.8x P/E reflects genuine uncertainty about the earnings trajectory, and the Q4 miss suggests consensus estimates may still be too high. The $50M cost reduction plan and strong group booking data provide some offset, but these are insufficient to overcome the combined headwinds of stagflationary consumer pressure, elevated energy costs, and intensifying competition from better-capitalized peers.

Per calibration guidelines, Consumer Discretionary BUY calls require positive sequential comparable revenue growth, FCF yield ≥5%, and non-declining consumer confidence—none of which are met here. The stock warrants a HOLD: cheap enough that further downside may be limited if cost cuts execute, but with too many unresolved headwinds and insufficient data (missing financial statements, no analyst targets, no balance sheet visibility) to justify a BUY. The primary monitoring trigger for upgrade would be Q1 2026 attendance data showing stabilization, combined with evidence that the cost reduction program is on track. A break below $29.62 (52-week low) would warrant reassessment toward AVOID.
