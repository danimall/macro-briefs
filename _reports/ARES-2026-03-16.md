## Executive Summary

Ares Management is a leading global alternative asset manager with approximately $484B+ in AUM, anchored in private credit — the precise asset class now facing its most severe stress test since the 2020 liquidity crisis. The stock has declined from $195.26 (52-week high in August 2025) to $101.76 as of March 13, 2026, a 48% drawdown. The immediate catalyst was the February 5 Q4 earnings report, which despite showing record 2025 fundraising and fee-related results, coincided with the onset of the Iran-Hormuz geopolitical crisis and the broader credit market deterioration.

The core problem is timing and regime. ARES operates an asset-light, fee-generating business model that is structurally sound in normal environments. But the current macro regime — confirmed stagflation across all six dimensions, 55-60% credit cascade probability with 21+ independent data points, JPMorgan marking down private credit loans, retail redemption waves from semi-liquid vehicles, and the March 31 quarter-end focal point — places Ares squarely in the crosshairs of a sector-wide repricing. At ~58x trailing P/E with GAAP net income of only $54.2M in Q4, the stock offers no valuation cushion against these headwinds.

The long-term franchise value is real: Ares has diversified into real assets, infrastructure, and AI-adjacent lending. But buying an alternative asset manager at 58x earnings during a credit cycle inflection with active private-to-public contagion is a bet that the stress resolves quickly — and the evidence points the other direction.

## Company Overview

Ares Management Corporation is one of the largest global alternative investment managers, operating across four primary segments: Credit Group, Real Estate Group, Private Equity Group, and Infrastructure/Real Assets. The firm is headquartered in Los Angeles and publicly listed on the NYSE. As of year-end 2025, management reported record fundraising, investment activity, and fee-related results. CEO Michael Arougheti is a co-founder and has guided the firm's expansion from a credit-focused shop into a diversified alternatives platform.

The company's revenue is generated primarily from management fees (based on committed or invested capital), performance/incentive fees (based on fund returns exceeding hurdle rates), and principal investment income. The fee-related earnings (FRE) component is the most stable and predictable, while realized performance income is inherently volatile and cycle-dependent.

## Valuation Assessment

ARES at ~58x trailing earnings is expensive by any standard metric for a financial services company. Context:

- **Peer comparison:** Apollo (APO) trades at ~13.9x forward P/E with permanent capital structures that cannot be gated. The APO/ARES valuation gap reflects Apollo's structural advantage in the current environment — Athene's permanent insurance capital is immune to retail redemption pressure.
- **Historical context:** ARES traded at $95.80 at its 52-week low (reached March 12, 2026), meaning the current $101.76 is only 6% above the cycle low. The 52-week high of $195.26 represents a 92% premium to current levels.
- **Dividend yield as valuation floor:** The 5.3% dividend yield provides some support, but only if the dividend is maintained. In a severe credit stress scenario where performance fees disappear and FRE growth stalls, dividend cuts become possible.
- **EPS quality:** GAAP EPS of $1.76 includes significant noise from fund consolidation and non-cash items. Even on a distributable earnings basis (likely $3-5/share based on typical alt manager GAAP-to-DE conversion ratios), the stock trades at 20-34x DE — still elevated for a financial company entering a credit downcycle.

The valuation offers no margin of safety. A stock trading at 58x earnings needs to deliver accelerating growth, and the macro environment is delivering the opposite.

## Risk Assessment

**Critical near-term risks (next 3-6 months):**

1. **Credit cascade (55-60% probability, with March 31 focal point):** 21+ independent data points now support private credit stress. If Ares or any major competitor restricts semi-liquid fund redemptions, the sector reprices further. ARES stock fell 3.7% on the JPMorgan markdown news alone (March 11).

2. **Stagflationary macro environment:** Core PCE at 3.1%, GDP at 0.7%, NFP -92K. This combination compresses transaction activity (fewer deals, lower exit multiples), reduces performance fee generation, and pressures portfolio company earnings — the underlying credit quality of Ares's loan books deteriorates.

3. **Oil at $98-108 and rising:** Higher energy costs directly impair the operating performance of portfolio companies across Ares's credit and PE books, increasing default risk in the underlying loan portfolio.

4. **Retail redemption exposure:** The degree to which Ares's semi-liquid vehicles face redemption pressure is unclear from public data, but the sector-wide trend (FT confirmed billions in retail redemptions) creates headline risk and potential forced selling.

5. **Earnings volatility:** Q4 GAAP net income of $54.2M on a $22.3B market cap produces a 0.97% quarterly earnings yield — remarkably thin. Even modest negative surprises in Q1 2026 could push GAAP EPS negative given the performance fee sensitivity to market conditions.

6. **Insider selling pattern:** General Counsel Sagati Aghili sold shares across multiple tranches in January and February 2026 at prices between $126-164. While some sales may be programmatic, the timing (ahead of earnings and during credit stress) is adverse. The offsetting positive is Director Ashish Bhutani's $1.27M open-market purchase at $126.61 on February 6.

## Investment Thesis

### Bull Case
- Ares's franchise is durable and diversified. The firm has navigated prior credit cycles (2008, 2020) and emerged stronger, gaining market share as weaker competitors retreated. Record 2025 fundraising provides a multi-year management fee base. AI infrastructure lending is a genuine secular growth driver. The 5.3% dividend yield provides income while waiting for credit conditions to normalize. At $101.76, the stock has already priced in significant distress, and Director Bhutani's $1.27M purchase suggests informed insiders see value. If the credit cascade fails to materialize or resolves quickly, the stock could recover to $140-160 within 6-12 months.

### Bear Case
- The stock trades at 58x GAAP earnings with no margin of safety, entering the most severe private credit stress cycle since these data series began. 21 independent data points support a 55-60% credit cascade probability, with March 31 as a structural focal point for retail redemptions. JPMorgan's loan markdowns and lending restrictions directly impair Ares's funding ecosystem. If performance fees compress and a single major competitor gates redemptions, ARES could decline another 20-30% to the $70-80 range. The 48% drawdown from highs may represent only the first phase of repricing if the stagflationary regime persists. Apollo's permanent capital structure makes it the superior way to own alternatives in this environment.
