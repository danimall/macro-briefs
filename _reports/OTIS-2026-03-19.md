## Executive Summary

Otis Worldwide has declined 23% from its April 2025 52-week high of $105.95 to $78.53 as of March 18, 2026. The selloff reflects two distinct catalysts: a Q4 2025 earnings report where revenue missed consensus (driven by softer China new equipment sales) and the broader March 2026 market drawdown tied to oil price spikes, hawkish Fed repricing, and stagflationary macro conditions. At $78.53, OTIS trades at approximately 22.4x forward earnings on $3.50 EPS.

The investment case for Otis rests on its large installed base (~2.3M units globally) generating high-margin recurring service revenue. This business model provides genuine defensive characteristics — elevators require maintenance regardless of economic conditions. However, new equipment sales (approximately 40% of revenue) are cyclically exposed to global construction activity, and the China market, which represents a substantial share of new installations, remains in structural decline. The Q4 miss and management's 2026 guidance for modest sales growth on service strength suggest the new equipment headwind persists.

The current valuation at 22x earnings is reasonable for a quality industrial compounder but does not offer a meaningful margin of safety given the macro environment: rising real yields, $119 oil threatening global construction economics, and the Fed now discussing rate hikes. The stock requires either a material further pullback or evidence of China stabilization to become compelling.

## Company Overview

Otis Worldwide is the world's largest elevator and escalator manufacturer and service provider. Spun off from United Technologies (now RTX) in April 2020, the company operates in two segments: New Equipment (manufacturing and installation of elevators, escalators, and moving walkways) and Service (maintenance, repair, and modernization of existing equipment). The company operates in approximately 200 countries and territories with roughly 2.3 million units in its global maintenance portfolio.

The business model's core strength is the "razor/razorblade" dynamic: new equipment installations create long-term service contracts, and service revenue carries significantly higher margins than new equipment revenue. Service contracts typically last 3-5 years and renew at high rates. This installed base grows with each new installation, creating a compounding annuity stream.

Market cap stands at $31.4 billion on 388.7 million shares outstanding. The company pays a dividend yielding approximately 2.0%.

## Growth Analysis

**Growth Drivers:**
1. **Service portfolio expansion:** Each new elevator installed adds to the maintenance annuity. The global installed base grows 3-4% annually.
2. **Modernization demand:** Aging global elevator fleet (especially in Europe and North America) creates upgrade opportunities at higher margins than new equipment.
3. **Pricing power in service:** Contractual escalators and limited competitive switching in maintenance provide consistent mid-single-digit service revenue growth.

**Growth Headwinds:**
1. **China construction downturn:** China represents the world's largest elevator market by new installations. The ongoing property market weakness directly suppresses Otis's new equipment pipeline. Q4 results confirmed this continues to be a drag.
2. **Currency translation:** With ~60%+ of revenue outside the US, dollar strength compresses reported results. The current macro environment (hawkish Fed, rate hike discussion) strengthens the dollar further.
3. **Macro cyclicality of new equipment:** Rising mortgage rates (7-month highs), $119 oil, and potential rate hikes all reduce global construction starts, directly impacting new equipment orders with a 6-12 month lag.
4. **Flat EPS trajectory:** Forward and trailing P/E are identical at 22.4x, confirming market expectations for near-zero earnings growth in the near term.

Growth is likely constrained to low-to-mid single digits for the next 12-18 months, with service providing stability while new equipment volume declines or stagnates.

## Valuation Assessment

**Valuation Context:**
- 22.4x forward P/E on flat-to-low-single-digit growth is reasonable for a quality industrial with recurring revenue, but it does not embed a discount for current macro risks
- The 2% dividend yield provides some downside support
- Market cap of $31.4B implies an EV (assuming ~$7-8B net debt based on historical leverage) of approximately $39-40B
- On estimated EBITDA of ~$2.2-2.4B, this implies EV/EBITDA of approximately 16-18x

**Peer comparison:** Competitors Schindler and KONE trade at similar or slightly higher multiples, reflecting the oligopolistic structure of the global elevator market. Otis's valuation is in-line with this peer group.

The stock does not screen as cheap. A 20x forward P/E (~$70) would provide a more compelling entry point given the macro headwinds, representing roughly 10% additional downside from current levels.

## Competitive Landscape

Otis holds the #1 global market position in elevator/escalator manufacturing and service. The competitive landscape is an oligopoly dominated by four players: Otis, Schindler, KONE, and TK Elevator (Thyssenkrupp). Together, they control approximately 65-70% of the global market.

**Moat characteristics:**
- **Installed base lock-in:** Building owners typically retain the OEM for maintenance because of proprietary components, software, and technician familiarity. Switching costs are high.
- **Regulatory barriers:** Elevator maintenance is heavily regulated in most jurisdictions, creating compliance barriers for new entrants.
- **Scale advantages:** Global footprint enables component sourcing efficiencies and the ability to serve multinational building owners across geographies.
- **Brand/trust:** In a safety-critical product category, building owners and developers strongly prefer established OEMs.

The competitive moat is genuine and durable. The primary competitive risk is price competition in China, where local manufacturers (such as Shenyang Yuanda and SJEC) compete aggressively on new equipment pricing.

## Risk Assessment

**Primary Risks:**

1. **China new equipment decline (HIGH):** Q4 results confirmed ongoing weakness. China property market structural adjustment could persist for 2-3 more years. Each quarter of weak new equipment sales in China means fewer units entering the future service portfolio.

2. **Stagflationary macro environment (HIGH):** Oil at $119 → higher construction input costs → fewer new building starts globally. Rate hike discussion → higher mortgage rates → reduced commercial and residential construction. These factors compress new equipment demand with a lag.

3. **Currency headwinds (MODERATE):** Hawkish Fed strengthens the dollar. With majority of revenue earned overseas, FX translation reduces reported earnings. A 5% dollar move can impact EPS by approximately 3-4%.

4. **Balance sheet leverage (MODERATE):** Historically 3-4x net debt/EBITDA. In a rising rate environment, debt service costs increase on refinancing. Limited capacity for major acquisitions or share repurchases beyond current pace.

5. **Insider selling (MODERATE):** Notable $4.4M sale by departing Asia Pacific president Stephane de Montlivault on February 12, 2026. CAO Ryan also sold ~$106K worth after vesting. No insider buying observed. The pattern is mildly negative — routine vesting disposals plus one outsized sale.

**Mitigating Factors:**
- Service revenue (60%+ of total) is largely non-discretionary
- Multi-year maintenance contracts provide revenue visibility
- Dividend provides floor support
- Oligopolistic market structure limits margin destruction

## Investment Thesis

### Bull Case
- The stock has already discounted significant China weakness and macro deterioration, trading 26% below its 52-week high
- Service business is genuinely recession-resistant with 85%+ retention rates and contractual price escalators
- If China stabilizes or global construction activity proves more resilient than feared, earnings recovery to $3.80-4.00 EPS on a 23-25x multiple implies $87-100
- Modernization demand in developed markets provides a secular growth vector independent of new construction
- 2% dividend yield provides income while waiting

### Bear Case
- China new equipment continues to deteriorate, dragging total revenue growth negative
- Rising rates and $119 oil suppress global construction starts, compounding China weakness
- Forward EPS of $3.50 could decline to $3.20-3.30 if FX headwinds and volume declines materialize simultaneously
- At 20x on $3.25 EPS, the stock trades at $65, representing 17% further downside
- Leverage limits management's ability to offset revenue declines with buybacks
- Josh Brown (prominent fund manager) publicly sold the stock in February 2026, citing negative momentum

## Conclusion

OTIS is a high-quality industrial franchise with a durable competitive moat and a business model that generates stable recurring revenue. These characteristics are well-understood and largely reflected in the current 22x valuation. The stock has corrected meaningfully from its highs, but the correction appears driven by legitimate fundamental concerns (China weakness, macro headwinds) rather than temporary sentiment. The current macro regime — stagflation, hawkish Fed, $119 oil — is hostile to the new equipment cycle, and China shows no signs of inflecting.

At $78.53, the risk/reward is balanced. The service business provides a floor, but macro headwinds cap near-term upside. A more attractive entry point would be in the $68-72 range (approximately 20x on potentially reduced forward estimates), which would provide a genuine margin of safety. **HOLD.**

Why not BUY: (1) Flat earnings trajectory with downside risks from China and FX. (2) No insider buying to signal management conviction at current levels. (3) Macro environment (rate hike discussion, $119 oil) directly pressures the new equipment cycle globally. (4) 22x forward P/E does not embed a discount for these known risks.
