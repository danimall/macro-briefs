## Executive Summary

TechnipFMC reported Q4 2025 revenue of $2,517M and adjusted EPS of $0.70, beating the consensus estimate of $0.51 by 37%. The beat was subsea-led, consistent with a multi-year deepwater capex upcycle that has driven FTI shares from a 52-week low of $22.12 to $63.03 — a nearly 3x move. The company's integrated subsea model (iEPCI) gives it structural advantages in project execution and pricing power on large offshore developments.

The problem is valuation and insider behavior. At ~26x forward earnings, FTI trades at a substantial premium to energy services peers (SLB ~15x, WFRD ~11x, BKR ~20x). HSBC downgraded to Hold on February 20 with an average analyst target of $50.67, implying ~20% downside. In early March, six insiders — including the CEO, CFO, CTO, and President of Subsea — collectively sold approximately $50M in stock across multiple transactions. The CEO alone sold $45.7M worth of shares. While some of these were option exercises, the magnitude and coordination of selling is notable.

The macro backdrop is mixed for FTI. Oil at $98-108 supports deepwater project sanctioning, but the current geopolitical disruption (Hormuz closure, Saudi production cuts) creates uncertainty about long-cycle investment decisions. FTI benefits from elevated oil prices supporting offshore economics but faces potential headwinds if global recession probability (38-42%) materializes into reduced E&P capital budgets.

## Company Overview

TechnipFMC operates primarily through two segments: Subsea and Surface Technologies. The Subsea segment — which designs, manufactures, and installs subsea production systems and provides services to offshore oil and gas fields — is the dominant revenue and earnings driver. Surface Technologies provides flow measurement, separation, and loading/offloading systems. The company was formed from the 2017 merger of Technip and FMC Technologies and is incorporated in the UK.

FTI's competitive differentiation centers on its integrated EPCI (iEPCI) model, which combines engineering, procurement, construction, and installation under a single contract. This reduces interfaces between contractors, lowers project risk for operators, and gives FTI pricing power on complex deepwater developments. The company operates a fleet of specialized installation vessels and has manufacturing facilities globally.

## Growth Analysis

FTI's growth thesis rests on the deepwater capex upcycle. Global deepwater project sanctions have accelerated since 2023, driven by: (1) oil prices sustaining above breakeven levels for most deepwater projects ($45-55/bbl), (2) energy security concerns driving resource diversification, (3) Guyana, Brazil pre-salt, and West Africa developments in execution phase.

The Q4 earnings call highlighted a "subsea-led upswing," and the 37% EPS beat suggests operational leverage is materializing as project execution improves and higher-margin iEPCI contracts move through the backlog.

Risks to the growth trajectory: (1) If recession probability (38-42% per macro model) materializes, E&P capex budgets contract with a 6-12 month lag; (2) the current Hormuz disruption, while supporting spot oil prices, creates uncertainty that could delay FID on long-cycle deepwater projects; (3) the identical forward and trailing P/E suggests the market already views current earnings as near-peak.

The company has annualized returns of 49.7% over 5 years per Benzinga, outperforming the market by 37.65pp annually. This kind of performance naturally compresses future expected returns as the stock prices in the recovery.

## Valuation Assessment

At 26.2x forward earnings, FTI is expensive relative to energy services peers and its own history. During the 2017-2020 period when deepwater spending was depressed, FTI traded at much lower multiples (and was unprofitable in some periods). The current premium reflects the structural improvement in subsea margins and backlog quality.

HSBC's average analyst target of $50.67 implies 19.6% downside from the current $63.03. While individual analyst targets can be stale, HSBC's fresh downgrade (February 20, 2026, one day after Q4 results) incorporated the strong earnings and still concluded the stock was overvalued.

Applying a peer-average multiple of ~18-20x to the $2.80 annualized adjusted EPS yields a fair value range of $50-56, broadly consistent with the analyst consensus. The stock would need to sustain EPS growth toward $3.00-3.50 to justify the current price at reasonable multiples.

## Competitive Landscape

FTI holds a strong competitive position in subsea. The market is effectively an oligopoly: FTI, SLB's OneSubsea (post-Aker integration), and to a lesser extent Saipem. FTI's iEPCI model is differentiated and has been gaining market share. Barriers to entry include specialized vessel fleets ($500M+ each), decades of engineering expertise, and customer relationships built on multi-year project track records.

Surface Technologies faces more competition and lower margins. FTI's strategic pivot toward subsea concentration (the 2021 spin-off of Technip Energies) was designed to sharpen this competitive advantage.

Current geopolitical dynamics provide a mixed competitive backdrop: elevated oil prices support project economics, but FTI's global operations expose it to supply chain disruptions, particularly for specialized components sourced through or near conflict zones.

## Risk Assessment

**Insider Selling — Material Concern:** Between March 9-10, 2026, six insiders sold approximately $50M in stock. CEO Pferdehirt sold $45.7M (733,424 shares across three transactions at prices of $61.22-$63.12). CFO Melin sold $7.9M. CTO Rounce sold $10.7M. President of Subsea Landes sold $7.2M. CAO Light sold $413K. EVP of New Energy Duffe sold $3.0M. While some sales followed option exercises and likely reflect pre-planned 10b5-1 arrangements, the breadth (6 insiders) and magnitude ($50M total) in a single week warrants attention. The CEO retained 2.41M shares ($152M at current prices), so this represents partial profit-taking rather than abandonment, but the signal is negative at the margin.

**Macro/Cyclical Risk:** Oil price sensitivity is the dominant risk. FTI's revenue correlates with deepwater capex with a 1-2 year lag. If oil prices fall below $60-65/bbl sustained, deepwater project sanctions would slow materially. Current oil at $98-108 is supportive, but the elevated recession probability (38-42%) creates a scenario where oil demand destruction pulls prices lower within 6-12 months.

**Geopolitical Risk:** FTI operates globally including in the Middle East and Gulf of Guinea. The current Iran-US conflict has not directly impacted FTI's operations based on available data, but sustained regional instability could disrupt project timelines.

**Valuation Risk:** At 26x earnings with HSBC targeting ~$51, the stock has limited margin of safety. Any earnings miss or guidance reduction could trigger a sharp de-rating.

## Conclusion

FTI is a high-quality company operating at peak cyclical conditions with a stock price that reflects this. The Q4 beat was genuinely impressive (37% above consensus), and the subsea competitive position is strong. But the combination of 26x earnings, HSBC's downgrade, and $50M in insider selling within a single week creates an unfavorable risk-reward from the current $63.03 entry point. The stock has tripled from its 52-week low — most of the upcycle re-rating has already occurred.

For existing holders, there is no urgent reason to sell given the supportive oil price environment. For new capital, the risk-reward is poor: limited upside to analyst targets, meaningful downside if cycle turns or earnings disappoint. **HOLD.**
