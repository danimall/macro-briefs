## Executive Summary

Integer Holdings is a medical technology outsourced manufacturer specializing in components for cardiac rhythm management (CRM), neuromodulation, and cardio & vascular devices. The company reported FY2025 sales growth of ~8% reported / ~6% organic with adjusted operating income up 13%, but a devastating guidance cut on October 23, 2025 triggered a 35% single-day stock decline (from ~$109 to ~$74) and spawned multiple securities fraud class action lawsuits alleging the company failed to disclose material information about deteriorating conditions during the preceding period.

The stock has partially stabilized in the $82-87 range as of March 2026, trading at ~30x trailing and forward earnings ($2.89 EPS) on a $2.9B market cap. The 2026 outlook calls for organic sales growth of flat to 3%, a sharp deceleration from FY2025's 6%+ organic growth. Three product headwinds were cited alongside the guidance cut. With active litigation, weak near-term growth, and a 30x P/E that requires sustained mid-to-high single digit growth to justify, the risk/reward is unfavorable for new positions. An institutional investor (Artisan Small Cap Fund) publicly exited the position, stating the thesis "did not materialize."

## Company Overview

Integer Holdings operates as a contract development and manufacturing organization (CDMO) for the medical device industry. The company's primary segments serve cardiac rhythm management & neuromodulation (CRM & Neuro) and cardio & vascular markets. Customers include large OEM medical device companies (Medtronic, Abbott, Boston Scientific, etc.) who outsource component manufacturing. The company has ~34.4 million shares outstanding and is headquartered in the US, listed on NYSE.

Key management: CEO Payman Khales, CFO Diron Smith, with segment presidents for Cardio & Vascular (Andrew Senn) and CRM & Neuro (Jim Stephens).

## Financial Analysis

**Revenue & Growth:** FY2025 full-year sales were guided to $1.840-1.854 billion (the revised lower range disclosed October 23, 2025), representing ~8% reported growth and 6%+ organic growth. Adjusted operating income grew 13% year-over-year. Q4 2025 results (reported February 19, 2026) beat expectations, with the CEO noting sales and adjusted EPS came in "towards the high end of the outlook range."

**Earnings:** Trailing EPS of $2.89 on a $84.70 current price yields a 30.2x trailing P/E, which equals the forward P/E, implying flat-to-marginal EPS growth expected. This is notable given the company guided 2026 organic sales growth of flat to 3%, a significant deceleration.

**Market Capitalization:** $2.91 billion on 34.4 million shares at $84.70.

**Balance Sheet & Cash Flow:** Detailed financial statements were not available in the data feed. The company does not pay a dividend. Without access to debt levels, free cash flow, or working capital metrics, a full balance sheet assessment is limited. The medical CDMO industry typically carries moderate leverage from acquisition-driven strategies.

**Valuation Multiples:** At 30x forward P/E with 0-3% organic growth guidance, the valuation implies the market either expects a significant growth reacceleration or is pricing in margin expansion. Neither appears well-supported by current guidance.

## Growth Analysis

**FY2025 Performance:** 8% reported revenue growth and 6%+ organic growth were solid. Adjusted operating income growing 13% implies meaningful margin expansion, likely from operating leverage on higher volumes and mix improvement.

**FY2026 Outlook:** The February 2026 guidance tightening calls for organic sales growth of flat to 3%. CEO Khales cited headwinds from "three new products," which appear to be customer-specific product transitions or discontinuations that create revenue gaps. This represents a dramatic growth deceleration from mid-single digit organic growth to near-zero.

**Structural Growth Drivers:** The medical device outsourcing trend remains intact as OEMs seek to reduce manufacturing complexity. Aging demographics support long-term demand for cardiac, neuromodulation, and vascular devices. These secular tailwinds are real but priced into a 30x multiple.

**Risk to Growth Trajectory:** The October 2025 guidance cut revealed that management either lacked visibility into near-term product pipeline transitions or chose not to disclose deteriorating conditions. Either interpretation is negative — the former suggests limited forecasting capability, the latter suggests governance issues now being litigated.

## Valuation Assessment

At $84.70 with $2.89 trailing EPS, ITGR trades at 30.2x earnings. For a company guiding flat-to-3% organic revenue growth in 2026, this multiple requires substantial faith in future reacceleration.

**Comparable Context:** Medical device CDMOs typically trade at 15-25x forward earnings depending on growth profile. A 30x multiple would be appropriate for a company delivering consistent 10%+ organic growth with margin expansion. ITGR's current growth trajectory does not support this.

**Price History Context:** The stock traded near $120 in March-April 2025, collapsed to $62 (52-week low) in late October 2025, and has recovered to ~$85. The current price is 34% below the 52-week high of $127.56 and 37% above the 52-week low of $62.00. The recovery from the October lows has been gradual, suggesting some investors see value, but the stock remains in a structurally lower range.

**Downside scenario:** If growth remains flat and the market re-rates to 20-22x earnings, fair value would be $58-64, near the October lows.

**Upside scenario:** If organic growth reaccelerates to 6-8% and EPS expands to $3.50+ by FY2027, a 25x multiple would imply $87.50 — roughly the current price. A return to the prior 30x multiple on $3.50 EPS would imply $105.

The risk/reward is roughly symmetric at current levels, with litigation risk skewing it negatively.

## Competitive Landscape

Integer's competitive position within medical device contract manufacturing is genuinely strong. The company has deep customer relationships with major OEMs, specialized manufacturing capabilities (batteries, leads, guidewires, catheters), and high switching costs inherent in medical device supply chain qualification. The 13D Activist Fund noted ITGR has "a competitive moat" even while the stock was down 40% YoY.

The barriers to entry are significant: FDA-regulated manufacturing processes require extensive qualification, and customer switching costs are measured in years of revalidation work. This creates sticky revenue once established.

However, this moat did not prevent the October 2025 guidance cut. The competitive advantage protects market position but does not insulate against customer product cycle transitions, which are inherently lumpy in the medical device industry.

## Risk Assessment

**Securities Fraud Litigation (HIGH):** Multiple law firms have filed class action lawsuits alleging Integer and its executives failed to disclose material information during the class period prior to the October 23, 2025 guidance cut. The February 6, 2026 deadline for lead plaintiff and a March 2, 2026 investigation announcement indicate this litigation is active and escalating. Even if ultimately settled or dismissed, legal costs and management distraction are real. Potential settlement liability is unquantifiable from available data but represents a genuine headwind.

**Guidance Credibility (HIGH):** The October 2025 guidance cut, the 10-Q amendment filed the next day (October 24), and the magnitude of the stock reaction (35% single-day decline) suggest the market lost confidence in management's forward visibility. The FY2026 guidance of flat-to-3% organic growth may itself be at risk if the three product headwinds prove larger than anticipated.

**Customer Concentration Risk (MODERATE-HIGH):** As a CDMO serving large OEMs, Integer likely has significant customer concentration. Product transitions at a single major customer can create the kind of revenue cliff observed in October 2025.

**Insider Activity:** The insider transactions are predominantly equity compensation awards and related tax withholdings, which are routine. The only discretionary sale was VP Corporate Controller Thomas selling 825 shares at $85.00 on March 13, 2026 ($70,125) — a small amount that is weakly negative but not alarming. CEO Khales' large RSU conversions and tax withholdings in February 2026 are mechanical, not directional signals.

**Institutional Exits:** Artisan Small Cap Fund publicly disclosed selling ITGR, stating the thesis "did not materialize." This is a negative signal from a sophisticated long-term holder.

**Macro Sensitivity:** Medical device volumes are moderately recession-resistant (elective procedures may defer, but cardiac and neuromodulation devices are largely non-discretionary). The current stagflationary macro regime creates modest headwinds through hospital capital budget pressure but is less damaging than for consumer discretionary companies.

## Investment Thesis

### Bull Case
- Competitive moat in medical device contract manufacturing is durable and the October 2025 guidance cut was a discrete product-transition event, not structural deterioration
- FY2025 results ultimately came in at the high end of guidance, suggesting management regained operational control
- Stock at $85 is 34% below its 2025 high and could rerate if FY2026 growth reaccelerates beyond the 0-3% guidance range
- Medical device outsourcing trend provides secular tailwind
- P/E of 30x could be justified if EPS grows to $3.50-4.00 over 2-3 years through organic growth recovery plus margin expansion

### Bear Case
- 30x P/E on flat-to-3% growth is expensive; comparable CDMOs trade at 15-25x
- Securities fraud litigation creates unquantifiable liability and ongoing management distraction
- Guidance credibility is damaged — the October 2025 cut blindsided investors with a same-day 10-Q amendment, suggesting either poor visibility or delayed disclosure
- Customer product transitions could create additional revenue headwinds beyond the three already identified
- An institutional holder (Artisan) publicly exited, citing thesis failure
- Downside to 20-22x earnings ($58-64) if growth disappoints further, while upside to $105+ requires multi-year execution with no further negative surprises

## Conclusion

ITGR presents a damaged franchise attempting recovery. The underlying business — medical device contract manufacturing with high switching costs — is sound, but the October 2025 guidance collapse and resulting litigation have structurally impaired investor confidence and management credibility. At 30x forward earnings on flat-to-3% organic growth guidance, the stock is priced for a growth reacceleration that has not yet materialized and faces meaningful downside if it does not.

The appropriate posture is HOLD for existing holders who maintained positions through the October decline, with the expectation of monitoring Q1-Q2 2026 results for evidence of growth stabilization. For new capital, the risk/reward is unattractive: litigation risk creates a left tail that is not offset by sufficient upside potential at current multiples. A more compelling entry point would be 20-22x forward earnings (~$58-64) or evidence of organic growth reaccelerating above 5% with litigation resolution progress.

**Why Not BUY:** Despite the 34% decline from highs and acknowledged competitive moat, the 30x P/E on near-zero growth guidance, active securities fraud litigation, institutional exits, and damaged management credibility create an unfavorable risk/reward. The stock needs either cheaper valuation or evidence of growth recovery to warrant a BUY recommendation.
