## Executive Summary

H&R Block trades at $30.52, down 53% from its 52-week high of $64.62, with a trailing P/E of 6.3x and a 5.6% dividend yield. The stock has been punished severely due to fears that AI-powered tax preparation tools (particularly from Intuit's TurboTax and emerging free AI alternatives) will structurally erode HRB's customer base. The company reported a fiscal Q2 2026 loss of $1.92/share (normal for the highly seasonal business where ~75% of revenue arrives in the March-June tax season), beat revenue estimates, and reaffirmed full-year guidance for revenue, EBITDA, adjusted EPS, and effective tax rate.

At 6.3x forward earnings, 0.37 beta, and a 5.6% dividend yield, HRB offers asymmetric value characteristics in the current stagflation regime. The company's cash generation profile, aggressive share repurchase program (shares outstanding have declined roughly 40% over the past decade), and essential-service demand floor create a margin of safety. Goldman Sachs maintains a Sell rating with a $32 target — only 5% above current price — which paradoxically suggests limited analyst-identified downside from here. The key question is whether AI disruption is a gradual share-loss dynamic (manageable with buybacks and cost discipline) or an existential threat that collapses the business model within 3-5 years.

The stock's current valuation prices in meaningful permanent revenue impairment. If the business merely sustains current earnings power, holders collect a 5.6% dividend plus the accretive effect of buybacks, generating mid-teens total returns annually. The bear case requires revenue declines of 5%+ annually sustained over multiple years — possible but not the base case given the complexity of the US tax code and the demographic profile of HRB's core customer (older, lower-income, comfort-seeking).

## Company Overview

H&R Block is the largest retail tax preparation company in the US, operating approximately 9,000+ locations across North America and Australia. The business has three primary channels: (1) assisted tax preparation (in-office with a tax professional), (2) DIY digital tax filing software, and (3) small business services including bookkeeping and payroll. The company's fiscal year ends June 30, making the January-April tax season the dominant revenue period.

The business is inherently seasonal — fiscal Q2 (October-December) consistently generates losses as the company maintains its infrastructure ahead of tax season. This explains the $242.2M loss and $1.92/share loss reported for Q2 FY2026. This is structural, expected, and consistent with the prior year's $1.73/share Q2 loss.

## Growth Analysis

HRB's growth profile is the weakest dimension of the investment thesis. The US tax preparation market grows roughly in line with population and tax code complexity — 1-3% annually. HRB's share of this market has been under pressure from:

1. **Free File alternatives and AI tools:** The IRS Direct File program and emerging AI-powered tax preparation tools threaten the low-complexity end of HRB's customer base. HRB is investing in its own AI capabilities (per the February 2026 press release about "AI Power with Digital Enhancements"), but the competitive moat around AI-assisted tax prep is thin.

2. **Intuit TurboTax dominance in DIY:** Intuit has captured the majority of the self-preparation digital market and continues to invest heavily.

3. **Demographic headwinds:** HRB's core assisted-preparation customer skews older. As this demographic ages out, replacement rates from younger filers (who are more comfortable with digital-only solutions) may be lower.

The company's response has been to emphasize the "transformation" narrative (per the August 2025 fiscal year results), expand small business services, and lean into hybrid digital-plus-human models. Management described FY2025 as "another year of meaningful progress in our transformation."

Revenue growth is likely to be 0-2% organic, with EPS growth of 3-6% driven primarily by share count reduction.

## Valuation Assessment

At 6.3x trailing and 6.38x forward earnings, HRB is cheap on an absolute basis and relative to the S&P 500's ~18-20x earnings multiple. The 5.56% dividend yield is at or near a record high for the company, per Seeking Alpha's analysis.

**Bear case valuation framework:** If HRB's earnings decline 5% annually for the next 5 years due to AI disruption, and the terminal multiple compresses to 5x, the stock is worth roughly $22-25 — representing 20-28% downside.

**Bull case valuation framework:** If HRB sustains current earnings and the multiple reverts to 8-9x (still well below historical averages of 10-14x), the stock is worth $38-43, representing 25-40% upside, plus the 5.6% annual dividend.

**Base case:** Flat-to-slightly-declining aggregate earnings, 3-5% annual share count reduction, maintained dividend. At 7x earnings, the stock is worth ~$34, or roughly 11% upside plus 5.6% yield = ~17% total return over 12 months.

Goldman Sachs' $32 Sell target (from February 6, 2026) is notable: even the most bearish major sell-side analyst sees only $1.50 of upside. This suggests the negative narrative is fully priced. When a Sell-rated stock's target is above the current price, the risk-reward skew has shifted.

**Investor accumulation signal:** A news article from March 12, 2026 indicates one investor built a $35M position with the stock down 40%. This is a meaningful capital commitment at current levels, suggesting at least one sophisticated participant sees value.

## Competitive Landscape

HRB operates in a three-player market for consumer tax preparation:

1. **Intuit (TurboTax)** — Dominant in DIY, increasingly pushing into assisted. Market cap ~$160B. Superior technology platform. The competitive threat is real and ongoing.

2. **H&R Block** — Dominant in assisted/retail, with a growing DIY presence. ~9,000 physical locations provide distribution advantage for complex returns and less tech-savvy customers.

3. **Jackson Hewitt / Liberty Tax** — Smaller competitors with less brand recognition.

4. **IRS Direct File / Free alternatives** — The IRS's own free filing tool and AI chatbot-based alternatives represent a new category of competition. However, the US tax code's complexity (particularly for filers with multiple income sources, investments, business income, or itemized deductions) creates a natural floor for professional preparation demand.

HRB's competitive moat rests on: (a) brand recognition and trust in tax preparation, (b) physical retail presence that serves customers who want human interaction, (c) the structural complexity of the US tax code, which makes fully automated solutions inadequate for a significant portion of filers, and (d) the annual, recurring nature of tax obligations.

The moat is narrowing but has not collapsed. The company's 0.37 beta reflects the essential-service, recession-resistant nature of tax preparation — people must file taxes regardless of economic conditions.

## Risk Assessment

**AI Disruption (Primary Risk):** The most significant risk is that AI-powered tools make assisted tax preparation obsolete for a critical mass of HRB's customer base. If ChatGPT-quality tools can accurately prepare complex returns within 2-3 years, HRB's in-office model faces secular decline beyond what buybacks can offset. Probability of existential disruption within 5 years: 15-20%. Probability of meaningful (5%+ annual) revenue erosion: 35-40%.

**Leverage Risk:** HRB's debt load constrains flexibility. In a scenario where revenue declines faster than expected, the combination of debt service, dividend, and buyback commitments could force a dividend cut — which would crush the stock given its income-investor base.

**CEO Transition:** Curtis Campbell became President & CEO in January 2026, replacing Jeffrey Jones. Leadership transitions during challenging periods introduce execution risk. Jones sold $13.9M in stock at $44-46 on his way out — not a vote of confidence in the stock at those higher levels.

**Macro Sensitivity:** HRB is relatively insulated from the stagflation regime — tax filing is non-discretionary, and the 0.37 beta confirms low macro sensitivity. A recession could modestly reduce filer volumes (fewer self-employed filers, fewer investment-related complications) but the core filing obligation persists.

**Regulatory Risk:** IRS Direct File expansion and any legislative simplification of the tax code would directly harm HRB. The probability of meaningful tax simplification remains low given Congressional dynamics, but the IRS Direct File program is a real and growing headwind.

## Conclusion

HRB is a BUY at $30.52. The stock trades at 6.3x earnings with a 5.6% dividend yield and 0.37 beta, offering a combination of deep value, high income, and low macro sensitivity that is rare in the current market. The AI disruption narrative is real but is already reflected in a 53% drawdown from 52-week highs. The base case of flat-to-modestly-declining aggregate earnings offset by buybacks generates mid-teens total returns over the next 12 months. The primary risk is that disruption accelerates faster than the buyback-supported EPS growth can absorb, but at 6.3x earnings, the required level of revenue destruction to justify the current price is severe.

Position sizing should be moderate (2-3% of portfolio) given the genuine structural uncertainty around AI disruption. The stock functions well as a low-beta income position that diversifies a portfolio tilted toward the defense, energy, and exchange themes dominating the current macro regime.
