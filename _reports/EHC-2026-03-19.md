## Executive Summary

Encompass Health is the largest operator of inpatient rehabilitation facilities (IRFs) in the United States, with a ~$9.7B market cap and shares trading at $99.68, roughly 22% below the 52-week high of $127.99. The company reported Q4 2025 earnings on February 5, 2026, which initially appeared strong — the stock gapped up from ~$99.56 to open at $115 on February 6 — but has since surrendered those gains entirely, closing at $99.68. Two law firms (Portnoy Law Firm and Lowey Dannenberg) have initiated investigations into potential federal securities law violations, which coincides with the stock's renewed decline from the $108-109 range in early March to current levels.

At ~18x forward earnings with an EPS of $5.55, EHC is neither cheap nor expensive for a healthcare services company with a demonstrated organic growth pipeline. The company continues to build new facilities (most recently a 40-bed hospital in Bear, Delaware). However, the legal investigations, Medicaid reimbursement uncertainty in a fiscal tightening environment, and elevated debt levels from its expansion program create a risk profile that precludes an upgrade from HOLD. The data limitations here are significant — financial statements were not available in the provided dataset, so this analysis relies heavily on market data, news, filings cadence, and insider transactions.

## Company Overview

Encompass Health operates inpatient rehabilitation hospitals across the US, serving patients recovering from strokes, brain injuries, spinal cord injuries, hip fractures, and other complex medical conditions. The company is the market leader in this niche, which benefits from an aging US population and the structural shift toward post-acute care settings that reduce overall healthcare costs relative to extended acute-care hospital stays.

The company files annual 10-K reports regularly (most recent: February 26, 2026) and quarterly 10-Qs on schedule, suggesting standard corporate governance on the disclosure front. The shares trade on the NYSE with ~99.4M shares outstanding.

Each new facility adds incremental revenue capacity, typically ramping to full utilization over 2-3 years.

**Capacity Additions:** The new Delaware facility, combined with what appears to be an ongoing national buildout program, provides a visible multi-year growth runway. Each 40-bed hospital generates roughly $30-50M in annual revenue at maturity based on industry averages.

## Valuation Assessment

**Relative to History:** The current ~18x multiple is roughly in line with the stock's historical average, suggesting the market has repriced EHC from a premium (at the highs) back to fair value.

**Key Valuation Consideration:** The stock price at $99.68 is essentially unchanged from March 2025 levels (~$97-100), meaning the full September highs and subsequent collapse have netted to zero price appreciation over the past year. Investors who bought at $127 have lost 22%.

## Competitive Landscape

Encompass Health is the dominant player in the US inpatient rehabilitation hospital market. Key competitive advantages:

1. **Scale:** As the largest IRF operator, EHC benefits from purchasing power, standardized clinical protocols, and brand recognition with referral sources (acute care hospitals, physicians).

2. **Certificate of Need (CON) Barriers:** Many states require CON approval for new healthcare facilities, creating regulatory moats for existing operators. EHC's established presence in 35+ states makes it difficult for competitors to replicate its network.

3. **Payor Relationships:** Established Medicare and commercial insurance contracts provide revenue stability. Medicare is the dominant payor for IRF services (~60-65% of industry revenue).

4. **Clinical Outcomes:** Recognition on Fortune and Forbes 2026 lists (February 24 announcement) suggests above-average performance on quality metrics, which matters for referral patterns and payor negotiations.

Competitors include Select Medical (SEM) and Kindred Healthcare (private), but neither matches EHC's scale in the pure-play IRF segment.

## Risk Assessment

### Securities Law Investigations (HIGH — New and Unresolved)

Two law firms — Portnoy Law Firm (March 10) and Lowey Dannenberg (March 16) — have initiated investigations into potential federal securities law violations. These announcements are ambulance-chasing in many cases, but the timing (coinciding with the October-December selloff and the failed February earnings recovery) suggests there may be a substantive underlying issue. The October 30 price collapse ($125.63 → $116.81 on 3.3M volume) and subsequent continued selling are consistent with a material disclosure that may form the basis of these investigations.

The investigations could lead to class-action lawsuits, which would create legal costs, management distraction, and potential settlement payments. Until the substance of the investigations is clarified, this represents a material unquantified risk.

### Medicaid/Medicare Reimbursement Risk (MODERATE-HIGH)

In the current fiscal environment, with the federal government discussing spending cuts and Medicaid facing state-level budget pressure, reimbursement rates for post-acute care facilities are at risk. Medicare accounts for the majority of IRF revenue, and any reduction in per-case reimbursement rates would directly compress margins.

The world model's stagflationary environment (40-45% recession probability, potential rate hikes) compounds this: healthcare labor costs are rising with inflation, while government payor rates may be flat or declining in real terms.

### Debt/Leverage Risk (MODERATE)

The expansion program requires significant capital expenditure. In a rising-rate environment (with the Fed discussing hikes), the cost of carrying and refinancing debt increases. News sources explicitly cite "higher debt" as a concern.

### Labor Cost Inflation (MODERATE)

Healthcare is labor-intensive, and rehabilitation hospitals require specialized nursing, physical therapy, occupational therapy, and speech therapy staff. Wage inflation in healthcare has been running above general inflation. With oil at $119 adding to cost-of-living pressures, labor cost containment will be challenging.

### Insider Selling (MILD NEGATIVE)

The SVP/Treasurer Edmund Fay sold 11,937 shares at $107 on March 9 ($1.28M), plus gifted 3,000 shares. While most other transactions were routine tax withholdings and option grants, the discretionary sale by a senior officer during the stock's downturn is mildly concerning. CEO Mark Tarr retains a substantial position (440,962 shares, ~$44M at current prices), which provides some alignment comfort.

## Investment Thesis

### Bull Case

- **Leader in a structurally growing market:** Aging demographics drive 3-5% annual volume growth for IRF services for at least the next decade. EHC's dominant market position means it captures a disproportionate share.
- **Facility expansion provides visible growth:** New hospitals (Delaware, others in pipeline) add capacity that compounds revenue growth above the industry average. At ~18x earnings, the stock doesn't require aggressive growth assumptions to justify the current price.
- **Valuation reset creates entry point:** Down 22% from highs, the stock has repriced from premium to fair value. If legal investigations prove immaterial and earnings growth continues at mid-to-high single digits, the stock has 15-20% upside back toward $115-120.
- **Defensive healthcare positioning:** In a recessionary or stagflationary environment, healthcare services are relatively non-discretionary. People still have strokes and hip fractures regardless of the economic cycle.

### Bear Case

- **Securities investigations may have substance:** The October 30 price collapse was unusually violent for a healthcare services company. If the investigations reveal material accounting issues, undisclosed operational problems, or misleading guidance, the stock could face another 15-25% downside.
- **Medicaid cuts in a deficit-reduction environment:** Federal and state governments seeking to reduce spending may target post-acute care reimbursement, directly compressing margins. A 5% reduction in Medicare IRF rates could reduce EPS by 15-20%.
- **Debt burden in a rising-rate environment:** If the Fed hikes rates, EHC's expansion-funded debt becomes more expensive to service. Refinancing risk increases.
- **Failed earnings recovery:** The February 6 earnings gap-up/reversal is a bearish signal. Strong fundamental results failed to arrest the decline, suggesting the market sees structural issues beyond quarterly performance.
- **Labor cost inflation:** With CPI heading toward 3.5-5.5% and healthcare wages typically running above headline inflation, margin compression is probable unless pricing power keeps pace.

## Conclusion

EHC is a well-positioned healthcare services company trading at a reasonable ~18x forward earnings with a clear organic growth pipeline. However, the combination of active securities investigations, a failed post-earnings recovery, Medicaid policy risk, and rising labor/interest costs creates a risk-reward profile that is approximately balanced at current levels. The October-December selloff cleared much of the premium, but the legal overhang is unresolved and the stock's inability to hold the February 6 earnings gap is a negative technical signal.

**Recommendation: HOLD.** The stock merits monitoring for: (1) resolution or escalation of the securities investigations, (2) clarity on Medicaid/Medicare reimbursement policy in the current fiscal environment, and (3) evidence that the expansion program is translating to margin-accretive revenue growth rather than debt-funded capacity that dilutes returns. A pullback to $90-92 (the 52-week low zone, representing ~16.5x earnings) with investigation clarity would create a more compelling entry. An upgrade to BUY would require either dismissal of the legal investigations or a demonstrated re-acceleration in earnings growth above 10%.
