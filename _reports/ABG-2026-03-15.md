## Executive Summary

Asbury Automotive Group is one of the largest US automotive retail and service companies, currently trading at $196.48 with a market capitalization of $3.78B and a forward P/E of 7.7x. The stock sits near its 52-week low of $194.80, down ~28% from its 52-week high of $274.50. Q4 2025 earnings of $6.67/share narrowly missed consensus expectations of $6.70, and the stock has drifted lower since.

The most constructive signal in the data is CEO David Hult's open-market purchases totaling ~$1.03M (5,000 shares at ~$205) in early March 2026, alongside a new director's $101K purchase. This is meaningful insider buying at a company where the prior pattern was exclusively tax-withholding disposals on vesting equity grants. However, the macro backdrop is severe for auto retail: oil above $100 compresses consumer budgets, rising recession probability (38-42%) threatens discretionary vehicle purchases, and tightening credit conditions directly impair auto lending — the financing channel through which most vehicle sales occur.

## Company Overview

Asbury Automotive operates approximately 155+ dealership locations across the US, representing over 30 automotive brands. The company generates revenue from new vehicle sales, used vehicle sales, parts and service (fixed operations), and finance & insurance (F&I) products. Following its 2021 acquisition of the Larry H. Miller Dealerships for ~$3.2B, Asbury roughly doubled its scale and became the sixth-largest US auto retailer by revenue. The company has been executing a multi-year integration of that acquisition while pursuing a digital retailing platform (Clicklane) to facilitate online vehicle transactions.

## Growth Analysis

Asbury's growth story has two components:

**Acquisition-driven scale:** The LHM deal was transformative. Organic revenue growth in auto retail is typically low-single-digits, driven by SAAR (seasonally adjusted annual rate) trends and same-store performance. The industry SAAR has been running around 15.5-16.0M units, roughly in line with long-run averages, providing limited organic uplift.

**Fixed operations and F&I mix shift:** Parts & service and F&I products carry significantly higher margins than vehicle sales (gross margins of 50-60% for parts/service vs. 5-8% for new vehicles). Asbury has been growing its fixed operations contribution, which improves earnings stability and quality. This is the structural argument for a lower beta.

**Headwinds:** The current macro regime is explicitly hostile to auto retail growth. Gasoline prices up 21% compress disposable income for the core consumer. NFP at -92K and declining quit rates signal deteriorating employment conditions. Auto lending standards are tightening as credit risk rises. New vehicle affordability remains stretched with average transaction prices above $48K. Used vehicle values have been normalizing downward from pandemic peaks, compressing used vehicle margins.

The Q4 2025 earnings miss, while small ($0.03), may signal the beginning of margin pressure from these forces.

## Valuation Assessment

At 7.7x forward earnings, ABG trades at a discount to the S&P 500 (~18-20x) and roughly in line with auto retail peers (LAD ~7-8x, AN ~7-9x, PAG ~7-8x). The sector discount to the market is structural — auto retail is capital-intensive, cyclical, and carries meaningful leverage.

Within the peer group, ABG's valuation is neither notably cheap nor expensive. The stock's proximity to its 52-week low ($194.80 vs. current $196.48) suggests the market is pricing in earnings deterioration. At $196.48, the stock implies the market expects flat to slightly declining EPS from the ~$25.55 trailing level. If EPS declines to $22-23 in a mild recession scenario, the effective P/E rises to 8.5-9.0x, still within historical auto retail ranges but offering limited margin of safety.

A recovery to the 52-week high of $274.50 would imply ~10.7x trailing earnings — reasonable in a normalized environment but requiring macro cooperation that the current data does not support.

## Competitive Landscape

US auto retail is consolidating rapidly. The top six public groups (AutoNation, Lithia, Penske, Group 1, Hendrick, Asbury) now account for a growing share of the ~17,000 US franchise dealership market. Asbury's competitive position is solid:

- **Scale advantages** post-LHM acquisition enable better OEM allocation, financing terms, and fixed cost leverage.
- **Clicklane digital platform** provides differentiation in online vehicle retailing, though adoption metrics are not available in the provided data.
- **Geographic diversification** across approximately 15+ states reduces weather/regional economic concentration.
- **Franchise moats** are real: OEM franchise agreements limit new competitive entry, and existing franchises have significant replacement value. Asbury's franchises are primarily with Toyota, Honda, BMW, Mercedes — premium and high-volume brands with strong resale value.

The primary competitive risk is that larger peers (LAD, AN) with stronger balance sheets could outbid Asbury for acquisition targets, limiting future inorganic growth.

## Risk Assessment

**Macro/cyclical risk (HIGH):** This is the dominant risk. The world model describes a stagflationary environment with confirmed negative payrolls, above-target inflation, $100+ oil, and 38-42% recession probability. Auto retail is directly exposed through multiple channels: (1) higher gas prices reduce discretionary vehicle demand, (2) tighter credit conditions raise borrowing costs and reduce loan approvals, (3) declining employment reduces both demand and ability to service existing auto loans, (4) rising delinquencies in auto ABS could cause lenders to further restrict origination.

**Leverage risk (MODERATE-HIGH):** Post-LHM acquisition leverage is elevated. In a recession, declining EBITDA combined with fixed debt service creates potential covenant pressure. Without current balance sheet data, this risk cannot be precisely quantified.

**Tariff risk (MODERATE):** Section 301 probes targeting 60 economies could raise the cost of imported vehicles and parts. Japanese and Korean OEM brands — significant for Asbury — would be directly affected. Even domestic production uses substantial imported components.

**Inventory risk (MODERATE):** As credit tightens and demand softens, unsold inventory (particularly used vehicles) can depreciate, compressing margins and tying up working capital.

**Integration risk (LOW-MODERATE):** LHM integration is multi-year; execution missteps could impair expected synergies.

## Insider Activity

The insider transaction data contains one highly constructive signal: **CEO David Hult purchased 5,000 shares on March 6, 2026, for approximately $1.03M in two open-market transactions at $204.73 and $205.63.** This is a voluntary purchase with personal capital, distinct from the numerous tax-withholding disposals (code F) that appear throughout the data. New Director Christopher DiSantis also purchased 500 shares at $202.30 on March 10 for $101K.

CEO open-market purchases at this scale ($1M+) at auto retail companies are uncommon and historically have been modestly predictive of near-term outperformance. The signal carries weight because: (1) Hult has access to real-time operational data (daily unit sales, F&I metrics, service revenue), (2) the purchase occurred after Q4 earnings and the stock's decline, suggesting he sees the current price as undervaluing the business.

The tax-withholding disposals by other executives (CFO Welch, COO Clara, CHRO Milstein, GC Calloway) are routine and non-informative — these are automatic sales to cover tax obligations on vesting equity grants.

## Investment Thesis

### Bull Case
- 7.7x forward P/E near 52-week lows offers an attractive entry for a company with franchise moats, growing fixed operations mix, and ongoing LHM integration benefits.
- CEO buying $1M+ of stock with personal funds suggests operational confidence not reflected in the stock price.
- Fixed operations (parts and service) provide recession-resistant earnings support — vehicles still need maintenance regardless of new vehicle sales trends.
- If recession is avoided or shallow, the stock re-rates toward 9-10x earnings ($230-255 range).
- Industry consolidation continues to benefit scaled acquirers.

### Bear Case
- Stagflation directly impairs the core business: $100+ oil + tighter credit + weakening employment = lower vehicle sales volumes and compressed margins.
- Elevated leverage from the LHM acquisition reduces financial flexibility precisely when the cycle turns.
- Used vehicle margin normalization continues, with pandemic-era GPU (gross profit per unit) returning to 2019 levels.
- Tariff risk on imported vehicles and parts adds cost pressure that cannot be fully passed through in a weakening demand environment.
- 38-42% recession probability implies a reasonable chance of EPS declining 20-30% from current levels, which would put the stock at 10-12x trough earnings — limited downside protection at current prices.
- My calibration data shows Consumer Discretionary BUY calls average -1.54% returns. Auto retail sits squarely in this danger zone.

## Conclusion

ABG presents a mixed picture: cheap valuation, constructive insider buying, and structural improvements in business quality (fixed operations growth, franchise moat, LHM scale) offset by a severely adverse macro backdrop for the core business. The stock at 7.7x is pricing in stagnation but not a recession. If the 38-42% recession probability materializes, earnings could decline to $18-20, and the stock could trade to $140-160 (7-8x trough earnings).

The CEO's $1M purchase is the strongest positive signal in the data and prevents an AVOID recommendation. However, the stagflationary macro environment — with its direct transmission mechanisms through oil prices, credit conditions, and employment to auto retail demand — creates a cyclical ceiling on the stock that limits upside in the near term.

**Recommendation: HOLD.** The valuation is reasonable but insufficient to compensate for the cyclical headwinds. Accumulate below $180 if recession fears peak and the stock overshoots to the downside. The CEO's buying provides a floor of confidence; the macro provides a ceiling on returns.
