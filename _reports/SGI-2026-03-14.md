## Executive Summary

Somnigroup International (formerly Tempur Sealy International) is a global bedding company trading at $76.94 with a market cap of ~$16.2B. The company reported Q4 2025 revenue of $1.87B, up 54.7% YoY, driven primarily by the Mattress Firm acquisition that closed in 2024. Adjusted EPS of $0.72 met one consensus estimate but GAAP EPS of $0.661 missed expectations. The stock has declined ~22% from its 52-week high of $98.56 and underperformed the S&P 500 by ~11 percentage points over the past six months.

At 42x forward and trailing P/E, SGI is priced as a growth company in a sector facing direct headwinds from the current stagflationary macro regime. Mattresses are among the most deferrable consumer purchases. With retail sales declining 0.2%, gasoline up 21%, quit rates falling, and GDP at 0.7%, the consumer spending environment for $1,000+ discretionary items is deteriorating. The Mattress Firm acquisition adds execution risk and balance sheet leverage at precisely the wrong point in the cycle. Third Point Management holds a position per their Q4 2025 letter, but the investment case requires confidence in both acquisition integration and consumer resilience that the data does not support.

## Company Overview

Somnigroup International is the holding company created from Tempur Sealy International's acquisition of Mattress Firm, the largest U.S. specialty mattress retailer. The combined entity operates across manufacturing (Tempur-Pedic, Sealy, Stearns & Foster brands), distribution, and retail. The company is headquartered in Lexington, Kentucky. Key executives include CEO & President Scott L. Thompson (who holds ~4.1M shares), CEO of Tempur Sealy H. Clifford Buster III (~248K shares), and Mattress Firm President & CEO Steven H. Rusing (~296K shares).

The vertical integration strategy — owning both premium brand manufacturing and the largest specialty retail channel — is the core strategic thesis. The risk is that vertical integration in a cyclical, discretionary category amplifies leverage when volumes decline.

## Financial Analysis

**Revenue:** Q4 2025 revenue of $1.87B represents 54.7% YoY growth. This is almost entirely inorganic, reflecting the Mattress Firm consolidation. The prior-year quarter had revenue of ~$1.21B. Without segment detail or organic growth disclosure in the available data, it's impossible to isolate underlying demand trends, but the stock's 8% decline over six months and StockStory's characterization of "softer quarterly results" suggest organic trends are weak.

**Earnings:** The adjusted EPS of $0.72 compared to $0.60 in the year-ago quarter, representing 20% YoY growth — substantially below the 54.7% revenue growth rate. This margin compression on a revenue base inflated by acquisition is concerning. GAAP EPS of $0.661 missed the $0.72 consensus, suggesting meaningful acquisition-related charges, integration costs, or other non-recurring items. The gap between adjusted and GAAP EPS (~8%) warrants scrutiny.

**Valuation Multiples:** At $76.94, the stock trades at 42.09x both forward and trailing P/E. For a consumer discretionary company in the bedding industry, this is elevated. The trailing P/E equaling forward P/E implies the market expects no earnings growth over the next twelve months.

**Dividend:** The ~0.89% dividend yield provides negligible income support.

**Balance Sheet:** Detailed balance sheet data was unavailable in the financial statements provided. However, the Mattress Firm acquisition was a multi-billion dollar transaction that historically required significant debt financing. Tempur Sealy carried ~$4-5B in net debt pre-acquisition. The leveraged balance sheet is a material risk factor in a rising rate / slowing growth environment.

**Insider Activity:** The insider transactions are entirely routine — RSU/PRSU vesting and tax withholding dispositions at $88.74 per share (January 2, 2026), plus new performance RSU grants in February 2026. No open-market purchases or sales. The absence of open-market insider buying after a 22% decline from highs is modestly negative — management isn't signaling conviction at these levels with their own capital.

## Growth Analysis

The 54.7% revenue growth is acquisition-driven and non-repeatable. The organic growth picture is unclear but likely negative or flat based on:

1. **Macro headwinds:** Mattresses are big-ticket, deferrable purchases. The replacement cycle averages 7-10 years, and consumers extend it during downturns. With retail sales contracting, gasoline costs surging, and consumer confidence deteriorating, mattress demand faces direct pressure.

2. **Housing cycle weakness:** New mattress purchases correlate with home sales and moves. Mortgage rates at 6.25%+ suppress housing turnover, reducing the natural replacement trigger.

3. **Acquisition integration:** Mattress Firm filed for bankruptcy in 2018 and has had operational challenges historically. Integrating a ~2,400-store retail operation while demand softens creates execution risk.

4. **Oil/input costs:** Rising oil prices flow through to foam chemicals (polyurethane, polyester), transportation, and retail store operating costs. The current $98-108/bbl oil environment compresses margins for a company with both manufacturing and retail exposure.

The bull case for growth rests on synergy realization from vertical integration (higher margins on Tempur-Pedic/Sealy products sold through owned Mattress Firm stores), potential market share gains from competitors, and DTC channel expansion. These are plausible over a 3-5 year horizon but face significant near-term cyclical headwinds.

## Valuation Assessment

At 42x forward earnings, SGI is priced like a high-growth consumer brand rather than a cyclical bedding manufacturer/retailer. Comparable consumer discretionary companies in adjacent categories typically trade at 15-25x forward earnings. Even premium brand-owning consumer companies rarely sustain 40x+ multiples without double-digit organic revenue growth.

**Implied earnings at current price:** $76.94 / 42.09 = ~$1.83 EPS. With Q4 at $0.72 adjusted ($0.661 GAAP), annualized adjusted EPS would need to be ~$2.88 to justify 27x earnings (a reasonable premium multiple). The gap between current valuation and a fundamental-based multiple suggests 30-35% downside risk if the market re-rates SGI to peer-level multiples.

**Market cap to revenue:** With $1.87B in Q4 revenue, annualized revenue runs ~$7.5B. At $16.2B market cap, price-to-sales is ~2.2x. For a combined manufacturer/retailer, this is moderately elevated.

The 52-week range of $53.10 to $98.56 shows significant volatility despite a 0.74 beta. The stock currently sits at 78% of its high and 45% above its low.

## Competitive Landscape

SGI holds a strong competitive position in the U.S. mattress market. The Tempur-Pedic brand commands premium pricing and genuine consumer recognition. Sealy and Stearns & Foster provide coverage across price tiers. Mattress Firm gives the company the largest specialty retail footprint in the country.

Key competitive dynamics:

- **DTC mattress brands** (Casper, Purple, others) took market share in 2016-2021 but have faced their own profitability crises. This competitive threat has diminished.
- **Mass retailers** (Costco, Amazon, Walmart) continue to pressure mid-tier mattress pricing.
- **Sleep Number (SNBR)** competes directly in premium segments.
- The vertical integration moat is real but untested through a downturn as a combined entity.

The competitive position score of 6.5 reflects genuine brand strength and channel dominance offset by the cyclical nature of the category and price competition from mass retail.

## Risk Assessment

**Macro/Consumer Risk (HIGH):** This is the primary risk. The world model confirms all six stagflation dimensions simultaneously negative. Mattresses are among the most deferrable consumer purchases. GDP at 0.7%, retail sales -0.2%, and declining quit rates all point to reduced consumer willingness to spend $1,000-3,000 on a new mattress.

**Leverage Risk (HIGH):** The Mattress Firm acquisition significantly increased debt load. Servicing this debt becomes more challenging if EBITDA compresses during a consumer downturn.

**Integration Risk (MODERATE):** Integrating ~2,400 retail stores while managing manufacturing operations adds operational complexity. Mattress Firm's bankruptcy history raises questions about the retail asset quality.

**Input Cost Risk (MODERATE):** Petrochemical-derived foam materials, textiles, and steel are all sensitive to commodity prices. Oil at $98-108/bbl pressures margins from the manufacturing side, while higher energy costs reduce foot traffic on the retail side.

**Valuation Risk (HIGH):** At 42x earnings, any earnings disappointment or guidance reduction will produce outsized stock price declines. The market is pricing in execution on synergies that has yet to materialize in reported numbers.

**Beta at 0.74** understates risk because it reflects historical Tempur Sealy data, not the post-acquisition combined entity's actual sensitivity to consumer spending downturns.

## Investment Thesis

### Bull Case
- Vertical integration creates structural margin advantage as Tempur-Pedic/Sealy products sold through Mattress Firm stores capture both manufacturing and retail margin (~$300-500/unit incremental gross profit)
- DTC mattress competitors are weakened; SGI can gain share
- Third Point's involvement suggests activist upside potential
- Housing market eventually normalizes, releasing pent-up replacement demand
- At $76.94, down 22% from highs, some risk is priced in

### Bear Case
- 42x earnings for a cyclical consumer discretionary company entering a recession is unjustifiable — peer multiples of 15-25x imply 30-35% downside
- Consumer spending is actively contracting; mattresses are among the first purchases deferred
- Acquisition-driven revenue growth masks organic weakness — GAAP EPS miss of $0.661 vs $0.72 consensus reveals the real earnings power
- Balance sheet leverage from Mattress Firm acquisition amplifies downside risk if EBITDA contracts
- Rising input costs (petrochemicals, transportation) compress margins from both manufacturing and retail sides
- No insider open-market buying despite 22% decline signals management lacks conviction at current levels

## Conclusion

SGI is a consumer discretionary stock trading at a growth multiple heading into a stagflationary environment where consumer spending on big-ticket deferrable items is contracting. The 54.7% revenue growth is acquisition-driven and will normalize. GAAP EPS missed consensus. The balance sheet carries acquisition-related leverage that becomes problematic if volumes decline. At 42x earnings, the stock needs flawless execution on Mattress Firm synergies and stable-to-improving consumer demand — neither of which the macro data supports.

Per calibration guidance: consumer discretionary BUY calls average -1.54% historically in this system. The elevated bar for consumer discretionary names requires positive sequential revenue acceleration (not evident), FCF yield >5% (42x P/E implies ~2.4% earnings yield, and FCF yield is likely similar or lower), and non-deteriorating consumer data (actively deteriorating). SGI fails all three criteria.

**Recommendation: AVOID.** The risk-reward is unfavorable at 42x earnings for a leveraged consumer discretionary company in a confirmed stagflationary regime. Investors seeking bedding sector exposure should wait for a valuation reset to 20-25x earnings (implying ~$37-46 share price at current earnings) or clear evidence of organic revenue growth reaccelerating.
