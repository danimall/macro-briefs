## Executive Summary

Somnigroup International (formerly Tempur Sealy International) is the dominant US bedding manufacturer and retailer, with a $16.2B market cap and brands including Tempur-Pedic, Sealy, Stearns & Foster, and the Mattress Firm retail chain. Q4 2025 revenue of $1.87B represented 54.7% YoY growth, driven primarily by the Mattress Firm acquisition rather than organic demand. EPS of $0.72 met consensus estimates but missed some expectations (one source cites reported EPS of $0.661 vs. $0.72 expected, suggesting GAAP vs. adjusted divergence).

The stock trades at 42x forward earnings — a premium valuation for a consumer products company in a macro environment where every indicator points against discretionary spending. GDP is 0.7%, retail sales are declining, gasoline is up 21%, quit rates are falling, and mortgage rates above 6.25% suppress the housing turnover cycle that historically drives mattress replacement purchases. At $76.94, the stock sits 22% below its 52-week high of $98.56 and has underperformed the S&P 500 by ~11pp over the past six months. The combination of a rich multiple, cyclical consumer exposure, and confirmed stagflation makes this a clear avoid.

## Company Overview

Somnigroup International is headquartered in Lexington, Kentucky and operates as the world's largest bedding company following its acquisition of Mattress Firm, the largest US specialty mattress retailer. The company operates across three segments: manufacturing (Tempur-Pedic, Sealy, Stearns & Foster brands), retail (Mattress Firm's ~2,300+ stores), and international operations. The vertical integration — owning both manufacturing and the dominant retail channel — was the strategic rationale for the Mattress Firm deal.

Key executives include CEO Scott Thompson (holding ~4.1M shares), Tempur Sealy CEO Clifford Buster III (~248K shares), CFO Bhaskar Rao (~354K shares), and EVP David Montgomery (~858K shares). The beta of 0.74 reflects modest cyclicality relative to the broader market, and the 0.89% dividend yield provides minimal income support.

The year-ago quarter would have had revenue around $1.21B. Without detailed segment breakdowns (financial statements were empty in the data provided), organic growth cannot be isolated, but the bedding industry's mid-single-digit growth rate and macro headwinds suggest organic growth was likely flat to low-single-digits.

**Earnings:** Adjusted EPS of $0.72 for Q4 2025 compared to $0.60 in the year-ago quarter — 20% YoY growth. One news source reports GAAP EPS of $0.661, implying ~$0.06 per share in adjustments (likely Mattress Firm integration costs, amortization of acquired intangibles, or restructuring charges). The gap between GAAP and adjusted is worth monitoring as integration proceeds.

**Valuation Math:** At $76.94 and 42x forward P/E, implied forward EPS is ~$1.83. Against the $0.72 Q4 run rate ($2.88 annualized), either forward estimates embed significant margin compression or the 42x figure reflects a blended trailing/forward calculation. Either way, the market is pricing SGI at a growth premium that its end-market dynamics cannot support.

**Balance Sheet:** No detailed balance sheet data was available. However, Tempur Sealy historically carried significant leverage (~4-5x net debt/EBITDA), and the Mattress Firm acquisition added substantial debt. Post-acquisition net debt likely exceeds $7-8B against a $16.2B equity market cap, implying an enterprise value of $24-25B. At roughly 3-3.5x EV/Revenue, this is modestly above consumer products peers.

**Insider Activity:** All insider transactions in the data are routine — RSU/PRSU vesting, tax withholding dispositions at $88.74 (above current price), and annual equity grants. ## Growth Analysis

**Mattress Firm Integration:** The acquisition creates the largest vertically integrated bedding company in the US. Theoretical synergies include: (1) eliminating wholesale margin on Tempur/Sealy products sold through Mattress Firm stores, (2) using retail data to optimize manufacturing, and (3) cost reduction through operational overlap. Management has guided to synergies but execution is unproven, and Mattress Firm's history (it went through Chapter 11 in 2018) raises questions about the retail asset's durability.

**Organic Growth Headwinds:** Mattress purchases correlate strongly with three variables: (1) housing turnover (new home = new mattress), (2) consumer confidence / discretionary spending capacity, and (3) replacement cycle timing (~7-10 years). Currently: mortgage rates above 6.25% suppress existing home sales (running ~30% below 2019 levels), consumer spending is contracting (retail -0.2%), and gasoline at +21% YoY directly reduces discretionary budgets for middle-income households who are the core mattress-buying demographic.

**International:** International operations provide some diversification, but the majority of revenue is US-centric, and the strong dollar (driven by flight-to-quality amid geopolitical tensions) compresses international earnings on translation.

**Third Point Involvement:** The hedge fund's Q4 2025 letter discussed SGI, suggesting activist or active interest. However, the letter's specific thesis was not available in the data. Third Point's involvement could create positive catalysts (operational improvements, capital return) but also suggests current management execution may be insufficiently aggressive for value realization.

## Valuation Assessment

At 42x forward earnings, SGI trades at a significant premium to consumer products peers. Comparable companies:

- **Sleep Number (SNBR):** Historically traded at 15-20x forward earnings
- **Leggett & Platt (LEG):** ~10-12x forward (components supplier)
- **Consumer Staples broadly:** 18-22x forward

SGI's 42x multiple would be defensible for a company growing earnings at 20%+ annually with secular tailwinds. The bedding industry grows at GDP-plus-1-2% in normal conditions and contracts during recessions. With GDP at 0.7% and recession probability at 38-42%, paying 42x for a cyclical consumer company with significant integration risk is aggressive.

**Dividend yield of 0.89% provides negligible total return support.** At the current payout, shareholders are paying almost entirely for price appreciation that requires sustained earnings growth at a level the macro environment is unlikely to deliver.

**Price action confirms market skepticism:** stock down 8% over six months while the S&P gained 3.1%, a -11.1pp relative performance gap. The stock has traded from $98.56 (52-week high) to $76.94, a 22% decline.

## Competitive Landscape

SGI holds a strong competitive position in the US bedding market. The Tempur-Pedic brand commands premium pricing and genuine consumer loyalty (memory foam innovation heritage). The Sealy brand covers the mid-tier. Mattress Firm provides owned retail distribution that competitors lack.

Competitive threats include: (1) DTC mattress brands (Casper, Purple, Saatva) that have taken share from traditional retail, though Purple (PRPL) has struggled operationally; (2) Amazon/Walmart distribution of commodity mattresses that pressures the mid-tier Sealy brand; (3) Costco and other warehouse clubs selling premium mattresses at lower margins.

The vertical integration with Mattress Firm creates a structural advantage: SGI can prioritize its own brands on the retail floor, manage inventory directly, and capture retail margins. However, this also concentrates risk — if physical retail traffic declines (as it does during consumer downturns), SGI bears the fixed costs of 2,300+ stores.

## Risk Assessment

**Macro Sensitivity (HIGH):** SGI is a textbook consumer discretionary stock entering the worst macro environment for its category. Housing turnover suppression (mortgage rates 6.25%+), declining retail sales (-0.2%), rising gasoline (+21%), and declining quit rates all point to reduced willingness and ability to purchase $1,000-3,000 mattresses. Mattress purchases are among the most deferrable consumer spending categories.

**Leverage Risk (HIGH):** Post-acquisition leverage is likely 4-5x net debt/EBITDA. In a recession scenario with declining EBITDA, leverage ratios could approach covenant thresholds. Rising credit spreads (credit cascade probability 55-60% per world model) increase refinancing costs for highly leveraged consumer companies.

**Integration Risk (MODERATE):** Mattress Firm integration is ongoing. Historical precedent (Mattress Firm's 2018 bankruptcy) suggests the retail asset requires careful management. Synergy capture typically takes 2-3 years and often disappoints.

**Input Cost Inflation:** Oil at $98-108 increases polyurethane foam costs (petroleum-derived), transportation costs for delivery logistics, and retail operating costs (store utilities, delivery fleet fuel). These costs are difficult to pass through fully when consumers are already under pressure.

**Tariff Risk:** Section 301 probes across 60 economies could increase costs for imported mattress components and raw materials. While SGI manufactures primarily in the US, supply chain inputs have global exposure.

## Investment Thesis

- Housing market recovery on potential rate cuts improves category fundamentals
- Third Point involvement drives shareholder-friendly capital allocation
- Dominant market position allows pricing power above inflation
- Implied forward EPS of ~$1.83 is overly conservative; actual earnings closer to $3.00+ would make 42x look more like 26x on normalized earnings

### Bear Case
- 42x forward earnings for a cyclical consumer company entering recession (38-42% probability) is structurally overvalued
- Housing turnover remains depressed through 2027 as mortgage rates stay elevated
- Mattress Firm retail footprint becomes a liability in consumer downturn (high fixed costs, lease obligations)
- Post-acquisition leverage of 4-5x constrains buybacks and dividend growth while exposing the company to refinancing risk in a widening credit spread environment
- Input cost inflation (petroleum-based foam, fuel for delivery) compresses margins
- Consumer trade-down from premium Tempur-Pedic to cheaper alternatives reduces mix and margin

## Conclusion

SGI is an AVOID at current levels. The core problem is straightforward: 42x forward earnings prices in strong execution and favorable macro conditions, while the actual environment delivers the opposite on both dimensions. The confirmed stagflationary macro regime (GDP 0.7%, retail -0.2%, gasoline +21%, recession probability 38-42%) is specifically hostile to high-ticket discretionary consumer purchases like mattresses. Housing turnover — the primary category demand driver — is structurally suppressed by 6.25%+ mortgage rates with no clear catalyst for relief.

The Mattress Firm acquisition adds execution complexity and balance sheet risk during a period when leverage is a headwind. The stock's -11pp underperformance vs. the S&P 500 over six months reflects the market pricing in these risks, but at 42x forward the multiple still embeds more optimism than the fundamentals warrant. A re-rating to 20-25x forward earnings (closer to consumer products peers) on potentially declining forward estimates could mean 40-50% downside. The 0.89% dividend yield provides no meaningful floor.

Per calibration guidelines on consumer discretionary: same-store sales acceleration is unconfirmed, FCF yield at 42x is well below 5%, and consumer confidence/employment data are actively deteriorating. All three criteria for a consumer discretionary BUY fail. This stock warrants avoidance until either: (1) valuation compresses to 15-20x forward earnings, or (2) housing turnover shows concrete improvement.
