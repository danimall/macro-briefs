## Executive Summary

Casey's General Stores reported Q3 FY2026 (ended January 31, 2026) results that split cleanly: EPS of $3.49 beat consensus by $0.52 (~50% YoY growth from $2.33), but revenue missed estimates by approximately $120M while remaining roughly flat year-over-year. The company raised its FY26 outlook, and same-store sales showed positive momentum that prompted BMO Capital to raise its price target from $540 to $700. The stock trades at $661, near its 52-week high of $697.

The core tension is valuation. At 38x trailing earnings and 45x forward P/E, CASY is priced like a high-growth compounder, yet revenue growth has been modest on an organic basis. The 14.17% revenue growth rate (as of October 2025) was partially driven by acquisitions rather than purely organic expansion. With a market cap of $24.4B, a beta of 0.67, and a 0.34% dividend yield, CASY functions as a defensive compounder with a premium multiple attached — appropriate for a specific risk environment (stagflation with consumer trade-down), but offering limited margin of safety if execution falters.

## Company Overview

Casey's General Stores operates one of the largest convenience store chains in the United States, concentrated in the Midwest. The company's differentiated model combines traditional c-store operations (fuel, tobacco, beverages) with a significant prepared foods business, particularly pizza — a category with higher margins than fuel or packaged goods. The company has been executing an acquisition-driven growth strategy, expanding its store footprint while investing in digital capabilities (loyalty program, mobile ordering).

Casey's fiscal year ends April 30, placing it on a different reporting calendar than most retailers. The company's most recent quarter (Q3 FY2026, ending January 31, 2026) represents its seasonally weaker winter period.

## Financial Analysis

**Earnings Quality:** Q3 FY2026 diluted EPS of $3.49 vs. $2.33 in the prior-year period represents 49.8% growth. The $0.52 beat relative to consensus suggests either analysts were too conservative on inside margins or cost discipline exceeded expectations. Revenue missing by $120M while earnings beat significantly implies margin expansion drove the result — likely a combination of lower fuel costs flowing through to gross margin and stronger inside (prepared food/grocery) margin performance.

**Revenue Dynamics:** Total revenue was roughly flat YoY in Q3 despite the 14.17% revenue growth rate reported for the nine months ending October 2025. This suggests either (a) Q3 fuel revenue declined (lower retail fuel prices compress top-line even as fuel margin per gallon remains stable or expands), or (b) acquisition-driven growth from earlier quarters wasn't replicated in Q3. For a convenience store operator, fuel price fluctuations create revenue volatility that doesn't map to economic value — fuel margin cents per gallon is the relevant metric, and evidence suggests this was healthy.

**Same-Store Sales:** Multiple news sources confirm a "same-store sales jump" in Q3, which is the most important organic growth metric for the business. This drove the stock's initial positive reaction and BMO's price target increase. Inside same-store sales (non-fuel, covering prepared food, grocery, and general merchandise) rising YoY confirms the consumer trade-down thesis: Casey's prepared food offering benefits when consumers shift spending away from restaurants toward lower-cost alternatives.

**Guidance:** The company raised its FY26 outlook following Q3 results, signaling management confidence in Q4 (February–April) performance. This is notable because Q4 includes the spring driving season, which tends to benefit fuel volumes.

**Valuation Metrics:**
- Market cap: $24.4B
- Trailing P/E: 38.0x
- Forward P/E: 45.2x
- Dividend yield: 0.34%
- Beta: 0.67
- 52-week range: $381–$697
- Current price: $661 (5.1% below 52-week high)

The forward P/E of 45.2x being *higher* than trailing P/E of 38.0x is worth noting. This implies either (a) earnings are expected to decline next fiscal year (FY2027), or (b) the forward estimate uses a period that includes seasonally weaker quarters. Given that the company just raised guidance, interpretation (b) is more likely — the forward estimate probably reflects the next twelve months from the most recent data point, which includes the already-reported weaker quarters.

**Balance Sheet:** Detailed financial statements were unavailable in the data feed. Casey's historically carries moderate leverage to fund acquisitions and store construction. The company's SEC filings show regular 10-K and 10-Q reporting cadence, with the most recent 10-Q filed March 9, 2026.

## Growth Analysis

Casey's growth strategy operates on three vectors:

1. **Acquisition-driven store count expansion.** The 14.17% revenue growth rate through October 2025 reflects significant inorganic contribution. Casey's has been actively acquiring smaller c-store chains in the Midwest and adjacent regions.

2. **Inside sales growth (prepared food, grocery).** Same-store inside sales growth is the highest-quality growth metric for the business. The Q3 "same-store sales jump" confirms this engine is functioning. Prepared food (especially pizza) carries gross margins of 50-60%, compared to fuel margins of ~5-8% and grocery margins of ~30%.

3. **Digital and loyalty penetration.** Casey's Rewards program drives higher-frequency visits and larger basket sizes. Digital ordering (mobile app) for prepared food reduces friction and increases capture of the trade-down consumer.

In the current macro environment (gasoline +21% in one month per the world model, consumer trade-down accelerating), Casey's occupies an interesting position: higher fuel prices increase fuel revenue but compress volume, while the trade-down effect from restaurants to prepared food benefits inside margins. The net effect for Casey's is likely modestly positive — the margin-rich inside business grows while fuel becomes more of a traffic driver than a profit center.

The risk to growth is concentration in the Midwest, which limits the total addressable market relative to national chains. Store count expansion through acquisition also carries integration risk and requires sustained capital deployment.

## Valuation Assessment

This is where the thesis encounters friction. CASY at 38x trailing and 45x forward earnings is expensive by any convenience store comp set. Alimentation Couche-Tard (ATD, parent of Circle K) trades at roughly 18-20x earnings. Murphy USA (MUSA) trades at approximately 18-22x. Even Costco (COST), the premium defensive retailer CASY is compared to in the Seeking Alpha article, trades at ~50x — and Costco has a substantially larger moat, membership model, and international growth runway.

The premium Casey's commands reflects: (1) its low beta (0.67) in a high-volatility macro environment, (2) the prepared food differentiation that delivers higher margins than pure-play c-stores, (3) consistent execution on acquisitions, and (4) the perception of a long runway for Midwest consolidation.

BMO's revised price target of $700 (from $540) with a "Market Perform" rating is instructive — BMO is saying the stock is fairly valued at $700, not that it's a buy. At $661, that implies ~6% upside to BMO's target. The prior target of $540 (now 18% below current price) suggests the stock has run significantly ahead of where sell-side analysts expected it to be.

At 38x trailing earnings, CASY needs to sustain ~15-20% earnings growth to justify the multiple, or the multiple needs to compress. Q3's 50% EPS growth is impressive but partially driven by favorable fuel margin comparisons and acquisition contributions — both of which are inherently lumpy.

## Competitive Landscape

Casey's operates in a fragmented market. Approximately 60% of U.S. convenience stores are single-store operators, giving well-capitalized chains like Casey's a structural advantage in acquisitions, procurement, and technology investment. Key competitors:

- **Alimentation Couche-Tard / Circle K:** Global scale, larger footprint, but less prepared-food differentiation.
- **Murphy USA:** Walmart-adjacent fuel and c-store model, lower prepared food emphasis.
- **Pilot/Flying J (Berkshire Hathaway):** Focused on travel centers/trucking, different customer base.
- **7-Eleven / Speedway (Seven & i):** National footprint but currently undergoing ownership transition.

Casey's moat is regional density in the Midwest combined with prepared food capabilities. The pizza business creates a genuine competitive advantage — it's difficult for smaller operators to replicate, and it drives higher inside margins than typical c-store merchandise. This regional density also creates procurement and distribution efficiencies.

The competitive position scores well because fragmentation creates a long acquisition runway, and the prepared food differentiation is difficult to replicate at scale. The limitation is geographic concentration — expansion outside the Midwest reduces the density advantage.

## Risk Assessment

**Fuel price volatility:** Oil at ~$100/bbl per the world model creates a complex dynamic. Higher retail fuel prices compress gallon volumes (demand elasticity) but expand margin per gallon in the short term as wholesale costs are passed through with a lag. Net effect is modestly negative for revenue, neutral to positive for fuel gross profit in the near term.

**Consumer spending pressure:** Gasoline acting as a regressive consumption tax ($3.50+ per gallon) reduces disposable income for Casey's core Midwestern consumer demographic. The trade-down from restaurants to prepared food partially offsets this, but if the consumer weakens further (recession probability 35-40%), even c-store inside sales could decelerate.

**Acquisition integration risk:** Growth depends on continued successful integration of acquired store chains. Overpaying for acquisitions or encountering operational difficulties in integration would compress returns on invested capital.

**Valuation risk:** At 38-45x earnings, any execution stumble (a quarter of negative same-store sales, an acquisition misstep) could trigger significant multiple compression. The stock's 73% move from its 52-week low of $381 to $661 has priced in substantial optimism.

**Labor costs:** Convenience store operations are labor-intensive. Wage inflation in the Midwest, while lower than coastal markets, still pressures operating margins.

**Low beta as partial hedge:** The 0.67 beta provides genuine portfolio diversification in the current high-volatility environment. This is a real source of value for investors seeking defensive positioning.

## Investment Thesis

### Bull Case
- Same-store inside sales acceleration confirms the trade-down thesis, and the prepared food business gains share from restaurant channels as consumer budgets compress
- Acquisition pipeline in a fragmented market provides a multi-year growth runway at attractive returns on capital
- Raised FY26 guidance signals confidence in near-term execution
- Low beta (0.67) and defensive positioning make CASY a relative safe haven in a stagflationary macro regime
- Digital/loyalty investments drive higher frequency and larger basket, expanding inside margins over time
- Upside to $700+ if Q4 execution confirms the Q3 trends and management continues delivering earnings beats

### Bear Case
- 38-45x P/E leaves no margin of safety; any execution miss will compress the multiple sharply
- Revenue missed by $120M in Q3, suggesting top-line growth is harder to achieve than bottom-line beats
- Acquisition-driven growth is inherently lumpy and carries integration risk; the premium multiple assumes flawless execution
- In a deeper recession (35-40% probability), even trade-down spending at c-stores contracts
- Geographic concentration in the Midwest limits the total addressable opportunity
- Rising fuel prices ($100+ oil) compress gallon volumes and may eventually pressure fuel margins if wholesale costs spike faster than retail prices adjust
- BMO's "Market Perform" at $700 target implies limited upside from current levels; the stock has already priced in the positive Q3 results

## Conclusion

Casey's General Stores is a well-run defensive retailer with genuine competitive differentiation through its prepared food business and a long acquisition runway in fragmented Midwestern c-store markets. Q3 FY2026 results confirmed strong earnings growth and positive same-store sales momentum, and the raised FY26 outlook supports near-term confidence.

The constraint is valuation. At 38x trailing earnings, CASY is priced at roughly 2x the multiple of comparable c-store operators. The premium is partially justified by higher margins, lower cyclicality, and consistent execution, but it requires sustained 15-20% earnings growth to avoid multiple compression. The stock sits 5% below its 52-week high after a 73% run from its low, and BMO's $700 target (highest available data point) offers only 6% upside.

**Recommendation: HOLD.** The quality of the business merits ownership, but the entry point at $661 offers insufficient margin of safety given macro uncertainty and a premium valuation. A pullback toward $550-580 (approximately 28-30x trailing earnings) would create a more attractive risk/reward profile. Existing holders should maintain positions given the defensive characteristics (0.67 beta, prepared food trade-down benefit), but new capital should wait for better pricing.
