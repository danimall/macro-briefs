## Executive Summary

Casey's General Stores reported Q3 FY2026 (quarter ended January 31, 2026) results with diluted EPS of $3.49, up approximately 50% from $2.33 in the year-ago quarter and beating consensus by $0.52. Revenue was roughly flat year-over-year and missed estimates by ~$120 million, likely reflecting lower fuel prices flowing through top-line revenue without proportional margin impact. The company raised its FY26 outlook, and same-store sales showed meaningful acceleration. BMO Capital raised its price target to $700 from $540 while maintaining a Market Perform rating.

At $660.87 per share and a $24.4 billion market cap, CASY trades at 38x trailing earnings and 45x forward P/E. This is a premium valuation for a convenience store operator — peer group multiples typically range from 15-25x. The premium reflects CASY's consistent execution, prepared food differentiation (pizza program), acquisition-driven growth strategy, and low beta (0.67). The stock has risen 73% from its 52-week low of $381, already discounting the earnings improvement trajectory.

The core tension: Casey's is a well-run, defensively positioned business operating in a segment with structural tailwinds (rural convenience consolidation). But the current valuation embeds expectations that leave no room for margin compression from $100+ oil (which simultaneously pressures fuel margins and consumer wallets), Section 301 tariff uncertainty on food inputs, or broader stagflation-driven consumer trade-down behavior. At these multiples, the risk-reward is asymmetric to the downside.

## Company Overview

Casey's General Stores operates one of the largest convenience store chains in the United States, concentrated in the Midwest and South-Central regions. The company differentiates from typical c-store operators through its substantial prepared food business, anchored by its pizza program — Casey's is one of the largest pizza chains in the US by store count. Revenue streams include fuel sales (high volume, low margin), inside sales (grocery, prepared food, beverages — higher margin), and other income. The company has grown aggressively through both organic store openings and acquisitions, including the transformative Fikes/CEFCO acquisition.

The business model benefits from rural market positioning where competition is limited, high switching costs for habitual shoppers, and a loyalty program (Casey's Rewards) that drives repeat traffic. The prepared food segment generates gross margins materially above the c-store industry average.

## Growth Analysis

**Organic Growth:** Same-store sales acceleration in Q3 is the most important near-term signal. Inside sales (higher-margin prepared food, grocery, beverages) growing on a same-store basis indicates the loyalty program, menu innovation, and store remodel investments are generating returns. The company's pizza program, private label expansion, and digital ordering capabilities provide vectors for continued inside sales growth.

**Acquisition Growth:** Casey's has a long track record of acquiring smaller c-store chains in its Midwestern footprint at attractive multiples. The CEFCO acquisition expanded the geographic reach. The fragmented nature of the US convenience store market (~150,000 stores, many independently owned) provides a long runway for consolidation. However, each incremental acquisition faces diminishing returns as the most attractive targets get absorbed.

**Store Count:** Casey's continues to grow its store base through both new builds and acquisitions. Each new store in a rural/suburban market with limited competition can generate attractive unit economics within 2-3 years.

**Growth Risks:** Revenue was flat YoY despite store count growth, meaning same-store fuel gallons or fuel revenue per gallon likely declined (consistent with lower fuel prices). In a $100+ oil environment, fuel retail prices rise, boosting top-line revenue but potentially compressing CPG margins as operators absorb some of the price increase to remain competitive. Simultaneously, higher gasoline prices reduce discretionary spending on inside-store purchases — the higher-margin revenue stream.

## Valuation Assessment

CASY at 38x trailing and 45x forward P/E is expensive by any reasonable c-store benchmark:
- **Alimentation Couche-Tard (ATD):** Typically 18-22x
- **Murphy USA (MUSA):** Typically 15-20x
- **Pilot/other private comps:** 10-14x EBITDA

Casey's premium is partially justified by its higher inside sales mix (better margin profile than fuel-heavy peers), consistent execution, and low beta defensive characteristics. But 38x is closer to a consumer staples premium or a technology growth multiple than a convenience retail multiple.

**BMO's $700 target** (raised from $540) implies ~6% upside from current levels, which is modest — and BMO maintained Market Perform, suggesting the target primarily reflects the earnings beat rather than conviction in further upside.

**Implied growth rate:** At 38x trailing P/E and a ~0.34% dividend yield, the market is pricing in sustained double-digit earnings growth for multiple years. Given that Q3 EPS grew ~50% YoY (partly aided by acquisition contribution and favorable fuel margins), the question is whether mid-to-high teens growth is sustainable. In a benign macro environment, 10-15% annual EPS growth from same-store sales leverage + acquisitions + share buybacks is plausible. In the current stagflationary environment with $100+ oil, achieving this becomes materially harder.

**DCF sanity check:** $24.4B market cap on ~$17.37 trailing EPS and ~37M diluted shares implies the market expects significant FCF growth over the next 5+ years. Without detailed cash flow data, I estimate FCF at roughly 60-70% of net income (capital-intensive business with store buildouts), or ~$240-280M annually. At a 3% terminal growth rate and 8% discount rate, the current market cap implies FCF growing to ~$600-700M within 5 years — ambitious but not impossible if the acquisition pipeline remains active.

## Competitive Landscape

Casey's occupies a defensible niche: the dominant c-store operator in small-to-mid-size Midwestern communities where the nearest competitor may be miles away. This geographic moat provides pricing power on both fuel and inside sales.

**Key competitive advantages:**
1. **Prepared food differentiation:** The pizza program generates foot traffic and margins unavailable to typical c-stores
2. **Rural market dominance:** Limited competition in many store locations
3. **Scale advantages in procurement:** Growing store base improves purchasing leverage with suppliers
4. **Loyalty program:** Casey's Rewards drives repeat visits and provides customer data for targeted promotions

**Competitive risks:**
- Dollar General and Dollar Tree expanding in rural markets compete for some of the same basket purchases
- Couche-Tard's acquisition of 7-Eleven (if completed) would create a substantially larger competitor, though overlap with Casey's rural footprint is limited
- EV adoption, while slow in rural markets, represents a long-term structural headwind to fuel traffic

## Risk Assessment

**Macro Sensitivity (Elevated):**
- Oil at $100+ directly impacts fuel margins (CPG compression) and indirectly impacts inside sales (consumer spending reduction). Casey's customers in rural Midwest are more fuel-price-sensitive than urban consumers — they drive more miles daily.
- Stagflation (GDP 0.7%, core PCE 3.1%, NFP -92K) pressures the consumer spending that drives inside sales growth. Casey's has some trade-down resilience (prepared food substitutes for more expensive restaurant meals), but overall consumer weakness is a headwind.
- Section 301 tariff probes affecting 60 economies could increase food input costs, pressuring prepared food margins.

**Valuation Risk (High):**
- At 38x trailing P/E, any earnings disappointment or guidance reduction would likely trigger a sharp de-rating. The stock's 73% rally from its 52-week low has front-loaded returns.
- The forward P/E of 45x is particularly concerning — it implies the street expects near-term earnings to decline from the trailing run rate, which could reflect seasonality or conservatism, but either way represents expensive near-term pricing.

**Acquisition Integration Risk (Moderate):**
- CEFCO integration is ongoing. Execution risk exists in achieving projected synergies and maintaining culture across a geographically dispersed store network.
- Debt taken on for acquisitions increases financial leverage in a rising-rate environment.

**Insider Activity (Neutral to Mildly Positive):**

- CLO Katrina Lindsey sold 200 shares at $683.04 post-earnings — small and likely routine.
- CHR Officer sold 3,487 shares at $555.40 in September 2025 — larger but predates the rally.
- Multiple gift transactions from CEO and other officers in December 2025 — tax planning, not sentiment signals.

The insider picture is benign. No pattern of aggressive selling at elevated prices.

## Investment Thesis

**Bull Case:**
- Same-store sales acceleration and raised FY26 guidance indicate the business is executing well, with the prepared food program and loyalty platform driving sustainable inside sales growth above the c-store industry average. The fragmented US convenience market offers 5-10+ years of acquisition runway. Low beta (0.67) provides portfolio protection in a volatile macro environment. If CASY can sustain 12-15% annual EPS growth through a combination of SSS growth, acquisitions, operating leverage, and buybacks, the current multiple may prove reasonable on a 2-3 year view. Trade-down from restaurants to prepared food at Casey's could actually accelerate in a recession.

**Bear Case:**
- At 38x trailing P/E, the stock prices in near-perfect execution for years. Oil at $100+ simultaneously compresses fuel margins and reduces consumer discretionary spending in Casey's rural Midwest markets. Revenue already missed by $120M in Q3 despite the earnings beat — top-line growth is harder to achieve than bottom-line cost management. If stagflation deepens (recession probability 38-42%), a reversion to even 25x earnings (still above peers) implies ~35% downside to $435. The forward P/E of 45x suggests consensus sees near-term earnings headwinds. Acquisition-driven growth requires continued access to attractively priced targets, and private market multiples for c-stores have risen.

## Conclusion

Casey's is a high-quality convenience store operator with genuine competitive advantages in rural market positioning and prepared food differentiation. Q3 results were strong on earnings and same-store sales. The raised FY26 outlook provides near-term confidence.

The problem is price. At 38x trailing earnings and 45x forward earnings, CASY trades at roughly 2x the peer group multiple. The 0.67 beta and defensive characteristics justify some premium, but the magnitude of that premium assumes sustained double-digit earnings growth in a macro environment where oil at $100+, contracting consumer spending, and potential tariff-driven food cost inflation create meaningful headwinds for a Midwestern c-store chain whose customers drive more miles per day than the national average.

BMO's $700 target — the highest visible in the data — implies only 6% upside and comes with a Market Perform rating, consistent with the view that the earnings beat is priced in. The stock has already rallied 73% from its 52-week low.

**Recommendation: HOLD.** The business quality is genuine, but the valuation offers no margin of safety. Initiating a position here requires conviction that CASY deserves a permanent re-rating to luxury-goods-like multiples, and the current macro backdrop argues against that. Existing holders should maintain positions given the defensive beta characteristics and operational momentum, but adding exposure at these levels is difficult to justify on risk-reward grounds. A pullback to 25-30x trailing earnings ($435-520 range) would create a more attractive entry point.
