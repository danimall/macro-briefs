## Executive Summary

First Horizon is a Memphis-based regional bank holding company with approximately $10.5B in market capitalization, trading at $21.74 as of March 14, 2026. The stock sits ~18% below its 52-week high of $26.56 and ~43% above its 52-week low of $15.19. FHN reported 7% year-over-year revenue growth to $3.42B in 2025, driven by loan expansion and rising net interest income. The dividend yield of 6.3% is among the highest in the regional banking sector.

The core tension: FHN's valuation at ~10.7x forward earnings and substantial yield make it look cheap on a standalone basis. But the macro environment has deteriorated materially. GDP printed 0.7% in Q4, NFP was -92K, oil is above $100/bbl, and recession probability has risen to 38-42% for Q1-Q2 2026. Regional banks with concentrated Southeast/commercial real estate exposure face direct headwinds from credit deterioration in a stagflationary regime. The 0.65 beta understates the true downside risk because bank earnings are levered to credit quality, which hasn't yet fully reflected the macro deterioration.

Without detailed balance sheet and income statement data in the provided financials, this analysis relies on profile metrics, news flow, insider activity, SEC filing timelines, and macro context. The recommendation is HOLD — the valuation provides some cushion, but the risk-reward is inadequate for a new BUY given the credit cycle trajectory.

## Company Overview

First Horizon Corporation operates as a financial holding company through three primary segments. The bank provides checking accounts, savings products, mortgage banking, lending, and financing to individuals and businesses across the Southeastern United States. FHN has a significant wealth management division, evidenced by the recent hiring of Eric Teal as Chief Investment Officer for First Horizon Wealth Management in February 2026.

The company filed its 10-K on February 26, 2026, covering fiscal year 2025. Based on news sources, FHN reported net revenues of $3.42B in 2025, representing 7% year-over-year growth. Management has been actively presenting at investor conferences (Raymond James in March 2026, RBC Capital Markets in March 2026, Bank of America Financial Services in January 2026), which suggests confidence in the story they're telling but also recognition that the stock needs marketing after pulling back from highs.

## Growth Analysis

FHN's 7% revenue growth in 2025 was driven by "solid loan expansion" and "rising net interest income" per the Zacks/sharewise article from March 6, 2026. The question from that headline — "Can First Horizon Sustain Its Revenue Growth After a Strong 2025?" — is the right one.

**Arguments for sustainability:**
- Southeast US has been a beneficiary of population migration trends (Tennessee, Florida, Carolinas)
- Wealth management division expansion (new CIO hire) adds fee income diversification
- If the Fed cuts rates, funding costs decline, potentially supporting NIM

**Arguments against sustainability:**
- Oil above $100 and rising input costs squeeze borrowers' cash flows, slowing loan demand
- GDP at 0.7% and NFP at -92K suggest the loan growth tailwind is fading
- Credit losses typically lag employment deterioration by 2-3 quarters; the worst provision expense increases may be ahead
- The flat forward-to-trailing P/E ratio tells you the market already prices zero growth
- Section 301 tariff uncertainty across 60 economies adds complexity for commercial borrowers

The near-identical trailing and forward P/E (10.70 vs. 10.69) is the market's verdict: 2025 was peak-cycle revenue growth for FHN, and 2026 will be flat at best. Given the macro backdrop, this seems realistic.

## Valuation Assessment

**Absolute valuation:** 10.7x earnings is cheap in historical context for a bank with FHN's profile. During the 2017-2019 period, regional banks traded at 12-15x. At the 2023 banking crisis trough, they traded at 7-9x.

**Relative valuation:** Without peer-specific data, FHN's 10.7x sits in the lower half of the regional bank range. Large regionals like USB, PNC, and TFC have typically traded at 11-14x in normalized environments. FHN's discount reflects its smaller scale, more concentrated footprint, and the overhang from the failed TD Bank acquisition in 2023.

**Dividend yield context:** 6.3% is attractive. The S&P 500 yields approximately 1.5%. FHN's yield is roughly 4x the market, reflecting both genuine income and embedded skepticism about dividend sustainability. If the dividend is maintained, the yield provides meaningful total return support. If the payout ratio expands above 80% due to rising provisions, a cut becomes plausible.

**Price-to-implied-book:** At $10.5B market cap for a bank generating roughly $1B in net income (estimated from ~$2.03 EPS × ~500M shares), the implied ROE suggests a price-to-book near 1.0-1.2x, which is fair value for a regional bank in a downturn.

## Competitive Landscape

First Horizon operates in the Southeast, competing with Regions Financial (RF), Truist (TFC), Synovus (SNV), and numerous community banks. The Southeast footprint is structurally favorable due to population growth, lower cost of living, and business-friendly regulatory environments. Tennessee has no state income tax, which supports deposit gathering.

However, FHN lacks the scale advantages of larger regionals. The failed 2023 TD Bank acquisition deprived FHN of a transformative scale event, and the $4.9B breakup fee it received has been largely deployed. The bank must compete on organic growth and relationship banking, which works in good times but provides limited differentiation in a downturn.

The wealth management build-out (hiring a CIO) and new CISO appointment suggest management is investing in fee income diversification and cybersecurity infrastructure. These are sensible strategic moves but won't materially change the earnings trajectory over the next 4-6 quarters.

## Risk Assessment

**Credit cycle risk (HIGH):** This is the dominant risk. GDP at 0.7%, NFP at -92K, retail sales at -0.2%, and oil above $100 create a multi-vector squeeze on borrowers. Regional banks with CRE (commercial real estate) and C&I (commercial and industrial) loan concentrations face the most direct exposure. Credit losses typically begin rising 2-3 quarters after employment deterioration, placing the peak provision expense risk in Q2-Q4 2026.

**Interest rate risk (MODERATE):** The FOMC faces an impossible decision at its March 18-19 meeting. If rates stay higher for longer (my base case for 2026), FHN's funding costs remain elevated. If the Fed cuts, NIM compression risk increases if asset yields reprice faster than liabilities. FHN is rate-sensitive in both directions given the macro setup.

**Macro stagflation risk (HIGH):** All six stagflation dimensions are confirmed simultaneously. This is the worst possible macro environment for a leveraged financial institution. Loan demand weakens (growth slows), credit quality deteriorates (unemployment rises), and funding costs remain elevated (inflation persists). The 0.65 beta is misleading — beta measures equity price volatility, not fundamental earnings sensitivity. Banks are operationally levered to the credit cycle, and the credit cycle is turning.

**Regulatory risk (LOW-MODERATE):** FHN is below the $100B threshold that triggers enhanced prudential standards. This provides some regulatory breathing room relative to larger peers.

**Concentration risk (MODERATE):** Southeast geographic concentration. If the regional economy weakens disproportionately (e.g., auto manufacturing exposure in Tennessee amid potential tariffs), FHN faces concentrated losses.

## Investment Thesis

### Bull Case
- **10.7x P/E with 6.3% yield provides margin of safety.** If EPS holds at ~$2.03 and the dividend is maintained, the stock yields over 6% while trading at a discount to historical averages. In a scenario where the economy avoids recession and the Fed cuts 2-3 times, FHN could re-rate to 12-13x, implying $24-26 (10-20% upside plus dividend).
- **Southeast secular growth.** Population migration to Tennessee, the Carolinas, and Florida supports long-term deposit and loan growth.
- **Revenue momentum.** 7% top-line growth in 2025 was above-trend for regional banks. Even if it slows to 3-4%, that's still positive.
- **Low beta provides relative protection.** In a broad equity selloff, FHN's 0.65 beta should limit drawdown relative to the market.

### Bear Case
- **Credit cycle deterioration is the dominant risk.** Rising provisions could reduce EPS by 15-25% from current levels, pushing the effective P/E to 13-14x and making the stock look less cheap. Dividend sustainability comes into question above an 80% payout ratio.
- **Stagflation is structurally hostile to banks.** Higher funding costs + weaker loan demand + rising defaults = margin compression from all directions. The 2025 revenue growth was a cyclical peak.
- **No open-market insider buying.** Management is not putting personal capital to work at these levels. All recent insider transactions were mechanistic (grants and tax withholdings).
- **Failed TD acquisition left FHN subscale.** Without a transformative deal, FHN competes at a structural cost disadvantage against larger regionals with better technology platforms and broader product suites.
- **Recession probability at 38-42% with accelerated timing.** If recession materializes in Q1-Q2 2026, regional bank stocks historically decline 25-40% from pre-recession levels. FHN at $21.74 would imply a downside target of $13-16.

## Conclusion

FHN is a reasonably well-run regional bank trading at a fair-to-slightly-cheap valuation with an attractive dividend yield. In a normalized environment, this would warrant a constructive view. The current environment is far from normal: simultaneous stagflation across all six measured dimensions, oil above $100, credit cycle turning, and recession probability approaching 40%.

The calibration data warns against both insufficient AVOID issuance and against high-conviction BUY calls on economically sensitive names. FHN sits in the middle — it has genuine valuation support that prevents an AVOID, but the macro headwinds and credit cycle risk prevent a BUY. The 6.3% yield provides meaningful income while waiting for clarity on credit quality trends.

**Recommendation: HOLD.** The specific reason this is not a BUY: recession probability at 38-42% with accelerated timing to Q1-Q2 2026 creates a credible path to 20-30% earnings decline from rising provisions, which would erode the apparent valuation cushion and potentially threaten the dividend. Wait for either (a) evidence that credit quality is holding despite macro deterioration, or (b) a more attractive entry point in the $16-18 range that prices in provision normalization.

For existing holders, the dividend provides 6.3% annualized income, which partially compensates for the capital risk. Do not add at current levels.
