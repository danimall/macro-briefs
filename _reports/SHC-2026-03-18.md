## Executive Summary

Sotera Health is a global provider of sterilization solutions (Sterigenics), lab testing (Nelson Labs), and advisory services (Nordion) to the healthcare industry. The company reported 2025 revenue of $1.164B (+5.7% YoY), its 20th consecutive year of revenue growth, with net income of $78M ($0.27/share). Management guided 2026 revenue of $1.233-1.251B (5-6.5% constant currency growth) with adjusted EBITDA of $632M+, reflecting continued margin expansion and an X-ray facility buildout.

The investment case hinges on a tension between business quality and financial structure. Sotera's sterilization business has genuine mission-critical characteristics — medical device manufacturers must sterilize products, regulatory barriers to entry are high, and switching costs are meaningful. However, the stock trades at 51x trailing earnings on depressed GAAP EPS, the company carries heavy LBO-era debt, and PE sponsors Warburg Pincus and GTCR have sold approximately $1.03B in shares over six months (September 2025 through March 2026). This sustained institutional liquidation, combined with a macro environment hostile to leveraged companies with thin earnings coverage, warrants patience rather than entry.

## Company Overview

Sotera Health operates through three segments: **Sterigenics** (ethylene oxide, gamma, and X-ray sterilization), **Nordion** (cobalt-60 supply and nuclear medicine), and **Nelson Labs** (microbiological and analytical testing). The company went public in November 2020 via an IPO backed by PE sponsors Warburg Pincus and GTCR, who acquired the business in 2015. It serves over 40 countries and claims to sterilize approximately 40% of all single-use medical devices sold globally.

The business model is attractive in principle: sterilization is a regulatory requirement, capacity is limited by permitting and capital intensity, and customer relationships are sticky. Sotera has compounded revenue for two decades without a single down year. The 2026 guidance implies this streak will continue.

## Financial Analysis

**Revenue and Earnings:** 2025 revenue of $1.164B grew 5.7% (5.2% constant currency). Q4 2025 EPS of $0.26 beat estimates of $0.23 by 13%. Full-year net income was $78M ($0.27/share), up from $44M ($0.16/share) in 2024. The 69% YoY increase in EPS reflects operating leverage on modest revenue growth — typical of a high-fixed-cost sterilization infrastructure business.

**Profitability:** The company guided 2026 adjusted EBITDA of $632M+, implying an EBITDA margin of approximately 51% on the midpoint of the revenue guide ($1.242B). This is a high-margin business, consistent with asset-heavy infrastructure services with regulatory moats. However, the gap between adjusted EBITDA (~$632M) and GAAP net income (~$78M in 2025) is enormous — approximately $554M disappears in depreciation, amortization, interest expense, taxes, and adjustments. This spread reflects the heavy debt load from the LBO.

**Balance Sheet:** Financial statements were not available in the data feed, but the company's history as a PE-backed LBO with a $3.9B market cap, $78M net income, and guided EBITDA of $632M+ strongly implies net debt in the range of $2.5-3.5B. Interest expense likely consumes $200-300M+ annually. In a stagflationary environment where refinancing costs are elevated (7-month high mortgage rates, bond safe-haven failure), this leverage profile represents meaningful financial risk.

**Cash Flow:** Without explicit cash flow data, we can infer from the EBITDA-to-net-income conversion that free cash flow is substantially constrained by interest payments and capital expenditure (including the X-ray facility buildout referenced in 2026 guidance). The company pays no dividend, consistent with prioritizing debt reduction.

## Growth Analysis

The 2026 revenue guide of $1.233-1.251B implies 5-6.5% constant currency growth, with an additional ~100bps FX tailwind. This is consistent with the company's historical mid-single-digit organic growth rate.

Growth drivers include: (1) volume increases as global medical device production grows, (2) pricing power driven by regulatory complexity and limited sterilization capacity, (3) the X-ray facility buildout, which expands available sterilization modalities and capacity, and (4) margin expansion from operating leverage.

The X-ray initiative is strategically significant. Ethylene oxide (EtO) sterilization, Sotera's core modality, faces ongoing regulatory scrutiny over emissions and community health concerns. X-ray sterilization offers a complementary technology that reduces EtO concentration risk. Management's investment here is defensive as much as offensive.

The 5-6.5% growth rate is credible but unexceptional. At 51x trailing earnings, the market is pricing significant earnings acceleration. If GAAP EPS grows from $0.27 toward $0.50-0.60 over the next 2-3 years (through revenue growth, margin expansion, and deleveraging), the current multiple compresses to more reasonable levels. But that path requires consistent execution across multiple years with no macro disruption to healthcare spending or refinancing markets.

## Valuation Assessment

At $13.85, SHC trades at:
- **51.3x trailing P/E** on $0.27 EPS
- **51.3x forward P/E** (per data feed — implying consensus forward EPS approximately flat at ~$0.27)
- **~6.2x EV/EBITDA** (estimated: $3.9B market cap + ~$3B estimated net debt = ~$6.9B EV on ~$632M EBITDA guide) — this is a more favorable metric
- **Market cap of $3.93B**

The P/E ratio is misleading in isolation because GAAP earnings are depressed by heavy interest expense and amortization of intangibles from the LBO. EV/EBITDA of ~6.2x is more relevant for a leveraged infrastructure business and appears reasonable relative to specialty services peers.

However, EV/EBITDA being "reasonable" while P/E is extreme signals precisely the problem: equity holders sit below a large debt stack, and small changes in interest rates or refinancing terms have outsized effects on residual equity value. The stock acts more like a levered option on the underlying business than a straightforward equity investment.

The 52-week range of $9.53-$19.85 reflects this optionality — 108% peak-to-trough. Current price at $13.85 sits 30% below the January 2026 high of $19.85, which coincided with peak optimism around the Q4 beat.

## Competitive Landscape

Sotera's competitive position is its strongest dimension. The company claims ~40% share of global single-use medical device sterilization. Barriers to entry include: (1) regulatory permitting for EtO and gamma radiation facilities takes years, (2) customers require validated processes that are costly to re-validate with new providers, (3) scale economies in cobalt-60 sourcing (Nordion), and (4) geographic density of facility networks creates logistics advantages.

Direct competitors include STERIS plc (STE) in sterilization and BGS Beta-Gamma-Service in gamma processing. STERIS, however, is primarily a hospital equipment company with sterilization as one segment, while Sotera is a pure-play.

The EtO regulatory risk is the primary competitive threat. If EPA or state regulators restrict EtO emissions further, Sotera faces facility closures or costly retrofits. The X-ray buildout partially mitigates this but cannot fully replace EtO capacity in the near term.

## Risk Assessment

**Leverage Risk (HIGH):** The LBO-era debt structure means equity holders absorb disproportionate downside from rate increases, revenue shortfalls, or refinancing challenges. In the current macro environment (stagflation, credit cascade probability at 55-60%), this is material.

**PE Sponsor Liquidation (HIGH):** Warburg Pincus and GTCR have sold approximately $1.03B in shares over 6 months:
- Sept 5, 2025: Warburg sold 12M shares at $15.11 ($181M)
- Nov 10, 2025: Warburg sold 18M shares at $15.65 ($282M); GTCR sold 12M shares ($188M)
- Mar 5, 2026: Warburg sold 15M shares at $15.27 ($229M); GTCR sold 10M shares ($153M)

After the March sale, Warburg retained 19.1M shares and GTCR retained 12.7M shares — a combined 31.8M shares (11.2% of shares outstanding). The pace of liquidation is accelerating. When sponsors with board representation are selling this aggressively, the signal is unambiguous: they believe the current price adequately compensates for remaining upside. The March 2026 secondary offering directly preceded the stock's decline from ~$15 to ~$13.85.

**EtO Regulatory Risk (MEDIUM-HIGH):** Community opposition and EPA scrutiny of ethylene oxide emissions remain ongoing. Historical litigation (the Illinois cases) has been a significant overhang. While the company has reached settlements, new regulatory actions could force operational changes.

**Macro Sensitivity (MEDIUM):** Healthcare sterilization has defensive characteristics — medical devices must be sterilized regardless of economic conditions. However, in a severe recession, medical device production volumes could decline, and Sotera's leveraged capital structure amplifies the earnings impact.

**Valuation Risk (HIGH):** At 51x P/E, there is no margin of safety. Any earnings disappointment, regulatory setback, or refinancing difficulty would compress the multiple aggressively.

## Investment Thesis

**Bull Case:**
- Mission-critical sterilization franchise with 20 consecutive years of revenue growth and ~40% global share in single-use medical device sterilization
- EV/EBITDA of ~6.2x is reasonable for this quality of business; GAAP P/E is artificially elevated by LBO debt structure
- If the company deleverages and grows EBITDA as guided, equity value could compound significantly — $0.27 EPS growing to $0.50-0.70 over 3 years would make the current price look cheap
- X-ray facility buildout diversifies technology risk and expands addressable capacity
- Healthcare sterilization is relatively recession-resistant

**Bear Case:**
- PE sponsors are selling aggressively — $1.03B in 6 months with ~$500M still to exit. This creates persistent supply pressure
- 51x P/E with no dividend provides zero margin of safety
- Heavy leverage amplifies any revenue shortfall, rate increase, or refinancing challenge
- EtO regulatory risk remains unresolved — further restrictions could impair the core business
- In stagflation with credit markets under stress (55-60% cascade probability), highly leveraged companies face elevated refinancing risk
- Stock declined 25% from January 2026 peak ($19.85) to March 2026 ($13.85) despite no deterioration in business fundamentals — suggesting the PE overhang is the primary price driver

## Conclusion

Sotera Health operates a genuinely high-quality sterilization franchise with durable competitive advantages. The business fundamentals are sound: 20 years of consecutive revenue growth, 51%+ EBITDA margins, and a credible 2026 growth guide of 5-6.5%.

The problem is the financial structure and the ownership overhang. At 51x trailing P/E, the stock requires multiple years of earnings growth to justify its current price, while PE sponsors are liquidating at a pace that creates continuous downward pressure. The leveraged capital structure means equity holders bear disproportionate risk from macro deterioration, and the current stagflationary environment with elevated credit stress (oil at $98-108, credit cascade probability 55-60%) is precisely the wrong macro backdrop for levered equity stories.

**Recommendation: HOLD.** The business quality warrants monitoring, and an entry point may emerge as PE sponsors complete their exit. A more attractive entry would be below $11 (roughly 40x trailing P/E, or lower if forward earnings grow toward $0.35-0.40), where the risk/reward better compensates for the leverage and overhang risks. Alternatively, material deleveraging (visible in reduced interest expense flowing through to GAAP EPS) would change the calculus.
