## Executive Summary

Ares Management is a leading global alternative asset manager with $622 billion in AUM as of Q4 2025, up from approximately $464B a year prior. The company raised a record $113 billion in 2025, announced a 20% dividend increase, and CEO Arougheti articulated a growth roadmap centered on AI-adjacent infrastructure and private credit expansion. These are real competitive achievements in an industry where scale begets scale.

The problem is that this franchise is now being tested by a private credit stress cycle that is accelerating rather than abating. JPMorgan marked down loans held by private-credit groups and tightened lending to the sector in mid-March 2026, directly pressuring ARES shares. The Q4 2025 earnings miss ($1.45 reported vs. $1.71 expected) on a GAAP net income of just $54.2 million raises questions about the durability of fee-related earnings when portfolio companies face stagflationary conditions — $119 oil, rising rates, and contracting consumer spending. At 61x forward earnings with credit cascade probability at 55-60% per our macro framework, the risk/reward is unfavorable for new positions despite the 45% drawdown from the August high.

## Company Overview

Ares Management operates across four primary segments: Credit Group (its largest by AUM), Private Equity, Real Assets, and Secondaries/Other. The firm's dominant position is in direct lending and private credit, where it has been a primary beneficiary of the secular shift of lending activity from banks to private capital. With ~$622B in AUM, Ares ranks among the top five global alternative asset managers alongside Blackstone, Apollo, KKR, and Carlyle. The company is publicly traded as a Class A common stock, with a current market capitalization of approximately $23.6 billion.

## Financial Analysis

**Earnings and Profitability:** Q4 2025 GAAP net income attributable to ARES was $54.2 million. Reported EPS of $1.45 missed consensus expectations of $1.71 by 15.2%. This is a material miss for a firm that has historically beaten expectations. The trailing P/E stands at 61.2x based on reported EPS of $1.76, which implies the earnings power of the business on a GAAP basis is roughly $390M annually against a $23.6B market cap. Alternative asset managers typically report fee-related earnings (FRE) and distributable earnings (DE) as supplemental metrics, which are higher than GAAP — but the GAAP miss signals that realization rates on carried interest and incentive fees are under pressure.

**AUM Growth:** The record $622B in AUM represents approximately 34% growth year-over-year, driven by $113B in total fundraising including a record fourth quarter. This organic growth rate is among the highest in the alternative asset management industry and reflects strong LP demand for Ares' credit-oriented strategies.

**Dividend:** The 20% dividend increase signals management confidence. At the current price of ~$108 and a dividend yield of approximately 5.1%, ARES offers meaningful income. However, the payout needs to be evaluated against distributable earnings sustainability in a credit stress environment.

**Balance Sheet:** Financial statement detail was not available in the data provided. For an asset management firm, the key balance sheet risk is exposure to co-investments and seed capital on the balance sheet. Ares carries meaningful balance sheet investments alongside its funds.

## Growth Analysis

**Secular Tailwind — Private Credit:** The structural shift from bank lending to private credit has been Ares' primary growth driver. Banks' retreat from middle-market lending, leveraged loans, and real estate credit has created a multi-trillion-dollar addressable market. Ares' $113B annual fundraise demonstrates that LP appetite for these strategies remains strong.

**AI Infrastructure:** CEO Arougheti highlighted AI-related investments at the Bank of America Financial Services conference on February 10, 2026. Ares has been deploying capital into data center financing, power infrastructure, and digital economy assets. This positions the credit business to capture financing demand from the $1T+ AI infrastructure buildout.

**Geographic Expansion:** Ares has been expanding its footprint in Asia-Pacific and European private credit markets, where bank disintermediation is earlier in its lifecycle than in the US.

**Counterpoint — Growth Quality Under Stress:** The Q4 miss raises a specific concern: AUM growth driven by fundraising is a leading indicator, but earnings growth requires successful deployment and realization. In a stagflationary environment with rising defaults, the conversion of AUM into realized earnings may deteriorate. The $50M investment in Propagate Content (entertainment) is a small example of the kind of deal activity that could face write-down risk in a consumer spending contraction.

## Valuation Assessment

ARES trades at 61.2x trailing earnings — a premium multiple even by alternative asset manager standards. For context, Apollo (APO) trades at lower multiples despite similar scale. The forward P/E of 61.2x implies the market expects no meaningful earnings acceleration, or that current GAAP earnings are depressed relative to normalized levels.

**Price History:** The stock has fallen from $195.26 (August 13, 2025 high) to $107.99, a 44.7% decline. The most severe leg down occurred from late January through mid-March 2026, with the stock falling from ~$176 to a low of $95.80. The volume profile confirms capitulation: 8.99M shares traded on March 12, 2026 (the day of the $95.80 low) versus average daily volume of 2.5M. A partial recovery to ~$108 has followed, but the stock remains deeply below its 200-day moving average.

**Valuation vs. Risk:** At 61x forward earnings, ARES is priced for a growth franchise operating in benign conditions. Current conditions — JPMorgan markdowns, private credit sector liquidity strain, credit cascade probability at 55-60%, and the March 31 stress date for semi-liquid funds — are the opposite of benign. Even after a 45% drawdown, the multiple remains elevated because the earnings denominator has compressed.

**Dividend Yield:** The ~5.1% yield provides some downside support, but dividend coverage could come under pressure if realization income falls further.

## Competitive Landscape

Ares holds a strong competitive position in private credit, which is the fastest-growing segment of alternative assets. Key competitive advantages include:

1. **Scale:** $622B AUM provides negotiating leverage with borrowers, access to larger transactions, and operating leverage on the fee base.
2. **Origination Platform:** Ares' direct lending capabilities and proprietary deal flow are difficult for smaller managers to replicate.
3. **Diversification:** Four operating segments reduce concentration risk relative to pure-play credit shops.
4. **Track Record:** A 10-year annualized return of 27.5% has built strong LP relationships and brand equity.

**Competitive Risks:** The private credit market has attracted significant new entrants and expanded existing players' allocations. Competition for deals has compressed spreads on new originations. In a stress cycle, the differentiation between high-quality and lower-quality private credit books will become apparent.

## Risk Assessment

**1. Private Credit Systemic Risk (PRIMARY):** JPMorgan marked down private-credit loans and tightened lending to the sector. This is exactly the kind of bank pullback that can trigger a liquidity cascade: banks mark down → restricted lending → redemption pressure on semi-liquid funds → forced selling → further markdowns. Our macro framework assigns 55-60% probability to this cascade, with the March 31 quarter-end as a key pressure point. If Ares or comparable managers restrict redemptions in semi-liquid credit funds, the probability rises to ~70%.

**2. Earnings Vulnerability:** The Q4 miss was driven by lower realization income. In a rising-rate, stagflationary environment ($119 oil, core PCE at 3.1%, NFP -92K), portfolio company distress will increase. Higher default rates in Ares' loan book would reduce net investment income, potentially trigger fee waivers, and impair carried interest accruals.

**3. Macro Sensitivity:** Ares' business model is procyclical despite management's claims of defensiveness. Fee-related earnings from management fees are relatively stable, but performance fees, carried interest, and balance sheet co-investment returns are highly sensitive to credit market conditions.

**4. Valuation Compression Risk:** If the market reprices alternative asset managers from growth multiples to cyclical multiples (which would imply 15-25x earnings), further significant downside exists even from current levels.

**5. Insider Activity — Mixed Signals:** Director Ashish Bhutani purchased $1.27M of stock on February 6, 2026, and Director Judy Olian purchased $60K on February 20 — both meaningful insider buys at depressed prices. However, General Counsel Naseem Sagati Aghili sold shares across multiple transactions in January and February, totaling approximately $830K in dispositions. CEO Arougheti's January 31 activity was tax-withholding on vesting equity (not discretionary selling), which is neutral. On balance, the Bhutani purchase is the most informative signal — a $1.27M open-market buy by a director during peak stress suggests informed confidence.

## Investment Thesis

### Bull Case
- $622B AUM and $113B annual fundraise represent a franchise that was generating extraordinary growth through year-end 2025. If credit stress proves manageable (CEO calls jitters "overblown noise"), the stock could recover toward $150-170 as the market re-rates the earnings power.
- The 5.1% dividend yield provides meaningful carry while waiting for recovery.
- Private credit's structural growth drivers (bank disintermediation, regulatory capital requirements) are intact regardless of cyclical conditions.
- $1.27M insider purchase by a director at ~$127 suggests informed insiders view the selloff as overdone.
- AI infrastructure financing opportunity adds a secular growth vector.

### Bear Case
- At 61x forward earnings, the stock is priced for perfection in conditions that are deteriorating. A credit cascade (55-60% probability) would pressure AUM through redemptions, compress performance fees, and potentially trigger write-downs on co-invested balance sheet capital.
- JPMorgan's sector-wide credit restrictions are an early warning. If additional banks follow, Ares' ability to warehouse and syndicate deals will be impaired.
- Q4 earnings miss ($1.45 vs $1.71) occurred before the most acute phase of the credit stress (which began in late February/early March 2026). Q1 2026 earnings could be worse.
- The General Counsel's repeated selling in January/February, while individually small, suggests at least one senior insider viewed the stock as fairly valued above $125.
- Calibration warning: APO (a comparable alternative asset manager) was this system's worst-performing BUY call at scores of 7.55 and 7.35. Cyclical alternative managers deserve lower conviction ratings. Cyclical score ceiling of 7.0 applies.

## Conclusion

ARES is a high-quality franchise under cyclical stress. The 45% drawdown reflects real risks: a Q4 earnings miss, JPMorgan credit markdowns, and a private credit liquidity squeeze that may worsen before it improves. The $622B AUM base and 20% dividend increase are genuine strengths, and the $1.27M insider purchase is a positive signal. However, at 61x forward earnings, the valuation does not adequately compensate for 55-60% credit cascade probability and a macro regime (stagflation, $119 oil, hawkish Fed) that is hostile to leveraged credit strategies. The stock has partially recovered from its $95.80 low to ~$108, but we would need to see either (a) a significant further price decline to create a genuine margin of safety, or (b) evidence that credit stress is stabilizing (redemption data, fund NAV disclosures) before upgrading.

**Recommendation: HOLD.** Existing holders should maintain positions given the income yield and franchise quality, but new capital should wait for either sub-$90 prices (where the earnings yield becomes compelling even on depressed earnings) or a demonstrable turn in private credit conditions. The March 31 quarter-end reporting cycle will be the most informative data point.
