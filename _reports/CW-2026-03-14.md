## Executive Summary

Curtiss-Wright reported Q4 CY2025 revenue of $947M (+14.9% YoY), beating consensus estimates, with adjusted EPS of $3.79 vs. $3.66 expected. Full-year 2026 guidance of ~$3.74B revenue (midpoint) came in 1.2% above analyst estimates, and management signaled operating margin expansion alongside double-digit EPS growth. The company sits at the intersection of three secular demand drivers: multi-year naval defense restocking (Columbia-class and Virginia-class submarine programs), commercial nuclear power renaissance, and industrial process control modernization.

The fundamental issue is valuation. At $656 per share, CW trades at approximately 50x trailing earnings with a market cap of $24.2B. This multiple embeds substantial growth expectations into the current price. The stock has appreciated roughly 146% from its 52-week low of $266.88, reflecting the market's aggressive repricing of defense exposure. Morgan Stanley's February 2026 price target raise to $760 (from $660) implies ~16% upside, but requires continued beat-and-raise execution with no margin compression. For a company generating sub-$1B in quarterly revenue, this valuation demands near-flawless delivery.

The macro backdrop is supportive: confirmed multi-year munitions depletion, $55B/month US war cost run rate, and bipartisan defense spending consensus all favor CW's end markets. The question is whether this is already reflected in the share price. At 50x earnings, the answer is substantially yes.

## Company Overview

Curtiss-Wright is a diversified industrial company with roots dating to the Wright brothers' original aviation enterprise. The company operates across three segments: Defense Electronics, Naval & Power, and Process & Industrial. Key programs include reactor coolant pumps and control rod drive mechanisms for the US Navy's nuclear submarine fleet, defense electronics for naval and ground platforms, and industrial valve and instrumentation products for commercial nuclear and process industries.

The company has paid dividends consistently since 1989, with the most recent quarterly dividend of $0.24/share (annualized yield of ~0.14%). The low yield reflects management's capital allocation priority toward acquisitive growth and share repurchases rather than income distribution.

## Growth Analysis

CW's growth drivers are concrete and multi-year in nature:

1. **Naval defense programs:** Columbia-class SSBN and Virginia-class SSN submarine programs are the company's anchor. These are 20+ year production programs with locked-in procurement schedules. CW provides reactor coolant pumps, control rod drive mechanisms, and other mission-critical nuclear propulsion components that are sole-sourced or limited-sourced. The current geopolitical environment (Iran conflict, Tomahawk depletion, 90% Hormuz transit decline) intensifies Congressional pressure for accelerated naval construction.

2. **Commercial nuclear renaissance:** Global investment in new nuclear capacity (SMRs, Generation III+ reactors) creates a secondary demand curve for CW's nuclear technology expertise. NRC licensing timelines mean revenue visibility extends 5-10 years.

3. **Defense electronics modernization:** Electronic warfare, sensors, and embedded computing for naval and ground platforms benefit from the broader defense recapitalization cycle.

4. **M&A track record:** CW has historically supplemented organic growth with tuck-in acquisitions in adjacent defense and industrial segments. The 14.9% Q4 revenue growth likely includes some inorganic contribution.

The 2026 guidance of ~$3.74B with margin expansion and double-digit EPS growth is credible given the backlog environment. The risk is execution, not demand.

## Valuation Assessment

This is where the thesis gets difficult. CW at ~50x trailing earnings is expensive by any historical standard for a diversified industrial/defense company:

- **Peer comparison:** LMT trades at ~18-20x earnings, RTX at ~30-35x, NOC at ~20-22x, GD at ~18-20x. CW's premium reflects its smaller size (~$3.7B revenue vs. $60B+ for primes) and perceived growth optionality, but a 50x multiple requires sustained 15%+ earnings growth for several years to grow into.
- **Historical comparison:** CW traded at 15-25x earnings as recently as 2022-2023 before the defense repricing. The stock has moved from $267 to $656 in roughly one year, a 146% appreciation.
- **Implied growth:** At 50x, the market is pricing in approximately $26-30 in EPS within 3-4 years (assuming a terminal 25x multiple and a target 10% annual return). From a ~$13 base, this requires a near-doubling of earnings, or ~25% CAGR. While achievable through a combination of organic growth, M&A, and margin expansion, it leaves no room for error.

Morgan Stanley's $760 target (from $660, raised February 13) implies the stock is still 16% undervalued even at these multiples. This is a momentum call backed by defense spending conviction, and may prove correct, but the valuation cushion is thin.

## Competitive Landscape

CW's competitive position is genuinely strong. The company occupies niche positions in naval nuclear propulsion components where switching costs are prohibitive and qualification cycles run 5-10+ years. The installed base of reactor components on existing Navy vessels creates a decades-long aftermarket revenue stream. In commercial nuclear, the company competes with Framatome and Westinghouse on specific subsystems, but enjoys protected positions on many US Navy platforms.

The diversification across defense electronics, naval/power, and process/industrial provides some cyclical buffer. Defense programs are largely insulated from commercial cycle volatility, and the nuclear power exposure is counter-cyclical to fossil fuel sentiment.

Key competitive risks: (1) program cancellation or delay (low probability for submarine programs, but possible for peripheral defense electronics); (2) acquisition integration risk if management pursues a larger deal; (3) nuclear regulatory changes that could delay commercial reactor deployments.

## Risk Assessment

**Valuation risk (HIGH):** At 50x earnings, any earnings miss, guidance reduction, or program delay could trigger a sharp de-rating. A reversion to even 35x (still premium to defense primes) implies ~30% downside.

**Macro sensitivity (MODERATE):** Beta of 0.94 suggests the stock moves roughly with the market. In a broader equity correction (40-50% probability per our macro framework), CW would likely participate despite defense fundamentals. The stock dropped from $730 to $656 (-10%) in recent weeks alongside the broader market selloff.

**Program concentration (MODERATE):** Heavy reliance on Navy submarine programs means any budget sequestration or program restructuring has outsized impact. The current bipartisan defense consensus reduces this risk, but it cannot be eliminated.

**Acquisition risk (MODERATE):** CW's historical M&A appetite could lead to a dilutive or poorly-timed acquisition at current elevated valuations.

**Conflict-duration dependency (LOW-MODERATE):** Unlike pure-play defense names like LMT or RTX, CW's revenue base is partially commercial (process/industrial, commercial nuclear). The defense spending tailwind is beneficial but the company is less directly leveraged to munitions restocking than the primes.

**Insider activity:** 20 Form 4 filings in the past 3 months suggest active insider transactions. Without detail on whether these are purchases, sales, or option exercises, the signal is ambiguous. The volume of filings (7 on March 10 alone) could indicate compensation-related transactions (annual RSU vesting) rather than directional insider conviction.

## Investment Thesis

**Bull Case:**
- Naval defense spending is structurally accelerating (multi-year submarine programs, Tomahawk depletion requiring fleet modernization, $55B/month war cost run rate)
- FY2026 guidance of double-digit EPS growth is achievable and likely conservative given the backlog environment
- Commercial nuclear renaissance provides a second secular growth vector with 5-10 year visibility
- CW's niche positions in sole-sourced naval components create pricing power and high barriers to entry
- Morgan Stanley's $760 target implies 16% upside; continued defense repricing could push multiples higher

**Bear Case:**
- 50x trailing earnings prices in substantial growth that must materialize flawlessly
- Any broader equity correction will drag CW down regardless of defense fundamentals
- The stock has already appreciated 146% from its 52-week low; the easy money has been made
- Low dividend yield (0.14%) provides negligible downside support
- A reversion to 30-35x earnings (still a premium multiple) implies 30-40% downside from current levels
- Section 301 trade probes could affect supply chain costs for industrial components

## Conclusion

CW is a well-managed, competitively advantaged defense/industrial company with genuine multi-year growth tailwinds. The Q4 2025 results and FY2026 guidance confirm the fundamental story. The problem is the price. At 50x earnings, the stock requires sustained 15%+ earnings growth for multiple years with no stumbles. For investors already long from lower levels, this is a reasonable hold with trailing stops. For new capital, the entry point offers limited margin of safety. The defense spending tailwind is real and durable, but it is also widely recognized and substantially priced in.

Recommendation: **HOLD.** The fundamental quality merits continued ownership but does not justify initiating new positions at 50x earnings. A pullback to 35-40x (roughly $460-530) would create a more attractive entry.
