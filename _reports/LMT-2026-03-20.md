## Executive Summary

Lockheed Martin is the world's largest defense contractor with a $145B market cap, trading at $637.51 as of March 19, 2026 — up 35.5% over the trailing twelve months and 66% from its July 2025 low of $410.11. The rally is grounded in a structural increase in defense demand: the Pentagon's $200B supplemental budget, $23B in emergency Gulf arms sales, NATO allies accelerating procurement (Greece's $36B air defense plan), and the ongoing Iran conflict which the IEA has labeled the largest supply disruption in history. These are not transient catalysts — the multi-year restocking cycle is conflict-duration-insensitive because depleted munitions inventories and expanded threat environments require sustained procurement regardless of ceasefire timing.

At ~29.7x trailing earnings ($21.49 EPS), LMT trades at a premium to its 5-year average P/E of ~18-20x. This premium is justified by the step-function increase in the addressable defense budget, but it limits near-term upside to earnings growth rather than multiple expansion. The backlog (historically $150B+), F-35 production ramp, missile restocking orders, and international FMS pipeline provide multi-year revenue visibility that few industrials can match. The primary risk is that the stock has already discounted a significant portion of the spending increase, and any de-escalation in the Middle East could trigger a 10-15% drawdown as the geopolitical premium compresses.

## Company Overview

Lockheed Martin operates four segments: Aeronautics (F-35, F-16, C-130J), Missiles and Fire Control (HIMARS, PAC-3, JASSM), Rotary and Mission Systems (Sikorsky helicopters, Aegis combat systems), and Space (Orion, GPS satellites, missile defense). Aeronautics generates roughly 40% of revenue, driven primarily by the F-35 program. The company has ~230M shares outstanding, pays a ~2.1% dividend yield, and has consistently returned capital through buybacks, reducing the share count from ~275M in 2020 to 230M today.

## Growth Analysis

**Structural Demand Drivers:**

1. **Pentagon $200B supplemental + $23B emergency Gulf arms sales** — $223B in identifiable incremental defense spending. LMT captures the largest share of DoD prime contracts (~25-28% of total procurement spending historically).

2. **F-35 production ramp** — The program remains the world's most popular fighter jet (per Motley Fool coverage). International orders continue to expand: Greece's $36B air defense plan includes F-35 orders. Over 3,000 aircraft are planned over the program's life; roughly 1,000 have been delivered. Each unit generates $80-100M in revenue plus decades of sustainment revenue.

3. **Missile restocking** — HIMARS, PAC-3, JASSM, and THAAD inventories have been depleted by Ukraine and now the Iran conflict. The Missiles and Fire Control segment (led by Timothy Cahill, whose insider sales are visible in the data) is the most direct beneficiary of the munitions replenishment cycle.

4. **$111.5M naval contract** (announced March 12, 2026) — Incremental but illustrative of ongoing order flow.

5. **Space/Artemis** — NASA's Artemis II mission targeting April 2026 launch. Orion spacecraft is an LMT program. This is a smaller revenue contributor but provides diversification.

6. **International FMS pipeline** — NATO allies are increasing defense budgets toward 2-3% of GDP. Germany, Poland, Japan, South Korea, Australia, and Greece are all active F-35 customers or prospects.

**Growth Rate Expectation:** Revenue growth of 5-8% annually over the next 3-5 years, with potential upside to 10%+ if supplemental spending flows through contracts faster than the typical 18-24 month lag. EPS growth could exceed revenue growth via operating leverage and continued buybacks.

## Valuation Assessment

**Current Multiples:**
- Trailing P/E: 29.7x ($637.51 / $21.49)
- Forward P/E: 29.7x (per Yahoo Finance data — implying flat forward estimates, which seems conservative)
- Market Cap: $144.5B
- Dividend Yield: 2.15%

**Historical Context:** LMT's 5-year average P/E has been ~18-20x. The current 29.7x represents a 50-65% premium to its historical average. This premium began expanding in late 2025/early 2026 as the Iran conflict drove defense spending expectations structurally higher.

**Justification for Premium:** The $223B in identifiable incremental spending represents a step-function increase in the defense budget that occurs once per generation (comparable to post-9/11 expansion). If LMT captures 25% of incremental procurement, that's $55B+ in additional contract value over the next 3-5 years, against a current revenue base of ~$70-72B. This justifies some multiple expansion. A reasonable "new normal" P/E for LMT in a structurally elevated demand environment is 22-25x, which would place fair value at $470-540 on current earnings, or $530-$600 on estimated FY2026 EPS of ~$24-25.

**Valuation Concern:** At $637.51, the stock is pricing in ~$25+ in EPS (at a 25x target multiple) or sustained premium multiple territory. The risk/reward is less favorable than it was at $460-480 in the fall of 2025. The stock is 7.9% off its all-time high of $692, suggesting the market is already digesting the spending thesis.

**Score Implication:** Valuation is stretched relative to history (5.5/10), but the fundamental demand backdrop is the strongest in two decades. This is a "pay up for quality and visibility" situation rather than a deep value opportunity.

## Competitive Landscape

**Market Position:** LMT is the #1 US defense contractor by revenue, with unassailable positions in tactical aircraft (F-35), missile defense (PAC-3, THAAD), and space launch (Orion). The F-35 program alone creates a 40-year competitive moat: once an allied nation integrates the F-35 into its air force, switching costs are effectively infinite (logistics, training, interoperability).

**Competitive Dynamics:**
- **RTX** — Strong in engines (Pratt & Whitney) and missiles (Raytheon), but restructuring integration complexity. LMT's platform-centric model is cleaner.
- **NOC** — Strong in bombers (B-21) and space. Less direct overlap with LMT.
- **GD** — Submarines and ground vehicles. Complementary rather than competitive.
- **L3Harris (LHX)** — Reported Q4 2025 alongside LMT (Jan 29). LHX focuses on communications and electronic warfare. LMT has the larger installed base and higher backlog.
- **KTOS** — Drone/unmanned systems competitor, but at 1/50th LMT's scale.

**Moat Assessment (9.0/10):** LMT's moat derives from: (1) sole-source contracts on major platforms (F-35, Trident, Orion); (2) classified programs that competitors cannot bid on without decades of security clearance infrastructure; (3) 50+ year maintenance/sustainment tails on delivered platforms; (4) foreign military sales lock-in (allied air forces standardizing on F-35). This is one of the widest moats in the industrial sector.

## Risk Assessment

**1. Valuation Risk (HIGH):** At 29.7x trailing, the stock is priced for a defense spending environment that persists for years. Any de-escalation in the Middle East, a ceasefire, or budget sequestration pressure could trigger a 10-15% valuation compression back toward 22-25x.

**2. Execution Risk (MODERATE):** F-35 has experienced delivery delays, cost overruns, and Technical Refresh 3 (TR-3) software issues. If production targets are missed, revenue recognition timing shifts backward.

**3. DOGE/Budget Risk (LOW-MODERATE):** The Department of Government Efficiency (DOGE) has created headline risk for government contractors. However, defense procurement is the area least likely to face cuts in a wartime environment. The $200B supplemental is Congress-directed and war-specific.

**4. Insider Selling (MODERATE):** Timothy Cahill (President, Missiles & Fire Control) sold 4,620 shares on March 11, 2026, at prices around $650-660. Gregory Ulmer (President, Aeronautics) sold 2,840 shares on February 27. These are non-trivial but represent small fractions of their holdings (Cahill retained ~9,591 shares; Ulmer retained ~5,661 shares). The February 25 RSU grants to all senior executives suggest these are compensation-related sales, which is routine. CEO Taiclet received 8,803 RSUs on February 25 and did not sell.

**5. Concentration Risk (MODERATE):** The US government accounts for ~70%+ of revenue. Bipartisan support for defense spending mitigates this, but a single customer creates binary political risk.

**6. Macro Sensitivity (LOW):** Defense spending is counter-cyclical to the broader economy. In the current stagflationary environment (GDP 0.7%, CPI 3.1%, oil $119), defense contractors are among the most insulated sectors. LMT's 0.6 beta (per world model) confirms low market correlation.

## Investment Thesis

### Bull Case
- The $223B in identifiable incremental defense spending flows through LMT's backlog over the next 2-4 years, driving revenue growth to 8-10% annually versus the historical 3-5%.
- F-35 international orders accelerate as NATO allies race to 2-3% GDP defense spending targets. Greece's $36B plan is one of 15+ allied procurement programs.
- EPS reaches $26-28 by FY2027 on operating leverage and buybacks.
- At 25x forward earnings, the stock reaches $650-700 in 12-18 months (3-10% upside from current levels), with the possibility of sustained premium multiples if the threat environment persists.
- The Iran conflict extends beyond 6 months (40% probability per world model), keeping defense spending urgency at peak levels.
- The stock functions as a portfolio hedge against geopolitical deterioration in a world where bonds have failed as safe-haven assets.

### Bear Case
- A ceasefire or diplomatic resolution (22% probability for negotiated wind-down) reduces the geopolitical premium embedded in the 29.7x P/E. A reversion to 22x trailing would imply $473 — a 26% drawdown.
- F-35 TR-3 software delays continue, slowing deliveries and creating negative headlines.
- Budget sequestration or political constraints on the supplemental appropriation cap spending below expectations.
- The stock has already rallied 66% from its 2025 low; most of the easy upside is captured.
- Insider selling from two segment presidents, while routine, is directionally negative at all-time-high prices.

## Conclusion

LMT remains a BUY with the understanding that the risk/reward profile has shifted from "compelling" to "favorable but with less margin of safety" after a 66% rally. The structural demand case is the strongest in 20+ years: $223B in identifiable defense spending, NATO rearmament, munitions restocking, and F-35 international orders provide multi-year backlog visibility. The company's competitive moat is among the widest in global industrials, and its low correlation to the broader market makes it a valuable portfolio allocation in a stagflationary, bond-safe-haven-failure environment.

The valuation at 29.7x trailing is above the historical range, and investors buying at current levels are paying for earnings growth that has yet to fully materialize in reported numbers. A reasonable 12-month target range is $600-700, with downside protection at $470-500 (the 22-25x trailing P/E floor). The risk of a 15-25% drawdown on de-escalation exists but is partially offset by the 40% probability that the acute conflict phase extends beyond 6 weeks and the structural nature of the restocking cycle.

For portfolio construction, LMT serves as the anchor defense allocation. Position sizing should reflect that the stock is no longer cheap and that the best entry point was $420-480 in H2 2025. At current levels, a moderate position with a willingness to add on a 10-15% pullback toward $550-570 represents the appropriate risk management approach.
