## Executive Summary

Cheniere Energy is the largest US LNG exporter, operating the Sabine Pass and Corpus Christi liquefaction terminals. The company has become the single most important beneficiary of the structural destruction of Middle Eastern LNG and oil export infrastructure. Qatar's Ras Laffan facility — responsible for ~17% of Qatar's LNG capacity — sustained damage requiring an estimated 5-year repair per QatarEnergy's CEO. The Strait of Hormuz closure has eliminated the primary export route for Middle Eastern hydrocarbons. These events, confirmed by the IEA as the "largest supply disruption in history of the global oil market," have fundamentally reordered global LNG trade flows in Cheniere's favor.

The stock surged from ~$220 in late February to an intraday high of $299.49 on March 19, settling at $281.87 — a 24% move in under a month. Q4 2025 earnings (reported February 26) showed record LNG production with revenues rising year-over-year, though adjusted EPS of $2.87 missed the $3.83 consensus estimate. The miss is backward-looking and largely irrelevant given the structural forward catalyst. At 11.7x forward earnings with confirmed new commercial contracts (Thailand expanding to 1.3M tons annually), the valuation reflects significant upside but does not fully capture a multi-year supply gap that cannot be filled by new liquefaction capacity (4-7 year build times for greenfield projects).

The key risk is the geopolitical premium's durability. If Hormuz reopens faster than expected or Qatar repairs proceed ahead of schedule, the supply tightness eases. However, the world model's assessment of conflict duration (40% probability of acute phase extending beyond 6 weeks, 25% continued escalation) and QatarEnergy's own 5-year repair timeline suggest the structural thesis has substantial staying power.

## Company Overview

Cheniere Energy operates through two primary subsidiaries: Sabine Pass Liquefaction (SPL) in Louisiana and Corpus Christi Liquefaction (CCL) in Texas. Combined, these facilities represent approximately 45 million tonnes per annum (MTPA) of LNG nameplate capacity, making Cheniere the largest LNG producer in the US and among the top three globally. The company's business model centers on long-term, take-or-pay contracts with creditworthy counterparties, providing revenue visibility. It also sells LNG on the spot market, which provides upside exposure to price spikes.

The company completed its "20/20 Vision" capital allocation plan as announced alongside Q4 2025 results, and authorized a new share repurchase program. The market cap stands at approximately $60.3 billion with 210.2 million shares outstanding.

## Growth Analysis

**Structural Demand Catalyst — Qatar LNG Capacity Destruction:**
The destruction of 17% of Qatar's LNG capacity at Ras Laffan, with a 5-year estimated repair timeline, removes approximately 13 MTPA from global supply. New greenfield liquefaction projects take 4-7 years to build, meaning this supply gap cannot be filled by new capacity on any reasonable timeline. Cheniere's existing operational capacity positions it as the primary beneficiary.

**Commercial Contract Wins:**
Blockonomi reported on March 19 that Thailand expanded its LNG order with Cheniere to 1.3 million tons annually. This is a concrete example of JERA-style buyer shifts — Asian buyers reducing Middle East procurement and redirecting to US LNG suppliers. These contracts are typically long-term (15-20 years), creating durable revenue streams.

**Corpus Christi Stage 3 Expansion:**
Cheniere's midstream expansion at Corpus Christi (Stage 3) adds approximately 10+ MTPA of capacity. In the current environment of structural supply scarcity, this expansion's economics have improved substantially. Long-term contract negotiations for this capacity carry significantly more pricing power than they did six months ago.

**Pricing Environment:**
European gas prices at €74/MWh and Asian LNG spot prices elevated due to Hormuz closure and Qatar capacity loss create a sustained spread environment favorable to US LNG exporters. Henry Hub domestic gas prices remain relatively low, widening the arbitrage.

## Valuation Assessment

At 11.7x forward P/E, Cheniere trades at a modest premium to historical averages (typically 8-12x) but below the levels implied by the structural supply thesis. The key valuation question is what forward earnings should look like given:

1. **Spot price upside:** With ~15-25% of volumes exposed to spot/short-term pricing, elevated global LNG prices could add $3-8/share in incremental annual EPS depending on duration and magnitude.
2. **New long-term contracts:** Thailand's 1.3M ton annual order and likely additional contract wins at improved pricing create multi-year earnings accretion.
3. **Stage 3 economics:** Improved contract terms for expansion capacity.

If forward EPS normalizes to $28-32 (incorporating spot tailwinds and new contracts), the stock trades at 9-10x — inexpensive for an infrastructure asset with 15-20 year contracted cash flows in a structurally undersupplied market. The 52-week low of $186.20 occurred during late 2025, before the geopolitical catalyst materialized. The stock has appreciated 24% since late February but remains ~4% below its March 19 intraday high of $299.49.

The 0.83% dividend yield is modest but supplemented by active share repurchases under the new authorization.

## Competitive Landscape

**US LNG Export Peers:**
- **Sempra Energy (SRE):** Developing Port Arthur LNG but with longer timelines and higher execution risk
- **Freeport LNG:** Smaller scale, privately held
- **Venture Global:** Pre-FID for expansion; regulatory uncertainty

Cheniere's competitive advantages are concrete:
1. **Operational scale:** Largest US LNG export capacity, fully operational
2. **Customer relationships:** Long-term contracts with investment-grade counterparties globally
3. **Reliability track record:** Record production in 2025 during a period when Qatar's facilities were destroyed
4. **Geographic advantage:** Gulf Coast location with pipeline access to prolific US shale gas basins

The destruction of Qatar's capacity and Hormuz closure effectively removed Cheniere's largest global competitor from the market. Qatar was the world's largest LNG exporter. The competitive dynamic has shifted from Cheniere competing with Qatari expansion for market share to Cheniere being one of the few reliable large-scale LNG suppliers available to global buyers.

## Risk Assessment

**Geopolitical Premium Reversal (Medium Probability, High Impact):**
If Hormuz reopens within weeks rather than months, or if Qatar's repair timeline accelerates, the supply tightness eases and the geopolitical premium in LNG's stock price deflates. The world model assigns only 3-5% probability to rapid de-escalation, but markets can reprice expectations ahead of actual resolution.

**Commodity Price Sensitivity:**
While ~75-85% of volumes are contracted, the remaining spot/short-term exposure creates earnings volatility. If global LNG prices decline from current peaks, the incremental earnings boost diminishes.

**Debt Load:**
Project-finance debt remains significant in absolute terms. Rising real yields (confirmed by the hawkish Fed posture and bond safe-haven failure) increase refinancing costs on variable-rate tranches and any near-term maturities.

**Regulatory Risk:**
US LNG export permitting has been subject to political shifts. The current administration appears supportive given geopolitical necessity, but any future permitting freeze would constrain growth.

**Execution Risk on Stage 3:**
Construction delays, cost overruns, or contractor issues could delay capacity additions. In the current inflationary environment (core PCE at 3.1%, diesel costs up sharply), capital project costs are escalating.

**Insider Activity:**

The Shear sale is small relative to his remaining 25,633 shares (~16% of position) and occurred before the March 18-19 spike, so its signal value is limited.

## Conclusion

Cheniere Energy is a BUY based on the structural thesis that the destruction of Qatar's LNG capacity and Hormuz closure have created a multi-year supply gap that cannot be filled by new infrastructure. The 11.7x forward P/E is reasonable for an asset-heavy infrastructure business with contracted cash flows, and it underprices the duration of the supply disruption (5-year Qatar repair, 6-month IEA restoration estimate for broader energy flows, 4-7 year greenfield LNG build times).

The primary tactical concern is that the stock has already moved 24% in a month, meaning some of the geopolitical premium is embedded. Position sizing should reflect this: a full position built today carries pullback risk if near-term de-escalation headlines emerge. The ideal approach is to establish a core position now and add on any pullback toward the $250-260 range, which would represent ~10x forward earnings on current estimates.

The structural supply reallocation thesis — confirmed by actual commercial contract flow (Thailand) and by the IEA's characterization of this as the largest supply disruption in history — supports multi-year outperformance. This is a high-conviction position within the energy sector reallocation framework.
