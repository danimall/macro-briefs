## Executive Summary

Amentum Holdings is a government engineering and technology solutions provider formed through the 2024 merger of Amentum (formerly AECOM's Management Services segment) and Jacobs Engineering's Critical Mission Solutions and Cyber & Intelligence businesses. The company reported FQ1 2026 revenue of $3.24B with 3% normalized growth, net income of $44M ($0.18 GAAP EPS, $0.52 adjusted EPS), and missed consensus revenue estimates of $3.3B. The stock fell ~20% following earnings on February 10, declining from $36.59 to $32.06 in a single session.

At $27.07, the stock trades at approximately 68x trailing GAAP EPS of $0.27, which is expensive by any government services peer comparison. The forward P/E of ~68x suggests limited near-term earnings expansion expectations. Revenue annualizes to roughly $13B, placing Amentum among the larger government services contractors, but the company's profitability profile appears thin — $44M net income on $3.24B quarterly revenue implies margins near 1.4%. Without access to detailed balance sheet and cash flow data, the financial health assessment carries meaningful uncertainty. The stock is a HOLD: the defense/nuclear positioning is strategically sound, but the valuation lacks margin of safety given profitability headwinds and data gaps.

## Company Overview

Amentum Holdings provides mission-critical services across defense, intelligence, nuclear, and environmental sectors. The company was created through a transformative merger in late 2024, combining Amentum's government services platform with Jacobs' critical mission and cyber/intelligence units. Headquartered in Chantilly, Virginia, the company serves U.S. and allied government customers with engineering, technology, and program management services.

Key business lines include nuclear decommissioning, intelligence community support, defense program management, environmental remediation, and IT/cyber solutions. The company recently secured a $112M nuclear decommissioning joint venture contract (announced March 10, 2026), reinforcing its position in the nuclear services market.

The stock began trading on the NYSE following the merger completion, with price history starting around $18-19 in March 2025. The 52-week range of $16.01–$38.11 reflects significant volatility characteristic of a post-merger entity still establishing its trading pattern.

- Implied GAAP net margin: ~1.4% — extremely thin for a services business

The gap between GAAP EPS ($0.18) and adjusted EPS ($0.52) is substantial — adjusted earnings are nearly 3x GAAP. This typically reflects merger-related amortization, restructuring charges, and integration costs. For a recently merged entity, some adjustment is expected, but the magnitude warrants scrutiny. The $0.27 trailing EPS provided by Yahoo Finance suggests cumulative GAAP earnings remain depressed.

Post-merger entities typically carry elevated debt from acquisition financing. The absence of this data prevents a thorough assessment of leverage, liquidity, and free cash flow generation.

The 0.51x price/revenue multiple is typical for government services contractors (peers like SAIC trade at 0.6-0.8x, Leidos at 1.2-1.5x). On an adjusted EPS basis, using a rough $2.00+ annual adjusted EPS run-rate (4 x $0.52), the forward P/E would compress to ~13-14x, which is more reasonable for the sector.

**Growth Risks:**
DOGE-related federal spending scrutiny and potential procurement delays could impact near-term booking velocity. The company acknowledged that guidance "failed to impress investors," suggesting management may be conservatively forecasting to manage expectations during the integration period.

## Risk Assessment

**High-Impact Risks:**
1. **Merger integration execution (HIGH):** Integration of two large organizations carries risk of customer disruption, talent attrition, and system consolidation challenges. The revenue miss in FQ1 may partly reflect integration friction.
2. **Federal spending uncertainty (MODERATE-HIGH):** DOGE-related spending reviews could delay contract awards or modify program scope. Continuing resolution risk affects new program starts.
3. **Debt and leverage (UNKNOWN):** Without balance sheet data, the post-merger debt load cannot be assessed. Government services mergers typically involve $3-5B+ in acquisition debt for entities this size. Interest expense in a 5%+ rate environment is a material drag on earnings.

**Moderate-Impact Risks:**
4. **Margin normalization timeline:** If merger-related charges persist longer than expected, the convergence of GAAP and adjusted EPS could be delayed, keeping the stock in valuation limbo.
5. **Customer concentration:** Government services firms typically derive 60-80% of revenue from DoD and intelligence community, creating single-customer concentration risk.
6. **Competition for cleared personnel:** Tight labor market for security-cleared workers pressures wages and constrains growth capacity.

**Macro Risks:**
The stagflationary environment identified in the world model cuts both ways for Amentum. Defense spending growth ($200B Pentagon supplemental) is a direct tailwind. However, rising rates increase debt service costs, and a potential recession could trigger bipartisan deficit reduction pressure that constrains non-emergency government spending.

## Conclusion

Amentum Holdings occupies an attractive strategic position in government services with strong exposure to defense, nuclear, and intelligence spending — all areas with structural tailwinds in the current macro environment. The $200B Pentagon supplemental directly benefits companies in this space, and nuclear decommissioning provides decades of funded work.

However, the investment case requires a leap of faith on earnings normalization. At 68x trailing GAAP EPS, the stock is pricing in substantial improvement that has yet to materialize in reported numbers. The FQ1 revenue miss and uninspiring guidance suggest the normalization timeline may be longer than originally anticipated. Without balance sheet data, the leverage profile remains opaque.

The stock has corrected meaningfully from its $38.11 high, and on an adjusted earnings basis (~13-14x), it is not expensive relative to government services peers. But the GAAP/adjusted divergence creates analytical uncertainty, and the absence of insider buying suggests management does not view current levels as a compelling entry point.

**Recommendation: HOLD.** The strategic positioning is sound, but the risk/reward at ~$27 requires more clarity on margin trajectory, debt levels, and integration progress before upgrading. A pullback toward $22-24 (where the stock found support in October-November 2025) or evidence of sequential margin improvement in FQ2 2026 results would warrant reassessment. Conversely, if adjusted EPS disappoints below $0.50/quarter, the stock likely has further downside toward $20.
