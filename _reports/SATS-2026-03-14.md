## Executive Summary

EchoStar reported FY2025 revenue of $15.0B (down from $15.83B in FY2024) and a net loss of $14.5B driven by impairments, restructuring charges, and declining subscriber counts across both pay-TV and wireless segments. KPMG, the company's auditor, issued a going-concern opinion in the 10-K filed March 2, 2026. The stock has risen from a 52-week low of $14.90 to $108.34, a 627% move driven primarily by two spectrum license sales — to AT&T and SpaceX — that injected liquidity and generated a SpaceX equity stake. The company was added to the S&P 500 on March 9, 2026.

The core tension in SATS is between the enormous value unlocked through spectrum monetization (with additional regulatory approvals pending in H1 2026) and the structural deterioration of the underlying satellite TV and wireless businesses. A $31.3B market cap on a company that lost $14.5B, carries a going-concern flag, and has declining revenue requires the spectrum/SpaceX transactions to not only close successfully but to be worth substantially more than the market currently credits. Multiple C-suite insiders sold shares at $107-115 in early March, which is a concrete negative signal given the pending deal closings.

## Company Overview

EchoStar Corporation operates satellite services, pay-TV (DISH Network), and wireless communications businesses. Following the 2024 Dish Network merger integration, the company controls significant spectrum holdings and satellite infrastructure. The strategic pivot has been to monetize spectrum licenses — selling to AT&T and exchanging with SpaceX for equity — while the legacy pay-TV and wireless subscriber bases continue declining. Charlie Ergen remains Chairman, President, and CEO, controlling the company through Class B supervoting shares.

## Financial Analysis

**Revenue:** $15.0B in FY2025 vs. $15.83B in FY2024, a 5.2% decline. The decline reflects ongoing pay-TV cord-cutting and wireless subscriber losses. The detailed income statement, balance sheet, and cash flow statements were unavailable in the provided dataset, so granular margin and leverage analysis is limited to what news sources report.

**Net Loss:** $14.5B, driven by impairments and restructuring charges. This loss is approximately equal to the entire year's revenue. While much of this is non-cash (asset write-downs), the magnitude signals that management is acknowledging the drastically diminished value of legacy assets.

**Going-Concern:** KPMG flagged substantial doubt about EchoStar's ability to continue as a going concern. This is the most severe audit opinion short of a disclaimer. It indicates that without the pending spectrum sale proceeds (expected H1 2026), the company may not be able to meet its obligations within the next 12 months.

**Liquidity Event:** Management stated during the Q4 2025 earnings call that final regulatory approvals for the spectrum sale are pending, with "material cash influx anticipated during the first half of 2026." The SpaceX equity stake acquired post-closing adds an additional asset, though its value is uncertain given SpaceX is private and illiquid.

**Insider Activity:** Three senior executives — Hamid Akhavan (CEO, EchoStar Capital), Dean Manson (CLO), and John Swieringa (President, Tech & COO) — all exercised options at $14-17/share and sold at $107-115/share in early March 2026. Akhavan sold $7.6M, Manson sold $2.2M, Swieringa sold $5.7M. These are significant dispositions occurring days after the 10-K filing that contained the going-concern warning. While exercise-and-sell transactions are common for liquidity and tax purposes, the timing and breadth across three C-suite officers is notable.

## Growth Analysis

**Legacy Businesses (Declining):** Pay-TV subscriber losses show no sign of abating. The secular cord-cutting trend is accelerating, and DISH Network's competitive position has weakened relative to streaming alternatives. The wireless business (Boost Mobile/Project Genesis) has similarly lost subscribers, and the 5G network buildout has consumed capital without generating returns commensurate with investment.

**Spectrum Monetization (One-Time):** The AT&T and SpaceX spectrum deals represent transformative liquidity events. However, they are one-time in nature. Once spectrum is sold, it cannot generate recurring revenue. The SpaceX equity stake creates exposure to SpaceX's valuation trajectory, which is arguably a growth asset but is (a) illiquid, (b) uncontrollable, and (c) impossible to value with precision.

**Satellite Services:** EchoStar's satellite broadband business (HughesNet) serves rural and underserved markets. This segment faces existential competitive pressure from SpaceX's Starlink, which offers superior performance. The irony of acquiring SpaceX equity while competing against Starlink in satellite broadband is strategically awkward.

**Growth Score Assessment:** Organic revenue growth is negative. The growth story depends entirely on the value of illiquid assets (SpaceX equity) and successful regulatory closings. There is no visible path to revenue stabilization in the core business.

## Valuation Assessment

At $108.34/share and a $31.3B market cap, SATS has no trailing P/E (massive net loss), no forward P/E available, and no dividend. The stock traded at $14.90 within the past 52 weeks. The current price embeds significant option value on the spectrum deals and SpaceX equity.

UBS raised its price target to $127 from $125 on March 3 while maintaining a Neutral rating. A Neutral rating with a $127 target on a $108 stock implies ~17% upside but insufficient conviction to recommend buying. The stock appears on lists of most-shorted names, indicating significant institutional skepticism.

S&P 500 inclusion (March 9, 2026) creates forced index buying, which likely explains some of the price appreciation. Post-inclusion, this mechanical demand fades.

Valuing SATS requires an estimate of: (1) net proceeds from spectrum sales after debt repayment, (2) value of SpaceX equity stake, (3) terminal value of declining pay-TV/satellite/wireless businesses, and (4) remaining debt load. Without detailed balance sheet data, this sum-of-parts exercise is imprecise, but the going-concern warning suggests the debt load is substantial enough to threaten solvency absent deal closings.

## Competitive Landscape

**Pay-TV:** DISH Network competes against cable providers (Comcast, Charter), telco bundles (AT&T/DirecTV), and streaming services. DISH has been losing this battle for years. Subscriber declines accelerating.

**Wireless:** Boost Mobile competes against T-Mobile, AT&T, and Verizon with a fraction of their network investment and scale. The 5G buildout has been capital-intensive with minimal subscriber traction.

**Satellite Broadband:** HughesNet competes directly against Starlink, which has superior technology, lower latency, and the marketing advantage of Elon Musk's brand. This is arguably the worst competitive position in the portfolio.

**Spectrum:** EchoStar's spectrum holdings have proven valuable as sale assets. This is the one area where the company has a genuinely differentiated position — mid-band spectrum licenses in the US are scarce and in high demand from carriers and satellite operators.

## Risk Assessment

**Going-Concern Risk (Critical):** KPMG's going-concern opinion means that without the pending spectrum sale proceeds, the company may not meet its obligations. If regulatory approvals are delayed or denied, the equity could face severe dilution or worse. This is a binary risk.

**Regulatory Risk (High):** The spectrum sale to AT&T and the SpaceX transaction both require FCC and potentially DOJ approval. In the current political environment, regulatory outcomes are somewhat unpredictable. Any delay beyond H1 2026 extends the going-concern timeline.

**Secular Decline Risk (High):** All three operating segments face structural headwinds. Revenue declined 5.2% in FY2025 and there is no evident catalyst for stabilization.

**Insider Selling (Moderate-to-High):** Three C-suite officers selling in the same week, at prices below the UBS target but well above exercise prices, suggests limited internal conviction about further upside relative to the risks.

**Concentration Risk (High):** The investment thesis depends almost entirely on two transactions (AT&T and SpaceX) closing successfully. This is extreme binary concentration.

**Short Interest (Elevated):** SATS appearing on "most shorted" lists indicates institutional skepticism that the current valuation is sustainable. High short interest can create volatility in both directions.

## Investment Thesis

### Bull Case
The spectrum sales close in H1 2026, generating sufficient cash to retire near-term debt and eliminate the going-concern flag. The SpaceX equity stake appreciates significantly as SpaceX's private valuation continues climbing (rumored $350B+ in recent secondary transactions). Post-deleveraging, the remaining satellite and media assets generate enough cash flow to sustain the business while management pursues further strategic alternatives. S&P 500 inclusion creates a permanent valuation floor through index demand. Price target in this scenario: $120-140.

### Bear Case
Regulatory delays push spectrum sale closings beyond H1 2026, forcing the company to seek emergency financing at dilutive terms. The going-concern risk materializes into actual covenant violations or liquidity shortfalls. Pay-TV and wireless subscriber losses accelerate, further eroding operating cash flow. The SpaceX equity stake proves illiquid and cannot be monetized at the needed time. Short sellers are vindicated as the stock reverts toward intrinsic value of declining legacy businesses minus substantial debt. Price target in this scenario: $30-50.

## Conclusion

SATS at $108 is a binary bet on spectrum transaction closings with a going-concern flag from the auditor and insiders selling at current prices. The underlying businesses are in secular decline. Revenue fell 5.2% to $15B while the company lost $14.5B. The S&P 500 inclusion provides temporary technical support but does not change the fundamental picture.

Per the calibration framework, stocks with scores at or below 5.0 carry a presumptive AVOID unless a specific, documented catalyst for reversal exists. The pending spectrum closings are that potential catalyst, but they are binary and uncertain — exactly the kind of event where the market has already priced in a favorable outcome (stock up 627% from lows) while the downside from failure is catastrophic (going-concern). The insider selling pattern within days of the going-concern disclosure tips the balance. **AVOID.**

Investors with high risk tolerance who believe the regulatory closings are near-certain may find the bull case compelling, but the risk-reward at $108 with a going-concern flag is asymmetrically skewed to the downside for most portfolio contexts.
