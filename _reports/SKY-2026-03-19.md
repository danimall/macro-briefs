## Company Overview

Champion Homes (formerly Skyline Champion) is one of the largest manufactured and modular home builders in the United States and western Canada. The company operates across multiple manufacturing facilities, producing factory-built housing that serves both the single-family and multi-family markets. It files annual 10-Ks on a late-May cycle (fiscal year ending around March/April) and has a consistent SEC filing history dating back over a decade. The most recent 10-Q was filed February 4, 2026, covering the quarter ended December 27, 2025.

Champion's position in manufactured housing gives it structural exposure to the affordable housing theme — factory-built homes typically cost 10-35% less per square foot than site-built equivalents. However, the company remains fundamentally a housing business, with all the interest rate sensitivity that implies.

## Competitive Landscape

Champion Homes competes primarily with Cavco Industries (CVCO) and Clayton Homes (Berkshire Hathaway subsidiary) in the manufactured housing space. The industry is moderately concentrated, with the top three players controlling a significant share of production.

Manufactured housing has a structural advantage: factory production is more efficient, less weather-dependent, and produces homes at lower cost per square foot. However, the industry faces persistent stigma, zoning restrictions, and financing challenges (chattel loans vs. traditional mortgages carry higher rates).

Champion's competitive position is adequate but not moat-like. The barriers to entry in manufactured housing are lower than in many industries — the manufacturing process is relatively standardized, and brand differentiation is limited. Switching costs for consumers are minimal.

## Risk Assessment

**Primary Risks:**

1. **Interest Rate Sensitivity (HIGH):** The Fed is now discussing rate hikes. Every 100bps increase in mortgage rates reduces housing affordability by approximately 10-12%. Manufactured housing is somewhat insulated (more buyers use chattel loans or cash), but not immune.

2. **Macro/Recession Risk (HIGH):** At 40-45% recession probability, housing is among the most cyclically exposed sectors. NFP at -92K and consumer spending declining create a directly hostile demand environment.

3. **Input Cost Inflation (MODERATE-HIGH):** Oil at $119 drives up transportation costs (manufactured homes must be shipped from factory to site), material costs (petroleum-based products, adhesives, insulation), and overall logistics expense.

4. **Valuation Compression Risk (MODERATE):** At 20x earnings, the stock carries a premium to traditional homebuilders that is vulnerable in a risk-off environment. If earnings decline 15-20% in a recession and the multiple compresses to 15x, downside to ~$43-49 is plausible.

5. **Limited Financial Transparency (MODERATE):** The absence of detailed financial statement data in this analysis makes it impossible to assess leverage, working capital trends, or margin trajectory with precision.

6. **Insider Selling (MODERATE):** Net insider selling since August 2025, with multiple executives disposing shares at $82-88, above current price. No open-market insider purchases observed.
