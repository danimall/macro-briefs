## Executive Summary

S&P Global has declined ~25% from its August 2025 all-time high of $579.05 to ~$433, driven by a combination of below-consensus 2026 EPS guidance ($19.40-$19.65 vs. $19.96 consensus) and broader market stress tied to the Iran conflict, oil shock, and credit market uncertainty. The stock now trades at approximately 21-22x forward earnings, its cheapest level in five years against a trailing five-year average near 30x. This compression reflects legitimate near-term concerns about debt issuance volumes in a rising-rate, stagflationary environment, but likely overweights cyclical headwinds relative to the structural durability of SPGI's franchise.

The company operates what amounts to a regulated duopoly in credit ratings (with Moody's), a monopoly-adjacent position in index licensing (S&P Dow Jones Indices), and a critical data/analytics infrastructure business post-IHS Markit merger. These businesses generate 39% free cash flow margins and carry pricing power that persists across economic cycles. The current macro regime — confirmed stagflation with a 55-60% credit cascade probability and 38-42% recession probability — creates genuine near-term risk to the Ratings segment, which is the most cyclically sensitive division. But the Market Intelligence, Indices, and Commodity Insights segments provide recurring revenue ballast, and any normalization of credit markets would be a powerful catalyst.

At 21x forward earnings, SPGI offers a rare opportunity to buy a structurally dominant franchise at a cyclical discount. The risk/reward skews positive on a 12-18 month horizon, though position sizing should account for the possibility that credit market stress extends through 2026.

## Company Overview

S&P Global operates across five segments: **Market Intelligence** (financial data, analytics, and workflow tools), **Ratings** (credit ratings for debt issuers), **S&P Dow Jones Indices** (index licensing, including the S&P 500), **Commodity Insights** (energy and commodity data, formerly Platts), and **Mobility** (automotive data and analytics, from the IHS Markit merger). The 2022 IHS Markit merger roughly doubled the company's revenue base and added significant commodity and automotive analytics capabilities.

The company's market cap stands at approximately $127B on ~299M diluted shares, with trailing EPS of $14.66. The forward P/E of ~29.5 (per Yahoo Finance data) appears to use the trailing EPS; using the guided 2026 EPS midpoint of ~$19.50, the forward multiple is closer to 21-22x.

## Growth Analysis

**Organic Growth Drivers:**
1. **Pricing power across all segments.** Credit ratings carry near-inelastic demand — issuers need ratings to access capital markets. Index licensing fees grow with AUM tracking S&P indices. Commodity benchmarks (Platts pricing) are embedded in physical commodity contracts.
2. **AI integration.** The Enertel AI acquisition (March 2026) adds real-time AI-powered nodal power price forecasting. This fits within SPGI's broader strategy of embedding AI into data products to increase customer stickiness and justify pricing increases.
3. **IHS Markit synergies.** The merger created cross-selling opportunities across data, analytics, and workflow tools. Synergy realization is still ongoing, with cost and revenue synergies likely contributing to the EPS growth bridge.

**Cyclical Sensitivity:** The Ratings segment is the key swing factor. Debt issuance volumes correlate with credit spreads and market conditions. In the current environment — oil at $98-108, bond safe-haven status compromised, credit cascade probability at 55-60% — issuance volumes face headwinds. If EA's $18B junk bond offering this week prices poorly or credit spreads widen further, Ratings revenue would be directly impacted. Conversely, any normalization of credit conditions would drive significant upside to Ratings revenue.

**Long-term structural tailwind:** Global capital markets continue to grow in complexity and data requirements. Regulatory mandates for credit ratings in fixed income persist. The shift toward passive investing (which relies on S&P indices) continues to drive index licensing revenue.

## Competitive Landscape

SPGI's competitive position is among the strongest in global financial services:

1. **Ratings:** Oligopoly with Moody's (MCO). Together they control ~80% of the global credit ratings market. Fitch is a distant third. Regulatory barriers to entry are enormous (NRSRO designation), and switching costs for issuers are effectively infinite — you need ratings from both S&P and Moody's to access capital markets.

2. **Indices:** The S&P 500 is the single most important equity benchmark globally. Over $16 trillion in assets are benchmarked to S&P indices. Licensing fees are a high-margin, recurring revenue stream that grows with global AUM.

3. **Market Intelligence:** Competes with Bloomberg, Refinitiv (LSEG), and FactSet. The IHS Markit merger strengthened this segment significantly. While competitive, the data business benefits from high switching costs and workflow integration.

4. **Commodity Insights:** Platts benchmarks are the reference prices for physical commodity trading globally. This creates an entrenched position analogous to the Ratings oligopoly.

5. **Mobility:** IHS Markit's automotive analytics (VIN data, registration data) serve OEMs, dealers, and insurers. Moderate moat, less dominant than Ratings or Indices.

## Risk Assessment

**Near-term risks (elevated):**
1. **Credit market stress.** The 55-60% credit cascade probability directly threatens Ratings issuance volumes. If credit spreads blow out, debt issuance freezes, and SPGI's most cyclically sensitive segment suffers. The March 31 quarter-end stress point adds urgency.
2. **Bond market dysfunction.** Treasuries losing safe-haven status means higher rates for longer, which suppresses issuance activity and increases refinancing costs for leveraged issuers.
3. **Recession probability at 38-42%.** A recession would reduce capital markets activity broadly, impacting Ratings, Market Intelligence terminal count, and potentially index AUM (though passive flows tend to be resilient).

**Structural risks (moderate):**
4. **AI disruption to data business.** Barclays noted AI has "exacerbated investor concerns" about SPGI's data moat. If LLMs can replicate some of the analytical value that Market Intelligence provides, subscription pricing could face pressure. This risk is real but likely overstated in the near term — SPGI's data is proprietary and feeds into regulated workflows.
5. **Regulatory risk.** Any changes to credit rating requirements in securities regulation would threaten the Ratings oligopoly. This has been discussed for decades without meaningful action.
6. **Concentration risk in Ratings.** Ratings is the highest-margin segment and the most cyclically volatile. A prolonged credit freeze would disproportionately impact earnings.

**Mitigants:**
- Diversification across five segments means Ratings weakness is partially offset by more stable segments.
- 39% FCF margins provide significant financial flexibility.
- Dividend King status and share repurchases provide downside support.
- The Enertel AI acquisition signals proactive positioning on the AI front.
