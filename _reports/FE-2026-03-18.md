## Executive Summary

FirstEnergy reported FY2025 GAAP earnings of $1.02 billion ($1.76 diluted EPS) on $15.1 billion in revenue, up from $978 million ($1.70 EPS) in 2024. The company reaffirmed 2026 core EPS guidance and presented a long-term outlook anchored by a $36 billion capital expenditure plan through 2030, positioning itself to benefit from data center-driven electricity demand growth. Multiple sell-side firms raised price targets post-earnings: Scotiabank to $56 (Outperform), Morgan Stanley to $53 (Overweight), and BofA to $52.

The stock has rallied 28.6% over the trailing twelve months, moving from ~$39.83 to $51.21. At 29.1x trailing and forward EPS, the valuation is stretched relative to regulated utility peers (typically 17-22x). The dividend yield of ~3.6% provides income support but is below the utility sector average of ~4%. CFO K. Jon Taylor sold 26,800 shares ($1.37M) on March 10 at $50.94 — an open-market disposal that, while modest relative to his 119,552-share remaining position, is a cautionary signal at this price level. The stock is now trading within $1 of the BofA price target and near its 52-week high of $52.02.

## Company Overview

FirstEnergy is a regulated electric utility headquartered in Akron, Ohio, operating transmission and distribution infrastructure across Ohio, Pennsylvania, West Virginia, New Jersey, and Maryland. The company serves approximately 6 million customers through 10 regulated distribution companies and a transmission segment. Following the resolution of the HB6 bribery scandal and associated regulatory penalties, FE has been executing a turnaround focused on regulated investment, grid modernization, and positioning for load growth from data centers and electrification.

## Financial Analysis

**Revenue & Earnings:** FY2025 revenue of $15.1 billion with GAAP earnings of $1.02 billion ($1.76 diluted EPS). This represents a 4.1% YoY increase in EPS from $1.70. Q4 2025 was weaker, reporting a GAAP loss of $49 million (-$0.08 per share), though adjusted Q4 EPS was $0.53, matching consensus estimates. The Q4 loss included impacts from Ohio regulatory orders, suggesting one-time charges rather than operational deterioration.

**Balance Sheet:** Detailed balance sheet data was not available in the financial statements feed. However, FirstEnergy's regulated utility model implies significant leverage — the $36B capex plan through 2030 will require ongoing debt issuance and potentially equity raises. The company's investment-grade credit ratings are critical to funding this plan at reasonable cost. In the current environment (mortgage rates at 7-month highs, bond yields elevated), the incremental cost of capital for utilities is rising.

**Capital Allocation:** The $36B capex plan ($6B+ annually) is aggressive for a company with $15.1B in revenue. This spending is directed at transmission upgrades, distribution modernization, and capacity additions to serve data center demand. The dividend (~$1.83 annually, ~3.6% yield) consumes a meaningful portion of earnings ($1.76 EPS), implying a payout ratio near or above 100% on a GAAP basis for FY2025. This tight coverage is typical for utilities funding large capex programs but leaves minimal buffer.

**Cash Flow:** Without explicit cash flow statements, the gap between GAAP EPS ($1.76) and the reported Q4 adjusted figure ($0.53) suggests meaningful non-cash and regulatory adjustments. Free cash flow after capex is likely negative given the scale of the investment program, requiring external financing.

## Growth Analysis

FirstEnergy's growth thesis rests on three pillars:

1. **Data center demand:** The Seeking Alpha coverage explicitly positions FE as an AI-driven electric demand beneficiary. PJM Interconnection (FE's grid operator) has seen significant interconnection queue growth from hyperscaler data centers, particularly in Ohio and Pennsylvania. This provides visible, contracted load growth that supports rate base expansion.

2. **Rate base growth:** The $36B capex plan translates to high-single-digit rate base growth annually. Regulated utilities earn allowed returns on invested capital, so rate base growth directly drives earnings growth — assuming regulatory commissions approve cost recovery.

3. **Regulatory execution:** FE's post-scandal regulatory relationships are improving but remain a risk factor. Ohio regulatory orders impacted Q4 results. The company's ability to recover capital investments through rates is the binding constraint on whether capex translates to earnings.

Management reaffirmed 2026 core EPS guidance, which analysts estimate in the $2.40-2.60 range based on the forward P/E of 29.1x and current price. This implies ~36-48% core EPS growth from FY2025 GAAP of $1.76, though the gap between GAAP and core figures suggests significant adjustments. If core 2026 EPS is ~$1.75 (using $50.96 / 29.1), the growth profile is more modest.

## Valuation Assessment

At $51.21 and 29.1x trailing P/E, FirstEnergy trades at a significant premium to the regulated utility sector. Typical regulated utility peers trade at 17-22x earnings. Even accounting for the data center demand tailwind, this premium is difficult to justify:

- **BofA target: $52** (raised from $49 on March 5) — stock is within 2% of this target
- **Morgan Stanley target: $53** (raised from $50 on February 20) — 3.5% upside
- **Scotiabank target: $56** (raised from $55 on February 19) — 9.4% upside

The dividend yield of 3.6% is below the utility sector average (~4-4.5% for regulated peers). On an EV/EBITDA basis, without explicit EBITDA data, the market cap of $29.5B plus likely net debt of $20B+ implies an enterprise value of ~$50B on $15.1B revenue — an EV/Revenue multiple of ~3.3x, which is elevated for a regulated utility.

The stock has appreciated 28.6% in twelve months, which for a utility is an exceptional move. Most of this has come since late September 2025, when FE broke above $45 and subsequently accelerated through $50 post-Q4 earnings. The rally has compressed the forward return profile considerably.

## Competitive Landscape

FirstEnergy operates in PJM territory, which is the epicenter of US data center growth. This is a genuine competitive advantage — peers without PJM exposure (Southern Company, Entergy, Xcel) cannot directly benefit from this demand wave. Within PJM, FE competes with AEP, PPL, and Exelon for data center interconnections.

The regulated utility model provides a natural moat: franchise territories are legally protected, and new entrants face prohibitive capital and regulatory barriers. FE's transmission assets are particularly valuable given the need for grid upgrades to support incremental data center load.

The post-HB6 overhang has largely dissipated, but FE's regulatory relationships in Ohio remain more fragile than peers who haven't faced bribery scandals. Ohio commission behavior on rate cases is the key variable.

## Risk Assessment

**Valuation risk (PRIMARY):** At 29x earnings and within 2% of the BofA price target, the stock prices in substantial growth that has yet to materialize in GAAP earnings. Any disappointment in regulatory outcomes, data center interconnection timelines, or capital plan execution could trigger a correction to more typical utility multiples.

**Insider selling:** CFO K. Jon Taylor sold 26,800 shares at $50.94 on March 10 for $1.37M. Director James O'Neil sold 7,945 shares at $50.60 on March 11 for $402K — notably reducing his position to just 1,869 shares (disposing of 81% of his holdings). While the March 1 activity was largely RSU vestings with tax withholding (routine), the subsequent CFO open-market sale and director near-complete liquidation at near-52-week highs carry signal value.

**Interest rate sensitivity:** Utilities are bond proxies. In the current macro environment — confirmed stagflation, bond safe-haven failure, mortgage rates at 7-month highs — regulated utilities face headwinds from both rising cost of capital and compressed equity risk premiums.

**Regulatory risk:** Ohio regulatory orders already impacted Q4 2025 results. The $36B capex plan assumes constructive regulatory treatment across multiple state jurisdictions. Any adverse rate case outcome could materially impair the return profile.

**Capex execution risk:** $36B through 2030 is substantial. Supply chain constraints, labor availability, and permitting delays could slow rate base growth and defer earnings accretion.

**Macro headwinds:** Stagflationary environment raises input costs (materials, labor) while potentially compressing allowed returns on equity if commissions resist rate increases during periods of consumer stress.

## Investment Thesis

### Bull Case
- Data center demand in PJM territory provides visible, contracted load growth for the next 5+ years
- $36B capex plan drives high-single-digit rate base growth, translating to 6-8% EPS CAGR if regulatory recovery is achieved
- Post-scandal management turnaround under Tierney is executing well; 2025 results met expectations
- Scotiabank $56 target implies 9.4% upside plus 3.6% dividend yield = ~13% total return
- Defensive sector positioning benefits from stagflationary macro (utilities historically outperform in slowdowns)

### Bear Case
- 29x P/E is 35-50% above typical regulated utility multiples; stock has already priced in the data center thesis
- CFO and director selling at 52-week highs suggests insiders see limited further upside at current prices
- Director O'Neil liquidated 81% of holdings — the most bearish insider signal in the dataset
- Rising cost of capital directly compresses utility valuations and increases financing costs for the $36B capex plan
- Ohio regulatory friction (evidenced by Q4 charge) could worsen, particularly in a consumer-stressed environment
- Dividend payout ratio near 100% of GAAP earnings leaves minimal safety margin
- GAAP Q4 loss of $0.08 vs. adjusted $0.53 suggests earnings quality questions

## Conclusion

FirstEnergy's fundamental positioning is sound: PJM territory data center demand, a credible capex plan, and improving post-scandal execution. The problem is price. At $51.21, the stock trades at 29x earnings, near its 52-week high, and within 2% of BofA's price target. The CFO's open-market sale and a director's near-complete liquidation at these levels reinforce the view that the easy money has been made.

For new capital, the risk-reward is unfavorable. A pullback to $44-46 (22-25x earnings) would provide a more appropriate entry point, aligning the valuation with the growth profile and offering a more substantial dividend yield buffer. At current prices, the stock is a **HOLD** — the underlying business merits exposure, but the valuation provides insufficient margin of safety for new purchases, particularly in a macro environment where utility cost of capital is rising and insider selling is accelerating near highs.
