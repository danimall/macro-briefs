## Executive Summary

CoStar Group reported FY2025 revenue of approximately $3.2B (up 19% YoY), Q4 revenue of $900M (up 27% YoY), and adjusted EBITDA of $442M (up 83% YoY). These topline and adjusted metrics demonstrate meaningful operating leverage as the company moderates its Homes.com investment spending. However, GAAP net income for FY2025 was only $7M — essentially breakeven on $3.2B of revenue — and Q4 GAAP net income of $47M was actually down from $60M in Q4 2024 despite the 27% revenue increase. The stock at $43.64 trades at roughly 2,600x trailing GAAP earnings, a valuation that requires sustained acceleration of profitability over multiple years to justify.

The company's competitive position in commercial real estate information services (CoStar, LoopNet, Apartments.com) remains strong, with high switching costs and deep data moats. The strategic question is whether Homes.com can replicate that success in residential real estate against Zillow and Realtor.com. Management's decision to restructure reporting segments in a way that eliminates standalone Homes.com financials triggered a public campaign from D.E. Shaw, a sophisticated institutional investor, who characterized the move as reducing transparency at a critical juncture. This governance concern, combined with the extreme valuation and macro headwinds to commercial real estate transaction volumes, places CSGP in HOLD territory.

## Company Overview

CoStar Group provides information, analytics, and online marketplace services to real estate and adjacent industries. Its portfolio includes CoStar (commercial real estate data/analytics), LoopNet (commercial property listings), Apartments.com (multifamily listings), and Homes.com (residential listings, the primary growth investment). The company has a market capitalization of approximately $18.3B. CEO Andrew Florance founded the company and holds approximately 1.65M shares (~$72M at current price).

2026 revenue guidance of $3.78B-$3.82B implies approximately 18-19% growth, roughly in line with FY2025's pace.

## Financial Analysis

**Revenue:** Q4 2025 revenue of $900M (+27% YoY from $709M) implies FY2025 revenue of approximately $3.2B. FY2025 revenue growth was 19% YoY. The Q4 acceleration to 27% suggests Homes.com is beginning to contribute meaningfully alongside continued strength in commercial and multifamily segments.

**Profitability:** This is where the story gets complicated.
- FY2025 GAAP net income: $7M on ~$3.2B revenue — a 0.2% net margin
- Q4 2025 GAAP net income: $47M (down from $60M in Q4 2024)
- Q4 2025 adjusted net income: $131M (+24% YoY)
- Q4 2025 adjusted EPS: $0.31 (+19% YoY), beat consensus by 13.8%
- FY2025 adjusted EBITDA: $442M (+83% YoY)
- Q4 2025 adjusted EBITDA: $177M (+58% YoY)

The wide gap between GAAP and adjusted figures — $47M vs. $131M in Q4 alone — reflects stock-based compensation and other adjustments. The 83% EBITDA growth indicates the company is pulling back on investment spending (primarily Homes.com marketing), but the near-zero GAAP full-year income means shareholders have been subsidizing this growth investment with minimal bottom-line return.

**Balance Sheet:** Detailed balance sheet data was not available in the dataset, but the company authorized $1.5B in share repurchases (announced February 2026, with $700M planned for 2026), suggesting the balance sheet has sufficient capacity. This is a meaningful positive signal — $700M represents roughly 3.8% of the current market cap.

**Cash Flow:** Not directly available, but adjusted EBITDA of $442M provides a rough ceiling for operating cash flow before working capital and capex. The buyback authorization implies positive FCF generation.

## Growth Analysis

**Revenue Growth Trajectory:**
- FY2025: +19% YoY
- Q4 2025: +27% YoY (acceleration)
- FY2026 guidance: $3.78B-$3.82B (+18-19% YoY)
- Record net new bookings of $308M in FY2025

The 19% revenue growth is strong for a company at CoStar's scale. The record bookings figure of $308M provides forward visibility, as bookings convert to recurring subscription revenue over time.

**Homes.com:** Management described FY2025 as capping the national brand launch investment phase, with FY2026 expected to bring "meaningful margin expansion" as investment levels moderate. The critical uncertainty is whether Homes.com can achieve the traffic and agent adoption needed to compete with Zillow (which has ~5x the residential traffic). Without standalone Homes.com disclosure going forward, investors cannot independently verify this trajectory.

**Margin Expansion Path:** The 83% adjusted EBITDA growth on 19% revenue growth implies significant operating leverage as Homes.com marketing spend moderates. If revenue grows 19% to ~$3.8B in 2026 and EBITDA margins expand from ~14% (FY2025) toward 18-20%, adjusted EBITDA could reach $680-$760M. This would still leave GAAP net income well below levels that justify the current market cap at reasonable multiples.

## Valuation Assessment

At $43.64 per share:
- Market cap: ~$18.3B
- Trailing P/E (GAAP): ~2,617x (on ~$7M FY2025 net income)
- Forward P/E: ~2,617x (per Finnhub, likely using trailing figures as proxy)
- Price/Sales (FY2025): ~5.7x
- EV/Adjusted EBITDA (FY2025): ~41x (assuming minimal net debt)

The trailing P/E is essentially meaningless due to the near-zero GAAP income. The relevant valuation metrics are Price/Sales at 5.7x and EV/EBITDA at ~41x. For context, Zillow (ZG) trades at roughly 8-10x sales but with a more established residential franchise. At 5.7x sales, CoStar needs to demonstrate a path to 20%+ EBITDA margins to justify even current levels on a growth-adjusted basis.

**Buyback Support:** The $700M 2026 buyback at current prices would retire approximately 16M shares (~3.8% of shares outstanding), providing meaningful EPS accretion and a price floor. This is the strongest near-term valuation support.

The stock has declined 55% from its 52-week high of $97.43. While this decline reflects legitimate concerns (Homes.com ROI uncertainty, reduced transparency, CRE macro headwinds), the magnitude creates a potential entry point if the margin expansion thesis materializes.

## Competitive Landscape

**Commercial Real Estate (CoStar, LoopNet):** CoStar's competitive position in CRE data is genuinely strong. The company has spent 30+ years building proprietary databases with high switching costs. This segment generates the vast majority of profits and has defensible recurring revenue characteristics.

**Multifamily (Apartments.com):** Market-leading position in apartment listings. Strong competitive moat with established brand and network effects.

**Residential (Homes.com):** This is the contested territory. Zillow dominates residential with ~200M monthly unique visitors. Homes.com is attempting to differentiate by providing leads directly to listing agents rather than selling leads to buyer's agents (Zillow's model). The agent-friendly model has theoretical appeal but requires massive traffic to monetize. The Super Bowl advertising spend and national brand launch were meant to close the traffic gap, but standalone metrics are no longer being disclosed — a critical information gap.

## Risk Assessment

**1. Homes.com Execution Risk (HIGH).** The company has invested hundreds of millions in Homes.com with no clear evidence of sustainable competitive advantage against Zillow. The removal of standalone segment reporting makes this risk unmonitorable by external investors.

**2. Transparency/Governance Risk (ELEVATED).** D.E. Shaw's public campaign is significant. When a sophisticated multi-strategy fund publicly criticizes reporting changes, the market interprets this as a signal that management may be obscuring deteriorating unit economics. The stock dropped meaningfully on D.E. Shaw's second letter (March 10). This overhang will persist until either Homes.com results are re-disclosed or the unit clearly inflects.

**3. Commercial Real Estate Macro (MODERATE).** Stagflation — rising rates, slowing growth — directly pressures CRE transaction volumes and property values. CoStar's subscription model provides some insulation (clients need data regardless of market direction), but new sales growth in CRE is tied to market activity levels.

**4. Valuation Risk (HIGH).** At 2,600x trailing earnings, the stock has virtually no margin of safety against execution disappointment. Even on an adjusted basis, 41x EBITDA requires consistent 20%+ revenue growth and margin expansion to grow into.

**5. Key-Man Risk.** Andrew Florance has been CEO since founding. His vision drives strategy, including the controversial Homes.com bet. His 1.65M-share stake provides alignment but also concentration of decision-making authority.

**6. Macro Sensitivity.** Beta of 0.89 understates the true cyclical sensitivity. CRE information services are counter-cyclical in some dimensions (research demand rises during downturns) but pro-cyclical in others (new subscription sales correlate with transaction volumes and new development activity).

## Insider Activity

Recent insider transactions are primarily equity compensation grants and related tax withholdings — standard annual compensation events. The one notable open-market purchase was Director Louise Sams buying 1,000 shares at $48.36 on March 6, a modest but positive signal. No open-market selling was observed, which is constructive given the stock's 55% decline from highs.

CEO Florance's tax withholding disposal of ~60K shares at $44.63 ($2.66M) was mandatory for tax purposes and not a discretionary sell signal.

## Investment Thesis

**Bull Case:**
- The Homes.com investment phase is peaking, and FY2026-2027 will show rapid margin expansion as marketing spend moderates while revenue continues growing 18-20%. If EBITDA margins reach 25% on $4.5B+ revenue by 2027, adjusted EBITDA of $1.1B+ would make the current $18B market cap look reasonable at ~16x forward EBITDA.
- The $1.5B buyback authorization ($700M in 2026) provides meaningful support at current depressed levels.
- The CRE data franchise remains a high-quality, recurring-revenue asset with limited competition.
- The stock is 55% off highs, pricing in significant pessimism that may overshoot if Homes.com shows even moderate traction.

**Bear Case:**
- Homes.com fails to achieve critical mass against Zillow, and the hundreds of millions invested represent permanent value destruction. Without segment disclosure, investors won't see the deterioration until it's obvious in consolidated results.
- D.E. Shaw's campaign could attract additional activist attention, distracting management and potentially forcing strategic changes that acknowledge sunk costs.
- CRE transaction volumes decline further in a stagflationary environment, pressuring the core business growth rate.
- At 2,600x GAAP earnings, even a modest disappointment in the margin expansion trajectory could send the stock materially lower.
- Management's choice to reduce transparency at precisely the moment investors most need it signals either deteriorating Homes.com economics or poor governance judgment — both negative.

## Conclusion

CoStar Group has a genuinely strong competitive position in commercial real estate data with a business model that generates real economic value. The growth trajectory is solid at 19% revenue growth with accelerating EBITDA. The $1.5B buyback provides a valuation floor. However, the near-zero GAAP profitability, extreme valuation multiples, reduced financial transparency at a critical moment, and active investor dissent from D.E. Shaw create a risk profile that prevents a BUY recommendation at current levels.

The critical question is whether Homes.com's economics justify continued investment. D.E. Shaw's concern — that management eliminated the only way external investors could evaluate this question — is legitimate and unresolved. The stock is cheap relative to its own history but expensive relative to demonstrated earnings power. The appropriate stance is HOLD: the downside scenarios (Homes.com failure + continued low profitability + governance concerns) are sufficiently probable to offset the upside from margin expansion, and the current information asymmetry (management knows Homes.com's trajectory, investors do not) makes conviction impossible in either direction.

For this to become a BUY, I would need: (1) re-disclosure of Homes.com standalone economics showing positive unit economics trajectory, or (2) two consecutive quarters of GAAP net income exceeding $100M demonstrating the margin expansion thesis is real, or (3) a price decline to ~$35-38 (4x sales) that provides sufficient margin of safety against continued uncertainty.
