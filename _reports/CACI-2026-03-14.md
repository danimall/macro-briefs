## Executive Summary

CACI International is a ~$13.4B market cap defense and national security IT services company with approximately 26,000 employees, trading at $606.72 as of March 14, 2026. The company occupies a strong position within the defense technology ecosystem, providing intelligence, cyber, electronic warfare, and enterprise IT solutions to the U.S. government. It has been named a Fortune World's Most Admired Company for nine consecutive years and maintains Truist Financial's Buy rating as of February 2026.

The macro backdrop is exceptionally favorable: active US military engagement in the Middle East, Trump's rejection of ceasefire negotiations, confirmed munitions depletion requiring multi-year restocking, and bipartisan support for elevated defense budgets. CACI's $500M senior note offering (6.375%, due 2033) closed on March 12, 2026, suggesting the company is raising capital to fund acquisitions or organic growth initiatives. However, at ~26x trailing earnings with a 0.58 beta, much of this tailwind appears reflected in the stock price, which has appreciated 70% from its 52-week low of $356.77.

The limited financial statement data available prevents a precise assessment of balance sheet leverage post-issuance, margin trajectory, and free cash flow conversion. This data gap, combined with a valuation that leaves limited margin of safety, supports a HOLD recommendation rather than an active BUY despite the strong sector tailwinds.

## Company Overview

CACI International provides technology and expertise to U.S. defense, intelligence, and federal civilian agencies. The company operates in two primary segments: (1) Expertise (professional services, consulting, operational support) and (2) Technology (software development, systems engineering, cyber operations, electronic warfare). With ~26,000 employees and headquarters in Reston, VA, CACI competes with Leidos (LDOS), Booz Allen Hamilton (BAH), SAIC, ManTech (acquired by Carlyle), and L3Harris in the government IT services space.

CFO Jeff MacLauchlan's comments at the Cantor Global Technology & Industrial Growth Conference (March 10, 2026) referenced a deliberate strategic repositioning over the past decade toward higher-growth, higher-margin technology areas within national security. This shift from legacy IT services toward differentiated technology solutions (cyber, electronic warfare, signals intelligence) aligns with where incremental defense spending is concentrated.

## Growth Analysis

**Structural Tailwinds (Multiple Independent Confirmations):**

1. **Active US military conflict with Iran** — Trump rejected ceasefire, Kharg Island struck, $55B/month war cost run rate. CACI's intelligence, cyber, and electronic warfare capabilities are directly relevant to active operations.

2. **Munitions depletion and restocking cycle** — While CACI doesn't manufacture munitions, the C4ISR (command, control, communications, computers, intelligence, surveillance, reconnaissance) systems that guide precision weapons require parallel investment. A 2-3 year restocking cycle benefits the technology infrastructure supporting those weapons.

3. **Defense spending baseline increasing** — Bipartisan support for elevated defense budgets, with cyber and electronic warfare growing faster than overall defense topline.

4. **Section 301 trade probes across 60 economies** — Increased geopolitical complexity raises demand for intelligence and signals processing capabilities.

5. **Strategic repositioning toward technology** — CFO MacLauchlan's conference comments indicate deliberate movement toward higher-margin technology work, which should improve mix over time.

**Growth Constraints:**
- Government IT services companies are structurally limited by contract award cycles and budget appropriation timelines
- DOGE/government efficiency initiatives could create near-term headwinds on some civilian contracts, though defense spending appears ring-fenced
- Competition for cleared technical talent remains intense, limiting organic growth rates

## Valuation Assessment

At 25.85x trailing and 26.81x forward earnings, CACI trades at a premium to historical government IT services multiples (typically 15-20x) but in line with the current elevated defense sector valuations. Peer comparison:

- Booz Allen Hamilton (BAH): ~26-28x forward P/E
- Leidos (LDOS): ~18-20x forward P/E
- SAIC: ~11x trailing P/E (but with operational headwinds)

CACI's premium over SAIC and Leidos reflects its technology mix advantage and stronger organic growth profile. The premium over historical norms reflects the active conflict environment and multi-year spending cycle.

The stock has appreciated from $356.77 (52-week low) to $606.72, a 70% move. At current levels, the valuation embeds significant optimism about both revenue growth and margin expansion. A reversion to 20x earnings on ~$520M trailing implies a price of ~$470, representing 23% downside. Sustaining 26x requires continued conflict-driven demand acceleration and evidence of margin expansion from the technology pivot.

The $500M debt issuance at 6.375% adds leverage that, absent corresponding revenue growth from acquisitions, dilutes return on equity. The coupon rate implies CACI is paying a meaningful spread over Treasuries.

## Competitive Landscape

CACI occupies a strong position within the defense IT services market:

**Competitive Advantages:**
- Nine consecutive years on Fortune's Most Admired Companies list — indicates employee retention and client satisfaction
- Deep domain expertise in intelligence community and special operations
- Technology pivot differentiates from pure-play IT services competitors
- ~26,000 cleared employees represent a significant barrier to entry (security clearance processing takes 12-18 months)
- Long-term contract structures provide revenue visibility

**Competitive Risks:**

- Palantir (PLTR) is disrupting traditional government analytics with AI-native platforms
- Large primes (LMT, RTX, NOC) competing for systems integration work that overlaps with CACI's technology segment
- M&A competition intense — smaller defense tech companies being acquired at premium valuations

## Risk Assessment

**Positive Risk Factors (Low Beta, Defense Exposure):**
- 0.58 beta provides genuine portfolio diversification during equity drawdowns
- Government contracts provide 12-24 month revenue visibility
- Active conflict extends demand runway for intelligence and cyber capabilities
- Bipartisan defense spending support limits political risk relative to civilian agencies

**Negative Risk Factors:**
- **Debt load increasing:** $500M issuance at 6.375% adds meaningful leverage without visibility into total debt stack
- **Government efficiency risk:** While defense appears protected, any budget sequestration or continuing resolution environment creates award delays
- **Talent competition:** Cleared workforce is scarce; wage inflation in cleared technical roles compresses margins
- **Valuation risk:** At 26x earnings, the stock is priced for continued above-trend growth; any revenue miss or margin compression would result in multiple contraction
- **Insider selling:** EVP/General Counsel sold $1.23M of stock on February 10 and Director Plunkett sold $133K on March 11. Both transactions are modest relative to remaining holdings (Koegel retains 25,931 shares worth ~$15.7M) but the pattern is net selling, not buying

**Concentration Risk:** Revenue is heavily concentrated in the U.S. federal government, with particular exposure to DoD and intelligence community budgets. This is both a strength (stability) and a risk (single customer dependency).

## Investment Thesis

### Bull Case
- Active military conflict and Trump's ceasefire rejection extend elevated defense spending for 2+ years
- Technology pivot drives margin expansion from historical ~8% EBIT margins toward 10-12%
- $500M debt issuance funds accretive acquisition in cyber, electronic warfare, or AI/ML defense applications
- Cleared workforce of 26,000 is an irreplaceable strategic asset that competitors cannot replicate quickly
- Low beta provides defensive characteristics while participating in defense spending upcycle
- Target price in bull case: $700-720 (28-30x forward earnings with mid-teens EPS growth)

### Bear Case
- Unexpected ceasefire or diplomatic resolution reduces defense urgency, compressing multiples toward historical 18-20x
- DOGE-driven government efficiency cuts reach defense IT services contracts
- Debt burden grows without corresponding margin improvement; 6.375% coupon creates earnings drag
- Palantir and AI-native competitors erode CACI's technology differentiation
- Insider selling accelerates
- At 20x earnings, stock is worth ~$450-470, representing 22-26% downside

## Conclusion

CACI is a high-quality defense IT services company positioned to benefit from the most favorable spending environment since the post-9/11 era. The macro backdrop — active conflict, ceasefire rejection, munitions depletion, rising defense budgets — supports sustained demand growth across CACI's core markets. The company's strategic pivot toward technology solutions and its cleared workforce of 26,000 provide genuine competitive advantages.

However, the stock at $606.72 and 26x trailing earnings already reflects much of this optimism. The 70% appreciation from the 52-week low, combined with increasing leverage from the $500M note issuance, limits the risk-reward at current levels. Insider transactions show net selling, though at modest levels relative to holdings.

**Recommendation: HOLD.** Existing positions should be maintained given the strong macro tailwinds, low beta characteristics (0.58), and multi-year defense spending visibility. New positions should wait for a pullback toward $500-520 (21-22x trailing earnings), which would offer a more favorable entry point with 15-20% margin of safety against the bear case. The defense sector thesis is correct; the question is whether CACI's current price already discounts 12-18 months of tailwinds.

**Why not BUY (per calibration framework):** The 70% appreciation from 52-week lows, 26x trailing P/E at the high end of historical range, increasing debt load with limited financial statement visibility, and modest net insider selling collectively argue that the risk-reward favors patience over urgency. The company is well-positioned, but the price reflects that positioning.
