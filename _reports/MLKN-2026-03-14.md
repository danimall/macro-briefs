## Executive Summary

MillerKnoll is the post-merger entity combining Herman Miller and Knoll, two legacy commercial/residential furniture brands. At $18.96 per share and a $1.29B market cap, the stock sits 18% below its 52-week high of $23.18 and 38% above the 52-week low of $13.77. The 4.0% dividend yield is notable for a cyclical industrial company, but the 1.34 beta signals significant exposure to the deteriorating macro environment.

The central analytical problem with MLKN is the intersection of two negative macro forces acting simultaneously on its business: (1) commercial office furniture demand is tied to return-to-office trends and commercial real estate investment, both of which are softening as GDP prints 0.7% and recession probability rises to 38-42%; and (2) the residential/retail segment (legacy Knoll, Design Within Reach) faces contracting consumer discretionary spending (retail sales -0.2%, gasoline +21%). ## Company Overview

MillerKnoll was formed through Herman Miller's $1.8B acquisition of Knoll in 2021, creating a portfolio spanning contract office furniture (Herman Miller, Knoll), luxury residential (Design Within Reach, Holly Hunt, Muuto), and international operations across ~100 countries. The company's brands are recognized — Herman Miller's Aeron chair is arguably the most iconic office chair ever produced — and the Fortune World's Most Admired Companies 2026 recognition confirms ongoing brand equity.

The merger rationale was portfolio diversification (contract + residential + luxury) and cost synergies. The company's fiscal year ends in late May/early June, with Q3 FY2026 results expected in March 2026 based on the conference call scheduling announcement.

## Growth Analysis

MLKN's growth trajectory depends on three distinct demand drivers:

**1. Contract office furniture (largest segment).** Return-to-office mandates drove some recovery in 2023-2024, but the growth vector has flattened. Corporate capital expenditure on office build-outs is sensitive to GDP growth and corporate confidence, both deteriorating. With Q4 GDP at 0.7% and recession probability at 38-42%, large office furniture orders face delay risk.

**2. Residential/luxury (Design Within Reach, Holly Hunt).** These brands target affluent consumers. While high-income consumers are more insulated from gasoline price increases, the wealth effect from equity market declines acts as a headwind. The S&P 500's worst three-week stretch directly reduces the propensity for $5,000+ furniture purchases.

**3. International operations.** European energy crisis (gas prices doubled) compresses European corporate and consumer budgets. International demand likely softening in tandem with domestic.

The company's inclusion on Fortune's Most Admired Companies list signals brand health but provides no revenue growth data. Self-describing as "growth-oriented" in the Q3 FY2026 conference call announcement suggests management is trying to reposition the narrative, which may reflect actual operational improvement or may be aspirational framing.

## Competitive Landscape

MillerKnoll holds strong competitive positioning within commercial furniture:

- **Brand portfolio:** Herman Miller, Knoll, Design Within Reach, Holly Hunt, Muuto, HAY, and Geiger collectively span nearly every price point in commercial and residential furniture
- **Dealer network:** Deep relationships with commercial interior designers and workplace consultants create switching costs for large corporate accounts
- **Design heritage:** Herman Miller's design legacy (Eames, Aeron) provides intangible brand value that competitors like Steelcase (SCS), Haworth, and HON cannot easily replicate
- **Scale advantages post-merger:** The combined entity has greater purchasing power and manufacturing efficiency than either standalone predecessor

**Competitive weaknesses:** The merger integration risk has largely played out, but portfolio complexity across multiple brands targeting different segments creates operational overhead. Direct-to-consumer furniture (Wayfair, Article, various DTC brands) continues to erode the mid-market residential segment, though MLKN competes primarily at the premium end.

**Competitive position score: 5.5.** Strong brands and market position within a structurally challenged industry (office furniture in a hybrid-work world).

## Risk Assessment

**Macro sensitivity is the dominant risk.** MLKN's 1.34 beta quantifies this. The company sits at the intersection of three negative macro forces: commercial real estate weakness (office vacancy rates at historic highs in many markets), consumer discretionary contraction (retail -0.2%, gasoline +21%), and input cost inflation from $100+ oil (raw materials, transportation, energy costs for manufacturing). Each of these independently would pressure the business; all three occurring simultaneously is the current reality.

**Leverage risk.** Without confirmed current debt figures, the post-acquisition debt load remains a concern. If the credit cascade (55-60% probability) materializes and spreads to industrial credit markets, refinancing costs for MLKN's term loans/bonds would increase meaningfully. A company generating ~$250-350M EBITDA with ~$1.2-1.5B in debt has adequate but not comfortable coverage.

**Tariff risk.** Section 301 probes across 60 economies could impose additional costs on imported components, fabrics, and finished goods. MLKN manufactures domestically and internationally; tariff exposure depends on supply chain specifics not available in this data.

**Dividend sustainability.** The 4.0% yield implies ~$52M annual dividend obligation. If EBITDA declines by 15-25% in a recession, free cash flow after interest and capex may not cover the dividend without drawing on revolver or cutting it.

**Risk factors score: 4.0.** High cyclical sensitivity, unconfirmed leverage position, and unfavorable macro alignment across all three business segments.
