## Executive Summary

Texas Roadhouse reported Q4 2025 revenue of $1.482B (+3.1% YoY from $1.438B) but net income declined 27% to $84.6M from $115.8M in Q4 2024, producing EPS of $1.28 — below the $1.53 consensus estimate and down 11.6% YoY. FY2025 revenue reached approximately $5.9B with positive comparable sales and traffic growth across all three brands (Texas Roadhouse, Bubba's 33, Jaggers). The company opened its 800th system-wide restaurant and plans 35 new company-owned openings in 2026 with a 1.9% menu price increase.

The margin compression story is the central issue. Revenue grew while profits contracted materially, implying cost pressures from labor, beef, and other input costs are accelerating faster than pricing power can offset. In the current macro regime — stagflation confirmed across six dimensions, $100+ oil driving gasoline up 21%, consumer retail sales contracting 0.2%, and quit rates declining — a casual dining chain dependent on middle-income discretionary spending faces compounding headwinds. The 1.9% planned menu price increase for 2026 appears insufficient to offset cost inflation that drove a 27% earnings decline on only 3% revenue growth.

At ~28x forward earnings with earnings declining, insider selling activity, and a hostile macro backdrop for consumer discretionary names, TXRH is a HOLD for existing positions. Initiating new positions here carries unfavorable risk-reward.

## Company Overview

Texas Roadhouse operates approximately 800+ system-wide restaurants across three brands: Texas Roadhouse (full-service steakhouse, ~700+ locations), Bubba's 33 (sports restaurant), and Jaggers (fast-casual). The company is headquartered in Louisville, Kentucky and trades on NASDAQ. Market capitalization stands at $11.2B as of March 14, 2026, with the stock at $170.24 — approximately 15% below its 52-week high of $199.99 and 14% above its 52-week low of $148.73. Beta of 0.876 indicates slightly below-market volatility, consistent with the restaurant industry's partial defensive characteristics (people still eat out, but trade down).

The company's competitive positioning within casual dining is strong. Texas Roadhouse has consistently gained share from weaker casual dining competitors (Applebee's, Chili's, Red Robin) through a value-oriented proposition: hand-cut steaks, fresh-baked bread, and a loud, energetic atmosphere at price points that undercut dedicated steakhouse chains. All three brands posted positive traffic growth in 2025, which is notable given the broader casual dining sector's traffic declines.

## Financial Analysis

**Revenue:** Q4 2025 revenue of $1.482B (+3.1% YoY). FY2025 revenue approximately $5.9B per CEO Gerald Morgan's commentary. Revenue growth has decelerated — 3.1% quarterly growth compared to what was likely mid-to-high single digits in prior quarters, given the full-year trajectory.

**Profitability:** Q4 2025 net income of $84.6M vs. $115.8M in Q4 2024, a 27% decline. EPS of $1.28 missed the $1.53 consensus by $0.25, or 16%. This margin erosion is severe: revenue grew 3.1% while net income contracted 27%, implying operating leverage is working in reverse. The cost structure — primarily beef (approximately 30-35% of COGS for a steakhouse), labor (~30-33% of revenue), and occupancy — is inflating faster than top-line growth.

**Dividend:** The board approved a quarterly dividend increase despite earnings pressure, which news sources described as a "hike despite earnings pressure." The dividend yield stands at approximately 1.77%. While the increase signals management confidence, raising dividends during earnings compression reduces financial flexibility. The payout ratio is rising mechanically as earnings decline.

**Balance Sheet:** Detailed balance sheet data was not available in the data feed, but the company has historically maintained a conservative balance sheet with moderate leverage. The $11.2B market cap against ~$5.9B revenue implies a revenue multiple of approximately 1.9x.

**Cash Flow:** Not available in the data feed. Historically, TXRH has generated strong free cash flow given its company-owned restaurant model, but the 35 new openings planned for 2026 will require significant capital expenditure (typically $5-6M per unit, implying ~$175-210M in new unit capex alone).

## Growth Analysis

**Unit Growth:** 35 planned company-owned openings in 2026 represents approximately 5% unit growth from a base of ~700+ company-owned locations. This is a healthy pace that reflects whitespace remaining in the U.S. market, particularly in under-penetrated regions. The 800th system-wide opening in 2025 was a milestone.

**Comparable Sales:** The 1.9% menu price increase planned for 2026 is modest. Given that labor costs typically inflate 4-5% annually and beef prices face upward pressure from multiple channels (feed costs tied to fertilizer prices, which are spiking due to sulphur supply disruptions from the Iran conflict), this pricing action appears conservative. If comparable traffic remains positive, total comparable sales growth could reach 3-5%, but net margins will likely continue compressing.

**Brand Extensions:** Bubba's 33 and Jaggers represent optionality, though they remain small relative to the core Texas Roadhouse brand. All three delivering positive traffic growth is a positive signal about brand health.

**Long-term Growth:** The restaurant industry's long-term growth rate approximates GDP + inflation. TXRH's ability to grow units 4-5% annually on top of low-single-digit comps could deliver high-single-digit revenue growth, but translation to earnings growth requires cost management that Q4 2025 results call into question.

## Valuation Assessment

At $170.24 with trailing and forward P/E both at approximately 27.7x, TXRH trades at a significant premium to casual dining peers (Darden at ~18-20x, Bloomin' Brands at ~10-12x) and even premium casual chains. This premium has historically been justified by TXRH's superior traffic growth and unit economics, but the Q4 2025 earnings miss and margin compression create a problematic setup: the stock is priced for continued execution while execution is deteriorating.

The market cap of $11.2B on ~$5.9B revenue yields a ~1.9x price/sales ratio, which is rich for a full-service restaurant operator. For context, at the Q4 2025 annualized net income run rate (~$84.6M × 4 = ~$338M, though seasonality varies), the implied P/E would be closer to 33x — suggesting the forward consensus assumes meaningful earnings recovery that may not materialize given the cost environment.

The dividend yield of 1.77% provides modest income support but is insufficient to compensate for the valuation risk if earnings continue declining.

## Competitive Landscape

TXRH's competitive position within casual dining is genuinely strong. The company has been a consistent share-gainer for over a decade, driven by: (1) a value proposition that resonates with middle-income consumers — hand-cut steaks at price points 30-40% below Ruth's Chris or Morton's; (2) a culture-driven operating model with high employee retention relative to peers; (3) menu consistency and operational execution that maintains quality across 700+ locations.

The competitive risk is less about direct competitors and more about the category itself. In a stagflationary environment with gasoline up 21%, quit rates declining (indicating labor market anxiety), and retail sales contracting, the core TXRH customer — a household earning $50-100K with 2-3 kids — faces budget compression. The trade-down from TXRH goes to fast casual (Chipotle, Wingstop) or home cooking, reducing traffic. While TXRH has outperformed this dynamic historically, Q4 2025's revenue deceleration to 3.1% may be an early signal.

Beef cost exposure is a structural competitive vulnerability. As a steakhouse concept, TXRH cannot easily substitute away from its primary input. Competitors with more diversified protein menus (chicken, seafood) have more flexibility to manage input cost inflation.

## Risk Assessment

**Macro Sensitivity (HIGH):** The current stagflationary environment is particularly hostile to casual dining. GDP at 0.7%, retail sales contracting, gasoline up 21%, and oil at $100+ all compress the disposable income of TXRH's core customer. The calibration document flags Consumer Discretionary BUY calls as destructive (-1.54% average), and TXRH falls squarely in this category.

**Input Cost Inflation (HIGH):** Beef prices face upward pressure from: (1) direct oil/energy cost passthrough to feed lots and transportation; (2) fertilizer cost increases (sulphur disruption from Iran conflict); (3) persistent labor cost inflation in a tight-but-weakening labor market. The 1.9% menu price increase planned for 2026 appears inadequate against these pressures.

**Insider Activity (MODERATELY NEGATIVE):** CTO Mujica sold 5,000 shares ($850K) on March 13, 2026. Director Moore sold 1,700 shares (his entire remaining position) on March 6. Director Epps sold 850 shares on March 6. While grant-related dispositions and tax withholdings are routine, the pattern of open-market sales — particularly Moore selling to zero — is a negative signal. Grant/award activity for officers in late February is routine compensation.

**Valuation Risk (HIGH):** At 28x earnings with earnings declining, any further earnings misses could compress the multiple. A re-rating to 22-24x (still premium to peers) on a reduced earnings base would imply 15-25% downside.

**Expansion Execution Risk (MODERATE):** 35 new openings require approximately $175-210M in capital. Each new unit dilutes per-unit economics during its ramp period (typically 12-18 months to mature). In a weakening consumer environment, new unit productivity may disappoint.

**Oil/Energy Passthrough (ELEVATED):** At $100+ oil, TXRH faces cost pressure through multiple channels: restaurant energy costs, food transportation, beef production costs, and reduced consumer spending power from higher gasoline prices.

## Investment Thesis

### Bull Case
- TXRH has consistently outperformed casual dining through multiple cycles, including 2008-2009 and COVID recovery. Positive traffic growth across all three brands in 2025 demonstrates category-leading execution.
- 35 new openings in 2026 add high-return incremental units to the system. If new units achieve historical $5-6M annual volumes at 15-17% restaurant-level margins, they contribute $26-36M in incremental restaurant-level profit.
- The 1.9% price increase is conservative and could be supplemented with additional pricing if cost pressures persist. TXRH has historically demonstrated pricing power without traffic destruction.
- The 0.876 beta provides some insulation from broad market volatility. The dividend increase signals management confidence in forward cash flow.

### Bear Case
- Q4 2025 earnings declined 27% on 3% revenue growth — margin compression is accelerating. The planned 1.9% price increase is structurally insufficient against 4-5% labor inflation and rising beef/energy costs.
- At 28x earnings with declining profitability, the stock is priced for a recovery that may not arrive given the macro backdrop (GDP 0.7%, retail sales -0.2%, oil $100+).
- Insider selling by the CTO and two directors (one to zero shares) within the past two weeks is a negative signal from those with direct visibility into operating trends.
- Consumer discretionary spending is in active contraction. TXRH's core middle-income customer is squeezed by gasoline (+21%), food-at-home inflation, and labor market anxiety (declining quit rates). Trade-down to fast casual or home cooking reduces traffic.
- The 35 new unit openings consume capital during a period when preserving financial flexibility may be more prudent.

## Conclusion

Texas Roadhouse is a high-quality casual dining operator with a strong competitive position and a history of outperformance. These attributes justify a premium valuation in a normal environment. The current environment is abnormal: stagflationary conditions compress both TXRH's margins (through input costs) and its customers' wallets (through energy and food inflation) simultaneously. Q4 2025's 27% earnings decline on 3% revenue growth quantifies this dual compression.

At 28x forward earnings with declining profitability, insider selling, and a macro regime that is specifically hostile to consumer discretionary names, the risk-reward for new positions is unfavorable. For existing holders, the low beta (0.876), dividend yield (1.77%), and competitive strength argue against selling into weakness. A more attractive entry point would be in the low-to-mid $140s (approximately 22-24x a reduced earnings base), which would provide a margin of safety appropriate for the current macro risk.

**Recommendation: HOLD.** Do not initiate new positions at current levels. Re-evaluate if the stock approaches $140-150 or if margin compression stabilizes in Q1-Q2 2026 results.
