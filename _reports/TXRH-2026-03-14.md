## Executive Summary

Texas Roadhouse reported Q4 2025 revenue of $1.482B (+3.1% YoY) and FY2025 revenue approaching $5.9B, with positive comparable sales and traffic growth across all three brands (Texas Roadhouse, Bubba's 33, Jaggers). The company celebrated its 800th system-wide restaurant opening and guides for 35 new company-owned openings in 2026 with a 1.9% menu price increase. These are solid operational results from a well-run casual dining chain that has consistently taken share from peers.

The problem is the earnings trajectory and the macro context. Q4 net income fell to $84.6M from $115.8M a year earlier — a 27% decline — despite top-line growth. EPS of $1.28 missed the $1.53 consensus estimate by 16%. Cost pressures (likely labor and commodity inputs) are compressing margins, and the company is hiking its dividend despite this earnings deterioration. At ~28x forward earnings, the stock prices in continued execution in an environment where consumer discretionary spending is actively contracting: GDP at 0.7%, retail sales down 0.2%, gasoline up 21%, and quit rates declining.

The calibration data explicitly warns against Consumer Discretionary BUY calls, which have averaged -1.54% across 11 prior calls. TXRH's quality is above average for the sector, but the macro headwinds, earnings decompression, and full valuation argue for patience. This is a quality franchise at the wrong price in the wrong environment.

## Company Overview

Texas Roadhouse operates a system of approximately 800+ restaurants across three brands: Texas Roadhouse (the core casual steakhouse concept), Bubba's 33 (sports bar/restaurant), and Jaggers (fast-casual chicken/burger). The company is primarily company-owned, which gives management direct control over operations but also concentrates capital expenditure risk and makes margins highly sensitive to labor and commodity costs.

The company is headquartered in Louisville, Kentucky. CEO Gerald Morgan leads the organization. The stock trades on NASDAQ at $170.24 with a market cap of ~$11.2B. Beta of 0.88 reflects moderate but below-market equity sensitivity, consistent with a restaurant operator with some defensive characteristics (value positioning within casual dining).

## Financial Analysis

**Revenue:** Q4 2025 revenue was $1.482B, up from $1.438B in Q4 2024 (+3.1% YoY). FY2025 revenue reached approximately $5.9B. Revenue growth is decelerating — the Q4 growth rate of 3.1% is modest for a company trading at 28x earnings and reflects the difficulty of maintaining comp-store momentum as the consumer weakens.

**Earnings:** Q4 2025 net income was $84.6M ($1.28/share), down sharply from $115.8M in Q4 2024. This represents a 27% decline in net income on 3.1% revenue growth — a severe margin compression signal. The $1.28 EPS missed the analyst consensus estimate of $1.53 by approximately $0.25, or 16%. The revenue-to-earnings gap implies that cost pressures (food costs, labor, occupancy, or some combination) accelerated materially during the quarter.

## Growth Analysis

**Unit Growth:** The company targets 35 new company-owned restaurant openings in 2026. With approximately 800+ locations system-wide, this represents ~4.4% unit growth. At an average unit volume likely in the $6-7M range (implied by ~$5.9B across ~800 locations, adjusting for franchise and non-company stores), each new unit adds meaningful revenue. Unit growth is the primary top-line growth driver.

**Comparable Sales:** All three brands delivered positive comparable sales and traffic growth in FY2025 per CEO Morgan's commentary. Positive traffic in the current consumer environment is genuinely impressive — most casual dining peers are seeing negative traffic offset by price increases. However, the 1.9% menu price increase planned for 2026 is below food-at-home inflation, which limits pricing's contribution to comp growth and may signal management concern about price elasticity.

**Brand Extensions:** Bubba's 33 and Jaggers provide smaller but growing revenue streams. The acquisition mentioned in the CEO's commentary (unspecified details) may relate to one of these brands or a new concept. These extensions provide optionality but are still immaterial relative to the core Texas Roadhouse concept.

**Growth Concerns:** The 3.1% Q4 revenue growth on a company trading at 28x forward earnings creates a growth-valuation mismatch. If comparable sales growth slows from the current pace (likely given the consumer environment), and unit growth delivers 4-5%, total revenue growth could settle into a 5-7% range — respectable but not justifying a premium multiple.

## Valuation Assessment

At $170.24, TXRH trades at 27.7x forward earnings. The casual dining peer group (Darden Restaurants at ~18-20x, Brinker International at ~15-18x, Bloomin' Brands at ~10-12x) makes TXRH's premium apparent. The premium has historically been justified by TXRH's superior traffic trends, consistent execution, and above-peer comparable sales growth.

The dividend yield of 1.77% provides modest income support. With the quarterly dividend recently hiked to $0.68/share ($2.72 annualized), the payout on FY2025 earnings (which appear to have come in around $6.00-6.50/share based on Q4's $1.28 contribution) represents a payout ratio of roughly 40-45%. This is manageable but worth monitoring if earnings continue to compress.

The stock sits at $170.24 versus a 52-week range of $148.73-$199.99. It's 15% below its highs and 14% above its lows, suggesting the market has partially priced in the Q4 earnings miss but hasn't fully de-rated the stock.

For the valuation to work at 28x, the company needs to demonstrate margin recovery and sustain mid-to-high single digit earnings growth. In the current macro environment — with oil above $100, consumer confidence deteriorating, and food input costs elevated — margin recovery is uncertain at best.

## Competitive Landscape

Texas Roadhouse's competitive position within casual dining is strong. The brand occupies a distinctive niche: value-oriented steakhouse dining with hand-cut steaks, fresh-baked bread, and a family-friendly atmosphere. Key competitive advantages include:

1. **Value perception:** Texas Roadhouse consistently scores well on value-for-money surveys. In a trade-down environment, this should provide some insulation versus premium casual dining.
2. **Positive traffic trends:** The company has sustained positive traffic when peers are losing it, indicating genuine consumer preference for the concept.
3. **Scale:** 800+ locations provide purchasing power on beef, the single largest cost input. Supply chain relationships with major packers are a tangible advantage.
4. **Culture:** The company's managing partner compensation model (where individual restaurant GMs have significant equity-like incentives) drives above-peer retention and operational consistency.

**Competitive risks:** Beef costs remain the dominant variable cost exposure. With oil at $100+, feed costs and transportation costs for cattle increase, potentially squeezing margins from the supply side. Fast-casual concepts (Chipotle, CAVA, Wingstop) continue to take share from full-service casual dining broadly, though TXRH has been more resilient than most.

## Risk Assessment

**Macro Risk (HIGH):** The world model documents a stagflationary environment with GDP at 0.7%, retail sales -0.2%, gasoline +21%, and declining quit rates. Consumer discretionary spending is in active contraction. While TXRH's value positioning provides relative insulation within casual dining, it remains a discretionary restaurant purchase vulnerable to consumers cutting dining-out frequency. Each $10 increase in oil prices adds roughly $0.15-0.25/gallon to gasoline, directly reducing disposable income for TXRH's core middle-income demographic.

**Cost Pressure Risk (HIGH):** The Q4 margin compression (27% earnings decline on 3.1% revenue growth) demonstrates that cost headwinds are already material. Beef prices, labor costs, and energy costs are all elevated. The 1.9% menu price increase for 2026 appears insufficient to offset these pressures, particularly if oil stays above $100 (which the world model assigns high probability).

**Insider Selling (MODERATE):** Director Moore's sale to zero holdings and CTO Mujica's $850K sale within weeks of the earnings miss are mildly concerning signals. These are not large-scale liquidations but they coincide with an unfavorable fundamental trajectory.

**Unit Growth Execution Risk (MODERATE):** 35 new openings require capital deployment (~$5-7M per unit typically) during a period of rising construction costs and potential credit tightening. If consumer conditions deteriorate further, new units may open into weaker-than-modeled demand.

**Regulatory/Labor Risk (MODERATE):** Minimum wage increases at state and local levels continue to pressure restaurant labor costs. The company's managing partner model partially mitigates this through performance-based compensation, but hourly crew wages are still subject to regulatory escalation.

## Investment Thesis

**Bull Case:** TXRH is the best operator in casual dining, with a proven ability to take share from weaker competitors. Positive traffic growth when peers are negative demonstrates brand resilience. The 35-unit 2026 pipeline provides visible revenue growth. If beef prices moderate and the consumer stabilizes, margins could recover sharply from the Q4 trough, driving earnings upside that justifies the premium multiple. The 1.77% dividend yield provides a floor, and the dividend hike signals management's confidence in forward cash flows.

**Bear Case:** The Q4 earnings miss was a leading indicator of sustained margin compression. Oil above $100 simultaneously raises input costs (beef, transportation, energy) and reduces consumer spending power — a double negative for a full-service restaurant chain. The 1.9% menu price increase for 2026 is insufficient to offset cost headwinds, and raising prices further risks breaking the value proposition that drives traffic. At 28x forward earnings, the stock prices in continued execution that the macro environment may not permit. Consumer discretionary stocks broadly face headwinds from declining retail sales (-0.2%), rising gasoline costs (+21%), and weakening employment (NFP -92K). The insider selling pattern adds a modestly negative signal.

## Conclusion

TXRH is a high-quality operator facing a hostile macro environment for its sector. The competitive position and execution track record are genuine strengths, but Q4's 27% earnings decline on positive revenue growth reveals cost pressure that a 1.9% price increase won't fully offset. At 28x forward earnings with falling profits, rising input costs (oil, beef, labor), and contracting consumer spending, the risk-reward is unattractive for new capital deployment.

The calibration data explicitly identifies Consumer Discretionary BUY calls as the system's weakest sector recommendation, averaging -1.54% returns. TXRH's quality is above average for the sector, but the macro headwinds and full valuation argue against a BUY here. The stock needs either (a) a valuation correction to ~22-24x (implying ~$135-150) or (b) a material improvement in the consumer environment, before the risk-reward improves sufficiently.

**Recommendation: HOLD.** Existing holders should maintain positions given the quality of the franchise, but should not add at current levels. New capital should wait for either a valuation reset or evidence of margin stabilization.
