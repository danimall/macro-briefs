## Executive Summary

RB Global is a commercial asset marketplace operator (formerly Ritchie Bros. Auctioneers) serving construction, transportation, agriculture, and automotive sectors through auction and remarketing services. Q4 2025 results showed $1.20B in revenue (+5.4% YoY) and adjusted EPS of $1.11, beating consensus of $0.97 by 14.4%. Management guided 2026 adjusted EBITDA to $1.47-$1.53B with 5-8% gross transaction value (GTV) growth, and the board authorized a $500M share repurchase program.

The stock trades at $98.68, down ~40% from its 52-week high of $164.85, implying the market has meaningfully repriced the company. At ~42x trailing and forward P/E, the valuation remains stretched for a business growing revenue mid-single digits in an environment where GDP is printing 0.7%, NFP is negative, and oil above $100 threatens equipment utilization rates across RBA's core verticals. The low beta (0.75) provides some defensive cushion, and the asset-light marketplace model generates recurring fee-based revenue, but the current price already discounts a lot of execution on integration synergies and market share gains.

## Company Overview

RB Global operates the world's largest commercial asset marketplace, facilitating buying and selling of used heavy equipment, trucks, and other commercial assets. The company's platform includes live on-site auctions, online auctions, and private marketplace transactions. Following its 2023 merger with IAA (automotive salvage auctions), RBA now spans four verticals: construction/industrial equipment, transportation, automotive remarketing, and agriculture. The BigIron acquisition announced March 2026 accelerates the agriculture expansion into U.S. farm equipment.

Revenue is predominantly fee-based, derived as a percentage of GTV. This asset-light model means revenue scales with transaction volumes without proportional capital investment. The company is dual-listed on NYSE and TSX.

## Financial Analysis

**Revenue:** Q4 2025 revenue was $1.20B, up $61M or 5.4% YoY. This is consistent with the company's 5-8% GTV growth guidance range.

**Earnings:** Reported GAAP EPS of $0.586 missed expectations of $0.99, but adjusted EPS of $1.11 beat consensus of $0.97 by 14.4%. The gap between GAAP and adjusted EPS ($0.52 difference) warrants scrutiny — it implies ~$130M+ in non-GAAP adjustments in a single quarter, likely related to IAA integration charges, amortization of acquired intangibles, or restructuring costs. Without detailed financial statements, the composition of these adjustments is opaque.

**2026 Guidance:** Adjusted EBITDA of $1.47-$1.53B. At the midpoint ($1.50B), and with a $24.8B market cap, the company trades at ~16.5x forward EV/EBITDA (assuming modest net debt from the IAA acquisition). This is more reasonable than the P/E multiple suggests, indicating significant depreciation/amortization from acquired intangibles is depressing reported earnings relative to cash generation.

**Capital Allocation:** The $500M buyback authorization (announced March 9, 2026) represents ~2% of market cap. At $98.68/share, this would retire approximately 5M shares. Combined with the ~1.25% dividend yield, total shareholder return commitment is ~3.25% annually. The BigIron acquisition adds to the M&A-driven growth strategy, though terms were not disclosed.

**Balance Sheet:** Financial statements were unavailable in the data feed. The IAA merger (completed 2023) was partially debt-funded, and the company likely carries significant goodwill and intangible assets from that transaction. The gap between GAAP and adjusted EPS confirms ongoing amortization charges from the acquisition.

## Growth Analysis

**Organic Growth:** 5-8% GTV growth guidance for 2026 reflects market share gains across sectors. Management specifically referenced renewed major automotive contracts contributing to the outlook. Construction equipment auction volumes tend to be countercyclical — when economic conditions weaken, fleet operators sell excess capacity, generating transaction volume for RBA. This provides a partial hedge against the current macro downturn.

**Inorganic Growth:** BigIron acquisition targets U.S. agriculture, a sector where RBA has historically had limited presence. U.S. farm equipment is a ~$20B+ annual market, and agricultural equipment auctions face less digital disruption than other categories. The strategic logic is sound, though execution risk exists given RBA is simultaneously still integrating IAA.

**AI and Technology:** Management referenced "AI-driven initiatives" in its 2026 growth targets, likely related to pricing algorithms, demand matching, and digital marketplace enhancements. These are efficiency improvements rather than transformative growth vectors.

**Structural Tailwind:** The secular shift from physical to digital auction formats favors the largest platform operators. RBA's scale advantage (largest global marketplace) creates network effects — more buyers attract more sellers, which increases realized prices, which attracts more consignors.

## Valuation Assessment

At $98.68 with trailing/forward P/E of ~42x, RBA is priced for sustained double-digit earnings growth. The company is guiding to mid-single-digit GTV growth, which — even with operating leverage and buyback accretion — likely translates to high-single-digit to low-teens EPS growth. That puts the PEG ratio at roughly 3-4x, which is expensive.

The EV/EBITDA of ~16.5x (estimated) is more reasonable and probably the better valuation metric given the heavy acquisition-related amortization. Marketplace businesses with recurring fee income and network effects can justify premium multiples, but 16.5x EBITDA for a company growing 5-8% topline is at the upper end of fair value.

The stock's 40% decline from its 52-week high of $164.85 to $98.68 has corrected some of the overvaluation, but at current levels it still prices in successful execution on IAA synergies, BigIron integration, and continued market share gains without macro deterioration in its end markets.

**Dividend yield of ~1.25%** provides minimal income support.

## Competitive Landscape

RBA holds a dominant position in commercial asset auctions globally. Key competitive advantages:

1. **Network effects:** Largest buyer base → highest realized prices → more consignors → more buyers. This flywheel is difficult for smaller competitors to replicate.
2. **Multi-channel platform:** Live, online, and hybrid auction formats serve different seller preferences.
3. **Data advantage:** Transaction history across millions of assets enables superior pricing and valuation tools.
4. **Vertical breadth:** Post-IAA, the company spans construction, transport, auto, and (soon) agriculture.

Competitors include Euro Auctions, Manheim (Cox Automotive), and various regional/niche players. None matches RBA's global scale across multiple verticals.

The competitive position is genuinely strong. The primary risk is not competitive displacement but rather volume sensitivity to macroeconomic conditions affecting equipment utilization and turnover rates.

## Risk Assessment

**Macro Sensitivity:** In the current stagflationary environment (GDP 0.7%, NFP -92K, oil $100+), RBA faces mixed effects. Construction equipment volumes could benefit if operators shed fleet, but buyer demand weakens if financing costs remain elevated and new project starts decline. The net effect depends on whether supply-side forced selling (positive for auction volumes) outweighs demand-side weakness (negative for realized prices and GTV).

**Oil Price Impact:** Higher fuel costs increase operating expenses for equipment end-users (construction, trucking, agriculture), potentially accelerating fleet rationalization and increasing auction consignment volumes. However, sustained $100+ oil could reduce buyer willingness to add equipment, compressing realized prices. The direction of the net effect is ambiguous.

**Integration Risk:** IAA was a transformative acquisition. Integration of salvage auto auctions with traditional heavy equipment auctions involves different customer bases, technology platforms, and operational workflows. The persistent GAAP-to-adjusted earnings gap suggests integration costs remain elevated nearly three years post-close.

**BigIron Acquisition:** Adding another vertical while IAA integration continues creates execution complexity.

**Valuation Risk:** At 42x P/E, any earnings disappointment or guidance reduction will result in disproportionate price declines. The stock has already fallen 40% from highs, partly reflecting this dynamic.

**Debt Level:** Unknown from available data but likely elevated from the IAA acquisition. Rising rates increase carrying costs.

**Beta:** 0.75 provides modest defensive positioning. In a further market selloff, RBA would likely underperform on a percentage basis less than the broad market.

## Investment Thesis

### Bull Case
- Countercyclical volume dynamics: economic weakness forces equipment owners to liquidate, driving auction volumes higher even as macro deteriorates
- IAA synergies fully realized in 2026-2027, driving margin expansion and closing the GAAP-to-adjusted earnings gap
- BigIron expands TAM into $20B+ agriculture market
- $500M buyback reduces share count at depressed prices (~2% of market cap)
- Marketplace network effects create durable competitive moat with high switching costs
- Stock already down 40% from 52-week highs, absorbing significant bad news

### Bear Case
- 42x P/E is expensive for a 5-8% GTV grower, particularly in a macro environment where recession probability is 38-42%
- GAAP EPS of $0.586 vs. adjusted $1.11 means ~47% of "earnings" are non-GAAP adjustments — real cash earnings may be lower than the adjusted figures suggest
- Credit tightening (55-60% credit cascade probability per macro model) reduces equipment financing availability, suppressing both buyer demand and realized auction prices
- IAA integration costs persist; BigIron adds another integration workstream
- Rising oil above $100 compresses margins for equipment operators, potentially reducing both new equipment purchases (less future auction supply) and auction buyer demand simultaneously
- Limited visibility into balance sheet and debt levels

## Conclusion

RB Global operates a high-quality marketplace business with genuine competitive advantages in commercial asset auctions. The IAA merger broadened the platform into automotive remarketing, and BigIron extends it into agriculture. Management is executing competently, beating Q4 consensus by 14% on adjusted EPS, and the $500M buyback signals confidence.

The problem is valuation. At 42x earnings for a mid-single-digit grower facing stagflationary headwinds, the stock offers limited margin of safety. The countercyclical argument (recession → more forced equipment sales → higher auction volumes) has merit but is unproven at this scale and already partially priced in. The 40% decline from highs reflects market recognition that the prior valuation was unsustainable, but the stock hasn't yet reached a level where the risk-reward is compelling for new capital.

**Recommendation: HOLD.** The quality of the business justifies maintaining existing positions, but initiating new positions at $98.68 / 42x P/E requires either (a) evidence that adjusted EBITDA guidance of $1.47-$1.53B is conservative, (b) a further pullback to the mid-$80s (closer to ~14x EV/EBITDA), or (c) demonstrated countercyclical volume acceleration as the macro worsens. Monitor Q1 2026 results for early signs of any of these catalysts.
