## Executive Summary

Cleveland-Cliffs is a vertically integrated steelmaker and iron ore miner that lost $1.4 billion in FY2025, a deterioration of $686 million from its FY2024 loss of $714 million, on revenue that declined by approximately $600 million year-over-year. Q4 2025 EPS was -$0.43 (beating the -$0.62 consensus, but still deeply negative). Adjusted EBITDA for the full year was only "slightly positive," per Forbes reporting, meaning the company's core operations are barely covering their cost structure.

The single most material data point in the dataset: CEO Lourenco Goncalves sold his entire 3,000,000-share common stock position on February 11, 2026, at $12.42/share for $37.3 million, leaving him with zero common shares. This occurred two days after the Q4 earnings release that sent the stock down 25%. He retains RSU and MSU grants (650,900 each awarded Feb 18), but the complete liquidation of common equity by the Chairman/President/CEO is a severe negative signal. COO Clifford Smith also sold 200,000 shares at $10.46 on Feb 17. Against this, director Edilson Camara purchased 19,700 shares at $10.13 on Feb 13 — a fraction of the insider selling volume.

The stock trades at $8.47, down 49% from its 52-week high of $16.70 and 50% above its 52-week low of $5.63. With a 2.0 beta, no dividend, negative earnings, and a stagflationary macro environment that pressures both steel demand (via recessionary GDP) and input costs (via $100+ oil), CLF is positioned at the intersection of multiple adverse forces with limited financial cushion.

## Company Overview

Cleveland-Cliffs is the largest flat-rolled steel producer in North America, with vertically integrated operations spanning iron ore mining, pellet production, steelmaking (blast furnace and EAF), and downstream processing. The company serves automotive, infrastructure, and industrial end markets. Its transformation from a pure iron ore miner to a vertically integrated steelmaker was driven by acquisitions (AK Steel in 2020, ArcelorMittal USA assets in 2020), which added significant debt to the balance sheet.

Management has indicated it "optimized its footprint and exited non-core assets" during 2025, and projects higher sales volumes and better steelmaking margins in 2026. BofA maintains a Neutral rating with a $13 price target (lowered from $14.50 on Feb 12, 2026).

Adjusted EBITDA was "only slightly positive" — likely in the low single-digit hundreds of millions, insufficient to service a multi-billion dollar debt load with meaningful margin of safety. Q4 2025 reported EPS was -$0.43, better than the -$0.62 consensus, but still a $243 million quarterly loss.

**Balance Sheet:** Detailed financial statements are not available in the dataset, but Cleveland-Cliffs carried approximately $4.6 billion in long-term debt as of recent filings, against a current market cap of $4.83 billion. This implies an enterprise value roughly double the equity market cap, and leverage ratios (net debt/EBITDA) that are functionally meaningless when EBITDA is near zero.

**Cash Flow:** Not available in detail, but the company's near-zero adjusted EBITDA in 2025 means free cash flow was likely negative after capex, interest expense, and working capital requirements.

**Market Cap:** $4.83 billion at $8.47/share implies approximately 570 million diluted shares outstanding. The Feb 18 RSU/MSU grants across six executives totaling approximately 2.7 million units (RSU + MSU combined) represent incremental dilution of ~0.5%.

## Growth Analysis

Management guided for higher sales volumes and improved steelmaking margins in 2026, driven by footprint optimization and non-core asset divestitures completed in 2025. The thesis depends on:

1. **Steel price recovery:** Hot-rolled coil (HRC) prices have been depressed. Any recovery helps CLF disproportionately given its high operating leverage. Section 301 tariff probes across 60 economies could theoretically benefit domestic steel through trade protection, but the timing and magnitude are uncertain.

2. **Automotive demand:** CLF is the largest supplier of steel to the North American automotive industry. Auto production volumes in a stagflationary environment (GDP 0.7%, gasoline +21%, consumer sentiment deteriorating) face headwinds. The world model's classification of auto as AVOID (F, GM) is directly relevant to CLF's largest end market.

3. **Infrastructure spending:** Federal infrastructure programs provide some demand floor, but these are multi-year and already partially priced.

4. **Cost reduction:** The company exited non-core assets and claims improved operational efficiency. Whether these translate to meaningful EBITDA improvement at current steel prices is the critical question, and 2025's barely-positive adjusted EBITDA despite management's cost discipline efforts suggests the answer may be no without a price recovery.

Growth score is limited by the combination of cyclical end markets in downturn, no demonstrated earnings power at current price levels, and dependency on external steel price recovery.

## Valuation Assessment

Traditional valuation metrics are largely inapplicable: trailing P/E and forward P/E are both undefined (negative earnings). Price-to-book is the relevant metric for a loss-making cyclical; at $4.83 billion market cap against likely tangible book value in the $4-6 billion range (given approximately $4.6 billion in long-term debt against total assets of approximately $15-16 billion based on historical filings), CLF trades at roughly 0.8-1.2x tangible book.

BofA's $13 price target implies 53% upside from $8.47 — a significant gap that reflects the market pricing a worse outcome than BofA's base case. The stock's 49% decline from its 52-week high ($16.70) suggests substantial earnings deterioration is priced. However, stocks can be cheap and get cheaper, particularly when the company is burning cash and the macro environment is deteriorating.

EV/EBITDA is functionally infinite at near-zero EBITDA. On a normalized basis (using, say, $1.5-2.0 billion mid-cycle EBITDA that CLF generated in 2021-2022), EV/EBITDA would be approximately 5-7x — reasonable for a steelmaker, but "mid-cycle" requires a steel price environment that does not currently exist and may not materialize for 12-18 months.

## Competitive Landscape

Cleveland-Cliffs' vertical integration (iron ore → pellets → steel) provides a cost advantage versus scrap-dependent EAF steelmakers when iron ore is cheap, and a disadvantage when scrap is cheap. The company is the largest flat-rolled producer in North America with significant automotive market share, giving it scale advantages and customer relationships that are difficult to replicate.

Against Vale (VALE) — the comparison raised in recent news — CLF looks weaker: Vale has rising earnings estimates and stronger output growth, while CLF carries losses and concentrated auto exposure. Nucor (NUE) and Steel Dynamics (STLD) operate EAF-centric models with structurally lower cost bases and have remained profitable through this cycle.

CLF's competitive position score of 5.5 reflects real structural advantages (scale, vertical integration, automotive relationships) that are currently overwhelmed by cyclical and financial headwinds.

## Risk Assessment

**CEO complete stock liquidation:** Lourenco Goncalves sold 100% of his common shares (3M shares, $37.3M) on Feb 11. This is an unambiguous negative signal. While he received new RSU/MSU grants on Feb 18, the common stock liquidation eliminates his direct equity alignment with shareholders at the current price. The timing — two days after a Q4 report that sent shares down 25% — compounds the negative optics.

**Leverage:** ~$4.6 billion in long-term debt against near-zero EBITDA creates refinancing risk. If credit conditions deteriorate (the world model assigns 55-60% probability to credit cascade), CLF's borrowing costs could increase materially, and covenant issues could emerge.

**Beta of 2.0:** In a market environment where VIX has not yet reached capitulation levels (35+) and further equity correction probability is 40-50%, a 2.0 beta stock with negative earnings carries extreme downside exposure. A 15% market decline implies a 30% expected CLF decline, which from $8.47 implies $5.93 — near the 52-week low of $5.63.

**Macro alignment:** Stagflation is the worst possible macro regime for a high-beta, high-leverage steelmaker with automotive exposure. Rising input costs (oil, energy, transportation) compress margins, while weakening demand (GDP 0.7%, retail sales -0.2%, auto sector under pressure) reduces pricing power and volumes simultaneously.

**Tariff optionality:** Section 232 steel tariffs and potential Section 301 actions provide some upside protection. If tariffs are imposed or maintained on steel imports, domestic producers like CLF benefit. This is partially reflected in management's optimistic 2026 guidance.

**Oil at $98-108:** Higher transportation and energy costs directly increase CLF's cost structure. Iron ore mining, pellet production, and blast furnace operations are energy-intensive.

## Investment Thesis

### Bull Case
- Steel prices recover in H2 2026, driven by infrastructure spending and potential tariff protection.
- Footprint optimization and cost reductions from 2025 translate to $1B+ EBITDA improvement at modestly higher HRC prices.
- Trading at ~0.8-1.2x tangible book with BofA's $13 target implying 53% upside.
- Section 232/301 tariff protection provides a demand floor for domestic production.

### Bear Case
- Stagflation persists: automotive demand weakens further, infrastructure spending insufficient to offset.
- Near-zero EBITDA cannot service $4.6B debt load; credit cascade risk (55-60% probability per world model) could trigger refinancing crisis.
- CEO sold 100% of common stock — the single most bearish insider signal possible.
- 2.0 beta exposes shareholders to 2x market downside in a potential VIX-spike/equity-correction scenario.
- Oil at $98-108 increases operating costs with limited ability to pass through in a weakening demand environment.
- Recession probability of 38-42% would crush steel demand and pricing simultaneously.

### Assessment of Cases
The bear case is supported by more independent data points (insider selling, macro regime, leverage, beta, oil costs) than the bull case (tariff speculation, management guidance from a CEO who immediately sold all his stock after providing that guidance). The asymmetry of outcomes also favors the bear case: downside to $5.63 (52-week low, -33%) is more plausible than upside to $13 (BofA target, +53%) given the macro environment.

## Conclusion

Cleveland-Cliffs scores 3.8 overall, reflecting a deeply cyclical company at the wrong point in the cycle with catastrophic insider selling, excessive leverage, and a 2.0 beta in a deteriorating macro environment. The CEO's complete liquidation of common stock within days of reporting a $1.4B annual loss, while simultaneously guiding for improvement, creates an irreconcilable information asymmetry — the words say one thing, the actions say another.

The stock could rally materially on a steel price recovery, tariff announcements, or ceasefire/de-escalation (which would reduce energy costs), but these are speculative catalysts against concrete negatives. At $8.47, the stock is 49% off highs and may appear cheap on a normalized basis, but normalized earnings require a macro environment that the world model assigns low probability to materializing in the near term.

**Recommendation: AVOID.** The combination of negative earnings, CEO complete stock sale, 2.0 beta, high leverage, and stagflationary macro headwinds creates an unfavorable risk-reward profile. Revisit if: (1) HRC prices recover above $900/ton sustainably, (2) leverage falls below 3x net debt/EBITDA, or (3) insider buying emerges at scale from senior management.
