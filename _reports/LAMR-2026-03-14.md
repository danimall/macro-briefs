## Executive Summary

Lamar Advertising is the largest owner and operator of outdoor advertising displays in the United States, structured as a REIT. Q4 2025 results showed $595.9M in revenue and $154.7M in net income, with hedge fund interest cited as supportive. The company declared a $1.60/share quarterly dividend (implying $6.40 annualized, ~4.8% yield at current prices) and extended its stock and debt repurchase programs.

The core tension: Lamar has a genuinely strong competitive position in an industry with high barriers to entry (permitting, zoning, location scarcity), and the digital billboard conversion tailwind remains ongoing. But the company operates at a 1.23 beta into a stagflationary macro environment where advertising budgets are among the first line items cut, GDP growth has decelerated to 0.7%, and retail sales are contracting. At 23x forward earnings, the stock prices in continued mid-single-digit revenue growth that may not materialize if recession probability (38-42% per macro assessment) plays out.

The limited financial statement data available constrains the depth of this analysis. The recommendation is HOLD: the competitive moat and dividend yield provide a floor, but cyclical exposure and valuation leave insufficient margin of safety for a BUY in the current environment.

## Company Overview

Lamar Advertising operates approximately 363,000 billboard displays across the US and Canada, including static billboards, digital billboards, interstate logos, and transit advertising. The company converted to REIT status in 2014, which requires distributing at least 90% of taxable income as dividends. Key characteristics:

- **Market cap:** $13.6B
- **Current price:** $134.14 (as of March 14, 2026)
- **52-week range:** $99.84–$139.76
- **Forward P/E:** 23.1x
- **Dividend yield:** ~4.8%
- **Beta:** 1.23

The company pursues a tuck-in acquisition strategy, most recently acquiring Cleveland Outdoor Advertising assets in early February 2026, consistent with its long-standing consolidation approach.

## Growth Analysis

Outdoor advertising revenue growth is driven by three mechanisms: (1) organic same-store revenue growth from rate increases and occupancy improvements, (2) digital billboard conversions that yield 2-4x revenue uplift per display, and (3) tuck-in acquisitions.

**Organic growth headwinds in current macro:** The stagflationary macro environment is directly adverse for advertising spending. Local and regional advertisers — Lamar's core client base — cut outdoor advertising budgets when facing margin pressure from $100+ oil, declining consumer traffic, and general uncertainty. Lamar's revenue mix skews toward local/regional advertisers (~75-80% historically), which are more cyclically sensitive than national brands. During the 2020 downturn, Lamar's revenue declined ~17% — outdoor advertising has meaningful cyclicality.

**Digital conversion tailwind persists:** Lamar has been converting static billboards to digital at a pace of roughly 200-300 per year. Digital billboards generate higher revenue per unit because multiple advertisers can rotate on a single display, and pricing is more dynamic. This structural shift provides some organic growth even in weaker macro environments, though it requires capex (~$100-150K per digital conversion).

**Acquisition pipeline:** The Cleveland Outdoor acquisition in February 2026 and Goldman Sachs' inclusion of LAMR among top REIT picks suggest institutional confidence in the growth-through-acquisition model. However, acquisition multiples for billboard assets have compressed less than other real estate categories, limiting accretion potential.

**Net assessment:** Mid-single-digit revenue growth (3-5%) is achievable in a neutral macro. In the current environment with recession probability at 38-42%, flat-to-low-single-digit growth is more realistic for FY2026, with downside risk to -5% to -10% in a full recession.

## Valuation Assessment

At 23.1x forward earnings and ~4.8% dividend yield, Lamar trades at a premium to the broader REIT sector (average forward P/E ~17-19x) but at a discount to its own historical range (traded above 30x in 2021). The premium over REIT peers reflects the asset-light nature of billboard operations relative to property-intensive REITs and historically stronger organic growth.

**Valuation in context of macro risk:** 23x forward earnings assumes continued earnings growth. If advertising revenue flattens or declines 5% in a recession, forward earnings would compress and the effective P/E could expand to 25-28x — expensive for a cyclically exposed REIT in a rising-rate environment. The Fed is unlikely to cut rates in 2026 (20-25% probability of no cuts), which means the discount rate applied to REIT cash flows remains elevated.

**Dividend yield as floor:** The ~4.8% yield provides some downside protection, but comparable risk-free rates (10-year Treasury ~4.3-4.5%) reduce the relative attractiveness. The yield spread over Treasuries (~30-50bps) is thin for an equity with 1.23 beta.

**Fair value range:** Without detailed DCF inputs, a rough assessment suggests fair value in the $110-125 range under a recessionary scenario (applying 18-20x to reduced earnings) and $135-150 in a growth scenario (23-25x on mid-single-digit growth). At $134.14, the stock is priced for the growth scenario. Risk-reward is asymmetric to the downside given macro conditions.

## Competitive Landscape

Lamar's competitive position is its strongest dimension. The outdoor advertising industry in the US is effectively an oligopoly among three players: Lamar, Clear Channel Outdoor (CCO), and Outfront Media (OUT). Lamar is the largest by number of displays.

**Moat characteristics:**
- **Regulatory barriers:** Local zoning and permitting restrictions limit new billboard construction. Many jurisdictions have moratoriums on new billboard permits, making existing inventory irreplaceable.
- **Scale advantages:** Lamar's national footprint allows bundled sales across markets, which smaller regional operators cannot match.
- **Location scarcity:** Prime highway and urban locations are finite, and existing billboard leases typically have long terms with renewal options.
- **Digital conversion capability:** Lamar's balance sheet and operational expertise allow it to execute digital conversions more efficiently than smaller competitors.

**Competitive threats:** Programmatic digital advertising continues to take share from traditional out-of-home. However, outdoor has actually been one of the more resilient traditional media categories because it cannot be ad-blocked, skipped, or ignored the way digital display ads can. The integration of programmatic buying into digital billboards has actually helped, allowing outdoor to capture some of the shift toward data-driven ad placement.

## Risk Assessment

**Cyclical advertising risk (HIGH):** This is the primary risk. Local/regional advertisers — Lamar's bread and butter — cut outdoor advertising early in downturns. With GDP at 0.7%, NFP at -92K, and retail sales declining, the advertising environment is deteriorating. Gasoline at +21% YoY reduces commuter miles driven, which directly reduces billboard impression counts and weakens Lamar's pricing power.

**Interest rate sensitivity (MODERATE-HIGH):** As a REIT, Lamar competes with fixed income for yield-oriented capital. If rates remain elevated or rise, LAMR's yield spread compresses further, pressuring the stock price. Additionally, Lamar carries significant debt; refinancing at higher rates compresses net margins.

**Oil price / commuter traffic (MODERATE):** Oil at $98-108 central range (potentially $115-130 in upside scenario) directly reduces vehicle miles traveled on highways — the primary delivery mechanism for Lamar's billboard impressions. Fewer impressions → lower advertiser willingness to pay → revenue pressure.

**Digital disruption (LOW-MODERATE):** Social media and digital advertising continue to take share of total advertising spend, though outdoor has held up better than print, radio, and linear TV.

**Insider activity (NEUTRAL):** Recent transactions are primarily LTIP grant/awards and tax-related dispositions — routine compensation activity. The CFO's sale of 1,260 shares at $137.56 ($173K total) on March 5 is de minimis relative to company size and likely related to the conversion exercise that preceded it. No concerning pattern.

## Investment Thesis

### Bull Case
- Dominant market position in an industry with structural barriers to new supply
- Digital billboard conversion continues to drive same-store revenue growth of 3-5%
- ~4.8% dividend yield provides income floor
- Goldman Sachs top REIT pick; hedge fund interest supportive
- Tuck-in acquisition strategy continues to consolidate fragmented regional operators
- If macro improves or Fed cuts, REIT multiples expand and LAMR re-rates toward $145-155

### Bear Case
- 23x forward P/E prices in growth that is at risk in a recession (38-42% probability)
- Advertising budgets are early-cycle cuts; local/regional advertisers are already under margin pressure from oil, tariffs, and consumer spending contraction
- $100+ oil reduces highway commuter traffic — directly reduces billboard impression counts
- REIT yield spread over Treasuries is only ~30-50bps — inadequate compensation for equity risk
- Payout ratio near 100% of net income leaves no buffer if earnings decline
- Beta of 1.23 means LAMR amplifies broader market declines; in a VIX spike / correction scenario (40-50% probability per macro model), LAMR likely underperforms defensive positions
- If recession materializes, revenue could decline 5-15%, compressing earnings and potentially threatening dividend sustainability

## Conclusion

Lamar Advertising has a strong competitive moat and an attractive dividend yield, but the stock is priced for continued growth at 23x forward earnings during a macro environment where advertising revenue is acutely vulnerable to contraction. The 1.23 beta, thin yield spread over Treasuries, cyclical revenue exposure, and near-100% payout ratio create a poor risk-reward profile for new capital deployment at $134.

This is a quality business at the wrong point in the cycle for aggressive positioning. The stock would become interesting at $105-115 (17-19x forward earnings), which would represent a 15-22% pullback from current levels and provide adequate margin of safety for the cyclical risk. At current prices, HOLD for existing positions; the dividend provides income while waiting for either macro improvement or a better entry point.

For investors seeking defensive REIT exposure in the current environment, alternatives with lower cyclical sensitivity (healthcare REITs, data center REITs, essential-services infrastructure) offer better risk-adjusted returns. LAMR should be monitored for a potential upgrade to BUY if (1) recession probability declines below 25%, (2) the stock pulls back to the $105-115 range, or (3) Q1-Q2 2026 results demonstrate resilience in advertising revenue despite macro headwinds.
