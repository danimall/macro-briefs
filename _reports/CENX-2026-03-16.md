## Executive Summary

Century Aluminum has been one of the strongest-performing mid-cap industrial names over the past year, rising from ~$19 in March 2025 to ~$55 in March 2026 — a 182% gain driven by three converging tailwinds: US aluminum tariffs providing domestic price premiums, the greenfield Oklahoma smelter project backed by DOE funding, and rising LME aluminum prices amid global supply disruptions. Q1 2026 adjusted EBITDA guidance of $215-235M represents a substantial step-up from prior quarters, with management targeting 630,000 tons of shipments for 2026.

The problem is what the stock prices in versus what the company currently earns. Trailing EPS is $0.42, producing a 130x P/E ratio at $56/share. The market cap of $5.6B is pricing in a future state where Oklahoma is fully operational, Midwest premiums persist at elevated levels, and LME aluminum stays above $2,500/ton — none of which are guaranteed. Meanwhile, insider selling has been extraordinary: Glencore sold 6.3M shares ($327M) in a single block on March 4, the CEO sold 150K shares ($7.2M) in January, the EVP of Global Operations sold $2.3M, the General Counsel sold $3.3M, and four other officers have sold between $560K and $1.2M each. This is the broadest insider selling cluster I've seen in a company this size.

The combination of extreme valuation, aggressive insider liquidation, cyclical commodity exposure in a stagflationary macro regime, and execution risk on a multi-billion-dollar greenfield smelter makes this a stock where the risk-reward is skewed negatively at current prices.

## Company Overview

Century Aluminum is a primary aluminum producer operating smelters in the US and Iceland. Its US facilities include Hawesville (Kentucky, recently sold — per B. Riley note), Mt. Holly (South Carolina, undergoing expansion), and Sebree (Kentucky). The Grundartangi smelter in Iceland provides lower-cost production via cheap geothermal/hydro power. The company has a 50% interest in the Jamalco bauxite/alumina joint venture in Jamaica.

The transformative catalyst is a planned new greenfield aluminum smelter in Oklahoma, supported by a DOE loan or grant (details from news coverage indicate significant government backing). This would be the first new US primary aluminum smelter in decades, adding substantial capacity. Management has guided to 630,000 tons of shipments in 2026, which implies the Mt. Holly expansion is contributing.

The company recently completed the sale of its Hawesville smelter, prompting B. Riley to raise its price target from $37 to $64 (February 3, 2026), suggesting the divestiture was viewed positively — likely simplifying the portfolio and generating capital for the Oklahoma project.

## Financial Analysis

**Earnings:** Trailing twelve-month EPS of $0.42 on a stock trading at $56.10 produces a trailing P/E of 130.3x. The forward P/E is also listed at 130.3x, suggesting analysts haven't meaningfully raised forward estimates above current trailing levels despite management's bullish Q1 2026 EBITDA guidance.

**Q1 2026 EBITDA Guidance:** Management guided $215-235M adjusted EBITDA for Q1 2026, attributing the improvement to higher realized LME and regional premiums, with an incremental $70-80M versus the prior quarter. If annualized at the midpoint (~$225M × 4 = $900M), this would imply substantial earnings power — but commodity companies rarely sustain peak-quarter EBITDA across a full year, and this guidance coincides with elevated Midwest premiums and high LME prices that are partially driven by the current geopolitical disruption.

**Balance Sheet:** Financial statements were not available in the data feed. The 10-K filing date of March 3, 2026 is recent, and the company noted a delay related to transitioning Jamalco from equity method to full consolidation. This accounting change introduces reporting complexity but doesn't change underlying economics.

**Market Cap:** $5.55B on 99M shares outstanding. No dividend. The market cap against $0.42 trailing EPS gives a trailing earnings yield of 0.75% — well below even the risk-free rate.

**Key Financial Gap:** Without income statements, balance sheet, and cash flow data in the feed, I cannot assess debt levels, working capital trends, or free cash flow generation. This is a material limitation. Aluminum smelting is capital-intensive with significant ongoing maintenance capex. The Oklahoma greenfield project will require billions in capital over multiple years.

## Growth Analysis

**Capacity Expansion:** The 630,000-ton 2026 shipment target and Oklahoma smelter buildout represent genuine structural growth. If the Oklahoma facility comes online at scale (likely 2028-2030 timeframe), Century would meaningfully increase its share of US primary aluminum production. The Mt. Holly expansion is a nearer-term contributor.

**Tariff Tailwind:** US aluminum tariffs (Section 232) create a domestic price premium over global LME prices. The Midwest premium — the incremental price US consumers pay above LME — has been elevated, directly boosting Century's realized pricing. This is a policy-dependent tailwind. The current administration's trade posture supports continuation, but Section 301 probes and tariff restructuring create uncertainty about the specific mechanism.

**LME Price Environment:** Global aluminum prices have been supported by supply disruptions (Hormuz closure, Chinese production restraints, energy cost pressures on European smelters). Current prices are well above the $1,600-1,800/ton range that prevailed in 2023. If oil stays at $98-108/ton, European smelters face continued margin pressure, supporting global supply tightness and elevated LME prices.

**Risk to Growth Thesis:** Greenfield smelter construction typically experiences cost overruns and schedule delays. The Oklahoma project has no operating precedent in modern US aluminum history. Execution risk is high.

## Valuation Assessment

At 130x trailing earnings and a $5.55B market cap, CENX is priced for a future state that requires: (1) Oklahoma smelter completion on time and budget, (2) sustained Midwest premiums above historical averages, (3) LME aluminum staying above $2,300-2,500/ton, and (4) no recession-driven demand destruction.

**Analyst Targets:** B. Riley raised to $68 (Buy), BMO Capital also raised its target (specific number not given in the snippet but the article references a consensus increase to $63.31 as of February 25). The consensus average of $63.31 implies ~16% upside from the current ~$55, but this is a narrow margin of safety for a commodity stock at 130x earnings.

**Comparable Context:** Primary aluminum producers typically trade at 5-10x EBITDA at mid-cycle. If we assume $900M annualized EBITDA (generous, based on management's peak-quarter guidance) and apply 6x, we get a $5.4B EV — roughly equal to the current market cap. This means the stock is fairly valued only if Q1 2026 represents normalized EBITDA, which for a cyclical commodity producer is a heroic assumption.

**Implied Expectations:** The market appears to be pricing in normalized EBITDA of $800M-1B, which requires sustained commodity prices, successful capacity ramp, and Midwest premiums persisting. Any mean-reversion in aluminum prices would compress this dramatically.

## Competitive Landscape

Century operates in the global primary aluminum market dominated by Alcoa (AA), Rio Tinto, Rusal, and Chinese state-owned producers. Century's competitive position rests on:

1. **US Domestic Premium:** Section 232 tariffs give US smelters pricing power versus imports. This is a policy moat, not a structural moat — it can be altered by future administrations.
2. **Iceland Operations:** Grundartangi benefits from cheap, renewable power — a genuine cost advantage in a world where energy costs for European smelters have risen sharply.
3. **Oklahoma DOE Support:** Government backing reduces but doesn't eliminate the capital cost of the new smelter.

Century remains a relatively small player globally. Its competitive position is meaningfully dependent on trade policy and energy costs — both of which are volatile inputs in the current macro regime.

## Risk Assessment

**Insider Selling — Critical Signal:** Between January 23 and March 12, 2026, insiders and major shareholders sold approximately $343M worth of stock:
- Glencore (10% owner): 6.3M shares at $51.75 = $327M (reducing stake from ~18.7M to 12.2M shares — a 34% reduction)
- CEO Jesse Gary: 150K shares at $48.19 = $7.2M
- EVP Global Operations: 43K shares = $2.3M
- EVP General Counsel: 66K shares = $3.3M
- SVP Human Resources: 18K shares = $1.0M
- SVP/CAO Hoffman: 20.5K shares across two sales = $1.2M
- SVP Strategy: 15.1K shares = $793K
- SVP/CCO: 22.9K shares = $1.2M

Seven different C-suite/SVP-level officers sold in a two-month window, plus the largest shareholder dumped a third of its position. This is the strongest insider selling signal possible. When the people running the company and its largest shareholder are all liquidating simultaneously at $48-58, the market should pay attention.

**Commodity Cyclicality:** Aluminum is one of the most cyclically sensitive industrial metals. In the 2008-2009 downturn, LME aluminum fell from $3,300 to $1,250. In a recession scenario (38-42% probability per our macro framework), demand destruction would compress prices and margins simultaneously.

**Energy Cost Exposure:** Smelting is extremely energy-intensive. While Iceland operations use cheap hydro/geothermal, US smelters depend on contracted power rates. The Oklahoma smelter's power sourcing is a key unknown. Rising energy costs in a $100+ oil environment could compress margins at US facilities.

**Accounting Complexity:** The Jamalco consolidation transition has delayed the annual report filing. While likely a technical matter, it reduces transparency at a time when the stock is near all-time highs.

**Execution Risk:** The Oklahoma greenfield smelter is the core of the long-term bull thesis. Any delays, cost overruns, or permitting issues would directly undermine the valuation premium.

**Macro Sensitivity:** In our confirmed stagflationary regime with -92K payrolls, 3.1% core PCE, and $100+ oil, industrial metals demand faces headwinds. Construction and automotive — key aluminum end markets — are both under pressure.

## Investment Thesis

### Bull Case
- Oklahoma smelter comes online on schedule (~2028-2029), adding 500K+ tons of capacity at competitive costs with DOE support
- LME aluminum sustains above $2,500/ton due to structural supply deficits (European smelter closures, Chinese production caps)
- Midwest premium persists at elevated levels under current trade policy
- Mt. Holly expansion contributes near-term volume growth to 630K tons in 2026
- Q1 2026 EBITDA of $215-235M represents a step-function increase in earnings power
- B. Riley $68 target implies ~24% upside

### Bear Case
- 130x trailing P/E prices in perfection; any shortfall in execution, commodity prices, or tariff policy causes significant multiple compression
- Unprecedented insider selling cluster — seven officers plus Glencore reducing by $343M — is the strongest possible signal that informed parties view the stock as fully valued or overvalued
- Recession probability of 38-42% would crush aluminum demand and prices; historical precedent: aluminum fell 62% in 2008
- Oklahoma smelter execution risk over multi-year construction timeline with potential for cost overruns
- Trade policy is a political variable; Section 232 tariffs could be modified or removed
- No dividend; no buyback program; shareholders entirely dependent on price appreciation
- Financial statements unavailable for independent verification of balance sheet health

## Conclusion

Century Aluminum has a legitimate long-term growth story with the Oklahoma smelter and supportive trade policy. The problem is timing and price. At 130x trailing earnings with the broadest insider selling cluster I've documented in a mid-cap industrial name, the risk-reward is unfavorable. The stock has already risen 182% in 12 months, discounting most of the positive catalysts. Commodity stocks at cycle-peak pricing premiums with mass insider selling are historically poor entry points.

The calibration data shows cyclical names at elevated valuations are where BUY calls have failed most catastrophically. CENX fits this profile precisely: economically sensitive, commodity-dependent, trading at an extreme multiple, with insiders liquidating. The correct posture is to wait for either (a) a meaningful pullback to $30-35, where the valuation reflects realistic normalized earnings, or (b) demonstrable progress on the Oklahoma smelter that de-risks the execution timeline. Initiating or adding at $55 with 130x P/E and $343M in insider sales is not justified by the available data.

**Recommendation: AVOID at current levels.**
