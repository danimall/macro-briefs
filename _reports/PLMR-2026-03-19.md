## Executive Summary

Palomar Holdings is a specialty insurance company focused on earthquake, crop, surety, and other niche lines, operating primarily through a fee-based platform with substantial reinsurance cession. The company reported Q4 2025 operating EPS of $2.24, beating consensus by ~10%, on revenue of $251M (+59.9% YoY). Full-year 2025 results showed net income of $56.2M in Q4 alone, and the company guided FY2026 adjusted net income to $260-275M including $8-12M of expected catastrophe losses.

The stock currently trades at $119.05, down 33% from its 52-week high of $175.85, and at approximately 16.6x trailing/forward earnings on $7.17 EPS. JPMorgan raised its price target to $160 in late February 2026. The combination of strong premium growth, a debt-free balance sheet, and expanding product lines (crop, surety) supports the growth thesis, but catastrophe tail risk, the current macro environment (rising reinsurance costs from geopolitical disruption), and the stock's sharp decline create competing signals. The position merits holding at current levels with a potential upgrade to BUY if the stock approaches $107-110 or if Q1 2026 results confirm guidance trajectory.

## Company Overview

Palomar Holdings is a La Jolla, California-based specialty insurer listed on NASDAQ. The company underwrites earthquake, hurricane, flood, crop, and surety insurance products. Its business model emphasizes a fronting/fee-based platform where substantial risk is ceded to reinsurers, limiting balance sheet exposure to catastrophe events relative to gross written premiums. Market capitalization is approximately $3.1B on ~26.7M shares outstanding.

The company has been publicly traded since 2019 and has consistently filed 10-K and 10-Q reports with the SEC. The most recent 10-K was filed February 24, 2026, covering FY2025.

## Financial Analysis

**Revenue and Earnings:** Q4 2025 revenue reached $251M, up 59.9% YoY, significantly exceeding the consensus estimate of $222M. Operating EPS of $2.24 beat the $2.02 estimate by 10.9%. For the prior quarter (Q3 2025), the company also beat by 8.7%. Full-year trailing EPS is $7.17.

**Forward Guidance:** FY2026 adjusted net income guided to $260-275M, implying approximately $9.74-$10.30 per share on the current share count. This represents 36-44% growth over FY2025 implied adjusted net income. The guidance embeds $8-12M of expected catastrophe losses, which is conservative relative to historical volatility.

**Balance Sheet:** Per the November 2025 article, Palomar operates with a debt-free balance sheet. For a specialty insurer with catastrophe exposure, zero debt is a meaningful positive. The company's reinsurance strategy limits net retained risk, which supports balance sheet durability through loss events.

**Profitability:** The company's fee-based platform model generates higher-margin revenue than traditional insurance underwriting because a significant portion of premium risk is transferred to reinsurers. The 47.4% YoY growth in operating EPS on 59.9% revenue growth suggests stable or slightly compressing margins, likely from mix shift as newer product lines (crop, surety) scale.

**Limitations:** Detailed income statement, balance sheet, and cash flow data were not available in the data feed, limiting granular margin, reserve, and capital analysis.

## Growth Analysis

Palomar's growth is driven by three primary vectors:

1. **Crop Insurance Expansion:** Rising crop premiums and expanding geographic footprint. This product line benefits from agricultural price inflation and climate volatility, both of which are elevated in the current macro environment.

2. **Surety Bond Growth:** The surety line is expanding as Palomar leverages its specialty underwriting capabilities into a fragmented market. Surety bonds are less correlated with catastrophe events, providing diversification.

3. **Fee-Based Platform Scaling:** As gross written premiums grow, Palomar's fronting fees and ceding commissions scale with relatively fixed overhead, creating operating leverage. Revenue growth of 59.9% in Q4 demonstrates this dynamic.

The FY2026 guidance midpoint of ~$267.5M adjusted net income implies ~40% growth. Sustaining this pace beyond 2026 will require continued product line expansion and/or favorable pricing cycles in catastrophe-exposed lines. The current hard market in specialty insurance supports near-term pricing power.

JPMorgan's $160 target implies ~34% upside from the current price and approximately 15.5-16.4x the FY2026 EPS guidance. This is reasonable but assumes no material catastrophe losses above the $8-12M guidance embed.

The sharpest single-day drop occurred on August 5, 2025 (from ~$131.5 to $112.3, ~15% intraday), which aligns with the Q2 2025 10-Q filing date and likely reflects catastrophe loss disclosure or guidance adjustment. A second notable drop occurred on October 1, 2025 (from ~$117 to $110), and more recently around the February 12 earnings release, when the stock gapped from $133 to $127 despite the earnings beat — suggesting the market had priced in more aggressive guidance or was reacting to some element of the results not captured in headline numbers.

## Competitive Landscape

Palomar operates in specialty insurance lines where barriers to entry include actuarial expertise (earthquake modeling), regulatory licenses, reinsurance relationships, and distribution networks. Key competitive dynamics:

- **Earthquake Insurance:** Palomar competes with the California Earthquake Authority (CEA) and a small number of private insurers. The CEA's pricing tends to be less flexible, giving private insurers an opportunity to capture commercial and higher-value residential risks.

- **Crop Insurance:** A larger, more competitive market dominated by players like Archer Daniels, Fairfax, and various MGAs. Palomar's entry is relatively recent, and scale matters for profitability.

- **Surety:** Fragmented market with major players including Travelers, Liberty Mutual, and CNA. Palomar's positioning as a specialty player gives it flexibility on deal structure.

- **Reinsurance Strategy:** Palomar's heavy use of reinsurance limits both downside risk and upside potential. In a hard reinsurance market (current environment, with Iran-driven tanker disruptions increasing global risk premiums), Palomar's reinsurance costs could rise, compressing margins.

The debt-free balance sheet and fee-based model provide structural advantages during periods of elevated catastrophe activity, as Palomar's net exposure is smaller than gross premiums suggest.

## Risk Assessment

**Catastrophe Tail Risk:** The primary risk for any earthquake/hurricane/flood insurer. The FY2026 guidance embeds only $8-12M of catastrophe losses. A major California earthquake or Atlantic hurricane season could produce losses multiples of this estimate. The stock's price action suggests the market periodically reprices this risk (August 2025 drop).

**Reinsurance Cost Inflation:** The current geopolitical environment (Hormuz disruption, global risk repricing) is driving reinsurance costs higher. Palomar's model depends on ceding risk at economic rates. If retrocession costs rise faster than primary pricing, margins compress.

**Macro Sensitivity:** In a stagflationary environment, crop insurance demand may increase (favorable), but commercial property values and insured exposures could decline in a recession (unfavorable). Mixed directional exposure.

**Insider Selling:** CEO Mac Armstrong sold approximately 5,000 shares (~$613K) on February 12, the day after the Q4 earnings release, at prices between $117.58 and $128.25. He retains ~343,000 shares (~$40M+ at current prices), so the sale represents <2% of his position. Multiple C-suite executives (CFO, President, CRO, CLO, COO) also sold shares in January-February 2026, though most sales appear related to RSU vesting tax obligations (sale volumes roughly equal to withholding percentages). The CEO's February 12 sales are more discretionary and occurred at significantly lower prices than the stock had traded at in prior months. This pattern is mildly negative but not alarming given the small proportion sold.

**Concentration Risk:** Heavy concentration in California earthquake exposure. Geographic diversification into crop and surety mitigates this but remains incomplete.

**Valuation Risk:** If FY2026 guidance proves optimistic (catastrophe losses exceed $12M, or premium growth slows), the stock could re-rate lower from an already depressed level.

## Investment Thesis

- Debt-free balance sheet provides resilience through catastrophe events and market volatility.

- Crop and surety expansion diversifies away from earthquake concentration.

## Conclusion

Palomar Holdings is a well-positioned specialty insurer with strong top-line growth, a debt-free balance sheet, and a differentiated fee-based model. Q4 2025 results demonstrated execution with revenue and EPS significantly beating estimates. The FY2026 guidance implies continued rapid earnings growth.

At $119.05 and ~12x forward earnings on guidance, the valuation is reasonable for the growth profile. However, the 33% decline from the 52-week high, the catastrophe tail risk inherent in the business, rising reinsurance costs in the current geopolitical environment, and the inability to fully verify balance sheet fundamentals from available data warrant caution. The stock would become compelling at $107-110 (the 52-week low area), where the forward P/E would compress to ~10.5x on guidance.

**Recommendation: HOLD.** The valuation is attractive for patient investors willing to accept catastrophe tail risk, but the declining price trend and limited data visibility argue against initiating a new position at current levels. A retest of the $107-110 support zone or confirmation of FY2026 guidance trajectory in Q1 results would provide a more favorable entry point.
