## Executive Summary

United Airlines Holdings trades at $93.19 as of March 17, 2026, down 22% from its 52-week high of $119.21 reached in early January. The stock has a trailing P/E of ~9.1x on EPS of $10.20 and a market cap of $29.8B. Wall Street consensus targets average ~$138.56 (per 24/7 Wall St.), with TD Cowen at $128 and Bernstein reiterating a Buy. The stock appears statistically cheap on backward-looking earnings.

The problem is that those earnings were generated in a fundamentally different oil environment. With Brent at $98-108, Hormuz tanker traffic down 90%, SPR buffer at 9-14 days of coverage, and Saudi Arabia cutting production 20%, United's fuel cost structure has shifted materially higher. Jet fuel typically represents 25-30% of airline operating expenses, and every $10/barrel increase in oil reduces United's annual operating income by approximately $1.0-1.5B (based on ~4.5B gallons annual consumption). At $100+ oil sustained through 2026, the $10.20 EPS figure is stale. Simultaneously, the consumer spending contraction documented across multiple retailers (Lululemon, Macy's, Ulta) and negative NFP prints (-92K in February) directly threaten premium revenue per available seat mile (RASM), which has been United's key margin driver under its "United Next" strategy.

Delta Air Lines raised Q1 revenue guidance recently, which is the strongest contrary signal to a blanket airline AVOID thesis. However, Delta hedges fuel more aggressively and has lower international Middle East exposure than United. United has explicitly warned that surging fuel prices could affect short-term financial performance. The risk-reward at current levels does not adequately compensate for the probability-weighted downside scenarios.

## Company Overview

United Airlines Holdings is the parent of United Airlines, the third-largest US carrier by revenue. The company operates a hub-and-spoke network centered on Newark/New York, Chicago O'Hare, Denver, Houston, San Francisco, Los Angeles, and Washington Dulles. The "United Next" fleet modernization strategy has focused on premium cabin expansion and gauge increases to improve unit economics. The company has ~323M shares outstanding and no dividend.

Recent news highlights infrastructure improvements at Washington Dulles (positive for long-term hub economics) and management's presentation at the JPMorgan 2026 Industrials Conference, where CEO Scott Kirby characterized United as one of only "2 brand loyal airlines" in the US, arguing the airline would outperform in a downturn due to premium customer stickiness.

## Growth Analysis

United's growth thesis centers on the "United Next" strategy: fleet modernization with larger-gauge aircraft (737 MAX 10, A321neo), premium cabin expansion, and hub densification. The Washington Dulles renovation improves long-term competitive positioning at a key hub.

Pre-conflict, United was generating strong revenue growth driven by:
1. Premium cabin revenue mix shift (Polaris business class, domestic first class)
2. International demand recovery
3. MileagePlus loyalty program monetization
4. Capacity discipline industry-wide

The Iran-Israel conflict disrupts multiple growth vectors simultaneously:
- International routes through/near the Middle East require re-routing, adding fuel burn
- Consumer spending contraction (confirmed by NFP -92K, retail sales -0.2%) threatens premium demand elasticity
- Aggressive capex plans for fleet modernization become riskier when cash flow is compressed by fuel costs
- Kirby's argument that "brand loyal airlines" outperform in downturns is plausible for relative outperformance but does not prevent absolute earnings declines

The Seeking Alpha article from March 4 explicitly flags "heightened risks from Middle East conflict, rising fuel costs, and aggressive capacity and capex plans" as the core bear thesis.

## Valuation Assessment

At $93.19 with $10.20 trailing EPS, UAL trades at 9.1x — cheap relative to its own history (typically 6-12x through cycles) and relative to the S&P 500. Wall Street targets range from approximately $86 (implied by the recent selloff floor) to $138.56 (consensus mean per 24/7 Wall St.), with TD Cowen at $128 (reduced from higher).

However, the trailing multiple is misleading because:
1. Fuel costs at $98-108 oil mechanically reduce EPS by $3-5/share relative to the period that generated the $10.20
2. Recession probability at 38-42% (per world model) would compress both RASM and load factors
3. Airlines historically trade at P/E compression during fuel spikes because the market correctly anticipates earnings declines before they appear in reported numbers

**Scenario analysis:**
- **Bull case** (oil returns to $75-85, conflict resolves): EPS ~$11-12, stock at 9-10x = $99-120. Upside 6-29%.
- **Base case** (oil sustained $95-105, mild recession): EPS ~$5-7, stock at 8-9x = $40-63. Downside 32-57%.
- **Bear case** (oil >$110, recession, demand destruction): EPS ~$2-4, stock at 7-8x = $14-32. Downside 66-85%.

Probability-weighting these scenarios (bull 15%, base 55%, bear 30%) yields an expected value of approximately $42-58, which is below the current price. The asymmetry is unfavorable — the upside scenario requires geopolitical resolution that current intelligence suggests has only 5% probability of occurring rapidly.

## Competitive Landscape

United competes primarily with Delta Air Lines (DAL), American Airlines (AAL), and Southwest Airlines (LUV) domestically, with significant international competition from European and Asian carriers.

**Relative positioning:**
- Delta has stronger fuel hedging and has demonstrated more resilient revenue guidance (raised Q1 guidance despite absorbing $400M war impact). This makes DAL the strongest relative performer in the group.
- American Airlines (AAL) is the most leveraged US carrier ($30B+ debt, no fuel hedging, 62x trailing P/E on $0.17 EPS) — clearly worse positioned.
- Southwest has lower international exposure but similar domestic fuel cost sensitivity.
- United's hub strategy at Newark and San Francisco creates competitive moats for premium transcontinental and international traffic.

Kirby's claim about "2 brand loyal airlines" (United and Delta) has merit — these carriers have invested most heavily in premium product, loyalty programs, and credit card partnerships that generate high-margin ancillary revenue. In a moderate downturn, premium demand may prove stickier than leisure demand. In a severe downturn, premium demand also contracts.

## Risk Assessment

**Primary risks (ranked by probability × impact):**

1. **Fuel cost escalation (CRITICAL):** Oil at $98-108 with upside skew to $115-130. SPR buffer at 9-14 days. Allied coalition for Hormuz security refused. Saudi production cut removes 2M bpd. Every $10/barrel increase costs United ~$1.0-1.5B annually. The world model assigns 65-75% probability to SPR exhaustion, with potential increase to 75-80% if Russian cooperation deteriorates post-Bushehr strike.

2. **Consumer demand destruction (HIGH):** NFP -92K, unemployment 4.4%, retail sales -0.2%, CPI heading toward 3.2-5.2%. Stagflation directly compresses both business travel budgets (corporate cost-cutting) and leisure demand (higher gasoline prices crowd out discretionary spending on air travel).

3. **Capex execution risk (MODERATE):** United's fleet modernization requires significant capital expenditure. Cash flow compression from fuel costs reduces financial flexibility to fund these investments without additional leverage. The company's balance sheet likely carries substantial pandemic-era debt (industry-wide, airlines took on $100B+ in 2020-2021).

4. **Geopolitical duration risk (HIGH):** The conflict duration probability distribution assigns only 5% to rapid de-escalation. Acute phase extending beyond 6 weeks has 39% probability. Continued escalation (Iraq theater) at 20%. United has direct exposure through Middle East route disruptions and indirect exposure through global fuel markets.

5. **No fuel hedging visibility:** Unlike Delta, United has historically maintained minimal fuel hedging. The company's explicit warning about fuel prices affecting short-term performance confirms limited hedging protection.

- Trailing EPS of $10.20 is materially overstated relative to the forward fuel cost environment — realistic 2026 EPS range of $5-7 under base case, $2-4 under bear case
- No fuel hedging program amplifies earnings volatility
- Aggressive capex in fleet modernization creates cash flow risk when operating cash flows are declining
- Airline stocks historically lose 40-60% during recessions with concurrent oil spikes (2008 precedent: UAL filed bankruptcy in the prior oil crisis)
- The "cheap" valuation is a function of stale consensus estimates that have not yet absorbed $100+ oil

## Conclusion

United Airlines is categorized as AVOID. The world model already classifies airlines as a high-conviction AVOID sector, and the UAL-specific analysis reinforces that classification. The 9x trailing P/E is a value trap: the denominator (earnings) is about to compress materially under sustained $98-108 oil, and the numerator (price) has not yet fully discounted this.

The strongest contrary signal is Delta's Q1 revenue guidance raise, which suggests premium air travel demand has near-term resilience. If Delta's bookings data is confirmed across the industry in Q2, the airline AVOID thesis would need re-evaluation, and Delta specifically could be reclassified to HOLD. United, however, lacks Delta's fuel hedging protection and faces more direct Middle East route exposure.

At $65-70 (roughly 10-12x on a stress-tested EPS of $5-7), the risk-reward would become more balanced. At $93 on trailing earnings of $10.20 that almost certainly cannot be replicated in the current environment, the stock is pricing in a benign outcome that has low probability.

**Score: 5.04/10 — AVOID**
