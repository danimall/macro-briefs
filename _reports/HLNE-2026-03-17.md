## Executive Summary

Hamilton Lane is a private markets investment management firm with ~$4.4B market cap, trading at $97.16 — down 46% from its 52-week high of $179.19 reached in late May 2025. The company reported Q3 FY2026 (December quarter) EPS of $1.55 vs. $1.28 consensus, a 21% beat, and forward P/E stands at approximately 17.5x on trailing EPS of $5.41. Despite the earnings strength, the stock has experienced accelerating decline since mid-February 2026, falling from ~$123 to $97 in the most recent four weeks, driven by broader macro concerns about private credit markets and alternative asset managers.

The insider activity is the most constructive signal in the dataset. On February 20, 2026 — when the stock was at ~$107 — both Co-CEOs (Erik Hirsch and Juan Delgado-Moreira), the COO (Andrea Kramer), and the Executive Co-Chairman (Mario Giannini) collectively purchased approximately $3.2M in open-market stock. Director David Berkman added $1.01M on February 24 at $101. Five executives buying simultaneously during a selloff is a strong conviction indicator. However, this buying occurred before the most recent leg down to $92.77 (March 12 low), and the stock has only partially recovered to $97.16.

The fundamental challenge is that Hamilton Lane operates directly in the crosshairs of the private credit stress cycle the world model tracks at 55-60% cascade probability, with March 31 quarter-end 14 days away. Jim Cramer's recent dismissal — "way too levered to private credit" — reflects the consensus concern. Hamilton Lane's own market overview asserts "there is no private credit bubble," which is either correct or a self-interested denial of risk. The stock warrants a HOLD: the insider buying and earnings quality prevent an AVOID, but the macro environment for private credit exposure prevents a BUY.

## Company Overview

Hamilton Lane is one of the largest private markets investment management firms globally, providing advisory services, fund investments, co-investments, and direct investments across private equity, private credit, real estate, infrastructure, and other alternative asset classes. The firm manages and supervises approximately $130B+ in assets under management and advisement (based on recent public commentary, though exact AUM figures are not in the provided financial statements).

The company operates an asset-light model: management fees provide recurring revenue, and incentive fees/carried interest provide upside tied to fund performance. This dual revenue structure creates earnings volatility — the Q3 beat of $1.55 vs. $1.28 likely reflects favorable incentive fee recognition — while the management fee base provides stability.

Hamilton Lane went public in 2017 and has a dual-class share structure. The current share count of ~43.9M Class A shares outstanding implies the $4.4B market cap, though total economic interest is distributed across both classes.

## Growth Analysis

Hamilton Lane benefits from the secular trend of institutional and retail capital flowing into private markets. AUM growth has been consistently strong over the past several years, driven by:

1. **Institutional mandate wins** — pension funds, endowments, and sovereign wealth funds increasing private markets allocations
2. **Retail/wealth channel expansion** — Hamilton Lane has been building products for non-institutional investors, a significant TAM expansion
3. **Technology/data platform** — the firm's Cobalt data analytics platform provides private markets data that creates client stickiness

The Q3 FY2026 earnings beat suggests operating momentum remains intact despite the macro selloff. The $500M VFN Holdings investment announced February 23 demonstrates continued deployment activity.

**Growth risks:** Private credit and private equity fundraising decelerate during risk-off environments and credit stress. If the credit cascade scenario plays out, institutional allocators may slow commitments to private markets funds, directly impacting Hamilton Lane's management fee growth and future incentive fee generation.

Unlike BX, KKR, or Apollo, which are primarily fund sponsors/GPs, Hamilton Lane's roots are in advisory and fund-of-funds, giving it a more diversified exposure across the private markets ecosystem. This model is inherently less concentrated in any single strategy or vintage.

- Advisory relationships with 600+ institutional clients across 100+ countries

**Competitive risks:**

## Risk Assessment

**1. Private Credit Cascade (PRIMARY RISK):** The world model identifies 55-60% probability of a private credit cascade, with March 31 quarter-end as a structural stress point. Hamilton Lane has direct exposure: (a) as a manager/advisor to private credit allocations, (b) through incentive fee sensitivity to credit fund returns, and (c) through potential slowing of new capital commitments if investors pull back. Jim Cramer's "way too levered to private credit" comment, while simplistic, captures the market's key concern. Hamilton Lane's own assertion that "there is no private credit bubble" is a data point — either they have better visibility into the market than consensus, or they are talking their book.

**2. Macro/Stagflation Sensitivity:** In a confirmed stagflationary environment with recession probability at 38-42%, private markets valuations face headwinds. Higher discount rates compress PE portfolio valuations, reducing NAV and therefore reducing AUM-based fees. Incentive fees dry up when funds are not generating positive performance above hurdle rates.

**3. Stock Volatility:** Daily volume has been elevated (frequently 800K-1.9M shares vs. 577K average) since mid-February, indicating institutional repositioning. The March 12 low of $92.77 on 1.97M shares was the highest volume day in the dataset, suggesting potential capitulation.

**4. Dual-Class Structure:** The dual-class share structure concentrates voting power with founders/insiders. While this is common in alternative asset managers, it limits minority shareholder influence.

**5. Earnings Volatility:** Incentive fee recognition is inherently lumpy. The Q3 beat may not be repeatable, and in a deteriorating private markets environment, incentive fees could decline materially in coming quarters.

## Conclusion

**Recommendation: HOLD**

Hamilton Lane is a high-quality private markets franchise trading at a compressed multiple following a 46% decline. The insider buying cluster is the strongest positive signal in the dataset and warrants close monitoring. However, the macro environment — specifically the 55-60% private credit cascade probability with the March 31 quarter-end approaching — creates a risk profile incompatible with a BUY recommendation at this time.

The stock could be a compelling BUY if: (1) March 31 quarter-end passes without semi-liquid fund gating by major alternative managers (ARES, KKR, BX), (2) Q4 FY2026 earnings (expected ~May 2026) confirm sustained management fee growth, or (3) the stock retests the $92.77 low on volume, holds, and builds a base.

Current entry at $97.16 offers potential upside toward the $150 UBS target (54%) but carries material drawdown risk to $80-85 if the credit cascade scenario intensifies. The risk/reward is approximately balanced, which is the definition of HOLD. Investors with existing positions should maintain them given insider conviction; new capital should wait for either a resolution of credit stress or a lower entry point that provides more margin of safety.
