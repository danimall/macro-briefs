## Executive Summary

Zions Bancorp is a mid-cap regional bank (~$7.9B market cap) headquartered in Salt Lake City, operating across seven Western and Southwestern US states. The bank delivered solid 2025 results with net revenues up 8.1% year over year to $3.43 billion, driven by higher net interest income and fee income. At $54.02 per share, the stock trades at approximately 9x both trailing and forward earnings with a 3.4% dividend yield, and the CEO purchased $266K in stock on February 24, 2026.

The core tension is between a reasonable valuation for a well-run regional bank and the macro environment. The world model identifies a 38-42% recession probability for Q1-Q2 2026, and Zions already experienced a sharp selloff in October 2025 (from ~$56 to ~$47 in a single session on October 16) driven by private credit exposure concerns. The March 6, 2026 news confirms a second selloff on the same thesis. With mortgage rates at 7-month highs, CRE exposure across Western markets, and a Fed unable to cut, the growth trajectory that powered 2025's results is unlikely to accelerate. Baird's March 9 upgrade to Outperform with a 22% upside target provides a contrary bullish signal, but does not override the credit cycle risk.

## Company Overview

Zions Bancorporation, N.A. operates as the holding company for Zions First National Bank and its subsidiaries, providing commercial banking, retail banking, and wealth management services. The bank operates through subsidiary brands including Zions Bank, California Bank & Trust, Amegy Bank (Texas), National Bank of Arizona, Nevada State Bank, Vectra Bank Colorado, and The Commerce Bank of Washington/Oregon. Total assets are approximately $87 billion based on the most recent 10-K filing (February 24, 2026).

The geographic footprint spans high-growth Western US markets (Utah, Texas, California, Arizona, Nevada, Colorado, Washington/Oregon), which provides both secular tailwinds from population migration and concentrated exposure to CRE and energy-linked economies.

## Growth Analysis

The 8.1% revenue growth in 2025 was primarily driven by NII expansion as the rate environment remained elevated. This growth rate is above the regional banking peer average of approximately 4-6% for the period. Key growth drivers and constraints:

**Tailwinds:**
- Western US markets continue to benefit from population inflows (Utah, Texas, Arizona in particular).
- Elevated rate environment supports NIM if deposit costs are managed.
- Fee income diversification (wealth management, treasury management, capital markets).

**Headwinds:**
- The FOMC is unlikely to cut rates in 2026 (20-25% probability of zero cuts), which means mortgage origination volumes will remain depressed. The 19% refinance demand collapse noted in the world model directly impacts ZION's mortgage banking revenue.
- Commercial real estate stress is building nationally, and Western US office/multifamily markets are among the most exposed.
- Oil at $98-108 creates a double-edged effect: supportive for Amegy Bank's Texas energy lending, but inflationary drag on consumer borrowers.
- Loan growth will decelerate if the economy enters recession (38-42% probability).

Forward P/E matching trailing P/E at 9x implies the market expects no earnings growth in 2026. This is a reasonable base case given tightening credit conditions and slowing loan demand.

## Valuation Assessment

At 9x earnings with a 3.4% yield, ZION is priced at a modest discount to the KBW Regional Banking Index (which typically trades at 10-12x forward earnings). The discount reflects:

1. The 2023 regional banking crisis residual stigma (SVB, Signature, First Republic).
2. CRE concentration risk in Western markets.
3. Private credit exposure concerns (two selloff episodes).
4. Smaller asset base (~$87B) relative to super-regional peers.

The valuation provides some downside protection. At 7x forward earnings (recession trough multiple for regional banks), the stock would trade at approximately $42 — consistent with the April 2025 trough of $39.32. At 11x earnings (recovery multiple), the stock would trade at $66 — consistent with the February 2026 peak. The current $54 price sits squarely at the midpoint of this range.

Baird's March 9, 2026 upgrade to Outperform with an implied price target of ~$66 (22% upside from February 25 levels) provides a credible bull case but requires credit quality to remain stable and NIM to expand — both assumptions that face macro headwinds.

## Competitive Landscape

Zions operates in a competitive but regionally differentiated market. Key positioning:

**Strengths:**
- Multi-state franchise across high-growth Western markets with established brand names.
- Technology platform modernization completed (core system conversion), reducing long-term operating costs.
- Diversified loan book across CRE, C&I, and consumer segments with geographic spread.

**Weaknesses:**
- Lacks the scale economies of super-regionals (PNC, US Bank, Truist, Regions).
- Geographic concentration: all seven markets are in the Western US, which limits diversification against regional downturns.
- No meaningful moat beyond relationship banking and local brand recognition — switching costs for commercial borrowers are moderate.

Peer comparison (approximate): ZION at 9x P/E vs. PNFP at ~12x, WTFC at ~11.5x, FHN at ~10x. The discount is partially justified by the smaller scale and CRE concentration but appears slightly wide given the 2025 revenue growth performance.

## Risk Assessment

**Credit Cycle Risk (HIGH):**
The world model identifies 55-60% probability of a credit cascade, with March 31, 2026 as a structural stress point. ZION has already been sold twice on private credit concerns. If broader credit stress materializes, regional banks with CRE exposure will be disproportionately affected. The mechanism: rising rates → CRE valuations fall → borrowers unable to refinance → charge-offs increase → provisioning expense compresses earnings.

**Recession Risk (ELEVATED):**
At 38-42% recession probability, ZION's earnings are vulnerable. In the 2020 recession, ZION's EPS fell from $4.74 (2019) to $2.21 (2020) — a 53% decline. Even a milder downturn could compress EPS to $4.00-4.50, implying 25-33% downside to current earnings.

**Interest Rate Risk:**
ZION historically benefits from higher rates through NIM expansion, but the current rate regime is paired with stagflation, meaning credit quality deterioration could offset NIM gains. The Fed's inability to ease also means no relief for distressed borrowers in ZION's markets.

**Technology Transition Risk:**
Jennifer Smith's departure as CTOO after 20 years creates continuity risk. The replacement structure (two-person leadership partnership) is untested. The timing — immediately following the core system conversion — is either fortunate (project complete) or concerning (institutional knowledge departing).

**Private Credit Exposure:**
The October 16 and March 6 selloffs were specifically attributed to private credit market concerns. Without detailed balance sheet data on specific exposures, the magnitude of this risk is difficult to assess, but the market's reaction indicates it is perceived as material.

- Two selloffs on private credit concerns suggest the market perceives hidden risks in the loan book.
- CRE concentration in Western US markets — office/multifamily valuations declining.

- Credit cascade probability at 55-60% creates systemic risk for all regional banks; ZION's smaller size makes it more vulnerable to deposit flight in a stress scenario.
- No Fed rate cuts means no relief valve for distressed borrowers.
- CTOO departure introduces execution risk at a critical technology juncture.

## Conclusion

ZION is a reasonably valued regional bank with genuine franchise value across Western US growth markets. The 2025 revenue performance was strong, the CEO is buying stock, and Baird's upgrade provides institutional support. However, the macro environment creates substantial headwinds: stagflation compressing both the numerator (credit losses reduce earnings) and denominator (no rate cuts means no multiple expansion). The private credit selloff episodes in October 2025 and March 2026 suggest the market has identified vulnerabilities in the loan book that are not fully visible in the available data.

At 9x earnings, much of the pessimism is priced in, but the downside to a 7x recession trough (~$42) represents a 22% decline from current levels, while the upside to an 11x recovery multiple (~$66) represents 22% upside. This symmetric risk/reward profile, combined with the uncertain credit cycle and elevated recession probability, supports a HOLD recommendation. The stock would become a more compelling BUY below $45 (7.5x earnings), where the risk/reward tilts more decisively positive, or if credit quality data from the Q1 2026 earnings report (expected April 2026) demonstrates stability.

The 3.4% dividend yield provides modest income while waiting for clarity on the credit cycle. Position sizing should reflect the bank's sensitivity to the credit cascade risk (55-60% probability) identified in the world model.
