## Executive Summary

MGE Energy is a small-cap regulated electric and gas utility holding company serving Madison, Wisconsin and surrounding communities. The company reported FY2025 GAAP EPS of $3.72, up 11.7% from $3.33 in 2024, supported by a return to normal weather patterns and ongoing regulated investments in renewable energy. The stock has declined 21.8% over the past 12 months, falling from ~$92.65 in mid-March 2025 to $72.45 currently, now trading just $0.28 above its 52-week low.

At a forward P/E of ~19.5x and a 2.55% dividend yield, MGEE trades at a modest discount to larger utility peers but offers limited growth acceleration potential given its small service territory and regulated return structure. The current macro environment — with the Fed discussing rate hikes and oil at $119 driving multi-channel inflation — creates two opposing forces: utilities benefit from defensive positioning during stagflation, but rising real yields compress the present value of their regulated cash flows and make their dividend yields less competitive relative to risk-free alternatives. Morgan Stanley's February price target raise to $77 (from $73) provides a thin margin of upside from the current $72.45 price, insufficient to justify a BUY recommendation.

## Company Overview

MGE Energy operates through its subsidiary Madison Gas and Electric Company, providing regulated electric and natural gas service to approximately 160,000 customers in Dane County, Wisconsin. The company has approximately $1.7 billion in rate base and operates under Wisconsin's constructive regulatory framework, which supports recovery of prudent investments. MGEE is classified as a Dividend King, having increased its dividend for 50+ consecutive years. The company has been investing in renewable energy assets, including solar and wind projects, as part of its long-term decarbonization strategy.

Market cap stands at $2.71 billion with 36.6 million shares outstanding. This is a micro-cap utility by sector standards — for context, NextEra Energy's market cap exceeds $150 billion.

## Financial Analysis

**Earnings:** FY2025 GAAP earnings were $135.9 million, or $3.72 per share, up from $3.33 in FY2024. Q4 2025 EPS was $0.64 vs. $0.61 a year ago, meeting consensus. The 11.7% YoY EPS growth is above the typical 4-6% utility growth rate and was driven by weather normalization and rate base expansion.

**Valuation metrics:**
- Forward P/E: 19.5x (trailing P/E identical, implying flat consensus EPS expectations for FY2026)
- Market cap: $2.71 billion
- Dividend yield: 2.55%
- Price/52-week high: 76.9% (trading 23.1% below the high of $94.22)

**Balance sheet and cash flow:** Detailed financial statements were not available in the data set. However, regulated utilities typically operate with 50-60% debt-to-capital ratios. Morningstar notes the $1.7 billion rate base and constructive Wisconsin regulation, which supports investment recovery. The Dividend King status (50+ years of consecutive increases) implies conservative financial management and adequate cash flow coverage.

**Key concern:** The identical forward and trailing P/E (both 19.5x) implies the market expects zero EPS growth in FY2026. After a strong FY2025 boosted by weather normalization, FY2026 may represent a reversion to the company's typical low-to-mid single digit growth trajectory.

## Growth Analysis

MGE Energy's growth is structurally constrained by its small, single-state service territory. Growth drivers include:

1. **Rate base expansion:** Investments in renewable energy (solar, wind) are expanding the rate base from the $1.7 billion level. Morningstar specifically cites "Renewable Energy Investments Drive MGE Energy's Growth."

2. **Regulatory recovery:** Wisconsin's regulatory environment is considered constructive, meaning prudent capital investments are typically recoverable through rate adjustments with reasonable lag.

3. **Population growth in Dane County:** Madison is a growing metro area anchored by the University of Wisconsin and state government, providing modest organic customer growth.

**Constraints:** (a) The service territory is geographically limited with no apparent acquisition strategy to expand. (b) Regulated returns cap upside — the ROE allowed by Wisconsin regulators (typically 9.5-10.5%) limits earnings growth to roughly rate base growth plus customer growth. (c) The renewable energy investment theme, while directionally positive, involves capital-intensive projects that increase rate base but also require equity issuance or debt, diluting per-share metrics.

Realistic long-term EPS growth rate: 4-6% annually. FY2025's 11.7% was likely above-trend due to weather normalization.

## Valuation Assessment

At $72.45 and a 19.5x forward P/E, MGEE trades at a slight discount to large-cap utility peers (sector median ~20-22x forward P/E). However, small-cap utilities typically deserve a discount given lower liquidity, narrower analyst coverage, and concentration risk. The 2.55% dividend yield is below the utility sector average of ~3.0-3.5%.

Morgan Stanley raised its price target to $77 on February 20, 2026, from $73. This implies ~6.3% upside from the current price. No other analyst targets were available. The lack of broad analyst coverage is itself a risk factor for a stock of this size.

**Dividend yield vs. rising rates:** With the Fed now discussing rate hikes and 10-year Treasury yields elevated, a 2.55% dividend yield becomes less competitive. Utilities have historically underperformed during rate-hiking cycles because their yield spreads compress. If the Fed raises rates by 25-50bps, MGEE's yield spread over Treasuries narrows further, likely capping valuation multiples.

**Downside risk assessment:** At the 52-week low of $72.17, the stock is essentially at a floor. A break below $72 would represent new lows and could trigger additional selling. At 18x earnings (~$67), the stock would offer a 2.8% yield — still not compelling relative to risk-free rates in a hawkish Fed environment.

## Competitive Landscape

MGE Energy operates as a regulated monopoly in its service territory, which provides pricing stability but also limits growth. Key competitive dynamics:

- **Regulatory moat:** The regulated utility model provides a durable, if modest, competitive position. Customers cannot switch providers for electric and gas service.
- **Size disadvantage:** At $2.71 billion market cap, MGEE lacks the scale, diversification, and capital market access of larger peers like WEC Energy ($28B), Alliant Energy ($14B), or Xcel Energy ($35B), all of which also operate in the upper Midwest.
- **Renewable positioning:** The company's proactive investment in renewables aligns with Wisconsin's clean energy goals and may support constructive regulatory outcomes.
- **Limited diversification:** Single-state, single-metro exposure means weather, regulatory, or economic disruption in Dane County flows directly to earnings with no offset.

## Risk Assessment

**Rate sensitivity (HIGH):** The primary risk. With the Fed discussing rate hikes for the first time since the current cycle began, utility valuations face sustained pressure. MGEE's 2.55% yield provides minimal buffer. Each 25bps increase in the risk-free rate theoretically compresses utility multiples by 0.5-1.0x P/E, all else equal.

**Inflation passthrough (MODERATE):** Oil at $119 and diesel passthrough will raise operating costs (fleet fuel, materials, contractor costs). Regulated utilities can typically recover these costs through fuel adjustment clauses and rate cases, but with a lag of 6-18 months. During the lag, margins compress.

**Geographic concentration (MODERATE):** All revenue derives from the Madison, WI metropolitan area. A localized economic downturn, severe weather event, or adverse regulatory decision would have outsized impact.

**Regulatory risk (LOW-MODERATE):** Wisconsin regulation is historically constructive, but political shifts or consumer pressure during high-inflation periods could slow rate case approvals.

**Liquidity risk (MODERATE):** Average daily volume of ~135,000 shares. Large position changes are difficult to execute without market impact.

**Stagflation scenario (MIXED):** Utilities benefit from defensive demand characteristics during recessions but suffer from rate-hiking regimes during inflationary periods. The current stagflation environment — 40-45% recession probability with potential rate hikes — creates cross-cutting pressures on utilities.

## Insider Activity

## Conclusion

MGEE is a competently managed, small-cap regulated utility with predictable but limited growth prospects. The 21.8% decline from 52-week highs has improved the valuation, but not enough to overcome the headwinds from a potential rate-hiking cycle and below-average dividend yield. The stock is appropriate for income-oriented investors with a long time horizon and tolerance for limited upside, but the current macro regime — stagflation with rate hikes on the table — is structurally unfavorable for utilities trading at sub-3% yields.

The stock does not meet the criteria for BUY (insufficient upside relative to rate-sensitivity risk, limited growth ceiling) or AVOID (stable regulated business, no deteriorating trajectory, Dividend King pedigree). HOLD is the appropriate recommendation at $72.45.

**Entry point for upgrade consideration:** Below $67 (18x forward earnings, ~2.8% yield) or a clear Fed pivot back toward cuts.
