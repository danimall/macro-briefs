## Executive Summary

StepStone Group is a global private markets investment firm providing customized investment solutions, advisory, and data services across private equity, infrastructure, real estate, and private debt. The stock has fallen 40% from its January 2026 peak of $77.80 to $46.59, reflecting broader stress across alternative asset managers driven by deteriorating private credit conditions and a stagflationary macro backdrop. Barclays upgraded the stock to Overweight on March 2, 2026, with an average analyst target of $81.43 — implying ~75% upside — but this target was set before the most severe phase of the current selloff.

The company's GAAP financials show a trailing EPS of -$2.52, producing a negative P/E ratio. This is characteristic of alt-managers where GAAP earnings are distorted by mark-to-market adjustments on carried interest and investment holdings. The $100M share repurchase program announced March 9, 2026 and a director's $565K open-market purchase at ~$44 on February 23 signal internal confidence at current levels. However, StepStone operates at the intersection of two macro risk factors that dominate the current environment: private credit cascade risk (55-60% probability per world model) and depressed private equity exit activity in a recessionary setup. These structural headwinds make a BUY call premature despite the selloff magnitude.

## Company Overview

StepStone Group (NASDAQ: STEP) is a private markets specialist managing and advising on approximately $170B+ in capital allocations (per prior public disclosures). The firm operates across four asset classes: private equity, infrastructure, real estate, and private debt. Revenue comes from management fees (recurring, AUM-linked), advisory fees, and performance-based income (carried interest/incentive fees). The company went public in September 2020 and has a current market cap of approximately $3.75B. The dual-class share structure means the ~80M Class A shares outstanding represent a minority of total economic interest, with significant partnership interests retained by insiders.

## Growth Analysis

StepStone's growth is tied to three drivers:

1. **Private markets AUM growth:** Secular trend toward institutional allocation to private markets remains intact, but the pace of new fund commitments has slowed. The Bain & Company / StepStone "2026 Private Equity GP Outlook" released March 2 explicitly addresses "shifting deal dynamics and evolving liquidity conditions" — language consistent with a more challenging fundraising environment.

2. **Fee-related earnings expansion:** As the firm scales, management fee revenue should grow with AUM. However, the current macro regime (recession probability 38-42%, oil-driven stagflation) depresses private equity exit activity, which in turn reduces realizations and slows fund recycling into new commitments.

3. **Performance fee / carried interest recovery:** The negative GAAP EPS suggests carried interest accruals are declining or reversing. Recovery requires improved PE portfolio valuations, which depend on (a) lower interest rates (unlikely in 2026 per world model — 20-25% probability of no cuts), and (b) improved exit multiples (unlikely in stagflationary environment).

**Growth trajectory is positive on a 3-5 year basis but faces 12-18 months of headwinds.** The near-term path for both fundraising and realizations is constrained by the macro regime.

At $46.59 with a 2.49% dividend yield, the stock prices in significant pessimism. The question is whether that pessimism is sufficient given the credit cascade risk and fundraising headwinds.

## Competitive Landscape

StepStone occupies a differentiated position in private markets:

- **Advisory + investment hybrid model:** Unlike pure-play GPs (Blackstone, Apollo, KKR), StepStone combines fund-of-funds investing, co-investing, secondary investing, and advisory/data services. This creates multiple revenue streams with different cyclical profiles.
- **Data and analytics capability:** The firm's SPI (StepStone Private Markets Intelligence) platform provides proprietary data on GP performance, fund terms, and market dynamics. This creates switching costs for institutional clients.
- **Diversification across private asset classes:** PE, infrastructure, real estate, and private debt exposure reduces single-sector concentration vs. peers focused primarily on credit (Blue Owl) or PE (Blackstone).
- **Competitive threats:** Larger alt-managers (APO, BX, KKR) are building or acquiring advisory and data capabilities, potentially encroaching on StepStone's niche. Fee compression in private markets advisory is a long-term secular risk.

The Bain partnership for the GP Outlook report signals industry thought leadership, which supports the advisory and data franchise.

## Risk Assessment

**Primary risks, ranked by probability × impact:**

1. **Private credit cascade (55-60% probability, HIGH impact):** The world model identifies 22+ data points supporting credit cascade risk with a March 31 structural stress point 12 days away. StepStone has private debt exposure and invests in/advises on credit strategies. If semi-liquid fund redemptions are restricted at Ares or KKR, spillover to all alt-managers — including StepStone — is likely through both direct portfolio impact and sector sentiment contagion. This is the dominant near-term risk.

2. **PE exit freeze / fundraising slowdown (HIGH probability, MODERATE impact):** Stagflationary macro → compressed exit multiples → slower fund recycling → lower new commitments → lower management fees and performance fees. The Bain/StepStone report explicitly flags "evolving liquidity conditions."

3. **GAAP earnings volatility (ONGOING):** Negative EPS creates headline risk and limits the stock's inclusion in screens requiring positive earnings. Until portfolio marks stabilize, GAAP losses may persist or worsen.

4. **Rate sensitivity (MODERATE):** Higher-for-longer rates compress PE and real estate valuations, reducing AUM growth and carried interest accruals.

5. **Dual-class structure / governance (LOW-MODERATE):** Insider-controlled voting structure limits minority shareholder influence. The partnership structure means significant economics flow through units not reflected in the Class A share count, creating dilution complexity.

6. **Concentration risk:** Unknown without financial detail, but advisory mandates from large institutional clients (sovereign wealth funds, pension funds) create client concentration risk.

## Investment Thesis

- StepStone trades at a steep discount to its January 2026 levels and to analyst consensus ($81.43 average target). If credit cascade fears prove overdone and private markets fundraising stabilizes in H2 2026, the stock has 50-75% upside potential.
- The advisory and data business provides recurring, fee-based revenue less sensitive to market-to-market volatility than carried interest.

- $100M buyback + director buying at $44 establish a credible floor.
- Barclays upgrade to Overweight (March 2, 2026) explicitly favors STEP over OWL (downgraded), suggesting relative positioning within alt-managers.

### Bear Case
- Credit cascade at 55-60% probability directly threatens the alt-manager sector. March 31 quarter-end is 12 days away and represents a structural stress point for semi-liquid fund redemptions.
- Negative GAAP EPS of -$2.52 with negative forward P/E suggests portfolio writedowns are ongoing. In a stagflationary environment with no Fed rate cuts expected in 2026 (20-25% probability), PE and real estate valuations face continued pressure.
- The stock has already bounced from $40.07 to $46.59 — if cascade materializes, retest of lows or new lows is plausible.
- Private equity exit activity is depressed, constraining both realizations and new fund commitments. The Bain/StepStone report itself acknowledges "shifting deal dynamics."
- Alt-manager sector trading as correlated group means STEP cannot fully escape BX/OWL/ARES contagion regardless of its differentiated model.

## Conclusion

StepStone Group is a high-quality private markets franchise trading at distressed levels due to legitimate sector and macro headwinds. The 40% decline from January highs prices in significant pessimism, and the director's $565K open-market purchase at $44 plus the $100M buyback program suggest internal confidence that current prices undervalue the business. The Barclays upgrade to Overweight with an $81.43 target provides sell-side validation.

However, the timing is wrong for a BUY. The credit cascade probability (55-60%) with March 31 quarter-end imminent, negative GAAP earnings, a stagflationary macro regime hostile to PE exits, and sector contagion risk from distressed peers (Blue Owl insolvency concerns, Ares/KKR redemption gating risk) create a 3-6 month window where downside risks dominate. The stock at $46.59 is only 17% above its 52-week low of $40.07, meaning limited downside protection if cascade materializes.

**Recommendation: HOLD.** Wait for (a) March 31 quarter-end to pass without semi-liquid fund redemption restrictions, (b) evidence of GAAP earnings stabilization, or (c) a price level below $40 that compensates for cascade risk. The $100M buyback and director buying establish that management sees value here, but the macro and sector environment require patience. Entry at $38-42 (where the buyback should be most active and downside is more fully priced) would be more compelling.

**Why not BUY (score ≥6.2 gate):** The credit cascade probability directly impacts this stock's sector, GAAP earnings are negative with no near-term path to positive, and the March 31 structural stress point is imminent. The selloff is severe but may be incomplete if cascade materializes.
