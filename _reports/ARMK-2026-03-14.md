## Executive Summary

Aramark is a ~$10.5B market cap food and facilities services provider trading at $40 per share, approximately 33x trailing earnings and 32x forward earnings on FY2026 adjusted EPS guidance of $2.18-$2.28. The company reported Q1 FY2026 net income of $96.2M ($0.36 GAAP EPS, $0.51 adjusted EPS) on revenue that grew ~$280M year-over-year. Management reaffirmed full-year revenue guidance of $19.55-19.95B and the adjusted EPS range at the BofA conference on March 12.

The bull case centers on organic growth from new contract wins, analyst price targets clustering around $46-50 (15-25% upside from current levels), and the company's scale advantages in institutional food services. The bear case is straightforward: at 33x earnings, a food services company with historically thin margins and meaningful debt is priced for execution perfection in a stagflationary environment where oil at $100+ drives up food, transportation, and energy input costs simultaneously, while institutional clients (universities, corporations, healthcare systems) face their own budget pressures. The stock has limited margin of safety at current levels.

## Company Overview

Aramark provides food services, facilities management, and uniform services across institutional and corporate clients in the US and internationally. The business operates through a Food and Support Services segment serving education, healthcare, business/industry, sports/entertainment, and corrections/facilities markets. Revenue guidance of $19.55-19.95B for FY2026 implies the company is among the largest contract food service providers globally, competing with Compass Group and Sodexo.

The company completed its spin-off of the Uniform Services business (now Vestis Corporation) in late 2023, which simplified the business but also removed a steadier revenue stream. The remaining food services operation is more operationally leveraged to food input costs and labor.

- **Q1 FY2026:** Net income $96.2M, GAAP EPS $0.36, adjusted EPS $0.51 (in-line with consensus). Revenue increased $280M YoY.
- **FY2026 Guidance:** Revenue $19.55-19.95B vs. $19.71B consensus. Adjusted EPS $2.18-$2.28 vs. $2.21 consensus.
- **Dividend:** $0.12/quarter ($0.48 annualized), yielding ~1.2% at $40. Payout ratio is ~22% of guided adjusted EPS, which is conservative.
- **Market Cap:** $10.5B implies ~263M diluted shares outstanding.
- **Trailing P/E:** 33.2x; Forward P/E: 32.2x. The narrow gap between trailing and forward P/E indicates limited earnings growth is priced in.

The GAAP-to-adjusted EPS gap ($0.36 vs. $0.51 in Q1) is notable — a 42% differential that raises questions about the quality of the earnings adjustments. Without seeing the reconciliation items, this warrants caution.

**Balance sheet concerns:** Aramark has historically carried significant debt from its LBO history. The Vestis spin-off redistributed some leverage, but the remaining food services business still operates with a debt load that constrains financial flexibility. Without current balance sheet data, I'm estimating net debt in the $5-7B range based on historical filings, which would put net leverage at 3-4x EBITDA — elevated for a company facing input cost pressure.

## Growth Analysis

Q1 revenue growth of ~$280M on an approximate $4.8B quarterly base implies ~6% YoY top-line growth. Management commentary at the BofA conference emphasized organic growth and new contract wins. Analyst targets shifting toward $50 suggest the Street is pricing in continued mid-single-digit organic growth plus margin expansion.

Growth drivers include:
- **New contract wins** in education and healthcare, where outsourcing penetration continues to increase
- **Pricing power** to pass through food cost inflation to institutional clients (though this is constrained by multi-year contract structures)
- **Return to on-site work/dining** trends benefiting the business & industry segment

Growth constraints:
- **Food input cost inflation** from $100+ oil (transportation costs, agricultural inputs, packaging). The fertilizer price spike from the Iran conflict will flow through to food costs with a 3-6 month lag.
- **Labor cost pressure** persists in food service, though quit rate declines suggest some moderation.
- **Institutional client budgets** are under pressure — universities face enrollment challenges, healthcare systems face margin compression, and corporate clients are cutting costs.
- **Mid-single-digit revenue growth with flat-to-modest margin expansion yields low-single-digit EPS growth**, which is what the minimal trailing-to-forward P/E compression reflects.

## Valuation Assessment

At $40 per share:
- **Trailing P/E:** 33.2x
- **Forward P/E:** 32.2x
- **Dividend Yield:** 1.2%
- **EV/EBITDA:** Likely 13-15x based on estimated enterprise value of $16-18B and EBITDA in the $1.1-1.3B range

This valuation is rich for a food services company. Compass Group (CPG.L), the closest global peer, trades at approximately 25-28x forward earnings with stronger margins and a better balance sheet. Sodexo trades at a similar or lower multiple. Aramark's premium relative to its own historical average (which has typically been in the 15-25x range) reflects post-spin optimism about the simplified business model, but the current multiple leaves no room for earnings disappointment.

The analyst-derived fair value of $46.63 and price targets near $50 imply 16-25% upside, but these targets assume continued organic growth execution and margin expansion in an environment where food input costs are rising. If adjusted EPS comes in at the low end of guidance ($2.18) and the multiple compresses to 25x (still above historical average), downside is to ~$54.5... actually, that math doesn't work — $2.18 × 25 = $54.5, which is above current price. Let me reframe: the floor scenario is more concerning if earnings miss. At $2.00 adjusted EPS (a miss driven by input cost pressure) and 25x, the stock is worth $50. At $1.80 (a significant miss) and 20x (de-rating), you get $36, or 10% downside. The range is asymmetric but modestly skewed to the upside if management executes.

The problem is that 32-33x for a low-growth, leveraged food services company requires sustained confidence in margin expansion that the macro environment is actively undermining.

## Competitive Landscape

Aramark operates in a consolidated oligopoly with Compass Group and Sodexo. This structure generally supports rational pricing and limits destructive competition. Scale advantages are meaningful: procurement leverage over food suppliers, ability to serve multi-site institutional clients, and technology/operational platforms that smaller competitors cannot replicate.

Competitive risks:
- **Compass Group** is the stronger operator with higher margins, better organic growth, and a superior balance sheet. In client retention battles, Compass typically wins on service quality.
- **In-sourcing risk** exists, particularly in corporate dining where companies may bring food services back in-house or shift to micro-market/vending solutions.
- **Labor availability** is a competitive dimension — Aramark's ability to staff locations reliably relative to competitors determines contract retention.

Aramark's competitive position is defensible but second-tier relative to Compass. The company has a meaningful moat from switching costs (institutional food service contracts are 3-5 year, operationally sticky) but limited pricing power given the commodity nature of the service.

## Risk Assessment

**Macro sensitivity is the primary risk.** Aramark's cost structure is directly exposed to food commodity prices, energy costs (fleet, facilities), and labor. In the current environment:
- Oil at $98-108 (central range) directly increases transportation and food production costs
- Fertilizer price spikes from the Iran conflict will increase agricultural input costs with 3-6 month lag
- Labor costs remain elevated though moderating
- Institutional client budgets are tightening (GDP 0.7%, retail sales -0.2%)

**Balance sheet risk:** Elevated leverage from LBO history constrains the company's ability to absorb cost shocks. In a credit cascade scenario (55-60% probability per world model), Aramark's refinancing costs could increase materially.

**Contract structure risk:** Multi-year contracts with fixed or semi-fixed pricing create margin compression when input costs spike unexpectedly. The company's ability to renegotiate or pass through costs is limited in the near term.

**Beta of 1.09** indicates the stock moves roughly in-line with the market, providing limited defensive characteristics despite the "essential services" nature of the business.

**Consumer spending contraction** affects the sports/entertainment and business dining segments, which are more discretionary than education or healthcare food services.

## Investment Thesis

### Bull Case
- Analyst fair value of $46.63 and price targets near $50 imply 16-25% upside
- Organic growth from new contract wins in education and healthcare outsourcing
- Post-Vestis simplification allows management to focus capital allocation on the higher-growth food services business
- Oligopolistic industry structure supports rational pricing
- If macro stabilizes and oil retreats, input cost pressure eases and margins expand

### Bear Case
- 33x trailing earnings for a low-growth, leveraged food services company provides no margin of safety
- $100+ oil → higher food, transportation, and energy input costs that cannot be passed through on existing contracts for 6-12 months
- Stagflationary macro (GDP 0.7%, retail -0.2%) pressures institutional client budgets, slowing new contract wins
- Significant leverage amplifies downside in a credit tightening environment
- Adjusted EPS consistently runs 30-40%+ above GAAP EPS, suggesting earnings quality concerns
- Second-tier competitive position relative to Compass Group

## Conclusion

Aramark at $40 is a hold. The company has a defensible competitive position in a consolidated industry with secular outsourcing tailwinds, and analyst targets suggest 15-25% upside to fair value. However, the 33x forward P/E provides minimal margin of safety for a leveraged food services company facing rising input costs in a stagflationary macro environment. The GAAP-to-adjusted earnings gap warrants scrutiny.

The stock is not expensive enough to avoid outright — at $46-50 it would become an avoid — but the risk-reward at $40 is insufficiently compelling for a BUY given oil at $100+, food cost inflation accelerating, and GDP at 0.7%. Accumulate below $34 (25x forward, implying 15% downside from here) where the margin of safety becomes adequate for the risk profile.

**Why not BUY:** 33x forward earnings on a leveraged, input-cost-sensitive business entering a stagflationary environment with $100+ oil and contracting consumer spending. The 42% GAAP-to-adjusted EPS gap in Q1 suggests the "real" P/E is meaningfully higher. Upside to analyst targets exists but is offset by downside risk from margin compression that the current valuation does not reflect.
