## Executive Summary

MGIC Investment Corporation is the largest private mortgage insurer in the United States, providing mortgage default insurance that facilitates homeownership by enabling lenders to extend credit to borrowers with less than 20% down payments. The stock trades at $25.99 with a market capitalization of $5.5 billion, a trailing P/E of 8.3x, and a dividend yield of 2.3%.

The fundamental challenge for MTG in the current environment is a direct collision between its core business model and the prevailing macro regime. Mortgage rates at 3-month highs, a Fed that discussed rate hikes at its March FOMC meeting, NFP at -92K, and a 40-45% recession probability all bear directly on both the volume of new mortgage originations (revenue driver) and the credit quality of the existing insured portfolio (loss reserves). While MTG has historically managed through credit cycles and maintains a lean balance sheet relative to its pre-GFC structure, the current stagflationary backdrop with oil at $119 and rising real yields creates headwinds that are specifically hostile to housing-adjacent businesses.

The stock has declined from its 52-week high of $29.97 to $25.99, a 13.3% drawdown. At 8.3x earnings, some of this risk is priced in, but the macro trajectory suggests further earnings pressure is plausible before conditions improve.

## Company Overview

MGIC Investment Corporation, headquartered in Milwaukee, Wisconsin, operates through its principal subsidiary Mortgage Guaranty Insurance Corporation. The company provides private mortgage insurance (PMI) to lenders, covering losses when borrowers default on residential mortgages with loan-to-value ratios above 80%. MGIC is classified under Reinsurance in Yahoo Finance's taxonomy but is functionally a monoline mortgage insurer.

The company's revenue is driven by two factors: (1) net premiums earned on its insurance in force (IIF), which is a function of the existing book size and premium rates, and (2) net investment income on its investment portfolio. New insurance written (NIW) feeds into IIF over time as persistency (the rate at which policies remain in force) determines book runoff.

The company has ~215 million shares outstanding and trades on the NYSE.

## Growth Analysis

Growth prospects for MGIC are constrained by three factors operating simultaneously:

**1. Origination Volume Decline.** New purchase mortgage originations are directly sensitive to mortgage rates. With the Fed discussing rate hikes and mortgage rates at 3-month highs, purchase volume is under pressure. Census Bureau new home sales data has shown weakness, and the 19% refinance application collapse documented in the macro environment eliminates the refi channel for NIW.

**2. Persistency Benefit Partially Offsets Volume.** Higher rates reduce refinancing, which means existing policies stay in force longer. This supports IIF and premium income even as NIW declines. This is the standard "higher-for-longer benefits persistency" argument for mortgage insurers.

**3. Credit Cycle Risk.** With unemployment at 4.4%, NFP at -92K, and oil at $119, consumer balance sheets are deteriorating. Rising delinquencies would increase loss provisions and could offset premium income stability. The 40-45% recession probability makes this a material near-term risk.

The net effect: revenue is likely flat to modestly declining, with the persistency tailwind partially offset by lower NIW and rising loss provisions. Growth score reflects limited upside optionality in this environment.

## Competitive Landscape

The private mortgage insurance industry is an oligopoly with six major participants: MGIC, Radian (RDN), Essent (ESNT), NMI Holdings (NMIH), Arch Capital (mortgage insurance segment), and Enact (ACT). This concentrated structure supports pricing discipline.

MGIC benefits from:
- Scale as the largest PMI provider by insurance in force
- Long-standing relationships with major lenders
- GSE-approved status under PMIERs
- A simplified business model (pure-play mortgage insurer)

Risks to competitive position:
- The GSEs (Fannie Mae, Freddie Mac) could alter charter requirements for PMI, compressing demand
- Any GSE reform or privatization could restructure the entire PMI value chain
- FHA/VA programs compete for low-down-payment borrowers

The competitive moat is moderate. Barriers to entry are meaningful (capital requirements, GSE approvals, lender relationships), but the business is ultimately a commodity — lenders choose PMI providers primarily on price, service speed, and capital strength.

## Risk Assessment

**Housing Market Sensitivity (HIGH).** MTG is a pure-play housing bet. The world model explicitly categorizes homebuilders as "structural avoid" with reinforced conviction. While mortgage insurers are not homebuilders, they sit in the same value chain. Rising mortgage rates → lower origination volume → lower NIW → declining IIF over time. The current regime (rate hikes discussed, mortgage rates at 3-month highs) is hostile to MTG's revenue generation.

**Credit Cycle Risk (ELEVATED).** Unemployment at 4.4%, NFP -92K, oil at $119, and gasoline +21% in one month all pressure consumer ability to service mortgages. Default rates have been abnormally low post-COVID due to accumulated home equity and strong employment. A recession (40-45% probability) would increase delinquencies and claims. MTG's loss reserves would need to increase, compressing earnings.

**Interest Rate Risk (DIRECT).** Unlike P&C insurers that benefit from rising rates through higher float income, MTG faces mixed effects. Higher rates improve persistency (positive) but crush origination volume (negative) and eventually increase defaults through unemployment (negative). Net effect is likely negative in a stagflationary regime.

**Regulatory/GSE Reform Risk.** Any changes to GSE conservatorship, capital requirements, or PMI eligibility standards could materially alter MTG's business model. This is a perpetual overhang for the sector.

**Oil/Energy Passthrough.** $119 oil → higher gasoline costs → reduced consumer discretionary income → higher probability of mortgage delinquency, particularly for lower-income borrowers who disproportionately use PMI products.

## Investment Thesis

### Bull Case
- At 8.3x earnings, much of the housing weakness is already priced in
- Persistency benefit from higher rates supports premium income on the existing book
- Accumulated home equity from 2020-2024 price appreciation provides a loss buffer — even if defaults rise, loss severity may remain low due to positive equity positions
- Share buybacks continue to reduce share count, supporting EPS even in a flat-revenue environment
- 2.3% dividend yield provides income floor
- If recession fears prove overblown and employment stabilizes, the stock re-rates toward $28-30

### Bear Case
- Recession probability at 40-45% with oil at $119 would drive material increases in delinquencies and claims
- Fed rate hike discussion implies mortgage rates could go higher, further suppressing origination volume
- NIW decline eventually erodes IIF as existing policies run off
- Loss provisions could increase significantly — a return to even modest loss ratios (20-30%) from the current near-zero levels would compress EPS by $0.50-1.00
- The stock's proximity to its 52-week low ($21.94, only 18% below current) suggests limited downside support
- GSE reform or policy changes remain a tail risk

## Conclusion

MTG is a well-run mortgage insurer trading at a reasonable multiple in an environment that is specifically hostile to its business model. The 8.3x P/E provides some valuation cushion, and the persistency benefit from higher rates partially offsets volume headwinds. However, the combination of rising recession risk (40-45%), discussed rate hikes, $119 oil pressuring consumer finances, and the explicit "structural avoid" designation for housing-adjacent names in the current macro regime makes it difficult to build a constructive near-term thesis.

The insider activity is exclusively director grant/award transactions (standard annual compensation grants in March 2026), with no open-market purchases or sales — providing no signal on management conviction.

**Recommendation: HOLD.** The valuation is fair for current earnings, but earnings are likely at or near a cyclical peak given the credit environment. Upside is capped by the housing headwinds, and downside risk from a credit cycle turn is material. The stock is a reasonable hold for existing shareholders collecting the dividend, but the risk-reward does not justify new capital deployment in the current stagflationary regime with housing under structural pressure.

For the "Why Not BUY?" gate (score 5.92, below 6.2 threshold): The macro regime is directly hostile to this business model. Rising mortgage rates, discussed rate hikes, 40-45% recession probability, and $119 oil all pressure both revenue generation and credit quality simultaneously.
