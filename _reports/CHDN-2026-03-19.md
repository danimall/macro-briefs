## Executive Summary

Churchill Downs Inc reported record 2025 results — $2.93 billion in full-year revenue and $383 million in net income — driven by Kentucky Derby strength and Historical Racing Machine (HRM) facility expansion. The stock has fallen 27% from its December 2025 high of ~$118 to $85.94, compressing the forward P/E to approximately 16.1x. This selloff reflects both the broader market drawdown and a sharp post-earnings decline on February 26 (stock dropped from ~$96 to ~$90 in a single session), followed by continued weakness into March as HISA regulatory friction and macro headwinds accumulated.

The core investment question is whether the Kentucky Derby's irreplaceable franchise value and the HRM growth runway justify buying at current levels, or whether consumer spending deterioration in the current stagflationary environment, regulatory uncertainty around HISA fees, and limited visibility into balance sheet leverage argue for patience. The valuation is historically reasonable, but the data set is incomplete — no detailed income statement, balance sheet, or cash flow data was available beyond top-line figures from news sources. Given the macro regime (consumer contraction confirmed, stagflation across all six measured dimensions), a consumer-facing gaming and entertainment company faces meaningful demand headwinds even with a differentiated asset base.

## Company Overview

Churchill Downs Incorporated operates through three primary segments: (1) the Kentucky Derby and Churchill Downs Racetrack, (2) Gaming (including Historical Racing Machines, or HRMs, and traditional casino gaming), and (3) TwinSpires (online wagering platform). The company is headquartered in Louisville, Kentucky, trades on NASDAQ, and has approximately 69.7 million shares outstanding, yielding a market capitalization of roughly $6.0 billion at the current price.

The Kentucky Derby is a singular asset — one of fewer than five annual American sporting events with global brand recognition and multi-decade pricing power. CDI has leveraged this franchise into a broader gaming platform, with HRM expansion across Kentucky, Virginia, and other states providing the primary growth vector over the past three years.

## Growth Analysis

CDI's growth thesis rests on three pillars:

**1. HRM Facility Expansion:** Historical Racing Machines — which allow patrons to wager on replays of historical races — have been the primary revenue growth driver. The London Company's Q4 2025 investor letter (cited in news) specifically highlighted HRM facility growth as a positive. CDI has been opening new HRM venues in Kentucky and expanding into Virginia and other states where legislation permits. Each new facility adds recurring, high-margin revenue with relatively predictable ramp curves.

**2. Kentucky Derby Premium Pricing:** CDI has systematically increased Derby ticket prices, hospitality packages, and sponsorship revenue. The 2025 Derby delivered record results per management commentary. This is a pricing power story with limited volume growth — the event is capacity-constrained — but consistent low-to-mid single digit revenue growth from the Derby segment alone is achievable.

**3. Digital Wagering (TwinSpires):** The online wagering platform competes in a crowded market against DraftKings, FanDuel, and others. Growth here is more uncertain and margin-dilutive compared to the physical gaming and Derby segments.

**Growth Concerns:** In a stagflationary environment with consumer spending actively contracting (per the world model: five+ independent headwinds, Lululemon and Macy's declining 2026 guidance), gaming and entertainment spending is vulnerable. CDI's HRM facilities draw from regional consumer bases that skew middle-income — precisely the demographic most pressured by $5/gallon diesel, 7% mortgage rates, and declining real wages. The Kentucky Derby's ultra-premium segment is more insulated, but the gaming segment is exposed.

## Valuation Assessment

At 16.1x forward earnings, CHDN is trading near the lower end of its historical valuation range. The stock has not consistently traded below 15x earnings in recent years outside of the COVID trough. The current P/S of ~2.0x is similarly compressed relative to the 3-4x range the stock has sustained during expansion periods.

**Context for the discount:** The 27% drawdown from the December high reflects: (a) the broader market selloff accelerating in March, (b) post-Q4 earnings disappointment (the Feb 26 single-day drop suggests the market found something in the results or guidance less favorable than the "record year" headline implied), and (c) the HISA fee dispute creating regulatory overhang.

**Valuation relative to peers:** Without detailed financial statement data to compute EV/EBITDA precisely, I estimate that CDI's enterprise value (incorporating likely $4-5B in net debt based on historical leverage) puts EV/EBITDA in the 10-12x range, which is reasonable for a gaming/entertainment company with a monopolistic franchise asset (the Derby) but significant leverage.

The valuation is not cheap enough to provide a margin of safety against a recessionary consumer spending decline. At 14x earnings (~$75) the risk/reward would be more compelling.

## Competitive Landscape

CDI possesses one of the strongest competitive moats in consumer entertainment: the Kentucky Derby is a permanent, irreplaceable franchise. No competitor can replicate a 150+ year tradition embedded in American culture with global media coverage. This gives CDI pricing power and brand equity that cannot be competed away.

In the HRM segment, CDI benefits from regulatory barriers to entry — HRM legalization is state-by-state, and CDI's existing relationships with regulators and real estate give it first-mover advantages in newly legalized markets. However, HRM legalization can also be reversed (Virginia has periodically debated restrictions), creating political risk.

The TwinSpires platform competes against better-capitalized digital gaming operators. This segment is the weakest competitively and may warrant strategic re-evaluation by management.

## Risk Assessment

**1. Consumer Spending Deterioration (HIGH):** In the current stagflationary macro regime, discretionary consumer entertainment spending is contracting. CDI's gaming revenue — the largest segment — is directly exposed. HRM handle volumes correlate with consumer disposable income. A recession (38-42% probability per the world model) would compress gaming revenue by an estimated 5-15%.

**2. HISA Regulatory Risk (MODERATE):** HISA ordered CDI to pay $5.28M in overdue 2025 fees by March 26 or face a simulcast racing ban. The dollar amount is immaterial (~0.2% of annual revenue), but the precedent matters. A simulcast ban during Kentucky Derby season would be catastrophic for the wagering ecosystem. CDI is likely to pay under protest and litigate, but this signals ongoing regulatory friction that could escalate over time.

**3. Leverage Risk (MODERATE-HIGH, unquantifiable):** CDI's acquisition-driven strategy has historically resulted in significant debt. Without current balance sheet data, the precise leverage ratio is unknown, but the company's enterprise value likely includes $4-5B in debt. In a rising rate environment where refinancing costs are elevated, interest expense could compress margins.

**4. Valuation Gap Risk (LOW-MODERATE):** The Feb 26 post-earnings drop (intraday from ~$95 to ~$86) suggests the market identified a gap between "record results" and forward guidance or cash flow quality. Without the earnings transcript or detailed guidance, the specific concern is unclear, but the magnitude of the decline (approximately 10% in a single session) is informative.

**5. Macro Sensitivity:** CDI has no direct exposure to oil, tariffs, or the Iran conflict, but second-order effects through consumer wallet compression apply. Energy costs flowing through to transportation costs reduce disposable income available for gaming.

## Investment Thesis

### Bull Case
- The Kentucky Derby is an irreplaceable monopoly asset with multi-decade pricing power; at 16x earnings, the market is undervaluing this optionality.
- HRM expansion into new states provides a visible, high-margin growth runway that can continue independently of the macro cycle.
- Record 2025 results ($2.93B revenue, $383M net income) demonstrate the business model's strength. The selloff creates an opportunity to buy a high-quality franchise at a historically discounted valuation.
- Management's large equity holdings (CEO holds 1.7M+ shares, COO holds 732K+ shares) align incentives with shareholders.

### Bear Case
- Consumer spending contraction in a stagflationary environment will compress gaming revenue — HRM handle volumes are directly tied to discretionary spending by middle-income consumers.
- The leverage profile (likely $4-5B in net debt) creates refinancing risk and limits financial flexibility if revenues decline.
- The post-earnings selloff on Feb 26 suggests the market identified forward guidance or cash flow concerns that the "record year" headline obscured. Without detailed financial data, this concern cannot be resolved.
- HISA regulatory disputes could escalate, particularly if the political environment shifts against historical racing machines in key states.
- The stock has found no durable support — it has declined from $118 to $86 over three months with no visible capitulation or stabilization pattern.

## Conclusion

CHDN possesses a genuinely rare franchise asset in the Kentucky Derby and a credible growth vector in HRM expansion. The 16.1x forward P/E is historically reasonable. However, three factors prevent a BUY recommendation: (1) the consumer spending environment is actively deteriorating, and CDI's gaming revenue is directly exposed; (2) the absence of detailed financial statement data — particularly balance sheet and cash flow — makes it impossible to assess leverage risk or free cash flow yield with confidence; and (3) the post-earnings price action suggests forward guidance disappointed, and without access to that guidance, the forward earnings estimate embedded in the 16.1x multiple may be stale.

The appropriate entry point is lower — closer to $75-78 (approximately 14x earnings), which would provide adequate margin of safety against a consumer recession scenario. At current levels, the risk/reward is balanced, warranting a HOLD.
