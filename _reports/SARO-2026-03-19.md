## Executive Summary

StandardAero is an aircraft engine maintenance, repair, and overhaul (MRO) provider authorized to work on 40+ engine platforms, including the dominant CFM56 and its successor LEAP. The company IPO'd via NYSE and remains majority-controlled by Carlyle Group, which has been aggressively liquidating its position. Full-year 2025 was described as a "record year" with double-digit revenue and earnings growth and a "sharp improvement in free cash flow," per earnings call commentary. UBS raised its price target to $35 (Neutral) on February 26, 2026. A Morningstar report highlights SARO's "lucrative position in the engine repair value chain."

The investment case rests on a structural MRO demand cycle: the installed base of commercial aircraft engines is aging, OEM delivery delays are extending engine lives, and authorized MRO providers operate within a narrow competitive moat. The March 2026 defense sector rally following Iran escalation has positioned SARO as a potential beneficiary of increased military spending, with Seeking Alpha flagging it as "the sole bargain in the soaring defense sector."

The problem is price versus risk. At 33x forward earnings with $2.5B+ in insider/sponsor selling over two months, the stock's valuation assumes a growth trajectory that is plausible but unverified by the limited financial data available. The CEO sold 240,000 shares ($7.3M) in a six-week span. Carlyle disposed of approximately 68M shares ($2.1B) in two tranches. These are not routine portfolio rebalancing volumes — they represent aggressive risk transfer from informed parties to public shareholders.

## Growth Analysis

MRO demand is driven by three structural factors:

1. **Aging fleet:** The average age of the global narrowbody fleet continues to increase as OEMs (Boeing, Airbus) struggle with production rates. Older engines require more frequent and expensive shop visits.

2. **LEAP transition:** As LEAP engines accumulate flight hours, they are entering their first major overhaul cycles. SARO's LEAP authorization positions it to capture this multi-decade wave.

3. **Military spending:** The $200B Pentagon supplemental request and Iran conflict escalation create a secondary growth vector. SARO's military engine work provides exposure to increased defense MRO budgets.

The "double-digit revenue and earnings growth" in FY2025 is consistent with this structural tailwind. The question is whether growth rates sustain at levels justifying 33x forward earnings, or whether they normalize to mid-single digits as the initial post-COVID catch-up cycle matures.

Seeking Alpha's characterization of SARO as "the sole bargain in the soaring defense sector" (March 3) reflects a view that the stock hadn't yet fully participated in the defense rally. Since that article, the stock has declined from ~$30 to $27, suggesting the "bargain" thesis has faced headwinds — likely from the broader insider selling overhang.

At 33x, SARO is priced for sustained high-teens earnings growth, which requires both revenue growth and margin expansion.

Without the actual financial statements to compute EV/EBITDA, net debt/EBITDA, or FCF yield, the valuation assessment is necessarily incomplete. The forward P/E alone places SARO at the premium end of its peer group, which is defensible given the CFM56/LEAP authorization moat but leaves little margin of safety if growth decelerates.

## Competitive Landscape

SARO's competitive advantages are real:

- **Authorization barriers:** OEMs (GE Aerospace, Pratt & Whitney, Rolls-Royce) limit the number of authorized MRO providers for each engine type. SARO's 40+ platform authorizations represent decades of accumulated capability and relationships.

- **Switching costs:** Airlines and military operators build multi-year MRO relationships based on quality, turnaround time, and parts availability. Switching providers involves qualification risk and potential fleet disruption.

- **Scale:** SARO operates multiple MRO facilities globally, enabling geographic coverage and capacity flexibility.

Competitors include Lufthansa Technik, ST Engineering, MTU Aero Engines, and the OEMs' own MRO divisions. The market is oligopolistic by engine type — a handful of authorized providers compete for each platform's shop visit volume. This structure supports pricing power and stable margins.

The Iran conflict and defense spending surge could benefit SARO's military engine work, though the military segment is typically lower-margin than commercial.

CEO Ford's selling is particularly concerning — six separate sales of 40,000 shares each between February 2 and March 4, a systematic liquidation pattern. Ford retained 607K shares after the last sale, so he maintains meaningful exposure, but the pace suggests either a pre-set 10b5-1 plan or deliberate profit-taking near the 52-week high.

The one countervailing signal: Paul McElhinney purchased 37,243 shares ($984K) on March 13 at $26.41. This is a meaningful open-market buy and provides some offset to the selling narrative. McElhinney's total position increased to 281,531 shares, suggesting confidence at current levels.

**Other risks:**
- **Leverage:** PE-backed companies typically carry significant debt. Rising rates (Fed hawkish shift, rate hike discussion) increase interest expense on floating-rate debt.
- **Oil prices:** $119 oil increases airline operating costs, potentially pressuring airlines to defer discretionary MRO work — though mandatory engine overhauls are non-discretionary.
- **Supply chain:** Engine parts shortages have periodically constrained MRO capacity and extended turnaround times.
- **Limited financial transparency:** Empty financial statements in the data set prevent proper leverage, coverage ratio, and margin analysis.

## Conclusion

SARO occupies a genuinely strong competitive position in a structurally growing market. The engine MRO business has attractive characteristics: non-discretionary demand, authorization barriers, and a multi-decade demand cycle driven by the CFM56-to-LEAP transition. The defense spending catalyst is real and aligns with the broader macro environment.

The problem is the price being paid and who is selling. At 33x forward earnings, SARO is priced for execution that is plausible but unproven by publicly available financials. Carlyle and GIC collectively sold $2.46B in shares in January–February 2026. CEO Ford sold $7.3M across six transactions. These informed sellers chose to reduce exposure at prices 10–25% above today's level.

McElhinney's $984K purchase provides a partial offset and suggests some insiders view the current price as attractive following the selloff. The stock has dropped 21% from its January highs.

**Recommendation: HOLD.** The structural thesis warrants monitoring, and the ~$26–27 level could prove to be a reasonable entry point if Q1 2026 results confirm the cash flow improvement trajectory. A BUY upgrade would require: (1) Carlyle overhang reduction below 15% of shares outstanding, (2) verified financial statements showing deleveraging and sustained FCF generation, and (3) a pullback to below 25x forward earnings or evidence of earnings acceleration above current consensus. The insider selling volume is too large and too recent to justify adding risk at current valuations.
