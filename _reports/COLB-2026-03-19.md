## Executive Summary

However, the current macro regime — confirmed stagflation with 38-42% recession probability, rising oil-driven inflation compressing consumer and small business cash flows, and a Fed on indefinite hold — creates direct headwinds for a regional bank with significant commercial real estate and small business lending exposure in the Pacific Northwest. ## Company Overview

Columbia Banking System (COLB) is a bank holding company headquartered in Tacoma, Washington, operating through its subsidiary Columbia Bank. Following the March 2023 merger with Umpqua Holdings, COLB became one of the largest West Coast-based community banking organizations, with approximately $50B+ in total assets and branches spanning Washington, Oregon, California, Idaho, Nevada, Arizona, Colorado, and Utah.

The combined entity operates primarily in commercial banking, commercial real estate lending, consumer banking, and wealth management. The Pacific Northwest footprint means significant exposure to technology-adjacent economies (Seattle/Portland metro areas), timber/natural resources, and agricultural lending in rural markets.

**Market cap:** $7.74B. **Shares outstanding:** 295.6M. **Current price:** $26.47 (as of March 17, 2026).

## Growth Analysis

### Growth Constraints

The forward P/E equal to trailing P/E signals consensus expects flat EPS. For a post-merger bank, this is somewhat disappointing — the typical post-merger narrative includes 15-25% cost synergies that should flow through to EPS growth for 2-3 years. Possible explanations:

1. **Synergies already captured:** The Umpqua merger closed in March 2023. By early 2026, most achievable synergies may already be in the run rate.
2. **NIM compression offsetting synergies:** With the Fed on hold and the yield curve flat, net interest margin expansion — the primary earnings driver for banks — is constrained. Banks like COLB that grew through acquisition during a higher-rate environment may face deposit repricing pressures.
3. **Credit cost normalization:** Post-pandemic credit costs were abnormally low. Normalization to historical provision expense levels consumes earnings growth.
4. **Macro headwinds:** The Pacific Northwest economy is exposed to technology sector layoffs and discretionary consumer spending contraction, both of which are accelerating in the current stagflationary environment.

### Organic Growth Potential

Loan growth for Pacific Northwest banks has been decelerating. Commercial real estate (CRE) lending, a traditional strength for banks like COLB, is under pressure from remote work-driven office vacancy and rising cap rates. Agricultural lending in the Willamette Valley and Eastern Washington faces fertilizer cost inflation (CF Industries at maximum conviction in the world model — the same input cost pressure hitting COLB's farm borrowers).

## Competitive Landscape

Columbia Banking System occupies the mid-cap regional bank tier on the West Coast. Key competitive dynamics:

- **Scale advantage post-merger:** The Umpqua combination created a ~$50B asset institution, large enough for operational efficiency but still below the $100B threshold that triggers enhanced prudential standards. This is a structural advantage.
- **Market position:** Dominant in Oregon, strong in Washington, growing in California and mountain West. The Pacific Northwest commercial banking market is moderately concentrated.
- **Technology/digital banking:** Regional banks face increasing digital competition from both neobanks (for deposit gathering) and large banks (for commercial lending). COLB's digital capabilities post-merger are unclear from available data.
- **Deposit franchise:** The quality of the deposit franchise is the single most important competitive differentiator for banks in the current environment. Low-cost core deposits (checking, savings) vs. rate-sensitive CDs determines NIM resilience. The 2023 banking crisis demonstrated that mid-cap banks face deposit flight risk during stress events.

## Risk Assessment

### Credit Quality Risk (HIGH)

The 38-42% recession probability, combined with Pacific Northwest CRE exposure and agricultural lending facing fertilizer cost inflation, creates meaningful credit risk. CRE office valuations continue declining nationally. COLB's provision expense trajectory is the key variable to monitor in the next earnings report.

### Interest Rate Risk (MODERATE-HIGH)

With the Fed on indefinite hold (20-25% probability of zero cuts in 2026 per the world model), deposit repricing pressure continues. Banks that acquired deposits during 2022-2023 at low rates now face rollover into higher-cost CDs and money market accounts. NIM compression is the base case.

### Macro Sensitivity (HIGH)

Regional banks are among the most macro-sensitive equity sectors. In the confirmed stagflationary regime:
- Rising oil prices → compressed small business margins → higher loan delinquencies
- Consumer credit deterioration (unemployment 4.4%, NFP -92K) → higher charge-offs on consumer portfolios
- Higher-for-longer rates → lower loan demand, lower housing turnover, lower fee income

### Concentration Risk (MODERATE)

Geographic concentration in the Pacific Northwest exposes COLB to regional economic shocks. The tech sector downturn (Amazon, Microsoft layoffs) directly impacts the Seattle-Tacoma-Portland corridor.

### Integration Risk (LOW-MODERATE)

The Umpqua merger is now nearly 3 years old, reducing integration risk, but cultural integration in community banking is a multi-year process. Talent retention in the combined entity and customer attrition in overlapping markets are ongoing concerns.

## Investment Thesis

### Bull Case

1. **Valuation floor:** 11.5x P/E and 5.6% yield provide downside protection if the dividend is maintained. At ~1.2-1.5x tangible book, the stock has merger-arbitrage floor value.
2. **Synergy upside:** If expense synergies from the Umpqua merger have not been fully captured, there is room for EPS to grow 5-10% even in a flat revenue environment.
3. **Rate cut optionality:** If the macro deteriorates enough to force the Fed to cut (despite inflation), bank NIMs would expand as deposit costs reprice faster than loan yields compress. The stock would re-rate to 13-14x on $2.50+ EPS, implying $32-35.
4. **Acquisition currency:** At $50B+ in assets, COLB is large enough to be an acquirer of smaller community banks distressed by credit losses, and small enough to be an acquisition target for a super-regional seeking West Coast presence.

### Bear Case

1. **Credit cycle deterioration:** Recession (38-42% probability) would drive provision expense higher, potentially cutting EPS to $1.80-2.00. At a distressed 8-9x multiple, the stock falls to $14-18.
2. **Dividend cut risk:** The 64% payout ratio has minimal buffer. A 20% EPS decline pushes the payout ratio above 80%, likely triggering a cut that would remove the yield support.
3. **CRE exposure:** Office CRE continues deteriorating nationally. A significant write-down in the CRE portfolio would impair tangible book value and sentiment.
4. **Deposit pressure:** In a prolonged higher-rate environment, deposit costs continue rising while loan yields are capped by weakening demand, compressing NIM.
5. **Stagflation is the worst macro for banks:** Inflation prevents rate cuts (blocking NIM expansion), while slowing growth drives credit deterioration. Banks are caught from both sides.

## Conclusion

Columbia Banking System is a fairly valued post-merger Pacific Northwest bank offering income (5.6% yield) at a reasonable multiple (11.5x). The zero-growth consensus embedded in the identical forward and trailing P/E is probably correct in the current environment. The stock lacks a positive catalyst — synergies may be largely captured, the rate environment is hostile, and credit quality faces cyclical headwinds.

The absence of insider buying despite reasonable valuation, combined with the stagflationary macro backdrop that is particularly damaging to regional bank earnings, prevents an upgrade to BUY. Equally, the valuation is cheap enough and the dividend high enough to prevent an AVOID recommendation.

**Recommendation: HOLD.** Monitor for (1) Q1 2026 earnings (next report likely April 2026) for credit quality trends — if provision expense is flat or declining, the stock becomes more interesting; (2) a pullback to $23-24 where the yield approaches 6.5% and the multiple compresses to ~10x, providing a better entry with more margin of safety; (3) any change in Fed posture that would support NIM expansion.

**Why Not BUY:** The macro environment is actively hostile to regional bank earnings. Credit cycle turning, NIM under pressure, no insider buying, and zero growth consensus. The 5.6% yield is attractive but carries cut risk if recession materializes. Better risk-adjusted opportunities exist in insurance (ACGL, PGR, RNR) and exchanges (CME, ICE) which benefit from the same volatility environment that hurts banks.
