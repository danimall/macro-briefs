## Executive Summary

International Paper Co (IP) is a global leader in sustainable packaging solutions currently trading at $49.17 per share with a market capitalization of approximately $26.0 billion. The company is in the midst of a transformative period: it reported a $2.8 billion operating loss, completed the divestiture of its Global Cellulose Fibers (GCF) business to American Industrial Partners, and announced plans to split into two independent publicly traded companies — one focused on North America retaining the IP name and another as a standalone EMEA packaging business.

Despite these headwinds, there are emerging positive signals. UBS analysts see a containerboard rebound in 2026, citing tightening supply and improving demand fundamentals. Citi recently raised its price target from $46 to $47, and hedge funds have included IP among their preferred packaging picks. However, the forward P/E of approximately 47x signals that the market is pricing in a significant earnings recovery that has yet to materialize, creating a challenging risk/reward setup at current levels.

The stock has recovered meaningfully from its 52-week low of $35.56 to trade near $49, but remains well below its 52-week high of $57.55. The 3.8% dividend yield provides an income cushion, but investors must weigh this against execution risks from the corporate restructuring, integration complexities from the DS Smith acquisition (implied by the EMEA operations), and a cyclical industry backdrop.

## Company Overview

International Paper is one of the world's largest packaging companies, with a primary focus on corrugated packaging and containerboard. The company is headquartered in Memphis, Tennessee and trades on both the NYSE (IP) and LSE (IPC). IP has historically operated across multiple segments including Industrial Packaging, Global Cellulose Fibers, and Printing Papers, though it has progressively streamlined its portfolio.

Key recent developments:
- **January 2026**: Completed sale of Global Cellulose Fibers business to American Industrial Partners
- **January 2026**: Announced plan to split into two independent public companies (North America and EMEA)
- **January 2026**: Reported full-year 2025 results including a $2.8 billion operating loss
- **Beta**: 1.08, indicating slightly above-market cyclical sensitivity
- **Dividend Yield**: ~3.76%

## Growth Analysis

**Positive Catalysts:**
1. **Containerboard Market Recovery**: UBS analysts project a rebound in 2026, driven by tightening supply and improving demand fundamentals. North American containerboard pricing could benefit from successful price increase implementations.
2. **Strategic Refocusing**: The divestiture of GCF and planned EMEA separation should allow the North American business to operate with greater focus and potentially improved margins.
3. **PSNA Operations**: Recent news highlights that the company's PSNA (Packaging Solutions North America) operations are generating bullish sentiment among hedge funds.
4. **Cost Synergies**: Post-restructuring, the streamlined North American entity may achieve operational efficiencies.

**Growth Headwinds:**
1. **Cyclical Industry**: Containerboard demand is tied to economic activity, e-commerce volumes, and industrial production — all subject to macro volatility.
2. **Execution Risk**: Splitting into two public companies is complex and could distract management for 12-18+ months.
3. **Weak 2025 Base**: Coming off a $2.8B operating loss, recovery will take multiple quarters to normalize earnings.
4. **Pricing Uncertainty**: The success of containerboard price increases in 2026 is not guaranteed.

## Risk Assessment

**High-Impact Risks:**
1. **Restructuring/Split Execution Risk**: Separating into two public companies introduces significant operational, financial, and managerial complexity. History shows corporate splits can destroy value if poorly executed.
2. **Earnings Recovery Uncertainty**: The $2.8B operating loss suggests deep structural or one-time issues. If recovery is slower than expected, the stock's elevated forward multiple will compress.
3. **Dividend Sustainability**: With trailing earnings negative and forward EPS of only ~$1.05, the implied annual dividend of ~$1.85/share may not be well-covered by earnings.

**Moderate Risks:**
4. **Macroeconomic Sensitivity**: Beta of 1.08 and cyclical industry exposure mean IP is vulnerable to recession or slowing economic growth.
5. **Tariff/Trade Risks**: As a global operator (especially with EMEA exposure), trade policy uncertainty could impact operations.
6. **Debt Levels**: While specific balance sheet data is unavailable, large packaging companies typically carry significant debt, and post-acquisition/restructuring leverage is likely elevated.

**Lower-Priority Risks:**
7. **Commodity Input Costs**: Fiber, energy, and chemical costs can pressure margins
8. **ESG/Regulatory**: Forestry and paper production face increasing environmental scrutiny

- Corporate split introduces 12-18 months of distraction, costs, and potential value destruction
- Dividend may be cut if earnings don't recover quickly enough

- Macro slowdown could delay the containerboard pricing recovery

## Conclusion

**Rating: HOLD / NEUTRAL**

International Paper is at a critical inflection point. The company is executing a significant strategic transformation — divesting non-core assets, splitting into two focused entities, and hoping to ride a containerboard market recovery in 2026. While the long-term logic of these moves is sound, the near-term risk/reward at $49.17 is unfavorable.

The forward P/E of 47x prices in a substantial earnings recovery that has yet to begin, while the $2.8B operating loss and upcoming corporate split create meaningful execution risk. Analyst targets cluster around current levels, suggesting limited near-term upside. The 3.76% dividend yield is attractive but may be at risk if earnings don't improve quickly.

For investors with a 2-3 year horizon and conviction in the containerboard cycle turning, IP could reward patience — particularly if bought on a pullback toward the $40-42 range. At current prices, however, the risk-adjusted return profile is unappealing. We recommend waiting for greater earnings visibility and evidence of successful restructuring execution before initiating or adding to positions.
