## Executive Summary

Knife River Corp is a construction materials and contracting company spun off from MDU Resources in 2023, focused on aggregates, asphalt, and ready-mix concrete across 14 states in the western and central US. The company reported Q4 2025 EPS of $0.56 vs. $0.41 consensus, beat revenue estimates, and guided 2026 to $3.3B-$3.5B revenue with $520M-$560M adjusted EBITDA (~16% margin at midpoint). The stock surged ~16% on earnings day (Feb 17) but has since given back most of those gains, trading at $82.41 as of March 18, 2026 — down roughly 20% from the May 2025 high of $103.17.

The investment case rests on infrastructure spending tailwinds (IIJA funding still flowing), a vertically integrated business model with natural competitive moats from aggregate reserves, and a management team executing an M&A strategy (most recently the Morgan Asphalt acquisition in Utah). Against this: a trailing P/E of ~30x on $2.76 EPS is expensive for a cyclical construction materials business, particularly in the current stagflationary environment where oil at $119 raises asphalt/bitumen input costs, potential rate hikes threaten construction activity, and recession probability sits at 40-45%. The lack of detailed financial statements in the data set limits the depth of balance sheet and cash flow analysis.

## Company Overview

Knife River Corp (NYSE: KNF) is headquartered in Bismarck, North Dakota. The company operates as an aggregates-based construction materials and contracting business, producing and selling aggregates (crushed stone, sand, gravel), asphalt, and ready-mix concrete. It also provides paving and construction services. The company was spun off from MDU Resources Group in mid-2023, making it a relatively new public entity with a ~$4.5B market cap and approximately 56.7M shares outstanding.

The business is geographically diversified across 14 states, predominantly in western and central US markets where population growth and infrastructure investment tend to run above national averages. The February 2026 acquisition of Morgan Asphalt in Salt Lake City expands operations into Utah, a fast-growing market.

## Growth Analysis

**Organic growth drivers:**
1. **IIJA (Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act) funding:** Federal infrastructure dollars continue flowing into state DOTs, supporting multi-year highway and bridge spending. Knife River's western/central US footprint captures this spending directly.
2. **Vertical integration strategy:** Management is pushing to increase internal sourcing of aggregates and reduce reliance on third-party materials, which structurally improves margins over time.
3. **Pricing power:** Aggregates have demonstrated consistent pricing gains above inflation in most US markets, driven by permitting barriers, transportation costs, and reserve depletion.

**Inorganic growth:**
- The Morgan Asphalt acquisition (announced Feb 27, 2026) adds a new state (Utah) to the footprint. Salt Lake City is among the fastest-growing MSAs in the US.
- The company appears to be running a systematic tuck-in acquisition strategy, which is the standard playbook for aggregates businesses (see: Martin Marietta, Vulcan Materials).

**Growth risks:**
- 2026 revenue guidance of $3.3B-$3.5B implies mid-single-digit organic growth at best (adjusting for acquisitions), which is respectable but priced aggressively at 30x earnings.
- A recession (40-45% probability per macro framework) would reduce both public and private construction activity. Public infrastructure is more resilient but not immune to state budget pressures.
- Oil at $119 directly raises the cost of asphalt binder (bitumen), which is a petroleum derivative. This compresses margins unless Knife River can fully pass through cost increases, which typically takes 1-2 quarters in public contracts.

## Competitive Landscape

**Structural advantages:**
1. **Aggregate reserve ownership:** Owning permitted aggregate reserves creates a natural moat. Permitting new quarries takes 5-10+ years in most US jurisdictions, and NIMBY opposition makes new permits increasingly difficult to obtain. Knife River's reserves across 14 states are difficult to replicate.
2. **Vertical integration:** Controlling the supply chain from aggregates through asphalt production through paving contracting captures margin at each stage and reduces dependency on third parties.
3. **Geographic positioning:** Western/central US markets (Montana, Oregon, Washington, Texas, Alaska, now Utah) benefit from above-average population growth and infrastructure needs.

**Competitive risks:**
- KNF competes against much larger players (VMC, MLM) in some markets, though aggregates are inherently local businesses (high weight-to-value ratio limits economic transportation distances to ~50-100 miles).
- As a recent spin-off, KNF lacks the capital markets access and balance sheet depth of established peers, potentially limiting M&A competitiveness.

## Risk Assessment

**Macro risks (elevated):**
1. **Oil/bitumen cost inflation:** Oil at $119 directly raises asphalt production costs. Diesel costs affect equipment operation and material transportation. Margin compression risk is real in the near term, particularly on fixed-price contracts bid before the oil spike.
2. **Rate hike potential:** If the Fed follows through on rate hike discussions, construction activity weakens via two channels: (a) higher mortgage rates reduce residential construction, (b) higher borrowing costs increase the cost of state/municipal bond-funded infrastructure projects.
3. **Recession risk (40-45%):** Would reduce private construction spending and potentially delay some public projects as state tax revenues decline.

**Company-specific risks:**
1. **Seasonal business:** Q4 and Q1 are inherently weak quarters due to weather. The Q4 beat was partly weather-driven, which is favorable but variable.
2. **M&A integration:** The acquisition strategy requires continuous execution. Overpaying or failing to integrate acquisitions (Morgan Asphalt, others) would erode returns.
3. **Limited public track record:** Less than 3 years as a public company. Limited historical data for assessing management through a full economic cycle.

**Insider activity:** Recent transactions are exclusively grant/award (compensation) and tax withholding disposals. The CEO (Brian Gray) had shares withheld for taxes totaling ~$2.4M in February — this is standard for equity compensation vesting and does not indicate discretionary selling. No open-market purchases observed, which would have been a stronger signal.

## Investment Thesis

**Bull Case:**
- IIJA infrastructure funding provides a multi-year floor under demand for aggregates and paving services, insulating KNF from the worst of a potential recession.
- Vertical integration strategy drives margin expansion from ~16% EBITDA margins toward 18-20% over 3-5 years, consistent with best-in-class aggregates peers.
- M&A into growing markets (Utah, other western states) extends the growth runway.
- At current prices ($82.41), the stock has corrected 20% from highs, providing a more reasonable entry than three months ago.
- If 2026 EBITDA hits the high end of guidance ($560M), EV/EBITDA may compress toward 8-9x, which is attractive for a quality aggregates franchise.

**Bear Case:**
- 30x trailing P/E is expensive for a cyclical business entering a potential recession.
- Oil at $119 raises bitumen and diesel costs directly, compressing margins on existing contracts. Full cost passthrough takes quarters.
- Rate hike discussions could slow construction activity and compress multiples on cyclical industrials/materials.
- The stock was at $58.72 just four months ago — a retest of those levels represents ~29% downside from here.
- No dividend provides zero downside protection while waiting for the cycle to improve.

## Conclusion

Knife River is a fundamentally sound aggregates business with legitimate competitive advantages (reserve ownership, vertical integration, geographic positioning) executing a credible growth strategy backed by infrastructure spending tailwinds. The Q4 beat and 2026 guidance affirm management execution.

However, at 30x trailing earnings with no dividend, the stock prices in considerable growth that may not materialize if the macro deteriorates further. Oil at $119 is a direct headwind to asphalt margins. Rate hike discussions and 40-45% recession probability create a hostile environment for cyclical industrials. The 20% pullback from highs improves the risk/reward relative to May levels but does not create a compelling entry given the macro backdrop.

**Recommendation: HOLD.** The position is worth monitoring for a more attractive entry in the low-to-mid $70s (approximately 7-8x midpoint EBITDA guidance), where the risk/reward better compensates for cyclical and macro risks. A confirmed infrastructure spending acceleration or oil price normalization would be catalysts to upgrade. Conversely, if recession materializes and the stock revisits the $58-60 range, that would represent a more compelling entry for a multi-year infrastructure play.
