## Executive Summary

Privia Health Group is a physician enablement platform that reported strong Q4 2025 results — revenue of $541M (+17.4% YoY), EPS of $0.07 (beating consensus of $0.04), and full-year adjusted EBITDA of $125.5M (+38.8% YoY). The company is guiding for approximately 19.5% EBITDA growth in 2026 while expanding its geographic footprint to 24 states. All 2025 operating and financial metrics came in at or above the high end of guidance ranges, demonstrating consistent execution.

The core problem is valuation. At $21.59, PRVA trades at approximately 120x trailing earnings on $0.18 EPS. The market cap of $2.67B implies investors are pricing in years of compounding growth at current rates. Simultaneously, CEO Parth Mehrotra sold approximately $3.7M in stock across six transactions from March 6-16, 2026. CFO David Mountcastle sold approximately $677K over four transactions in the same period. General Counsel Edward Fargis sold approximately $215K. This coordinated insider selling — occurring immediately after equity grant vesting — is a negative signal, particularly at a company where stock compensation is a meaningful portion of total comp.

The healthcare services sector is relatively insulated from the direct impacts of oil price shocks and tariffs, which provides some defensive quality. But PRVA's asset-light model generates thin GAAP margins, and the 120x P/E leaves essentially no room for execution missteps or macro-driven slowdowns in physician recruitment.

## Company Overview

Privia Health operates a technology-enabled healthcare platform that partners with medical groups, health plans, and health systems. The company provides practice management, population health management, and revenue cycle management services to physician practices, enabling them to participate in value-based care arrangements. Privia earns revenue through management service fees (a percentage of practice revenue) and shared savings from value-based contracts.

The business model is capital-light: Privia does not employ the physicians directly but provides the technology platform and administrative infrastructure. This creates high incremental margins on new provider additions but means the company's top-line revenue includes significant pass-through costs (practice collections that flow through Privia's P&L as both revenue and cost of revenue).

As of Q4 2025, the company operates in 24 states with a growing national footprint. The CEO highlighted "new provider signings" as a key growth driver during the Q4 earnings call.

## Growth Analysis

**2026 Guidance:**
Management is targeting approximately 19.5% adjusted EBITDA growth for 2026. This represents a deceleration from the 38.8% EBITDA growth achieved in 2025 but remains a strong absolute growth rate.

**Growth Drivers:**
1. **Provider network expansion:** New provider signings across expanding geographic footprint (now 24 states). Each new provider added generates incremental management fees.
2. **Value-based care adoption:** As more practices transition from fee-for-service to value-based arrangements, Privia's shared savings revenue increases. This is a secular tailwind driven by CMS policy and payer preferences.
3. **Operating leverage:** The platform model means each additional provider added to the network has higher incremental margins than the average, as fixed technology and administrative costs are spread over a larger base.

**Growth Risks:**
- Provider recruitment could slow if economic uncertainty makes physicians reluctant to change practice management arrangements.
- Value-based care shared savings are inherently variable and depend on patient outcomes and healthcare utilization trends.
- The deceleration from 39% to 19.5% EBITDA growth suggests the law of larger numbers is beginning to apply.

## Valuation Assessment

At $21.59 per share and a market cap of $2.67B, PRVA trades at:
- **Trailing P/E:** 120x ($0.18 EPS)
- **Forward P/E:** 120x (per data provided)
- **EV/EBITDA (approx):** ~21x on 2025 EBITDA of $125.5M (assuming minimal net debt)

The P/E ratio is extremely elevated because GAAP earnings include significant non-cash stock compensation and the pass-through revenue structure depresses net margins. The EV/EBITDA of ~21x is more informative. On 2026 projected EBITDA of ~$150M (applying the 19.5% growth guidance to $125.5M), the forward EV/EBITDA is approximately 18x.

For a healthcare services company growing EBITDA at ~20% with an asset-light model, 18x forward EBITDA is within a reasonable range but not cheap. Comparable physician enablement and health IT companies have traded at 15-25x forward EBITDA during growth phases. PRVA sits in the middle of that range.

The stock has traded between $18.77 and $26.51 over the past 52 weeks. At $21.59, it sits 18.5% below the high and 15% above the low. The post-earnings spike on February 26, 2026 (from ~$22.65 to $24.91) has fully faded, with the stock declining 13% from that post-earnings peak to the current level.

## Competitive Landscape

Privia competes in the physician enablement and value-based care enablement space alongside companies like agilon health (AGL), Oak Street Health (acquired by CVS), ChenMed, Aledade, and larger health system owned management service organizations. The market is fragmented, which provides both opportunity (large TAM) and risk (competition for physician recruitment).

Privia's competitive advantages include:
- **Platform scale:** Operating in 24 states provides geographic diversification and enables multi-state health plan relationships.
- **Technology integration:** Proprietary practice management and population health tools create switching costs once physicians are onboarded.
- **Track record of execution:** Consistently meeting or exceeding guidance builds credibility with physicians and health systems considering partnership.

Competitive risks include:
- **Low barriers to entry** for basic practice management; the differentiation is in value-based care capabilities.
- **Physician retention:** Physicians can theoretically exit Privia's management service agreements if they find better alternatives.
- **Larger competitors** (UnitedHealth/Optum, CVS Health) have substantially more resources and are actively building competing physician networks.

## Risk Assessment

**Insider Selling (HIGH CONCERN):**
Between March 6-16, 2026, three senior executives sold approximately $4.6M in stock:
- CEO Mehrotra: ~$3.7M across six sale transactions (while also receiving ~313K shares in grants)
- CFO Mountcastle: ~$677K across four sale transactions (while also receiving ~78K shares in grants)
- General Counsel Fargis: ~$215K across two sales (while receiving ~28K shares in grants)

The pattern is consistent with selling shares immediately upon vesting/grant to cover tax obligations and realize compensation, which is standard practice. However, the consistent daily cadence of sales and the fact that CEO retained shares-after count decreased from ~385K to ~555K (with intermediate grants) suggests a mixed picture. The sales represent a relatively small fraction of total holdings (CEO retains 555K+ shares worth ~$12M). This is a moderate negative signal — it would be more concerning if insiders were selling without concurrent grants, or if the selling represented a larger share of their holdings.

**Valuation Risk:**
A 120x trailing P/E creates extreme downside risk if growth decelerates faster than expected or if the multiple compresses. A 10% EBITDA miss could drive 15-20% stock price decline given the embedded expectations.

**Macro Sensitivity:**
Healthcare services are relatively insulated from oil prices and tariffs. However, in a stagflationary environment with potential recession (40-45% probability per world model), physician practices may face revenue pressure from declining patient volumes or payer mix deterioration. If unemployment rises, employer-sponsored insurance coverage declines, increasing uninsured rates and pressuring practice revenue.

**Regulatory Risk:**
CMS value-based care policies are a tailwind, but changes in Medicare Advantage reimbursement rates or risk adjustment methodology could affect shared savings revenue. The current administration's healthcare policy priorities are secondary to defense/energy/trade focus, reducing near-term regulatory risk.

**Concentration Risk:**
Without detailed 10-K data, the degree of payer or geographic concentration is unclear. Expansion to 24 states mitigates geographic risk.

## Investment Thesis

- Asset-light model generates strong free cash flow (operating cash flow of $163M vs $125.5M EBITDA)
- Secular tailwind from fee-for-service to value-based care transition
- Geographic expansion to 24 states opens new recruitment territories
- ~18x forward EBITDA is reasonable for a 20% grower with platform economics

### Bear Case
- 120x trailing P/E embeds expectations for years of compounding execution — any stumble gets punished severely
- Coordinated insider selling of $4.6M by three executives in a single 10-day period
- EBITDA growth already decelerating from 39% to 19.5% guidance
- No dividend, no buyback — pure growth story with no shareholder return in the interim
- Competition from Optum/UHG, CVS/Oak Street, and well-funded private competitors could slow provider recruitment
- Recession-driven decline in patient volumes or payer mix shift would impair practice revenue and Privia's management fees
- Stock has declined 8.6% over the trailing 3-month period, suggesting negative momentum

## Conclusion

PRVA is a well-executed growth story in physician enablement with genuine secular tailwinds from value-based care adoption. The company consistently beats its own guidance and is expanding its national footprint. However, the 120x P/E and ~18x forward EV/EBITDA leave limited margin of safety. Coordinated insider selling by the CEO, CFO, and General Counsel in March 2026 adds a cautious signal, even if partially explained by standard vesting-related sales.

The stock trades at $21.59, down from a post-earnings peak of ~$25 and well below the 52-week high of $26.51. This pullback makes the valuation marginally more attractive than it was six weeks ago, but not enough to overcome the growth deceleration, insider selling pattern, and macro uncertainty. A more compelling entry would be in the $18-19 range (approximately 15x forward EBITDA), where the risk/reward shifts meaningfully in favor of buyers.

**Recommendation: HOLD.** The growth trajectory is intact and the business model has structural appeal, but valuation demands near-perfect execution, and insider behavior does not inspire confidence. Wait for either a pullback to $18-19 or evidence that EBITDA growth can sustain above 20% for multiple years before upgrading.
