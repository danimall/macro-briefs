## Executive Summary

Mosaic is trading at $29.31 with a market cap of $8.8B after a volatile twelve months that saw the stock range from $22.36 to $38.23. The Q4 2025 results were poor: a GAAP loss of $1.64/share (versus +$0.53 a year earlier) and adjusted EPS of $0.22, both below consensus estimates, driven by weak U.S. phosphate demand. Management guided 2026 capex to approximately $1.5B — a near-term peak — with production targets rising for both phosphate and potash, and expects capex to drift toward $1.0B by 2030.

The stock has found a near-term catalyst in its March 2026 joint development agreement with Rainbow Rare Earths on the Uberaba rare earths project in Brazil, which drove a 7.6% single-day move. This diversification into critical minerals aligns with structural supply chain reshoring themes. However, Barclays downgraded MOS to Equal Weight on March 3 citing geopolitical cost risks, and the company faces a challenging combination of elevated capital spending, commodity price volatility, and macro uncertainty. The fertilizer supply disruption from the Hormuz conflict provides a potential pricing tailwind, but MOS's cost structure also faces pressure from the same disruption. At ~17x trailing earnings on depressed EPS, the valuation is neither cheap nor expensive, warranting a HOLD pending evidence that spring planting demand materializes and that the rare earth strategy creates tangible value.

## Company Overview

The Mosaic Company is one of the world's largest producers and marketers of concentrated phosphate and potash crop nutrients, headquartered in Tampa, Florida. The company operates through three segments: Phosphates, Potash, and Mosaic Fertilizantes (Brazil operations). With 317.5M shares outstanding and a current price of $29.31, market capitalization stands at approximately $8.8B. MOS pays a quarterly dividend of $0.22/share ($0.88 annualized), yielding approximately 3.0% at current prices.

The company's strategic positioning spans North American phosphate production, Canadian potash mining, and Brazilian distribution. More recently, MOS has begun diversifying into rare earths through its Brazilian subsidiary, partnering with Rainbow Rare Earths on the Uberaba project in Minas Gerais.

## Financial Analysis

**Earnings:** Q4 2025 was materially weak. GAAP loss of $519.5M ($1.64/share) compared to EPS of $0.53 in Q4 2024. Adjusted EPS of $0.22 indicates the GAAP loss included substantial one-time charges (likely asset impairments or environmental provisions related to the gypsum stack and clay settling projects). The trailing twelve-month EPS of $1.70 suggests the remaining three quarters of 2025 generated approximately $3.34 in aggregate earnings, meaning Q4 was an anomaly relative to the full-year trend.

**Capex:** Management guided to $1.5B in 2026 capital expenditures, which is elevated relative to the company's historical run rate. This is driven by environmental remediation projects (gypsum stack, clay settling) that are regulatory requirements, not discretionary growth investments. The guided decline to ~$1.0B by 2030 implies approximately $500M/year in above-maintenance spending through mid-decade. Against an $8.8B market cap, this is a significant capital burden.

**Dividend:** The $0.22/quarter dividend ($0.88 annualized) represents approximately $279M in annual cash outflows. Given the depressed Q4 earnings and elevated capex, dividend coverage bears monitoring. The 3.0% yield provides modest income but is not extraordinary for the sector.

**Balance Sheet:** Detailed balance sheet data was not available in the financial statements provided. The 10-K filed on February 27, 2026, would contain this information. The company's ability to fund $1.5B in capex while maintaining the dividend depends on operating cash flow generation and available credit facilities.

## Growth Analysis

**Core business:** CEO Bruce Bodine noted that "U.S. demand is emerging as farmers prepare for spring planting in North America and global ag fundamentals are solid." Production targets for both phosphate and potash are rising in 2026, suggesting management expects volume recovery from Q4's weak levels. The North American spring planting season (March-May) is the critical demand period, and fertilizer supply disruptions from the Hormuz conflict create a supportive pricing backdrop.

**Rare earths optionality:** The Uberaba JDA with Rainbow Rare Earths is strategically significant. MOS's existing phosphate mining operations in Brazil generate waste streams that contain rare earth elements. Extracting rare earths from existing tailings is lower-risk than greenfield mining because the material is already above ground and partially processed. This gives MOS a potential second revenue stream with minimal incremental mining cost. The 7.6% stock move on this news suggests the market assigns meaningful value to this optionality. However, rare earth extraction from phosphate byproducts is commercially unproven at scale, and revenue contribution is likely years away.

**Macro tailwinds:** The world model identifies Reuters-confirmed U.S. farmer fertilizer supply disruption during spring planting as a key catalyst. The Hormuz disruption has reduced sulphur supply (sulphur is a key input for phosphoric acid production), while simultaneously disrupting competing fertilizer exports from the Middle East. For a domestic producer like MOS, this creates a complex picture: input costs may rise (sulphur, energy), but reduced import competition and supply tightness should support finished product pricing.

## Valuation Assessment

At $29.31 with trailing EPS of $1.70, MOS trades at 17.2x trailing earnings. The forward P/E is also 17.2x, implying consensus expects roughly flat earnings over the next twelve months. This valuation is moderate for a commodity producer — typically these trade at 8-12x mid-cycle earnings. The elevated multiple reflects either depressed current earnings (denominator effect) or market skepticism about near-term recovery.

If MOS can return to mid-cycle earnings of $2.50-3.50/share (which it achieved in several recent years), the stock would trade at 8.4-11.7x, making it reasonably cheap. If earnings remain depressed near $1.70, the 17x multiple offers limited margin of safety.

The 52-week range of $22.36-$38.23 places the current price at approximately 44% of the range — below midpoint, suggesting the market has priced in considerable headwinds but with room for recovery. The stock has rallied 17% from its March lows around $25-26, partially reflecting the rare earth news and improving fertilizer market conditions.

**Dividend yield of 3.0%** provides a floor return while waiting for commodity cycle recovery.

## Competitive Landscape

MOS is one of only two major publicly traded North American phosphate producers (alongside Nutrien). This duopoly structure provides meaningful pricing power in the domestic market. In potash, the competitive set is broader (Nutrien, K+S, ICL, Belaruskali), but MOS's Esterhazy mine in Saskatchewan is among the lowest-cost potash operations globally.

The rare earth diversification could create a differentiated position. Few fertilizer companies are pursuing critical minerals extraction from phosphate byproducts, and MOS's Brazilian operations are well-positioned given the Uberaba deposit's scale.

Key competitive risk: Chinese fertilizer exports. China periodically restricts or releases fertilizer exports depending on domestic food security priorities. The current geopolitical environment (Section 301 probes, China-related trade tensions) could cut both ways — restricting Chinese exports would support MOS's pricing, while increased Chinese exports would pressure it.

## Risk Assessment

**Geopolitical cost exposure:** Barclays specifically cited this in their March 3 downgrade. The Hormuz disruption raises sulphur and energy input costs for MOS while simultaneously supporting output prices. The net effect is ambiguous. If sulphur costs rise faster than DAP/MAP prices, margins compress despite headline price increases.

**Capex overhang:** $1.5B in 2026 capex against an $8.8B market cap (17% of market cap in a single year) is substantial. Much of this is non-discretionary environmental spending. If fertilizer prices decline, MOS has limited ability to reduce capex without regulatory consequences.

**Commodity price volatility:** Potash and phosphate prices have been highly volatile since 2022. A return to 2023 lows would compress earnings below the already-weak Q4 2025 levels.

**Brazilian operations:** Currency risk (BRL/USD), regulatory risk, and political risk in Brazil affect the Mosaic Fertilizantes segment. The rare earth JDA adds execution risk in an unproven commercial application.

**Insider activity:** The March 9 transactions were routine RSU vestings with tax withholding — no discretionary open-market purchases or sales by insiders. The CEO (Bodine) holds only 11,816 shares after tax withholding, which is notably low relative to his compensation level. This is a mild negative signal.

## Investment Thesis

### Bull Case ($35-38)
- Spring planting demand normalizes, driving volume recovery from Q4 2025 trough
- Hormuz disruption reduces competing fertilizer imports, supporting North American pricing
- Rare earth extraction from Brazilian phosphate byproducts proves commercially viable, creating a new revenue stream and re-rating the stock
- Capex peaks in 2026 and begins declining, improving free cash flow generation from 2027 onward
- Mid-cycle earnings of $2.50-3.50/share would support $30-42 at 12x P/E

### Bear Case ($20-23)
- Fertilizer prices decline as Hormuz situation resolves and competing supply returns
- Sulphur and energy cost inflation outpaces finished product price increases, compressing margins
- $1.5B capex absorbs cash flow, potentially threatening dividend coverage
- Rare earth project delays or proves uneconomic at scale
- Recession scenario (38-42% probability per world model) reduces farmer purchasing power and fertilizer application rates
- Earnings remain at or below $1.70, and 17x multiple contracts to 12-13x as growth expectations fade

## Conclusion

MOS occupies an interesting intersection of near-term commodity volatility and longer-term strategic optionality through rare earths. The Q4 2025 results were genuinely weak, and the $1.5B capex burden constrains free cash flow through mid-decade. However, the fertilizer supply disruption from the Hormuz conflict provides a credible pricing tailwind that may not yet be fully reflected in the stock. The rare earth JDA is a meaningful strategic development but requires years to generate revenue.

At 17x depressed earnings, MOS is neither cheap enough to buy aggressively nor expensive enough to avoid. The stock needs either a demonstrable earnings recovery (Q1-Q2 2026 results) or further progress on the rare earth front to warrant upgrading to BUY. The 3.0% dividend yield provides a modest carry while waiting. **HOLD** at current levels, with a potential upgrade to BUY if Q1 2026 earnings show meaningful sequential improvement from Q4's trough, or if the stock revisits the $23-25 range where the risk/reward becomes more compelling.

The Barclays price target of $31 and the 52-week high of $38.23 provide a reasonable estimate of the analyst target range ($25-$38), though formal consensus data was not available.
