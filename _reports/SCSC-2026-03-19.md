## Company Overview

ScanSource operates as a technology distributor serving resellers, VARs, and managed service providers. The company distributes barcode/point-of-sale hardware, networking equipment, communications/collaboration technology, and increasingly cloud and connectivity solutions. It has historically served as a middleman between OEMs (Zebra Technologies, Honeywell, Cisco, etc.) and channel partners.

The company is headquartered in Greenville, SC, listed on NASDAQ, with approximately 21.5M shares outstanding. It pays no dividend. The fiscal year ends June 30.

## Growth Analysis

ScanSource's stated strategy is transitioning from hardware distribution toward recurring-revenue models via software, cloud, and connectivity. The March 2026 appointment of Mark Morgan as President of Specialty Technologies — a 23-year company veteran who led the barcode/mobility business — suggests the company is formalizing this pivot with experienced internal leadership rather than bringing in external talent.

Technology distribution is a low-single-digit organic growth industry, with secular pressures from direct-to-customer sales by OEMs and cloud-native procurement bypassing traditional channels. ScanSource's opportunity lies in serving the mid-market and smaller resellers that lack the scale to procure directly from vendors, and in adding value through multi-vendor integration, financing, and cloud orchestration.

Without revenue trend data, the growth trajectory cannot be quantified. The flat forward P/E suggests the market prices minimal near-term growth. The Raymond James conference presentation (February 25) indicates management is actively marketing to institutional investors, which could signal either confidence or a need to shore up the shareholder base after the February selloff.

## Risk Assessment

**Insider selling is the most concerning signal.** CEO Baur's selling pattern is aggressive:
- September 17-18, 2025: Exercised 125,000 options at $38.19/share and immediately sold 150,000 shares at ~$44.50-$44.96, netting approximately $6.7M.
- December 11-16, 2025: Sold an additional ~20,000 shares for ~$823K.
- March 13-16, 2026: Sold ~25,000 shares for ~$894K.

Total CEO dispositions over six months: approximately $8.4M. Baur still holds ~157,500 shares (~$5.4M at current prices), so he has not entirely exited, but the selling pace is notable. CFO Jones also sold $212K in December.

The partially offsetting signal is Mathis's $211K purchase on February 11, immediately after the earnings selloff. Mathis appears to be a director (title field is blank but he received a board grant in September). This is a smaller amount but directionally meaningful as a voluntary open-market purchase at the post-selloff price.

**Macro risk:** In a stagflationary environment with oil at $119 and recession probability at 40-45%, technology spending by ScanSource's mid-market customer base faces headwinds. Distribution margins compress during demand slowdowns as inventory turns slow and pricing power weakens.

**Tariff risk:** As a distributor of hardware manufactured globally (barcode scanners, networking equipment, communications hardware), ScanSource faces direct exposure to Section 301 tariffs on imported goods. Depending on country-of-origin composition, this could compress margins or reduce demand if prices are passed through.

**Transformation execution risk:** The hardware-to-cloud pivot is strategically sound but operationally difficult. Distribution companies that fail to add recurring-revenue services risk margin erosion as hardware becomes more commoditized.

## Conclusion

SCSC is a **HOLD**. The 10.6x P/E is undemanding, and the strategic direction is reasonable, but the heavy CEO selling pattern, the severity of the February earnings reaction, and the inability to assess financial fundamentals due to missing data create too much uncertainty for a BUY recommendation. The calibration data warns against issuing BUY calls on cyclically sensitive, data-limited situations — roughly 4 in 10 BUY calls lose money, and the burden of proof should be on the upside case. The single director purchase ($211K) does not offset $8.4M in CEO sales.

Investors should wait for: (1) the next quarterly earnings report to assess whether the February disappointment was transitory or structural, (2) visibility into balance sheet condition and free cash flow generation, and (3) a deceleration or cessation of CEO selling. If the stock revisits the $28-30 range (its April 2025 lows) with improving operational metrics, the risk/reward would become more compelling.
