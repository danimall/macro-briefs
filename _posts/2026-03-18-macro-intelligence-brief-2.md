## Executive Summary

The March 18 data batch represents the densest single-day convergence of macro-moving events since the Iran conflict began: FOMC meeting day with hot PPI (0.7% MoM, 3.4% YoY), oil at $108 on South Pars gas field strikes, consumer sentiment at 56, S&P 500 at 2026 lows, $307B Gulf deposit flight warning from S&P, and credit spreads widening with >$90B of new issuance demand hitting bond markets simultaneously. Every element of the stagflationary thesis is receiving independent confirmation from different data sources, different geographies, and different asset classes.

The analytical picture has shifted from "confirmed stagflation" to "stagflation with broadening second-order effects." The Hormuz disruption is no longer an oil story — it is now confirmed as disrupting fertilizer (WFP 45M hunger warning), aluminum (prices skyrocketing), pharmaceuticals (50% of US generics transit Hormuz), fashion supply chains (Zara/H&M delays), iron ore freight, and semiconductor inputs (helium scarcity). Each channel operates on a different lag structure: diesel passthrough hits CPI in 4-8 weeks, fertilizer hits food prices in 3-6 months, aluminum hits manufactured goods in 2-4 months. The multi-channel CPI impact estimate of 3.2-5.2% continues tracking toward the upper end, with the probability of exceeding 5% now at 25-30%. The February PPI print (0.7% MoM) was captured before the oil shock's full force — it confirms that pipeline inflation was already elevated before the exogenous supply shock compounded it.

Two developments warrant specific attention for their deviation from prior analysis. First, the Mizuho downgrade of CF Industries to Sell — CF being the S&P 500's top performer since the conflict began — introduces a credible counter-signal to the fertilizer thesis. The downgrade argument (rally overshot fundamentals) depends entirely on conflict duration assumptions; the 5+ structural data points supporting CF (WFP warning, spring planting disruption, domestic nat gas advantage, ~9x P/E, Reuters supply confirmation) remain intact, but position management should account for the possibility of mean reversion if a ceasefire materializes faster than the 5% rapid de-escalation probability suggests. Second, the MSFT-AMZN legal dispute over OpenAI hosting exclusivity reveals fragility in Microsoft's AI differentiation narrative that has been underappreciated. If OpenAI diversifies its cloud provider relationships, Azure's AI growth premium compresses.

## Key Events & Analysis

### FOMC Day: Hot PPI Removes Any Remaining Ambiguity on Near-Term Cuts

February PPI at 0.7% MoM (3.4% YoY) is the third consecutive month of surging wholesale prices, and it was measured before the oil spike's full force hit the economy. This is the most important single data point for the Fed meeting because it eliminates any argument that inflation was cooling sufficiently to justify near-term rate cuts. Core PCE at 3.1%, PPI at 3.4%, NFP at -92K, unemployment at 4.4%, and oil at $108 creates a data matrix where no policy response is correct. Cutting rates risks embedding inflation. Holding risks accelerating the employment deterioration already visible in February's negative payroll print. Hiking would crash already-stressed housing and consumer credit markets.

The hold is a certainty. What matters is the dot plot: if median 2026 rate cut projections decline from December's three cuts to two or fewer, it validates the "no cuts in 2026" probability at 20-25% and mechanically reprices every rate-sensitive asset. Mortgage rates, already at 7-month highs after a 19% refinance demand collapse, would push higher. Consumer credit costs, already squeezing households dealing with -92K payrolls and $5 diesel, would tighten further. The RBA hiking rates in Australia citing Iran-driven inflation confirms that global coordinated easing is impossible — each central bank is trapped by the same supply shock but responding differently based on domestic conditions.

Trump's demand for immediate rate cuts creates political pressure that the Fed must publicly navigate. Powell's communication strategy at the press conference will determine whether markets interpret the hold as "patient" (equity neutral) or "trapped" (equity negative). The political pressure dimension raises the credibility crisis probability to the upper end of the 10-15% range estimated in the world model.

### South Pars Strike Expands Energy Disruption to Gas

Israeli strikes hitting South Pars — the world's largest gas field, shared with Qatar — expand the energy disruption from crude oil to natural gas and LNG. South Pars provides feedstock for Iranian fertilizer plants, petrochemical facilities, and domestic power generation. Damage to this infrastructure would remove not just Iranian crude exports but Iranian gas-derived products from global supply chains. Combined with Saudi production cuts (20% reduction to 8M bpd), UAE production down >50% from infrastructure attacks, and Hormuz effectively blocked, the supply gap is now structural rather than transactional.

The $108 price level reached today sits within the world model's central range ($98-108) but tests the upper boundary. If South Pars damage proves significant, the ceiling range of $115-130 comes into play. The refiner demand destruction signal (refiners balking at elevated premiums per Bloomberg) introduces a self-correcting mechanism — but correction through demand destruction is the recession pathway, not the inflation relief pathway. Prices may cap below $130 because the economy breaks before supply is restored.

For fertilizer specifically, South Pars disruption compounds with the existing spring planting emergency documented by Reuters. CF Industries' domestic natural gas feedstock advantage becomes more pronounced with each escalation. Mizuho's downgrade to Sell argues the rally has overshot, but this call assumes conflict resolution on a timeline shorter than the growing season. If Hormuz remains disrupted through May-June, current CF pricing may prove conservative.

### Credit Market Stress Approaches Structural Test

The convergence of >$90B in new capital demand on credit markets in a single week ($18B EA junk bond offering + $63B fallen angel risk + €14.5B Amazon + $11B Novartis) provides the most informative test of the credit cascade thesis since its formation at 22+ data points. Euro IG markets went a full week without issuance before reopening to strong demand — suggesting stress but not seizure. GCC bond spreads retraced after initial widening — a modest contrary signal suggesting regional markets absorbed geopolitical risk faster than expected.

The EA offering remains the single most informative event. If it prices at reasonable spreads with healthy demand, the credit cascade probability remains at 55-60%. If it requires significant concessions or is pulled, the probability rises toward 65%. The March 31 quarter-end (now 12 days away) is the timing variable that matters: semi-liquid fund redemption requests must be submitted before quarter-end. The Blue Owl insolvency from the prior brief, combined with private credit secondaries market emergence, creates visible portfolio deterioration that may accelerate redemption requests.

The $307B Gulf deposit flight risk flagged by S&P introduces a potential paradox: if Gulf capital flees to US Treasuries, it could partially offset the Treasury safe-haven failure by creating forced demand for dollar-denominated safe assets. This would moderate Treasury yield increases but would also signal global financial instability severe enough to trigger capital flight from one of the world's wealthiest regions.

### Broadening Supply Chain Disruption Validates Multi-Channel Inflation Model

The most analytically significant development in this batch is independent confirmation from multiple sources that Hormuz disruption extends far beyond crude oil. Global X confirmed fertilizer, helium, and semiconductor impacts. WFP warned of 45M facing acute hunger from fertilizer disruption. FT reported aluminum prices skyrocketing. Fashion supply chains disrupted (Zara, H&M). Iron ore freight surging. Pharmaceutical supply chains from India impaired. Iran's statement that Hormuz needs a "new protocol" suggests prolonged disruption even after fighting stops.

Each channel operates on a different lag and impacts different CPI components. The 4-8 week diesel passthrough will appear in April CPI. Fertilizer's 3-6 month lag hits food prices in Q3. Aluminum's 2-4 month lag hits manufactured goods in Q2-Q3. Pharmaceutical disruptions could create acute shortages within weeks for drugs with limited domestic production. The standard CPI models that track oil prices and core PCE will systematically underestimate the compounding effect of simultaneous disruption across all six channels.

This broadening is the mechanism by which the "transitory" argument fails. A single oil shock can be absorbed and reversed. Six simultaneous commodity disruptions with staggered lag structures create overlapping inflation waves that persist even if crude prices moderate. The 25-30% probability of multi-channel inflation exceeding 5% reflects this compounding logic.
