## Executive Summary

The macro environment as of March 12 has consolidated into a stagflationary pattern with unusual clarity across every major signal. Oil's rebound from $88 to $101 within 24 hours confirmed what the physical evidence already showed: the SPR release was a temporary price suppressor deployed against an intensifying, not stabilizing, disruption. Mojtaba Khamenei's public declaration that Hormuz should remain closed, the IEA calling this the "largest supply disruption in history," and six confirmed vessel attacks collectively overwhelm the SPR's 3-5 week buffer. Trump's statement that the US "benefits from high oil prices" and the priority is "stopping Iran" removes the last assumption undergirding a rapid de-escalation scenario — the administration is signaling comfort with sustained elevated energy prices. The central oil range settles at $95-105 through end-March, and the five directional position changes across six briefs finally yield the correct conclusion: set the energy position at 50-60% retained and stop adjusting on headlines.

The credit cascade thesis has crossed a threshold. With the 17th independent data point (Partners Group chair publicly warning default rates could double to >5%), four separate fund-level withdrawal restrictions (BlackRock, Starwood, Morgan Stanley, Cliffwater), and JPMorgan already marking down software loans, the self-reinforcing mechanism is operating across four simultaneous vectors. Deutsche Bank's decision to expand its €26B private credit book on the same day Partners Group warned about doubling default rates encapsulates the late-cycle dynamic: some institutions are leaning in precisely as the most experienced operators are raising alarms. If Ares or KKR restrict semi-liquid fund redemptions within the next 30 days, probability approaches 70%.

The labor market's unresolved claims-vs.-NFP divergence (213K vs. -92K) persists into a third week. This is the most important domestic macro signal heading into March 18-19 FOMC. February CPI at 2.4% is likely the trough: oil passthrough, supply chain broadening across pharma, fashion, semiconductors, and fertilizer, and gasoline above $3.50/gallon all feed into Q2 prints with a 3-6 month lag. The globally synchronized hawkish shift (ECB hawks pushing 2021/22-style response, BOJ emboldened, EM central banks constrained) removes the traditional backstop of "other central banks easing." The March FOMC faces maximum ambiguity: stale CPI, contradictory labor data, oil at $101 (not $88 or $120), and IEEPA tariffs struck down but Section 301 initiated. Any forward guidance will be wrong relative to at least one of these signals.

## Key Events & Analysis

### The SPR Buffer Calculus: 3-5 Weeks Against Worsening Disruption

The analytical framework from Brief #57 (morning) is confirmed and strengthened by this afternoon's additional sourcing. The critical update: Trump explicitly stating the US benefits from high oil prices removes the assumption that the administration would urgently pursue de-escalation to bring prices down. Combined with US intelligence assessing Iran's government is not near collapse, the conflict duration distribution shifts toward the longer tail.

At 400M barrels and current disruption severity (90% tanker traffic decline, six vessel attacks, mines laid, drones deployed), the effective SPR buffer is 3-5 weeks. If the conflict extends past this window — which the intelligence assessment and Trump's comments both suggest is likely — oil must re-price to physical fundamentals. Iran's selective transit (India-flagged tankers allowed, Chinese crude continuing) means this is a partial blockade, not a total closure, which caps upside but also means the disruption is harder to resolve through military means. You can't sweep mines from a channel that's partially open to some traffic.

Russia earning an additional $150M/day from elevated oil prices creates a perverse incentive structure: Russia has no interest in de-escalation. Rerouted Russian crude to India (via the same transit corridor Iran is keeping open for India-flagged vessels) suggests emerging Russia-Iran coordination on energy trade routes that bypasses Western-aligned infrastructure.

Revised de-escalation probability: 18-25%, down slightly from 20-28%. The combination of Trump's comments, intelligence assessment of Iranian regime stability, and Russian financial incentives to sustain elevated prices all argue against rapid resolution.

### Credit Cascade at 55-60%: The Deutsche Bank Signal

Partners Group's default warning and four fund withdrawal restrictions were covered in Brief #57. The new element is Deutsche Bank expanding its €26B private credit portfolio on the same day these warnings were published. This pattern — late entrants scaling up while early movers restrict — is a recurring feature of credit cycles. It does not change the cascade probability but it identifies which institution is likely to bear concentrated losses if the 5%+ default scenario materializes. DB's emphasis on "conservative underwriting standards" echoes identical language from every institution that subsequently experienced material losses during prior credit stress episodes.

The AI hyperscaler bond boom adds a structural dimension to credit markets that didn't exist in prior cycles. Amazon's $42B issuance, combined with the broader AI capex funding wave, is absorbing credit market capacity that would otherwise be available to leveraged borrowers and private credit refinancings. This crowding-out effect is gradual but persistent: every $10B in AAA/AA-rated AI infrastructure bonds that institutional investors absorb reduces their appetite for lower-rated credit. The mechanism is indirect but real, and it compounds the direct stress from rising defaults and fund gating.

### Supply Chain Broadening: Now Confirmed Across Five Independent Channels

The multi-channel supply disruption thesis from Brief #57 receives additional sourcing today without changing the analytical framework. Five channels are now confirmed by independent sources:

1. **Oil/energy** (IEA, Reuters, CNBC — Tier 1): $100+ crude, $3.50+ gasoline
2. **Fertilizer** (CNBC, FT — Tier 2): Gulf sulphur disruption during spring planting
3. **Pharmaceuticals** (TechStory, Mathrubhumi — Tier 3): ₹5,000 crore Indian pharma impact, 10-20% medicine price increases
4. **Semiconductors** (Samsung CEO — Tier 1): Cost pressure from petrochemical input disruption
5. **Fashion/consumer goods** (AP, NBC — Tier 1): Zara, H&M supply delays

Each channel hits CPI with different lags: oil is immediate, gasoline within weeks, apparel in 2-3 months, food in 3-6 months, pharma in 1-3 months. Standard CPI models that estimate oil passthrough alone will understate the cumulative price impact. I revise the combined plausible peak CPI to 2.8-4.5% in 2026, with the upper bound reflecting simultaneous activation of multiple channels.

The Jones Act waiver consideration signals the administration recognizes domestic supply chain strain is approaching a threshold where regulatory relief is necessary. If enacted, this benefits foreign-flagged shipping operators at the expense of domestic carriers (negative for Jones Act-dependent US shipping).

### Software Sector: One Contrarian Voice Against Three Institutional Confirmations

Wedbush's Dan Ives calling the IGV 19% decline "massively overblown" is a single analyst opinion against three independent institutional actions: JPMorgan marking down software loans, Goldman marketing short-loan strategies targeting AI-disrupted software, and BlackRock reducing exposure to riskier credit. Per the calibration framework, analyst commentary is noise while bank balance sheet actions are signal. The IGV 19% decline may ultimately prove overdone, but the evidence base supporting continued pressure (3 institutional-level data points) vastly outweighs the contrarian case (1 sell-side analyst).

Atlassian's 10% workforce reduction to fund AI investment is the latest data point in the industry-wide pattern: established software companies cannibalizing their own economics to prevent AI disruption. This is deflationary for software sector margins even if the AI pivot succeeds, because it represents a permanent increase in R&D intensity without guaranteed revenue offset.

### Gold's Non-Response and Ruffer's Reallocation

Gold remaining stagnant near ~$5,000 despite active military conflict is now a two-week pattern, which is long enough to require an explanation rather than dismissal. The mechanism is clear: dollar strength from safe-haven flows plus rising nominal yields from inflation expectations offset gold's traditional conflict bid. The structural thesis (sustained negative real rates from Fed hold + rising CPI) remains valid but the tactical signal is weakening. Ruffer's documented shift to credit spread hedges and yen over gold represents institutional capital finding alternative crisis protection instruments. I maintain the bullish thesis on NEM and GLD but note this as a weakening signal that warrants re-evaluation if gold breaks below $4,800 for five consecutive sessions.

### Housing: Brief Window Closed

The sub-6% mortgage rate window that opened briefly in February and drove an uptick in existing home sales has closed. Rates back at 6.11-6.25% and rising (driven by oil-inflation expectations → higher Treasury yields) re-freeze the market. The housing affordability bill passing the Senate 89-10 with institutional investor restrictions adds a new variable: if signed into law, it reduces marginal demand from INVH-type single-family rental operators while potentially improving affordability. The net effect on homebuilders is ambiguous — lower institutional competition for inventory but also weaker overall market conditions. Homebuilders remain structural AVOIDs per the world model.
