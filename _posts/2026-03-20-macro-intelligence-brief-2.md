## Executive Summary

This data batch consolidates the full picture of the March 18-20 period, capturing the FOMC decision, Iranian strikes on Qatar and Saudi infrastructure, synchronized global central bank hawkishness, and cascading supply chain disruptions across commodities, credit, and currencies. The regime has shifted from "confirmed stagflation" to "stagflation under active policy constraint tightening with supply shock broadening" — and the evidence base for this characterization is now overwhelming, with 8+ independent Tier 1 sources confirming different dimensions of the same structural problem.

Three developments from this batch warrant particular attention for their cumulative significance. First, the IEA calling this the "largest supply disruption in history of global oil market" with a 6-month restoration timeline converts what had been an analyst estimate into an institutional forecast. Iraqi output cut 70% means approximately 3M bpd of non-Iranian Middle East production is also offline. Bloomberg's explicit argument that markets are underpricing the shock — rates too low, equities too high, dollar not strong enough — represents a consensus shift among institutional research that typically precedes the next leg of repricing. Second, China's ban on fuel and fertilizer exports adds a seventh independent confirmation to the CF Industries fertilizer thesis and, more importantly, reveals China's strategic calculus: preserve domestic stockpiles while Western competitors face shortages, creating a 30-40% manufacturing cost advantage that becomes structural rather than temporary. Third, the Micron sell-the-news reaction despite blockbuster earnings, combined with Samsung's $73B AI chip investment commitment, signals that the AI semiconductor trade is entering a maturation phase where execution and valuation discipline matter more than narrative momentum.

Since the prior brief, the world model's key parameters have moved directionally as projected: oil breached the $108-115 range into $119 territory, the Fed confirmed rate hikes were discussed, gold fell below $4,800, and defense received its most concrete funding signal ($200B Pentagon request + $23B Gulf arms sales). The credit cascade thesis enters its critical test window with March 31 eleven days away and hawkish Fed repricing raising borrowing costs at exactly the moment $90B+ of issuance demands market absorption.

## Key Events & Analysis

### Energy Infrastructure War: All Export Pathways Now Threatened

The most analytically significant development is the combined effect of the Qatar LNG strike (17% capacity destroyed, 5-year repair timeline per QatarEnergy CEO) and the SAMREF refinery strike at Yanbu on the Red Sea coast. These two attacks eliminate the last geographic diversification assumptions that had provided a floor under Gulf supply estimates. Qatar had been neutral — targeting its infrastructure signals Iran will strike any Gulf energy asset regardless of political alignment. SAMREF sits on the Red Sea, meaning the alternative Saudi export route that bypassed Hormuz is also vulnerable.

The IEA's 6-month restoration estimate and its characterization as the largest supply disruption in oil market history provides institutional weight to what had been an analyst-level assessment. Combined with Iraqi output down 70% (~3M bpd offline beyond Iran), the total supply removal is likely 10-12M bpd when accounting for all affected producers, transit disruption, and refining capacity damage. This exceeds even the world model's prior 7-9M bpd estimate.

The JERA buyer-shift statement is the structural consequence that outlasts the conflict. When Japan's largest power company explicitly states it will permanently reduce Middle East procurement, this is a signal to every Asian energy buyer. US crude rerouting to Asia via Panama Canal is already happening. This benefits US E&P (COP, EOG, OVV, SM), Canadian production (EWC), and US LNG (Cheniere/LNG at all-time highs). The trade flow reallocation will persist for years regardless of when the conflict ends because buyers who have been burned by supply disruption will maintain diversified sourcing.

Cheniere's all-time high deserves specific attention. Qatar's 5-year capacity loss of 17% represents approximately 13 mtpa of LNG — roughly equivalent to adding another major LNG project's worth of demand for non-Qatari suppliers. Cheniere, as the US's largest LNG exporter with ~45 mtpa capacity, is the single most direct beneficiary. The Thailand orders confirm that Asian buyers are actively contracting with US suppliers. LNG was not in the world model's tracked universe previously but should be added as a high-conviction BUY based on 3+ independent data points (Qatar capacity destruction, JERA buyer shift, Thailand contracts, European gas crisis at EUR74/MWh).

### Synchronized Central Bank Hawkishness Creates Global Monetary Trap

The Fed discussed rate hikes. The ECB warned of significant inflation risk. The BOJ flagged Iran-driven inflationary pressure. The SNB held rates watching franc strength. All four major developed-market central banks are simultaneously on hold with hawkish inflation warnings. This is the first time since the 2022-2023 tightening cycle that all major central banks have been synchronized in hawkish messaging, and the trigger is an exogenous supply shock rather than demand overheating.

The mechanism differs from 2022: then, central banks could tighten because labor markets were overheating and demand was excessive. Today, the Fed faces -92K payrolls, 4.4% unemployment, and declining consumer spending alongside PPI at 3.4% YoY and oil at $119. Tightening kills employment. Easing embeds inflation. The only tenable policy is to hold and communicate uncertainty — which is exactly what Powell's 14 "we don't know" repetitions conveyed.

The practical portfolio consequence is that any asset whose thesis depends on rate cuts is structurally impaired for the foreseeable future. This includes homebuilders (now facing rate hike risk, not just delayed cuts), growth tech at high multiples (discount rate compression thesis is dead), and consumer credit businesses (cost of funds rising while credit quality deteriorates). The beneficiaries are businesses that profit from higher rates or rate volatility: P&C insurance (ACGL, PGR, RNR invest float at higher yields), exchanges (CME, ICE, CBOE capture hedging activity), and fortress cash positions (BRK.B's $373B earning higher returns on short-duration instruments).

### Credit Market Stress Enters Terminal Test Phase

The credit cascade thesis now has 22+ data points, and the March 18-20 period adds three new stress vectors: (1) hawkish Fed repricing raises the cost of every new bond offering; (2) Amazon's €14.5B euro bond deal absorbs credit market capacity that might have gone to lower-quality borrowers; and (3) rising NIPs (Akzo Nobel paid the highest corporate concession of the year) signal deteriorating investor appetite for new risk at current spreads.

The EA $18B junk bond offering remains the live test. Post-FOMC conditions are materially worse than when banks committed to the deal: rate hike discussion, rising Treasury yields across the curve, and $119 oil compounding inflation fears. If the offering prices poorly, requires significant concessions, or is pulled, this moves the cascade probability from 55-60% toward 65%. March 31 is 11 days away. BX's BCRED redemptions at 7.9% of NAV, combined with JPMorgan restricting private credit lending, creates visible stress in the semi-liquid fund space that could accelerate if quarter-end triggers additional redemption requests.

The bond safe-haven failure is now globally confirmed: US Treasuries, UK gilts, Eurozone government bonds, and Indian government bonds all sold off during active geopolitical crisis. This is the definition of a broken hedging paradigm. The 60/40 portfolio provides zero diversification benefit when both stocks and bonds sell off simultaneously on supply-shock-driven inflation. This structural shift benefits P&C insurance (float invested at higher yields with uncorrelated underwriting income), BRK.B ($373B cash as the new safe haven), and commodity producers (real assets as inflation hedge).

### China's Strategic Position Continues Strengthening

China's ban on fuel and fertilizer exports is analytically important because it reveals Beijing's assessment: the energy crisis will persist long enough that domestic stockpile preservation is worth the diplomatic cost of restricting exports. China's preferential Hormuz passage (not disrupted by the conflict due to separate bilateral arrangements with Iran) combined with this export ban creates a 30-40% energy cost advantage over Western manufacturers. The fertilizer export ban specifically compounds the existing global shortage, adding a seventh independent data point to the CF Industries thesis.

The Reuters report that Iran's war could flip China from deflation to "bad inflation" introduces a potential downside for China-exposed companies. If cost-push inflation replaces the deflationary environment that had allowed Beijing to maintain easy monetary policy, the stimulus calculus changes. Chinese consumer demand recovery — already described as a "mirage" by NYT's NPC analysis — becomes even less likely if energy-driven inflation compresses household purchasing power.

### AI Semiconductor Trade Showing Signs of Exhaustion

Micron's 5%+ decline despite beating on both revenue and earnings is the clearest sell-the-news signal from the AI semiconductor trade in 2026. The implication: the market has fully priced the AI demand story, and incremental positive data generates selling rather than buying. Samsung's $73B investment commitment validates long-term AI infrastructure demand but simultaneously signals intensifying competition that will compress margins over 12-18 months — particularly in HBM where Samsung is directly competing with SK Hynix and indirectly pressuring NVDA's packaging partners.

The SMCI co-founder chip smuggling charge adds a governance and compliance dimension. Export control enforcement is tightening, and any company in the AI supply chain with exposure to China-bound chip flows faces increased compliance costs and legal risk. This reinforces the world model's hierarchy: power/infrastructure > optical > compute > memory > software, because infrastructure and power plays have less export control exposure.
