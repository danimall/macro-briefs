## Executive Summary

The March 18-19 data batch resolves the most important open question from prior briefs — the FOMC dot plot and forward guidance — and the answer is definitively hawkish. Powell disclosed that rate hikes were *discussed* at the meeting. Futures now price higher probability of a rate hike by June than a rate cut. This represents a regime change in monetary policy expectations. Combined with February PPI at 0.7% MoM (3.4% YoY), oil breaching $119 on Iranian strikes against Qatar's LNG hub and the Saudi SAMREF refinery at Yanbu, and the Pentagon requesting $200B+ for Iran war spending, the macro picture has shifted from "confirmed stagflation" to "stagflation with active policy constraint tightening." The Fed cannot cut, may have to hike, and the fiscal deficit is widening by $200B from war spending alone.

The oil price trajectory has broken through the world model's prior ceiling range. The $115-130 ceiling estimated in the world model was breached at $119 with the Qatar LNG strike, which represents a qualitative escalation: Iran is now destroying the supply infrastructure of countries that had been attempting neutrality. The SAMREF refinery strike at Yanbu demonstrates Iran can reach Red Sea coast facilities, threatening Saudi Arabia's alternative export routing that bypassed Hormuz. JERA executives explicitly stated this will produce a lasting structural shift of buyers to non-Middle East suppliers — a permanent trade flow reallocation that benefits US, Canadian, and Latin American producers. The world model's oil central range should be revised upward to $108-120, with a new ceiling of $130-150 if South Pars retaliation occurs.

Gold's 4% decline below $4,800 is the most significant contra-signal to the safe-haven thesis. Rising real yields from the hawkish Fed are overpowering geopolitical demand. This triggers the world model's 5-session conditional exit monitoring for NEM and gold miners. If gold fails to recover above $4,800 within 4 more sessions, the gold mining position should be exited. The credit cascade thesis faces its live test with the EA $18B junk bond offering this week, but the hawkish Fed repricing of rates *increases* credit stress by raising borrowing costs at exactly the moment when $90B+ of new issuance is hitting the market.

## Key Events & Analysis

### The FOMC Has Shifted From Dovish Hold to Hawkish Hold — Rate Hike Now on the Table

This is the single most important development in this data batch. Powell disclosing that rate hikes were discussed, combined with "several participants" suggesting the next move could be tightening, fundamentally changes the rate path expectation. The world model estimated "no cuts in 2026" at 20-25%. This should be revised upward to 25-30%. The probability of a rate hike in 2026 — previously negligible — is now 10-15%, consistent with the futures market repricing that shows hike odds exceeding cut odds by June.

The mechanism matters: PPI at 0.7% MoM (3.4% YoY) was captured *before* the oil spike's full force. April-May CPI will capture the diesel passthrough (4-8 week lag from $5+ diesel). If headline CPI prints above 3.5% in April, the rate-hike discussion becomes a rate-hike debate. If it prints above 4%, the debate becomes a decision. The multi-channel inflation model from the world model (combined peak CPI of 3.2-5.2%) now tracks toward the upper end with greater confidence, given that pipeline PPI was already elevated before the exogenous shock.

For portfolio construction, the rate-hike-on-the-table development raises the bar for every rate-sensitive position: homebuilders face even stronger headwinds than the already-severe 19% refinance demand collapse, consumer credit companies face rising cost of funds alongside deteriorating borrower quality, and growth tech at high multiples faces valuation compression from rising discount rates. The only direct beneficiaries are short-duration financial assets (money markets, floating rate instruments), bank net interest margins (though credit quality deterioration may offset), and volatility products.

### Oil at $119: Iran Strikes Qatar and Saudi Red Sea Infrastructure

Iran's missile strike on Qatar's LNG export facility is a qualitative escalation because Qatar had been attempting to maintain neutrality in the conflict. Targeting a neutral country's energy infrastructure signals that Iran will attack any Gulf energy asset, regardless of the country's political alignment. This eliminates the "selective targeting" assumption that had provided a floor under Gulf production estimates. The SAMREF refinery strike at Yanbu (a Saudi Aramco-ExxonMobil joint venture on the Red Sea coast) demonstrates that Saudi Arabia's alternative export routing through Red Sea ports — which was the workaround for Hormuz closure — is also vulnerable.

The combined effect is that *every* Middle East export pathway is now threatened: Hormuz is effectively closed, Red Sea/Yanbu-routed exports face missile strikes, and Qatar's LNG — which ships from Ras Laffan in the Gulf but also has pipeline connections — is directly impaired. The supply gap has widened beyond the 7-9M bpd estimated in the world model because these strikes are removing refining and processing capacity, not just export routes.

JERA's statement about lasting buyer shifts is the most consequential second-order effect. If Asian buyers permanently reduce Middle East procurement in favor of US, Canadian, Australian, and Latin American supply, this restructures global energy trade flows for a decade. US LNG export infrastructure (not adequately represented in the tracked universe — Cheniere/LNG is the primary beneficiary), Canadian heavy crude (EWC, OVV), and US E&P (COP, EOG) all benefit structurally.

Trump's threat to strike South Pars — the world's largest gas field, shared between Iran and Qatar — introduces an asymmetric risk: damage to South Pars would reduce Iranian gas production but could also impair Qatar's connected production infrastructure, further tightening LNG supply. European gas prices already surged 25% on the Qatar LNG strike; South Pars damage would send European gas toward crisis levels, potentially triggering European industrial shutdowns reminiscent of 2022.

### Pentagon's $200B Request: Defense Thesis Reaches Maximum Independent Confirmation

The $200B Pentagon budget request for Iran war spending is the 14th (or more) independent data point for the defense thesis and the first with a specific dollar figure attached to the supplemental. At $200B, this exceeds the annual peak of Iraq/Afghanistan supplementals (~$180B in FY2008) and would represent a ~25% increase to the annual defense budget. The spending flows to: munitions replacement (Tomahawk, Patriot batteries → LMT, RTX), naval operations (carrier strike groups, submarine deployments → HII, GD), intelligence/surveillance (PLTR, LHX), and logistics/support (KBR).

Saudi Arabia's statement reserving the right to military action against Iran, combined with UAE signaling willingness to join a US-led Hormuz security effort, represents a potential expansion of the conflict's scope but also the first credible pathway to Hormuz reopening: a multinational naval coalition rather than a unilateral US operation. The expanded coalition, if it materializes, would increase total military spending across multiple countries, further reinforcing defense procurement demand. Russia's sharing of satellite imagery and drone technology with Iran deepens the Russia-Iran military axis and makes the loss of the Russian sanctions waiver more likely (previously estimated at 15-25%, now tracking toward 20-30%).

Ukraine peace talks being paused due to the Iran war removes a potential positive catalyst for European energy markets and extends the dual-conflict overhang that has driven European defense spending commitments.

### Credit Market Stress Compounded by Hawkish Fed

The EA $18B junk bond offering arrives in a market where: (1) the Fed just discussed rate hikes, pushing borrowing costs higher; (2) $63B of bonds sit at fallen angel risk; (3) Amazon absorbed €14.5B from euro credit markets; (4) Novartis took $11B; and (5) total new issuance demand exceeds $90B in a single week. The hawkish Fed repricing mechanically raises the cost of every new bond offering and widens credit spreads, which is precisely the environment that stress-tests the credit cascade thesis.

The euro IG market reopening to strong demand after a week-long freeze is a modest positive signal for credit market functioning. However, IG and HY markets behave very differently under stress: IG markets can reopen while HY markets remain shut. The EA offering, being junk-rated, is the more informative test.

AI hyperscaler capex driving record bond issuance (separate event) adds supply pressure from a different direction: when the highest-quality borrowers (Amazon, Meta, Alphabet) are issuing massive amounts of debt to fund AI infrastructure, they crowd out lower-quality borrowers by absorbing investor demand for corporate bonds. This crowding-out effect intensifies during periods of risk aversion.

### Gold's Failure: Real Yields Trump Geopolitics

Gold falling 4% to below $4,800 during peak geopolitical crisis is analytically significant because it breaks the standard safe-haven model. The explanation is straightforward: real yields have risen sharply as the Fed discussed rate hikes while inflation expectations adjusted. Gold's opportunity cost is the real yield on Treasuries, and when that real yield rises faster than geopolitical risk premiums, gold falls. Silver's 16% decline is more severe because silver has a larger industrial demand component that is impaired by recession expectations.

This triggers the world model's conditional monitoring: gold below $4,800 for 5 consecutive sessions warrants exiting gold mining positions (NEM, RGLD). Today is session 1. The structural case (central bank de-dollarization, China's Hong Kong gold hub, bond safe-haven failure) remains intact for the medium term, but near-term price action is driven by real yields, and the Fed just signaled rates could go higher, not lower.

### Crypto Regulatory Framework Reaches Institutional Threshold

The combined weight of SEC/CFTC joint classification (most crypto as commodities, not securities), SEC-CFTC MOU, IRS reporting relief, Indiana pension law, Australian licensing framework, and institutional allocation surveys now constitutes 12+ independent data points establishing a comprehensive regulatory infrastructure for institutional crypto participation. Each data point addresses a different barrier: classification uncertainty (SEC/CFTC), custody standards (Australian framework), tax reporting (IRS), pension fiduciary duty (Indiana), and institutional risk frameworks (survey data).

COIN is the primary beneficiary because Coinbase has built the compliance infrastructure that institutional allocators require. The SEC commodity classification reduces COIN's largest regulatory risk (enforcement for listing unregistered securities) to near-zero for the classified assets. Position sizing should continue reflecting Bitcoin's price volatility, which adds ~40% more daily return variance than typical equity positions.
