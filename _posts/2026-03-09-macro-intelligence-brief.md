## Executive Summary

The March 9 data set represents a material escalation beyond the framework established in Brief #50. The central development: oil has surged to ~$120/barrel, a $30 move above the $90 level around which my prior analysis was anchored. Brent at $119 is the highest since 2022. This is no longer a "first-stage" oil shock with forward curves pricing short-lived disruption. Iraqi oil production has collapsed. Kuwait has declared force majeure. Saudi Aramco is conducting rare emergency tenders. Iran has named Mojtaba Khamenei — a hardliner — as supreme leader, directly contradicting the de-escalation thesis I upgraded to 15-20% probability in Brief #50 just 24 hours ago. The IRGC challenged the US Navy on Hormuz escorts. Iran threatened EU countries with "legitimate target" status. Wealthy individuals are fleeing the Gulf at a rate that overwhelmed Muscat airport.

**My prior de-escalation thesis requires a significant downward revision.** I raised it to 15-20% based on six signals including the forward curve pricing temporary disruption, Iran's succession panel "reaching consensus," and GCC credit retracement. The Mojtaba succession specifically contradicts signal #4 (I interpreted the succession process as preparing for transition/negotiation — instead, it produced a hardliner signaling defiance). The oil forward curve that I cited as a de-escalation signal has been overwhelmed by $120 spot. GCC credit retracement may prove to be a false positive as conflict intensity has materially escalated since that data point. I revise de-escalation probability from 15-20% down to 8-12%, weighted by the NYT ceasefire approach report (single source, potentially outdated by subsequent escalation). The base case shifts back toward prolonged conflict: 40% probability of acute phase extending beyond 6 weeks, 25% continued escalation, 25% 3-6 week resolution, 10% rapid de-escalation.

The macro implications of $120 oil on top of -92K NFP, declining retail sales, and a divided Fed are severe. The three-channel inflation model I described (oil, tariffs, food) now needs recalibration upward. Oil at $120 adds approximately 1.0-1.5% to YoY CPI if sustained for 2+ months, compared to the 0.5-1.0% I estimated at $90. Combined with Bessent's 15% tariff and fertilizer/food passthrough, peak CPI of 4-5% in 2026 becomes the central case rather than a tail scenario. The G7 emergency meeting to discuss 300-400M barrels of coordinated SPR release underscores the severity: this would be among the largest coordinated releases in history and signals governments believe the disruption could persist.

## Key Events & Analysis

### Oil at $120: The Qualitative Shift From Shock to Crisis

The move from $90 to $120 is qualitatively different from the move from $72 to $90. At $90, forward curves were pricing temporary disruption and the economic impact was manageable. At $120, we cross multiple thresholds simultaneously: (1) gas pump prices approach $5/gallon nationally, creating political and consumer behavioral inflection; (2) airline margins move from "compressed" to "potentially cash-negative" on unhedged fuel; (3) the CPI impact exceeds the 1% contribution that central banks can characterize as transitory; (4) consumer confidence effects compound — $5 gas changes spending behavior in ways that $3.50 gas does not.

Five hard data points support the $120 price being structural rather than panic-driven: Iraqi production collapse (millions of barrels/day removed), Kuwait force majeure (legal declaration of inability to export), Saudi emergency tenders (physical tightness requiring unusual market intervention), Hormuz effectively closed to commercial transit, and hardline succession reducing resolution probability. Against this, the G7 SPR release provides temporary supply of 300-400M barrels — approximately 3-4 days of global consumption. SPR releases are a ceiling tool, not a resolution. If the physical disruption persists, prices recalibrate upward once releases end.

I revise crude >$100 sustained through March from 60-70% to 80-85%. Crude >$120 sustained through end March: 45-55%. Crude >$150 if Hormuz remains closed into April: 20-25%.

### Prior Call Assessment: De-Escalation Thesis Premature

In Brief #50 (24 hours ago), I cited six signals pointing toward conflict compression and raised de-escalation probability to 15-20%. This call needs to be partially walked back in light of today's data.

What went wrong: I weighted the forward curve signal too heavily. Forward curves reflect trader positioning, which can be wrong — and in this case was overwhelmed within 24 hours by physical market events. I interpreted the succession panel movement as preparing for transition/negotiation rather than hardline consolidation. The Mojtaba selection directly falsifies this interpretation. The GCC credit retracement I cited reflected conditions before the March 8-9 escalation and may reverse.

What remains valid: G7 coordination is real and proceeding. The NYT ceasefire approach report has not been explicitly refuted. Iraqi proxy non-participation persists. China's diplomatic openness persists.

Net assessment: de-escalation from 15-20% → 8-12%. The XOM scale-out I recommended may have been premature. At $120 oil with hardline succession, the risk/reward of holding energy equity has shifted back toward holding. I would not add at these levels but would slow the exit pace.

Updated CPI impact model at $120 oil (sustained 2+ months):
- Oil passthrough: +1.0-1.5% to YoY CPI (up from +0.5-1.0% at $90)
- Bessent 15% tariff: +0.3-0.5% (unchanged)
- Fertilizer/food with 3-6 month lag: +0.2-0.5% (unchanged)

This shifts Fed calculus further toward the impossible corner. At 4-5% CPI, rate cuts are untenable. But -92K NFP and declining retail demand easing. The stagflation trap is now deeper than at any point since the conflict began. Recession probability: 35-40% (up from 30-35%), driven by the higher oil price impact on consumer spending and corporate margins.

Kalshi recession at 32% has moved substantially toward my prior 30-35% estimate. With the new oil data, my estimate has moved further out to 35-40%, maintaining a ~5-8pp gap. ### Credit: KKR Strong Earnings Don't Resolve Structural Vulnerability

KKR's $129B fundraising and $972M quarterly fee-related earnings are impressive and prove institutional demand for alternatives remained robust through Q4 2025. But this is backward-looking data from before the credit stress intensified. Blue Owl crossing $300B AUM is similarly a lagging indicator.

The critical question remains forward-looking: will KKR or Ares semi-liquid funds gate in the next two weeks? Strong historical earnings do not prevent redemption-driven gating. The Barclays CDS recommendation, AI hyperscaler bond issuance creating concentration risk, and European bond sale delays all point toward tightening conditions that will test semi-liquid structures in Q1 2026 reporting.

Credit cascade probability: I maintain 42-48% but acknowledge the $120 oil shock increases corporate margin pressure and could accelerate credit quality deterioration in energy-intensive sectors. Energy-exposed high-yield names face particular risk — paradoxically, even as energy producers thrive, energy-consuming companies (airlines, trucking, chemicals) see credit metrics deteriorate.

### Defense: From Thesis to Confirmed Multi-Year Procurement Cycle

Trump's meeting with defense CEOs from LMT, NOC, RTX, BA, BAE, HON, and LHX, explicitly discussing quadrupling production of "exquisite class" weapons, converts what was an analytical projection into a stated policy objective. Combined with: (1) Mojtaba succession extending expected conflict duration, (2) IRGC challenging US Navy on Hormuz, (3) Iran threatening EU, and (4) Congress rejecting war powers constraints, the defense procurement cycle has the strongest policy backing I've seen in this conflict.

Palantir's 15% weekly rally demonstrates the defense-tech rotation is real, but at 199x P/E the risk/reward is poor despite the thematic tailwind. The better expressions remain LMT, RTX, NOC, and GD at single-digit or low-teen P/E multiples with the same multi-year procurement visibility.

### Consumer Economy: $120 Oil Accelerates Spending Compression

The consumer spending trajectory was already deteriorating: December retail flat, January retail -0.2%, February -92K jobs. Adding $120 oil to this sequence creates a rapid escalation in consumer distress. Gas prices approaching $5/gallon will trigger behavioral changes: delayed vehicle purchases, reduced discretionary travel, trade-down in groceries, increased credit card usage at higher rates.

The E-shaped economy divergence deepens: wealthy consumers (who own energy equities and have lower fuel-cost sensitivity) continue spending; middle and lower-income consumers (higher fuel-cost share of wallet) cut back aggressively. This benefits DPZ (trade-down dining), WMT/COST (staples), and UBER (fuel passthrough model) while pressuring NKE, LULU, BBY, W, and travel/leisure names.

American Airlines' $1B Miami expansion announcement is notable as a contrarian signal — either AA sees through the current disruption to structurally higher travel demand, or this commitment was made before the March escalation and will be quietly deferred.

### Figma Earnings Beat: Isolated Positive in Enterprise Software

Figma's 15% post-earnings surge on revenue beat offers a data point on enterprise software demand resilience, but the February 18 date makes this stale relative to current conditions. I hold FIG at $30 with a target entry below $25. The earnings beat is encouraging but insufficient to change positioning given the macro deterioration since mid-February. Enterprise IT spending is the sector most likely to face budget compression in a stagflationary environment where companies need to cut costs.
