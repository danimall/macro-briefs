## Executive Summary

The past 72 hours have produced the most information-dense environment since the Iran conflict began, with every major macro variable moving simultaneously and in mutually reinforcing directions. The data now describes a fully formed stagflationary regime confirmed across all six measurable dimensions: Q4 GDP at 0.7%, core PCE at 3.1%, NFP at -92K, retail sales at -0.2%, gasoline up 21%, and quit rates declining. Historical episodes where negative payrolls and above-target inflation occurred simultaneously (1974, 1980, 2008) all resulted in recession. I raise the recession probability to 38-42% with timing accelerated to Q1-Q2 2026.

The conflict intensification since the prior brief on March 14 adds three developments that materially shift the probability distribution. First, Fujairah resumed oil loadings after the Iranian attack, which is modestly positive for alternative routing but sets the precedent that Gulf infrastructure is targetable. Second, Zelenskyy's claim that Russia is supplying Iran with Shahed drones formally links the two theaters, meaning Ukraine peace talks are stalled (confirmed by FT) while Russia earns $150M/day from elevated oil prices plus sanctions relief — Moscow's optimal strategy is conflict continuation. Third, the White House AI Czar publicly calling for the US to exit the war represents the first confirmed internal administration dissent on strategy, which creates a non-zero probability of an abrupt policy reversal that neither the oil market nor the defense sector is pricing. This is a genuine two-tailed risk: ceasefire rejection + continued strikes is the base case, but a sudden "declare victory" pivot would crash oil 15-20% and defense stocks 5-10% within days while lifting airlines, consumer discretionary, and homebuilders. I weight the abrupt exit probability at roughly 8-12% within 30 days, slightly higher than the prior brief's 12% de-escalation estimate, specifically because the dissent is now public rather than rumored.

The convergence of FOMC (March 18-19), NVIDIA GTC, and quad witching options expiration this week creates a mechanical volatility amplifier layered on top of the macro stress. The FOMC faces an information environment where no available data supports a clean directional call: stale CPI at 2.4% (pre-oil), core PCE at 3.1%, GDP at 0.7%, employment contracting, oil at $100+, and the president publicly demanding immediate cuts while the DOJ appeals a ruling blocking subpoenas to the Fed Board. I expect a hold with maximally ambiguous guidance that changes nothing but increases uncertainty about the path forward. The market is pricing no cuts until September 2026 at earliest; my estimate of no cuts in all of 2026 is 20-25%.

## Key Events & Analysis

### The SPR Arithmetic Problem Is Now Acute

Saudi Arabia's 20% production cut to 8M bpd removes approximately 2M bpd from global supply on top of the Hormuz disruption. Total supply loss now approaches 7-9M bpd at peak. The IEA announced its largest emergency stockpile release in 50 years (400M barrels), and the US issued a 30-day Russian oil sanctions waiver. Despite both measures, oil rose 17%+. The market is telling us the supply interventions are insufficient.

The revised buffer calculation: 400M barrels against 4-6M bpd of daily draw requirement (after accounting for partial Hormuz transit, Russian waiver, and alternative routing) implies 9-14 days of effective coverage. The conflict has been running approximately 10+ days. If the SPR has been drawn at this rate, the buffer may be near exhaustion without public acknowledgment. The probability of SPR depletion before conflict resolution rises to 50-60%. If depletion occurs without resolution, the supply gap becomes unmanageable and oil overshoots to $115-130, which would push core PCE toward 4%+ and make recession essentially certain.

The $63B windfall estimate for US oil producers at $100 average (FT) quantifies the earnings impact: XOM, COP, CVX, DVN, FANG, EOG all receive direct benefit. Refiners (VLO, MPC, PSX) face a more nuanced situation — the Russian sanctions waiver provides cheaper feedstock at elevated product prices, creating temporary margin expansion. This is an identifiable tactical catalyst.

### Internal Administration Dissent Changes the Tail Risk Distribution

The White House AI Czar publicly calling for the US to "declare victory and get out" is analytically significant because it transforms what was a latent possibility into a visible policy option. When a senior official breaks with the president on war strategy, it signals either: (a) the official expects imminent policy change and is positioning ahead of it, or (b) internal frustration is high enough that message discipline has broken down. Either interpretation increases the probability of an abrupt policy reversal relative to the prior brief.

For portfolio construction, this creates a genuine two-tailed exposure problem. The base case (70-75% probability) is continued conflict with ceasefire rejection holding, which supports energy, defense, insurance, and exchange positions. But the 8-12% probability of an abrupt "declare victory" exit means hedging against a rapid oil collapse and defense selloff has material expected value. The Fujairah port resumption provides partial evidence for gradual de-escalation, while Iran's selective passage of Indian tankers suggests a negotiated workaround is possible.

The practical implication is that energy positions should remain at 50-60% retention (not 100%) and defense positions, while maximum conviction on the multi-year restocking thesis, face near-term two-way risk. The restocking demand exists regardless of whether the war ends tomorrow — consumed Tomahawk missiles must be replaced — but defense stocks at peak war-premium valuations could give back 10-15% on an abrupt ceasefire, even if the medium-term demand thesis is intact.

### The Fed's Impossible Week

The March 18-19 FOMC occurs during the worst policy environment since 2022. Every input contradicts every other input. GDP at 0.7% argues for cuts. Core PCE at 3.1% argues against. Employment at -92K argues for emergency action. Oil at $100+ argues against easing into a supply shock. The president is publicly demanding immediate cuts while the DOJ appeals a ruling blocking subpoenas to the Fed Board.

Boston Fed's Collins already telegraphed the answer: "no urgent need to cut." The meeting will produce a hold, as markets expect at ~99% probability. The question is whether the dot plot shifts and what Powell says about the interaction between energy prices and the labor market. If the dot plot shows fewer 2026 cuts than previously signaled, it would validate the "no cuts in 2026" thesis I've been building. If Powell explicitly states the oil price shock creates "transitory" inflation (echoing the 2021 error), markets would sell bonds on the credibility damage.

Trump's public pressure adds a dimension that didn't exist at prior FOUCs: the possibility that political intimidation affects the statement language, even marginally. The judge blocking the Fed subpoenas provides temporary insulation, but the DOJ appeal creates ongoing overhang. I maintain the 10-15% probability of a Fed credibility crisis from political pressure within the next 6 months.

### The Credit Cascade Continues to Build

Amazon's €14.5B euro bond, the third major hyperscaler issuance in 12 months, directly crowds out leveraged borrowers from credit markets. The mechanism is concrete: institutional investors filling $42B+ of investment-grade Big Tech paper have less capacity for BB/B-rated private credit and leveraged loans. Credit spreads widening in software and PE sectors (confirmed by multiple sources) show this crowding-out effect is already manifesting. Private credit concerns are now pressuring financial stocks broadly.

The credit cascade thesis has accumulated approximately 19 independent data points across three weeks. The threshold trigger remains Ares/KKR semi-liquid fund redemption data. The Amazon issuance adds urgency: if a $42B hyperscaler bond deal absorbs credit capacity while PE funds face redemptions, the refinancing wall for leveraged portfolio companies becomes steeper. I maintain 55-60% cascade probability, rising to ~70% if Ares/KKR restrict redemptions.

### The AI Sector's Contradictory Signals

Nvidia's GTC this week, featuring a new chip incorporating Groq technology (~$20B investment context), arrives at a moment when the AI infrastructure thesis faces both confirmation and challenge. Confirmation: Nvidia's $2B investments each in Nebius, Lumentum, and Coherent validate the optical networking and cloud infrastructure supply chain. Meta cutting staff to fund AI capex confirms hardware demand persists. Amazon's €14.5B bond issuance partly funds AI infrastructure. Challenge: the AI-to-energy sector rotation identified in prior briefs is driven by capital migrating from growth-duration tech to inflation-beneficiary sectors. GTC could temporarily interrupt this rotation but cannot reverse the macro driver ($100+ oil repricing the economy's cost structure).

The hierarchy remains: power/infrastructure (CEG, EQIX, GEV) > optical (COHR, LITE at HOLD, not BUY, given extreme valuations) > compute (TSM, AVGO) > memory (MU at TRIM, triple risk) > software (structural underweight). The 32% S&P 500 tech concentration creates amplification risk: every passive fund sale is 32% tech, creating a mechanical selling pressure that compounds any fundamental rotation.
