## Key Events & Analysis

### Russian Sanctions Relief: Insufficient Supply, Sufficient Diplomatic Damage

Treasury Secretary Bessent's authorization to purchase Russian oil stranded at sea is the administration's first explicit acknowledgment that the SPR release alone cannot contain energy prices. The volume of stranded Russian crude at sea is modest relative to the 90% tanker traffic decline through Hormuz, so the supply impact is marginal. But the diplomatic consequences are meaningful: Germany's Merz publicly criticized the decision, adding trade friction at the same moment the US is launching Section 301 probes against allies (UK, EU, Canada). The US is simultaneously asking allies to coordinate on Iran while undercutting Russian sanctions that allies have maintained at significant economic cost.

For oil price modeling, Russian sanctions easing adds perhaps 200-500K barrels/day of accessible supply against a 5-7M barrel/day Gulf disruption. The arithmetic doesn't work to solve the supply problem. It does, however, confirm the world model's assessment that Russia has zero incentive to support de-escalation: Moscow is earning $150M/day in additional revenue *and* now watching the US weaken its own sanctions architecture for Russia's benefit.

The six-party incentive analysis for conflict persistence has strengthened: US (Trump comfortable with high prices), Russia ($150M/day + sanctions easing), Iran (using Hormuz as leverage country-by-country), China (strategic stockpiling at scale, though duration unknown), India (intermediary role in selective transit), and Gulf states (scrambling to reroute through Red Sea). Only Gulf states and European allies have clear incentives for rapid resolution, and they lack the leverage to achieve it. De-escalation probability: 15-22%, a further decline from 18-25%.

### The Helium Supply Chain: A Sixth Disruption Channel

Qatar's LNG operations halt as collateral damage from the conflict has exposed helium supply fragility. Qatar is one of the world's largest helium producers (as a byproduct of LNG processing), and the disruption creates immediate scarcity for three critical end-use markets: semiconductor manufacturing (process gas for plasma etching and cooling), healthcare (MRI machine superconducting magnets require liquid helium), and aerospace (testing and pressurization). JPMorgan upgrading an industrial gas supplier on this thesis represents early Wall Street recognition.

This is the sixth independent supply disruption channel, adding to oil, fertilizer, pharmaceuticals, semiconductors (direct petrochemical costs per Samsung CEO), and fashion/consumer goods. The compounding CPI impact of six simultaneous channels with staggered lags raises the peak CPI risk further. I revise upward to 3.0-4.8%, with the upper bound reflecting the scenario where helium-driven semiconductor supply constraints combine with existing petrochemical cost pressures to amplify the Samsung CEO's cost warning into actual production curtailment.

Air Products (APD) and Linde (LIN) as the two largest industrial gas companies are the most direct beneficiaries. Neither had been on the tracked universe prior to this event. This is a new thesis with a single data point (Qatar disruption + JPM upgrade) — monitoring rather than conviction.

### Consumer Deterioration: Ulta's Explicit Attribution

Ulta Beauty's weak guidance explicitly citing "global conflicts" as a driver of consumer selectivity is the first major retailer to directly attribute spending weakness to the Iran war's psychological and economic impact. This is different from prior consumer data points (retail sales -0.2%, Campbell's 23-year low) which showed *what* was happening without C-suite attribution of *why*.

Combined with UK consumer spending weakness from BRC data, the pattern is now globally synchronized across premium discretionary (Ulta), value discretionary (Campbell's), and international (UK BRC). The only segment showing resilience is trade-down value (DPZ thesis, WMT). Gasoline at $3.50+, up 21% in one month, functions as a regressive consumption tax that disproportionately compresses lower-income spending. The proposed $3,000 unemployment benefit increase, if passed, would partially offset this compression for the unemployed but is unlikely to pass quickly enough to matter for Q1-Q2 data.

### The VIX Hasn't Capitulated

Wolfe Research's observation that the VIX hasn't reached capitulation levels on the Dow's 700-point drop (lowest close of 2026) deserves attention. In prior equity drawdowns of similar magnitude, VIX spikes above 35-40 typically accompanied the bottoming process. If the VIX is still below these levels, it implies either (a) options market participants are not yet positioned for further downside, meaning more selling is ahead, or (b) the nature of this selloff (slow-grinding stagflation rather than acute crisis) may not produce a VIX spike. Either interpretation argues against buying this dip aggressively.
