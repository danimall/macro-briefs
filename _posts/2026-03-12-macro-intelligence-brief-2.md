## Key Events & Analysis

### Fed Internal Division: Stagflation's Policy Manifestation

The split between Collins/Hammack/Goolsbee and Miran is the FOMC's internal version of the macro problem I've tracked across five briefs. Miran's argument — that -92K February payrolls strengthen the case for cuts — directly conflicts with the other three officials' position that inflation remains "too high." Both positions are simultaneously supported by hard data. This is what stagflation looks like at the institutional level.

The practical implication: the March 18-19 FOMC statement will likely attempt to bridge this divide with intensely hedged language, probably emphasizing "data dependence" and declining to signal any near-term direction. The oil retrace from $120 to $88 gives the committee slightly more room than it would have had at $120, but the lagged CPI impact of the spike (I estimate +0.4-0.8% to YoY CPI at the revised $88-100 central oil range) means inflation readings will worsen in Q2 regardless of where oil sits on March 18.

Rate markets pricing two cuts by year-end to 3.0-3.25% implies oil normalizes, inflation moderates, and labor weakness persists. This is a specific scenario within my distribution, not the central case. My world model assigns 20-25% probability to no cuts in 2026. If February CPI at 2.4% was the trough (likely, given oil passthrough), each subsequent CPI print will constrain the FOMC further.

### India: Global Transmission of the Oil Shock

The Indian market selloff provides three analytical contributions. First, it confirms the conflict's impact extends beyond the Gulf and US — oil-importing EM economies are experiencing capital flight and fiscal stress simultaneously. India importing ~85% of crude faces a direct current account hit; the government prioritizing domestic LPG supply is an adaptation to sustained disruption, contradicting the market's rapid-resolution pricing.

Second, FII outflows from India risk broadening into a general EM risk-off if other oil-importing economies (Turkey, South Korea, Thailand) experience similar pressures. This creates a second channel of liquidity withdrawal beyond the hedge fund deleveraging already identified (Caxton, Citadel, ExodusPoint): institutional investors reducing EM exposure.

Third, Indian defense stocks outperforming within the selloff confirms the defense bid is geographically distributed. This is the sixth independent data point supporting the multi-year restocking thesis: (1) LMT CEO/Trump production mandate, (2) Rheinmetall 45% growth, (3) KeyBanc AVAV upgrade, (4) France warships to Hormuz, (5) US Navy unable to provide escorts (validating naval investment urgency), (6) Indian defense rally.

### The Whipsaw as Information: What Professional Macro Losses Tell Us

The $32 crude round-trip in under a week (with S&P 500 swinging hundreds of points on individual sessions) has generated confirmed losses at three major hedge funds. The Dow's 784-point intraday drop on March 5 followed by recovery represents the type of price action that breaks systematic strategies dependent on trend-following or mean-reversion signals. When both momentum and mean-reversion fail simultaneously, the result is forced deleveraging.

The investment implication is concrete: these three funds deleveraging simultaneously remove liquidity from markets. They must sell liquid positions (blue-chip equities, government bonds, gold) to meet margin calls, creating temporary mispricings in otherwise fundamentally sound assets. This is the primary reason BRK-B at 11.8x P/E with $373B cash is a high-conviction position: Berkshire is the natural counterparty to forced sellers. Exchange operators (CME, ICE, CBOE) benefit from the volume spike on both legs of the whipsaw.

### ADBE Tomorrow: The Software Litmus Test

Adobe reports Q1 FY2026 earnings on March 12. The setup is hostile: JPMorgan marked down software company loans, Goldman is marketing short-loan strategies targeting AI-disrupted software, and the world model classifies commodity SaaS as a structural underweight. At ~16x P/E and 10x P/FCF with $385M net cash, ADBE is priced at a significant discount to software peers, which means the market has already embedded substantial pessimism.

The binary question: can Firefly AI monetization demonstrate that ADBE is differentiated from the leveraged SaaS category under credit attack? If creative AI tools generate measurable revenue uplift and management guides to AI-driven pricing power, ADBE could decouple from the sector. If results confirm enterprise spending compression without an AI offset, the stock becomes another data point in the anti-software thesis and the NEM/CRM pair trade strengthens further.
