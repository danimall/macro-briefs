### Supreme Court Tariff Ruling: Positive but Overwhelmed by Oil Shock

The Supreme Court striking down Trump's 'Liberation Day' tariffs is the most significant positive macro development in weeks. It removes a source of cost-push inflation (tariff-driven metal and consumer goods price increases), eliminates trade policy uncertainty that was suppressing business investment, and reduces one of the two headwinds on consumer spending. Manufacturing ISM data explicitly showed tariff-driven metal prices weighing on demand — that channel now closes.

However, the quantitative impact is modest relative to the oil shock. Tariff costs were estimated to add 0.2-0.5% to CPI annually and reduce GDP by 0.3-0.5pp through demand destruction and supply chain disruption. The oil shock at $100+ Brent adds an estimated 0.5-1.0% to CPI through three channels (crude, gas, diesel) and reduces GDP by 0.3-0.7pp through consumer spending compression and margin degradation. The tariff ruling removes the smaller of two negative forces while the larger one intensifies.

The net effect for portfolio positioning: the tariff removal supports a modest reduction in recession probability from the upper end of the 22-28% range toward the lower end — approximately 22-25% now, versus 22-28% before. This is not sufficient to change the war-economy allocation. The ruling benefits import-heavy companies (AMZN, WMT, AAPL) at the margin but does not offset oil-driven cost pressures for most companies.

One second-order implication deserves attention: without tariffs, the Fed faces a simpler inflation picture. Oil is the sole dominant cost-push channel. This makes the monetary policy response more predictable — the Fed will focus entirely on oil-driven inflation expectations rather than parsing complex tariff-versus-energy dynamics. If oil sustains above $100, the path to zero cuts and potential hikes is cleaner without tariff noise.

### Consumer Sentiment Divergence: Recession Leading Indicator

MarketWatch's analysis of consumer sentiment splitting — growing gloom about both the broader economy and personal finances despite solid headline data — merits attention because this pattern has historically correlated with elevated recession probability. The mechanism: when consumers feel worse about their own finances (not just abstract "economy"), spending behavior actually changes. This is distinct from the surveys where consumers report the economy is bad but their personal situation is fine (which tends not to translate to behavioral change).

Combined with the fifteen consumer spending data points from the world model (including the latest NCLH sales miss), this sentiment data adds a psychological dimension to the hard spending data. The consumer is entering the oil shock already psychologically vulnerable.

This week's retail earnings (Target Mar 4, Costco Mar 5, Best Buy Mar 4) will test whether sentiment divergence has translated to actual spending behavior. If Target misses on both sales and guidance, it becomes the sixteenth consumer data point and strengthens the slow-grind earnings compression thesis.
