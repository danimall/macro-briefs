## Executive Summary

The oil market's $88-to-$101 rebound in 24 hours invalidates the aggressive energy scale-out urgency from Brief #55 and confirms that the $88 trough was an overshoot driven by the SPR release announcement rather than any resolution of physical disruption. Six merchant vessel attacks, 90% tanker traffic decline through Hormuz, and the IEA declaring this the "largest supply disruption in history" all occurred *after* the record SPR release. The 400M barrel reserve is being consumed against ongoing and intensifying physical disruption rather than declining disruption — a fundamentally different depletion calculus than assumed yesterday. The oil price is now oscillating in a $88-$101 range with the SPR release providing a temporary ceiling and physical disruption providing a floor. I revise the central oil range to $95-105 through end-March, splitting the difference between Brief #55's $88-100 and the pre-retrace $95-110.

The credit cascade thesis has reached what I now assess as 55-60% probability, a meaningful upgrade from 48-53% in Brief #55. Morgan Stanley and Cliffwater capping withdrawals from private credit funds represent the 14th and 15th independent data points, and critically, we now have *four* separate fund-level gating events (BlackRock, Starwood, Morgan Stanley, Cliffwater). The world model's trigger — "two or more managers gate within 30 days" raising probability to 60-70% — has been breached, though these are withdrawal caps rather than full gates. Partners Group's chair warning that default rates could double to above 5% is the most senior industry insider to publicly quantify downside. Goldman's executive privately admitting clients are "glad" the Iran war distracts from private credit scrutiny is the most revealing qualitative signal: the industry knows its valuations face a reckoning and prefers attention elsewhere.

The Supreme Court's 6-3 ruling striking down IEEPA tariffs introduces a variable not in the world model: the primary tariff mechanism has been legally invalidated. The administration's pivot to Section 301 investigations delays tariff implementation by 6-12 months, marginally reducing the near-term inflation trajectory (Bessent's 15% tariff CPI contribution of +0.3-0.5% is pushed out). This is modestly positive for growth expectations and modestly negative for the "inflation too high for cuts" thesis, but the shift is small relative to the oil-driven inflation channel.

## Key Events & Analysis

### Oil: The SPR Buffer Is Being Consumed Against Worsening, Not Improving, Disruption

The most important analytical development since Brief #55 is that the physical Hormuz disruption has intensified *after* the SPR release. Brief #55's framework assumed the $88 price reflected a combination of SPR supply and de-escalation rhetoric against *stable* physical disruption. Today's data shows the disruption is worsening: six vessels attacked (up from the single tanker hit that drove the initial spike), tanker traffic down 90% through Hormuz, and the IEA explicitly labeling this the largest supply disruption in history.

This matters for the SPR depletion calculus. If the physical disruption is getting worse while reserves are being drawn, the effective buffer period is shorter than the 4-6 weeks estimated in Brief #55. At $101 oil with ongoing draws, the market is consuming SPR supply at a faster rate relative to the disruption than projected. I revise the effective buffer to 3-5 weeks before prices must re-adjust to physical fundamentals.

Iran's selective transit — allowing India-flagged tankers, continuing to ship crude to China — confirms the world model's framing: this is a partial, instrumentalized blockade rather than a uniform closure. Iran is using energy transit as diplomatic leverage, country-by-country. This reduces total-closure tail risk but creates a more complex and persistent disruption pattern that's harder to resolve through coordinated policy. The Macron G7 call to coordinate response may address this but diplomatic fragmentation (different countries getting different access) undermines collective action.

The oil equity positioning question: Brief #55's "accelerated scale-out to 30-40% retained" was premature at $88. At $101, the original moderate scale-out from Brief #53 (50-60% retained) is the correct positioning. The energy sector remains a hedge against SPR exhaustion and re-escalation, but the $32 round-trip in five days ($88→$120→$88→$101) has demonstrated that energy positions generate portfolio-damaging volatility regardless of directional correctness. Professional macro traders lost $630M+ on this exact whipsaw. The appropriate response is position sizing discipline, not directional conviction changes.

### Credit Cascade: Four Fund Gating Events, Partners Group Default Warning

The credit deterioration thesis has accumulated evidence at an accelerating rate. In Brief #55 (yesterday), I counted 13 independent data points and assessed 48-53% cascade probability. Today adds:

- **14th data point:** Morgan Stanley capping withdrawals from private credit funds
- **15th data point:** Cliffwater capping withdrawals from private credit funds
- **16th data point (qualitative):** Partners Group chair warning default rates could double to >5%
- **17th data point (qualitative):** Goldman executive admitting clients welcome Iran war as "distraction" from valuation scrutiny

Four fund-level withdrawal restrictions (BlackRock, Starwood, MS, Cliffwater) across four different fund complexes is a pattern that cannot be explained by idiosyncratic factors. These are independent institutions reaching the same conclusion: redemption requests exceed their ability to liquidate underlying assets at current marks. The world model's 60-70% trigger ("two or more managers gate within 30 days") requires full gates rather than withdrawal caps. The caps are a precursor — they buy time to manage an orderly process, but they also signal to remaining investors that exit optionality is diminishing, which accelerates the incentive to request redemption.

Partners Group's >5% default rate warning is the most analytically important number. Current private credit default rates are approximately 2.5%. A doubling implies $75B+ in additional losses across the $3T market. This level of loss, if realized, would impair bank warehouse lines (the JPM markdown from Brief #55), reduce fund NAVs (validating Weinstein's 34.9% discount), and force additional fund-level restrictions. The self-reinforcing mechanism I described in Brief #55 is now being publicly articulated by the industry's own leadership.

I revise credit cascade probability to 55-60%, reflecting the withdrawal caps falling short of full gates but the volume and velocity of confirming evidence exceeding my prior framework's expectations.

**APO/BX remains the highest-conviction pair trade.** APO's Adani $500M bilateral bond deal from today's data demonstrates continued origination capacity during stress — permanent capital with deal flow. BX's simultaneous Atlas PE acquisition while facing semi-liquid fund withdrawal pressure demonstrates strategic expansion that relies on the same fundraising channels now showing stress. The fundamental divergence is widening.

### Supply Chain Broadening: Semiconductors, Pharma, Fertilizer, Fashion

The AP and SupplyChainBrain reports on supply chain disruption extending well beyond oil directly confirm and extend the sulphur thesis from Brief #55. Samsung Display's CEO warning on cost pressures is the first C-suite confirmation that semiconductor manufacturing costs are being affected. India's ₹5,000 crore pharma export impact is a quantified second-order effect. Fashion retail delays (Zara, H&M) represent a consumer-facing inflation channel that will hit CPI with 2-3 month lag through apparel prices.

The timing matters: if fertilizer disruption is occurring during Northern Hemisphere spring planting (March-May), farmers making input purchase decisions now face higher costs that get embedded in the 2026 crop production cost structure regardless of whether Hormuz reopens in June. Food price inflation from this channel has a 6-12 month persistence that outlasts the conflict. CF Industries' competitive advantage strengthens on both energy cost and sulphur availability dimensions simultaneously.

The semiconductor sulphur risk I flagged as 10-15% probability in Brief #55 should be upgraded to 15-20% given Samsung's CEO confirmation that cost pressures are materializing. The 4-8 week timeline for wafer start constraints remains speculative, but the direction is now confirmed by an industry source.

### CPI 2.4% + IEEPA Tariff Ruling: Inflation Trajectory Marginally Moderated

February CPI at 2.4% YoY is backward-looking and stale relative to the oil shock, as universally acknowledged. The more interesting development is the Supreme Court's IEEPA tariff ruling, which removes the near-term tariff inflation channel. The Bessent 15% tariff assumption in the world model's CPI forecast (+0.3-0.5%) is now delayed by the 6-12 months required for Section 301 investigations. This modestly reduces the combined peak CPI projection from 3.0-4.5% to approximately 2.8-4.2%.

However, the supply chain broadening into pharmaceuticals, fashion, and food partially replaces the tariff channel with a conflict-driven cost-push channel. The net effect on inflation trajectory is roughly neutral: lower tariff contribution, higher supply disruption contribution. The Fed's impossible calibration for March 18-19 FOMC remains: oil at $101 with 2.4% backward CPI, -92K NFP vs. 213K claims. The IEEPA ruling removes one uncertainty (tariffs) but the dominant drivers remain oil and labor market ambiguity.

### Gold's Unusual Non-Response

Gold stagnating despite the most significant military conflict in decades warrants attention but does not override the structural thesis. The explanation is straightforward: dollar strength from safe-haven flows and rising nominal yields are offsetting conflict-driven demand. Gold at ~$5,000 may have already priced the conflict premium when it surged initially, and the subsequent stagnation reflects the cross-current between inflation hedging and rate expectations. Ruffer's shift to credit spread hedges and yen over gold (from earlier data) suggests some institutional allocators are finding alternatives for crisis protection. I note this as a weakening signal but maintain the bullish thesis based on the more durable driver: sustained negative real rates from Fed hold + rising CPI.
