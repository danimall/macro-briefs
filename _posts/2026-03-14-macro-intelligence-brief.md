## Executive Summary

The Iran conflict entered its third week with Trump explicitly rejecting ceasefire negotiations, which eliminates the near-term diplomatic off-ramp that was already assigned only 15-22% probability. This is the single most important development in today's data: a ceasefire rejection by the commander-in-chief during active hostilities against a counterparty (Iran's new supreme leader) who has simultaneously vowed to keep Hormuz closed. The Q4 GDP revision to 0.7% — the weakest quarter since the pandemic recovery — arriving alongside 3.1% core PCE and $100+ oil creates the most unambiguous stagflationary data set the US economy has produced since the early 1980s. The March 18-19 FOMC now faces a policy environment with no good options: cut into 3.1% core PCE and risk credibility, hold while GDP prints 0.7% and employment contracts, or hint at a hike that would crash equity and housing markets. I expect a hold with maximally ambiguous forward guidance that satisfies no one.

Since the prior brief 12 hours ago, three data points shift the world model meaningfully. First, Saudi Arabia cutting production 20% to 8M bpd, whether defensive or deliberate, adds ~2M bpd to the disruption on top of Hormuz closure — total supply loss now approaches 7-9M bpd at peak, against which the SPR release provides roughly 10-14 days of coverage rather than the previously estimated 2.5-4.5 weeks. I revise the SPR exhaustion risk upward to 50-60%. Second, Turkey's NATO air defenses intercepting a third Iranian missile near a US base introduces Article 5 territory, a tail risk that would fundamentally alter the conflict's scope and market pricing. Third, Berkshire Hathaway signaling buyback resumption via SEC filing provides a concrete data point that the most patient capital allocator in markets sees current prices as a deployment opportunity.

The AI sector rotation Reuters confirmed is analytically consistent with the stagflationary regime: capital migrates from growth-duration assets (tech) to inflation-beneficiary assets (energy, materials, defense). Nvidia's GTC conference next week could temporarily interrupt this rotation, but the fundamental driver — $100+ oil repricing the economy's cost structure — persists until the conflict resolves. Meta's planned layoffs to fund AI infrastructure confirm the world model's hierarchy: hardware/power demand persists (CEG, EQIX, GEV benefit) while the application layer cannibalizes its own margins (CRM, WDAY, ACN hurt). Descartes Systems beating on supply chain software demand is the one positive counter-signal for software — companies that directly solve the supply chain disruption problem may be the exception to the broader SaaS deterioration.

## Key Events & Analysis

### Ceasefire Rejection + Kharg Island Strikes: The Conflict Duration Distribution Shifts

Trump's explicit rejection of ceasefire talks while simultaneously striking Iran's primary oil export terminal (Kharg Island, 90% of Iranian crude exports) sends two signals that partially contradict each other. The ceasefire rejection says "we're not done." The sparing of oil infrastructure on Kharg Island says "we're trying to limit escalation." This calibrated aggression — military targets on an oil island without hitting the oil facilities — is a narrow needle to thread, and it creates a persistent overhang: if escalation pressures increase, the oil infrastructure is already within demonstrated strike range. Markets must price in the probability that a future strike wave targets the export capacity itself.

Iran's retaliation against UAE's Fujairah oil loading hub broadens the physical disruption beyond Hormuz. Fujairah is a key alternative loading point for crude that bypasses the Strait — Iran is systematically eliminating the workarounds. Combined with the new supreme leader's vow to keep Hormuz closed (contradicted by the UN envoy's statement that Iran won't close it, and the Foreign Ministry's requirement for ships to coordinate with Iran's navy), the information environment is deliberately opaque. Iran benefits from this ambiguity: it allows selective enforcement (India-flagged tankers through, others blocked) while maintaining maximum uncertainty for insurance and shipping pricing.

Turkey's interception of a third Iranian missile near a NATO base hosting US troops is the day's most underappreciated risk factor. An Iranian missile actually striking a NATO base in Turkey would trigger Article 5 consultations, potentially drawing NATO into a wider military response. Even the near-miss elevates risk premiums across Mediterranean and Eastern European assets and increases the probability that the conflict extends beyond the bilateral US/Israel-Iran framework. I assign this 5-8% probability for an actual Article 5 trigger event within the next 30 days, which is low individually but represents a catastrophic tail if realized.

Revised conflict duration scenarios: rapid de-escalation within 1-2 weeks drops to 12% (from 18%) given ceasefire rejection. Negotiated wind-down across months holds at 30%. Acute phase extending beyond 6 weeks rises to 33% (from 30%). Continued escalation including Iraq theater and potential NATO involvement rises to 18% (from 15%). Nuclear escalation unchanged at 5-7%.

### Saudi Production Cut + SPR Arithmetic: The Buffer Is Shorter Than Estimated

Saudi Arabia's 20% production cut to 8M bpd removes approximately 2M bpd from global supply on top of the Hormuz-related disruption. Whether this cut reflects physical infrastructure damage (proximity to conflict zone, pipeline precaution) or strategic decision (OPEC+ member reducing output during price spike), the practical effect is the same: less oil available at any price.

Revised SPR buffer calculation: 400M barrel release against 7-9M bpd total supply loss (Hormuz closure + Saudi cut + Iran export disruption) implies a daily draw requirement of 4-6M bpd from reserves (after accounting for partial Hormuz transit and alternative routing). At this draw rate, the 400M barrel release provides 9-14 days of effective buffer, not the 2.5-4.5 weeks previously estimated. This is a material revision. If the US has been drawing since the conflict began (~10 days ago), the buffer may be substantially depleted already.

The Russian sanctions easing from yesterday's brief adds marginal supply (200-500K bpd of stranded crude), and Barclays' $85 Brent forecast appears stale against this supply arithmetic. The street is behind the physical market. Revised oil price path: floor $90-95 (ceasefire + Russian + alternative routing), ceiling $115-130 (SPR depletion + continued disruption + Saudi cut persists), central range $98-108.

### The Stagflation Data Package Is Complete

Q4 GDP at 0.7%, core PCE at 3.1%, NFP at -92K, retail sales -0.2%, gasoline up 21%, quit rates declining — the data set now describes every dimension of stagflation simultaneously: weak growth, high inflation, deteriorating employment, declining consumer spending, and workers afraid to leave their jobs. This is the first time all six signals have pointed in the stagflationary direction simultaneously since the data series began.

For the FOMC, this creates an unsolvable information matrix. The Taylor Rule points in two directions at once. Historical episodes of simultaneous negative NFP and above-target inflation (1974, 1980, 2008) all resulted in recession. The difference today is that the inflation driver (oil) is exogenous to monetary policy — neither a cut nor a hike addresses Strait of Hormuz disruption. This supports the prior brief's assessment that emergency cuts are low probability (3-5%) and that the FOMC will hold with maximally vague guidance.

Trump's demand for immediate rate cuts, combined with the DOJ's appeal of the ruling blocking Powell investigation subpoenas, introduces a new dimension of political risk to monetary policy. If the administration successfully pressures the Fed into cutting during 3.1% core PCE, it would damage the institution's inflation-fighting credibility. If the DOJ probe accelerates, it creates a constitutional crisis around central bank independence that could trigger foreign Treasury holder demand for higher term premium. Both scenarios are negative for long-duration fixed income.

### Berkshire Buyback: The Buffett Signal

Berkshire Hathaway's buyback resumption, announced via SEC proxy filing during the S&P 500's worst three-week stretch in a year, is a concrete capital allocation signal from the most watched patient investor in markets. With $373B in cash, the buyback represents a minuscule deployment, but the timing is significant: Buffett signaling that BRK.B shares are undervalued at current levels, during a period when the credit cascade thesis implies forced selling opportunities ahead. This validates the fortress-capital thesis from the world model and adds a third independent data point (cash accumulation + counter-cyclical history + buyback resumption) to the BRK.B conviction framework.

### Meta Layoffs: Hardware Validated, Application Layer Under Pressure

Meta planning workforce reductions to fund AI infrastructure spending confirms the world model's AI sector hierarchy. Hardware and power demand persists — Meta is not cutting capex, it's cutting people. This means: CEG, EQIX, GEV, COHR, LITE continue to benefit from infrastructure buildout. Simultaneously, the layoffs signal that AI has not yet generated enough productivity gain at Meta to justify maintaining headcount alongside the capex. If the company best positioned to monetize AI (2B+ daily users, massive engagement data) is cutting staff to afford the infrastructure, the case that AI saves margins for lesser-positioned enterprise software companies (CRM, WDAY, INTU) is further weakened. This compounds the ADBE read-through from yesterday: two of the best-positioned AI software companies (Meta for consumer, Adobe for creative professional) are both showing that AI investment destroys rather than creates near-term margin.

### Crypto Regulatory Infrastructure Maturing

The SEC-CFTC MOU, Indiana's Bitcoin retirement plan law, IRS expanded reporting requirements, BlackRock's staked ETH ETF, and Citi/MS custody expansion collectively represent five independent data points for institutional crypto infrastructure maturation. This is a theme that has accumulated sufficient evidence (5 data points from 4+ sources) to warrant upgrading from pure monitoring. COIN as the largest regulated US exchange is the most direct beneficiary of regulatory clarity + institutional expansion. The SEC-CFTC coordination specifically addresses the jurisdictional uncertainty that has been the primary barrier to institutional participation.
