## Key Events & Analysis

### Conflict Duration: Regime Change Rhetoric Removes Diplomatic Off-Ramp

Trump telling Reuters the US "should have a role in selecting Iran's next leader" and encouraging Kurdish opposition fundamentally changes the conflict's expected duration. This transforms the objective from "degrade Iran's nuclear/missile capability" to "regime change" — a mission that historically requires months to years (Iraq: 2003-2011, Libya: ongoing instability since 2011). Senator Warner's confirmation that the administration has no Phase Two plan means escalation is occurring without a defined end state.

The diplomatic signal from Brief #42 (Iran intelligence operatives open to CIA talks) has now been explicitly rejected by Iran's foreign minister, who rejected ceasefire talks. Combined with Senate backing and regime-change rhetoric, I revise de-escalation probability down from 12% to 8%. The mechanisms for conflict cessation have narrowed: no congressional constraint, no diplomatic channel, no defined military objective that ends the campaign.

This has direct pricing implications. Markets pricing "temporary disruption" in oil, shipping, and travel are systematically underestimating duration. A conflict with no defined end state generates sustained, not temporary, supply disruption premium.

### Oil: Convergence Accelerating, Goldman Institutional Signal

Crude >$100 by March end: Polymarket 42.5% (up from 30.5% this morning, 20.5% two days ago). My estimate: 45-55%. The gap has narrowed from 25-35pp to 3-13pp in 48 hours. The market is rapidly repricing physical reality.

Goldman Sachs raising Q2 Brent by $10 is institutionally significant because Goldman's commodity desk is among the most influential price-setting voices. When Goldman revises, institutional portfolios rebalance. This creates a self-reinforcing dynamic where the forecast revision itself drives buying.

The heavy crude premium surge deserves analysis. Buyers scrambling for non-Gulf heavy crude are paying premiums for Canadian, Latin American, and African grades. This benefits Canadian producers (Suncor, Imperial Oil, CNRL) and by extension the Canadian market (EWC). The US energy secretary characterizing the impact as "temporary" and a "small price" is verbal intervention that contradicts the physical evidence — six days of stranded tankers is not temporary by any definition of the word.

Bessent's escort/insurance plan faces "major obstacles" per analysts, confirming my Brief #44 assessment. Iran's shift from area-denial to direct tanker attacks means escorts cannot protect individual vessels against drone/missile strikes. This is the most important single dynamic in the oil market: if Iran can consistently target vessels despite Navy escorts, the insurance premium never normalizes and oil stays above $90+.

### Labor: NFP Consensus at 50K — Recession Gate Test

Economists expect just 50,000 jobs on Friday's NFP. If confirmed, this would be the third consecutive data point (ADP Jan 11K, ADP Feb 63K, NFP consensus 50K) showing employment creation well below the ~150K breakeven rate for population growth. The Challenger layoff data (down 55%) confirms employers aren't firing — they're frozen.

Kroger's earnings beat this thesis from the consumer side: profits exceeded expectations while revenue missed, meaning Kroger is managing margins through the consumer trade-down rather than growing the top line. This is exactly what happens in early-stage stagflation: companies protect margins while consumers contract spending.

If NFP prints below 100K, recession probability should move to 28-32%. The mechanism: hiring freeze → falling consumer income growth → oil/tariff cost increases → spending compression → further hiring pause → potential Q2 layoffs as the stasis breaks.
